Who Represents Whom in Court?
A Complete Guide to Legal Pleaders in India

Legal Desk April 5, 2026 12 min read Legal
India's court system — different representatives at every level

In Indian courts, a pleader is any person licensed and entitled to represent a party before judicial or quasi-judicial forums. The Advocates Act, 1961 and the Bharatiya Nagarik Suraksha Sanhita, 2023 (BNSS — successor to the CrPC) together govern who can appear at different stages of a proceeding — from Magistrate level all the way to the High Court. Which representative appears depends entirely on the nature of the case, the court before which it is listed, and the side being represented.

Understanding who does what — and at which stage — is indispensable to following any legal proceeding in India. This guide walks through each category of legal pleader, their statutory basis, their limitations, and their role in a live EC Act enforcement scenario from Odisha.

1. The Essential Commodities Act — Context

The Essential Commodities Act, 1955 (EC Act) empowers the Central and State Governments to regulate the production, supply, distribution, and pricing of commodities essential to public welfare — including foodgrains, sugar, edible oils, and fertilisers. Enforcement is a two-track process involving both administrative (quasi-judicial) action by the District Collector and criminal prosecution in courts.

Key Enforcement Officers

The Inspector of Supplies (Food Inspector / EC Inspector) is empowered to raid storage facilities, seize excess stocks, record statements, and file criminal complaints for violations of Control Orders under Section 3. The Collector (District Magistrate) exercises quasi-judicial powers under Section 6A to confiscate seized commodities, and must follow the show-cause procedure mandated by Section 6B.

Each of these authorities — the Inspector and the Collector — is represented by a different legal officer when matters reach the courts. Understanding this mapping is the key to following any EC Act proceeding.

2. Who Are the Pleaders?

India's system of public law officers creates a structured cadre of state representatives matched to the court's level and the nature of the proceedings. Here is a complete breakdown:

APP
Assistant Public Prosecutor

Represents the State and the police/Inspector of Supplies in Magistrate Courts (JMFC and CJM). The primary voice of criminal prosecution at the lowest rung of the court hierarchy. Examines witnesses, argues for conviction, and opposes bail at the Magistrate level.

Criminal JMFC / CJM
PP
Public Prosecutor

Represents the State in Sessions Court and High Court for criminal matters and criminal appeals. Handles Sessions trials, criminal revisions, and also defends the Collector's confiscation orders before judicial authorities appointed under Section 6C of the EC Act.

Criminal Sessions Court
GP
Government Pleader

Represents the State Government in civil and administrative matters in the High Court — including writ petitions, civil appeals, and challenges to executive orders. Defined under Section 2(7) of the Code of Civil Procedure, 1908. Does not handle criminal matters.

Civil Administrative High Court
AGP
Additional Govt. Pleader

Assists the GP in the High Court when civil and administrative caseload is heavy. Appears when the GP is engaged elsewhere or when multiple matters are listed simultaneously. Equal in function to the GP but subordinate in seniority.

Civil Administrative High Court
SPP
Special Public Prosecutor

Appointed by the State Government specifically for sensitive, high-profile, or special law cases — such as POCSO, NIA matters, large-scale EC Act violations, or cases involving organised hoarding syndicates. May be appointed at any court level. Governed by Section 24 of BNSS (formerly CrPC).

Criminal Any Court
Defence Advocate
Private Counsel / Legal Aid Lawyer

Represents the accused or petitioner at every stage and in every court. Either privately retained or assigned free of cost by the District Legal Services Authority (DLSA) under the Legal Services Authorities Act, 1987, for those unable to afford representation.

3. Statutory Basis — BNSS and CPC

Under BNSS 2023 (Criminal)
  • Section 18: PP and Additional PP for every High Court and Sessions Court — appointed by State/Central Government
  • Section 19: APP for Magistrate Courts — State Government appointment
  • Section 20: SPP — for specific cases at any court level
  • Minimum eligibility: 7 years as an Advocate
  • PP is not a mouthpiece of the investigating agency; independent duty to assist the court
Under CPC 1908 (Civil)
  • Section 2(7): Defines "Government Pleader" — any officer appointed by State Government to conduct civil proceedings on its behalf
  • GP handles civil suits, writ petitions, service matters, revenue appeals
  • AGP assists when GP is otherwise occupied
  • State Advocate General heads the government's legal representation at the High Court
  • GP role is purely civil/administrative — criminal matters fall to PP
Key principle: The Public Prosecutor under BNSS/CrPC is not merely a mouthpiece of the investigating officer or the complainant. The Supreme Court has consistently held that the PP's primary duty is to assist the court in arriving at the truth — not simply to secure a conviction. This distinction is important in EC Act cases where the Inspector's complaint must still be tested by independent prosecution scrutiny.

4. Stage 1 — Inspector Files Complaint (JMFC Court)

When the Inspector of Supplies raids a trader's godown and finds excess stocks in violation of a Control Order, he files a criminal complaint or police report before the Judicial Magistrate First Class (JMFC) — the entry point for criminal prosecution under the EC Act.

Real-World Example — Balangir, Odisha

Inspector of Supplies, Balangir raids trader Ramesh's godown and seizes 600 quintals of rice in excess of the permitted stock limit under the Odisha Rice (Control) Order, 2015 read with Section 3 of the EC Act. He files a criminal complaint before the JMFC Court, Balangir, under Section 7 of the EC Act.

SideRepresentativeRole at JMFC
State / Inspector of Supplies APP (Assistant Public Prosecutor) Presents the complaint, applies for remand, opposes bail, examines prosecution witnesses including PW-1 (the Inspector), and argues for conviction at trial
Accused — Ramesh Private Defence Advocate / Legal Aid Counsel Argues bail, cross-examines prosecution witnesses, challenges the complaint and seizure procedure, files discharge applications
Critical Distinction

The Inspector of Supplies does not argue the case himself. He has no locus standi to address the court as an advocate. His role at trial is as Prosecution Witness No. 1 (PW-1) — he is examined by the APP and cross-examined by the defence. The APP is his sole legal voice before the court.

5. Stage 2 — Collector Passes Confiscation Order (Administrative)

Simultaneously with — or following — the criminal complaint, the Collector (District Magistrate) exercises quasi-judicial administrative powers under the EC Act. This is a separate track from the criminal prosecution and proceeds independently.

Real-World Example — Balangir

The Inspector submits a seizure report to the Collector of Balangir. The Collector, under Section 6A, directs the production of the seized rice for inspection. Finding a clear violation, the Collector issues a show-cause notice under Section 6B. Ramesh fails to justify the excess stock. The Collector passes a confiscation order for 400 quintals.

No App / GP at this stage

The Collector's confiscation proceeding is an administrative quasi-judicial review — not a court proceeding. No APP or GP appears before the Collector. The State is already the decision-maker at this stage. Ramesh may be represented by his own private advocate before the Collector when making his written representation under Section 6B.

6. Stage 3 — Appeal under Section 6C (Judicial Authority)

Ramesh is aggrieved by the Collector's confiscation order. Under Section 6C of the EC Act, he may appeal within one month of communication of the order to the judicial authority appointed by the State Government. That authority must hear the appellant and may confirm, modify, or annul the confiscation order.

Section 6C — EC Act

"Any person aggrieved by an order of confiscation under section 6A may, within one month from the date of the communication to him of such order, appeal to any judicial authority appointed by the State Government concerned and the judicial authority shall, after giving an opportunity to the appellant to be heard, pass such order as it may think fit, confirming, modifying or annulling the order appealed against."

Section 6D — EC Act — Concurrent Proceedings

Section 6D expressly provides that the Collector's confiscation award does not prevent separate criminal punishment under the Act. Confiscation (administrative) and prosecution (criminal) run on entirely parallel tracks — a confiscation order is not a "conviction" and does not attract double jeopardy protection under Article 20(2) of the Constitution.

7. Stage 4 — Writ Petition in the High Court

If Ramesh remains aggrieved — whether because his Section 6C appeal failed or because he wishes to challenge the proceedings on constitutional grounds — he may approach the Orissa High Court, Cuttack through a Writ Petition under Article 226 of the Constitution or a Criminal Revision under Section 442 of the BNSS.

Real-World Example — Orissa High Court

Ramesh files a Writ Petition in the Orissa High Court arguing that the Collector passed the confiscation order without adequate opportunity of hearing, violating principles of natural justice, and that the Section 6B notice was defective. He seeks a stay of the confiscation order and ultimately its quashing.

SideRepresentativeRole at High Court
State of Odisha / Collector (Respondent) GP / AGP (Government Pleader) Defends the State and the Collector's order in the civil/administrative jurisdiction of the High Court; files counter-affidavit; argues that due process was followed and the order be sustained
Petitioner — Ramesh Private Advocate (High Court) Argues constitutional and procedural violations; seeks interim stay and final quashing of confiscation order; may also challenge the validity of the Control Order itself
Section 6E — Bar of Jurisdiction

Section 6E of the EC Act creates an exclusive jurisdiction clause: once goods are seized pending confiscation under Section 6A, no court, tribunal, or authority — other than the Collector (and appellate authority under Section 6C) — may make orders regarding possession, delivery, disposal, or release of the seized property. The High Court's writ jurisdiction survives, but it cannot direct release on administrative grounds while confiscation proceedings are pending.

10. Common Misconceptions

Common Mistakes
  • Assuming the Inspector argues the criminal case himself — he only testifies as PW-1
  • Treating the GP and PP as interchangeable — GP is civil only; PP is criminal only
  • Assuming that acquittal in a criminal case means return of confiscated goods — it does not (Section 6D)
  • Thinking the High Court can direct release of seized goods while Section 6A proceedings are pending (Section 6E bars this)
  • Confusing the Collector's Section 6A order with a "conviction" — it is administrative, not criminal
Correct Understanding
  • Inspector → files complaint and testifies as PW-1. APP → argues the prosecution case
  • GP appears only in civil/administrative High Court matters. PP handles criminal proceedings
  • Acquittal triggers the compensation mechanism under Section 6C(2), not automatic return of goods
  • Section 6E creates exclusive jurisdiction in the Collector and Section 6C authority over seized property
  • Confiscation and criminal conviction are independent consequences — both can co-exist (Section 6D)
"The Public Prosecutor is not a mere agent of the police or the investigating officer. His primary obligation is to the court and to justice — to see that the guilty are punished and that the innocent are protected." — Supreme Court of India (consistent line of judgments on the role of the PP)

The key principle to remember: The Inspector of Supplies never argues in court — he investigates, seizes, and testifies. It is the APP who is his legal voice at the Magistrate level, the PP at the Sessions/Section 6C level, and the GP or AGP when the matter reaches the High Court on the civil or administrative side. Understanding this mapping transforms a confusing web of abbreviations into a clear, logical system of state legal representation.
