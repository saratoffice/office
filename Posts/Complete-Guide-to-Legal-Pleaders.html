What is a Pleader?

In Indian courts, a pleader is any person entitled and licensed to represent a party before the courts. The Advocates Act and Code of Criminal Procedure (CrPC) govern who can appear at different stages — from the Magistrate level all the way up to the High Court. Which representative appears depends on the nature of the case, the court, and the side being represented.

The legal representatives of the state and defence are different individuals who step in at different stages of the process. Understanding who does what — and at which stage — is crucial to following any legal proceeding under Indian law.

The Essential Commodities Act — A Quick Context

The Essential Commodities Act, 1955 (EC Act) empowers designated officers to regulate and control the distribution of essential items such as food grain, sugar, and fertiliser under its provisions.

Key Powers

The Inspector of Supplies (also called Food Inspector or EC Inspector) is empowered to raid storage facilities, seize excess stocks, and file complaints for violations of Control Orders. The Collector (District Magistrate) holds quasi-judicial powers to confiscate seized commodities.

When the Inspector files a criminal complaint and the Collector exercises administrative powers — each has distinct legal representatives appearing on their behalf at different stages of a legal proceeding.

The Pleaders — Who Are They?

APP
Assistant Public Prosecutor

Represents the state and police/Inspector in Magistrate Courts. The primary voice of prosecution in JMFC and CJM courts.

Criminal Magistrate Court
PP
Public Prosecutor

Represents the state in Sessions Court and High Court for criminal matters. Handles sessions trials, criminal appeals, and revisions.

Criminal Sessions Court
GP
Government Pleader

Represents the State Government in civil and administrative matters in the High Court. Defends government entities challenged through writ petitions.

AGP

Assists the GP in the High Court when civil case load is high. Appears when GP is otherwise engaged elsewhere.

Appointed specifically for sensitive, high-profile, or special law cases such as POCSO, NIA, or large-scale EC Act violations.

The Stages — A Real EC Act Case

1

Inspector Files Complaint in JMFC Court

When the Inspector of Supplies raids a trader and finds excess stocks violating a Control Order, he files a complaint or police report before the Judicial Magistrate First Class (JMFC).

Real-world example

Inspector of Supplies, Balangir seizes 600 quintals of rice from trader Ramesh's godown under Section 6A of the EC Act and the Odisha Rice (Control) Order. He files a complaint before JMFC Balangir.

At the JMFC Court:

SideRepresentativeRole
State / Inspector of Supplies APP (Assistant Public Prosecutor) Presents the chargesheet, examines witnesses, argues for conviction at trial
Accused (Ramesh) Private Defence Advocate / Legal Aid Advocate Argues bail, cross-examines prosecution witnesses, challenges chargesheet
Important note

The Inspector of Supplies does not argue the case himself. He appears only as Prosecution Witness No. 1 (PW-1) and is examined by the APP and cross-examined by the defence. The APP is his legal voice in court.

2

Collector Passes Confiscation Order

Simultaneously or subsequently, the Collector (District Magistrate) exercises quasi-judicial administrative powers. Under Section 6B, no confiscation order shall be passed unless the owner is first given a written show-cause notice and an opportunity to make a representation. He may then issue a confiscation order under Section 6A. His order can include: confiscation of stocks, imposition of fines, and penalties on the firm.

Real-world example

Collector of Balangir issues show-cause notice to Ramesh. Ramesh fails to justify the excess stock. Collector passes an order confiscating 400 quintals of rice under Section 6A, EC Act.

This is an administrative / quasi-judicial review — not a court proceeding. No APP or GP appears at this stage; Ramesh may appoint his own advocate before the Collector.

3

Accused Appeals Confiscation — Section 6C (Judicial Authority)

Ramesh is aggrieved by the Collector's confiscation order. Under Section 6C of the EC Act, any person aggrieved by a confiscation order under Section 6A may, within one month from the date of communication of such order, appeal to the judicial authority appointed by the State Government. That judicial authority shall give the appellant an opportunity to be heard and may confirm, modify, or annul the order.

Section 6C — EC Act (verbatim)

"Any person aggrieved by an order of confiscation under section 6A may, within one month from the date of the communication to him of such order, appeal to any judicial authority appointed by the State Government concerned and the judicial authority shall, after giving an opportunity to the appellant to be heard, pass such order as it may think fit, confirming, modifying or annulling the order appealed against."

Section 6D — EC Act

Section 6D clarifies that the award of confiscation by the Collector does not prevent separate criminal punishment under the Act. In other words, confiscation (civil/administrative) and criminal prosecution run on parallel tracks — one does not bar the other.

Collector's Confiscation Order (Section 6A, EC Act) — aggrieved party files appeal within 1 month
↓
Judicial Authority appointed by State Govt. (Section 6C, EC Act) — confirms, modifies, or annuls order
↓
Orissa High Court, Cuttack — Writ / Revision if still aggrieved

At the Section 6C Judicial Authority (appointed by State Govt.):

SideRepresentativeRole
State / Collector (respondent) PP (Public Prosecutor) Defends the Collector's Section 6A confiscation order before the judicial authority; argues for upholding the government decision
Accused / Appellant (Ramesh) Private Defence Advocate Challenges the Collector's order on merits and procedure; seeks quashing of confiscation
4

Writ Petition in High Court

If Ramesh is still aggrieved, he can approach the Orissa High Court, Cuttack in its extraordinary writ jurisdiction, challenging the confiscation order as being contrary to principles of natural justice.

Real-world example

Ramesh files a Writ Petition in Orissa High Court arguing that the Collector passed the confiscation order without providing adequate opportunity of hearing — a violation of the principles of natural justice.

SideRepresentativeRole
State of Orissa / Collector (respondent) GP / AGP (Government Pleader) Defends the State and Collector; argues that due process was followed and the order be sustained
Petitioner (Ramesh) Private Advocate Argues constitutional/procedural violations before the High Court; seeks stay or quashing of confiscation order

The Complete Court Hierarchy

Orissa High Court, Cuttack — GP / AGP vs Private Advocate (Writ / Revision)
Judicial Authority (Sessions Court), Balangir — PP vs Defence Advocate (Section 6C Appeal against Confiscation / Criminal Trial)
SDJM / CJM Court, Balangir — APP vs Defence Advocate (Criminal EC Act prosecution)
JMFC Court, Balangir — APP vs Defence Advocate (Remand, Bail, Chargesheet)
Inspector of Supplies — Raids godown, seizes stock, files complaint (PW-1 in trial)

Key principle to remember: The Inspector of Supplies never argues in court — he only investigates, seizes, and testifies as a witness. It is the APP who is his legal representative at the Magistrate level, the PP at the Sessions level, and the GP/AGP when the matter reaches the High Court on the civil or administrative side.
