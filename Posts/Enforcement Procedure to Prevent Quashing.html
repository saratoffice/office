Enforcement Procedure to Prevent Quashing of EC Act Cases

Sarat Rout April 11, 2026 14 min read Essential Commodities Act
Proper seizure procedure is the cornerstone of a prosecution-proof EC Act case — a single procedural lapse can lead to quashing at the High Court

High Courts across India routinely quash prosecutions under the Essential Commodities Act, 1955 — not because the accused is innocent, but because the enforcement officer failed to follow mandatory procedural steps during seizure, sampling, and investigation. The Bombay High Court in Dhanraj v. State of Maharashtra (2016) is unambiguous: "In absence of it being shown that there was any order made under Section 3 of the said Act that had been contravened, the proceedings for an offence punishable under Section 7 … would not be tenable."

This guide consolidates the mandatory steps that Supply Inspectors and enforcement officers must follow during raids, seizures, and post-seizure investigation — together with the five most common procedural mistakes that allow defence counsel to seek quashing.

1. Legal Framework

Essential Commodities Act, 1955 — Key Provisions

Section 3: Empowers the Central/State Government to issue Control Orders regulating production, supply, distribution, and price of essential commodities. Every prosecution must cite the specific Control Order violated.

Section 7: Prescribes penalties for contravening any Control Order — minimum 3 months imprisonment, up to 7 years, plus fine. For a repeat conviction, the minimum rises to 6 months. Forfeiture of property and vehicles used in the offence may also be ordered by the court.

Section 10A: Every offence under the Act is cognizable — police may arrest without a warrant.

Section 11: No court shall take cognizance of any offence except on a written report by a public servant, an aggrieved person, or a recognised consumer association. A Collector's sanction is not required for prosecution under Section 7.

The EC Act requires a specific Control Order violation — mere seizure is not enough for prosecution under Section 7
High Courts routinely quash EC Act prosecutions where mandatory procedural steps have not been followed
Dhanraj s/o Anandrao Mohod v. State of Maharashtra
Criminal Application (APL) No. 728 of 2015 · Bombay High Court (Nagpur Bench) · March 16, 2016
Citation: 2016 ALLMR(Cri) 2208

"In absence of it being shown that there was any order made under Section 3 of the said Act that had been contravened, the proceedings for an offence punishable under Section 7 of the said Act would not be tenable. The continuation of these proceedings therefore would amount to an abuse of the process of law."
Part A

Part A — Mandatory Steps During Enforcement & Investigation

Every enforcement action under the EC Act — whether a raid on a godown, interception of a vehicle, or inspection of a distributor's premises — must follow a strict sequence of procedural steps. Each step below represents a requirement whose omission has, in reported cases, led to quashing of the prosecution.

1 Preparation of the Seizure List

a. Presence of Witnesses

The seizure list must be prepared in the presence of at least two independent witnesses. Witnesses must be genuinely independent — not colleagues of the enforcement officer or employees of the accused. Their full names, addresses, and contact details must be recorded.

b. Complete Address Details

The full address of all concerned persons — the person from whose possession the commodity is seized, the vehicle owner, the transporter — must be recorded in the seizure list. Merely recording a name or "C/o" (care of) is legally insufficient and opens the door to identity challenges at trial.

c. Time Gap Requirement

A clear and reasonable time gap must be maintained and recorded between each stage:

  • Time of interception / detection
  • Time of weighment
  • Time of formal seizure
  • Time of Zimanama (custody handover to Zimadar)

Gaps that are too short raise credibility concerns; unexplained gaps raise chain-of-custody questions. Both give defence counsel grounds for challenge.

d. Seizure of Vehicles — LPG / SK Oil Cases

  • Where a vehicle has been used for transportation or illegal use of LPG or SK oil, it must be clearly stated in the seizure list that complete drainage of SK oil has been carried out before any weighment.
  • If weighment cannot be conducted at the spot, maintain a proper time gap and record the reason. Since the fuel has been fully drained, the vehicle must be towed to the police station using another vehicle (e.g., tractor) — not driven on the seized fuel.
  • LPG cylinders must be detached from the vehicle before shifting.
  • The vehicle should be moved using alternate fuel if available; otherwise towed.

e. Weighment Procedure — LPG

Weighment of LPG cylinders may be conducted at an authorised LPG distributor's premises, ensuring a proper and recorded time gap between seizure and weighment.

f. Vehicle Description in Seizure List

The seizure list must record:

  • Colour of the vehicle
  • Make and model of the vehicle
  • Direction of movement at the time of interception

g. Details in the Final Report

The final report / chargesheet submitted to the court must contain the engine number and chassis number of any seized vehicle. Without these, vehicle identity cannot be established at trial.

h. Acknowledgement of Seizure

After preparation of the seizure list, a copy must be handed over to the person from whom the commodity was seized, and a signed acknowledgement of receipt must be obtained from that person in the presence of witnesses.

2 Mention of Sample Quantity

The seizure list must clearly specify:

  • The number of sample bottles collected
  • The exact quantity of liquid in each bottle (e.g., 1.5 litres)
  • The description and quantity of all other seized items
Why This Matters

Any mismatch between the quantity recorded in the seizure list and the quantity later mentioned in the FSL report gives defence counsel grounds to challenge the authenticity of the sample tested. Courts have quashed cases on this basis alone.

3 Consistency Between FIR and FSL Report

Investigating officers must ensure that the sample quantity recorded in the FIR and seizure list exactly matches the quantity mentioned in the FSL (Forensic Science Laboratory) report.

Classic Discrepancy — Leading to Quashing

Example: FIR states that 1.5 litres were collected in each bottle (2 bottles = 3 litres). FSL report arrives stating that three bottles were received, each containing 750 ml. The mismatch in both the number of bottles and the quantity per bottle creates reasonable doubt and is routinely exploited by defence counsel to challenge the chain of custody.

Such discrepancies do not require the accused to prove innocence — they are enough to shift the burden back onto the prosecution and often result in acquittal or quashing at the revision or writ stage.

4 Mandatory Chemical Analysis

Whenever suspected PDS kerosene oil or any petroleum product is seized from a person other than an authorised retailer or wholesaler, representative samples must be taken and sent for chemical analysis to the Regional Forensic Science Laboratory (RFSL).

Legal Position

Chemical analysis is not merely advisable — it is the essential evidentiary link that establishes what the seized commodity actually is. Without a valid FSL report, the prosecution cannot prove that the substance seized was indeed the essential commodity alleged in the FIR. Courts have quashed cases where samples were not sent or the chain of custody to the RFSL was broken.

5 Preparation of Sample Slip

A sample slip must be prepared at the time of seizure — not later at the office. It must contain:

  • Number of bottles collected
  • Exact quantity in each bottle
  • Description of the commodity (kerosene, diesel, LPG, etc.)
  • Signatures of the accused
  • Signatures of at least two independent witnesses
  • Signature of the Zimadar (custodian of seized property)
  • Date and time of preparation
  • Place of preparation (exact location)
Sealing Requirement

Sample bottles must be properly sealed in the presence of witnesses. The seal must remain intact until opened at the RFSL. Any broken seal at the laboratory will render the analysis results challengeable.

6 Delivery of One Sample Bottle to the Accused

Out of the sample bottles collected, one bottle must be delivered to the person from whose possession the commodity was seized. This is a mandatory legal requirement that allows the accused to get the sample independently tested if they wish to challenge the RFSL result.

  • A signed receipt acknowledging delivery must be obtained from the person
  • The receipt must be signed in the presence of witnesses
  • The delivery must be at the time of seizure — not at the police station later
Failure = Prosecution Risk

Failure to give one sample to the accused is a procedural irregularity that courts have used to cast doubt on the fairness of the sampling process. It is also relevant to the question of whether the accused's right to a fair trial was respected.

7 Mention of Sample Delivery in the FIR

The FIR must expressly state:

  • That one sample bottle was handed over to the person from whose possession the commodity was seized
  • The person's name and acknowledgement of receipt
  • The presence of witnesses at the time of delivery

If this is not reflected in the FIR, the fact of delivery cannot be proven at trial even if it actually occurred.

8 Reporting Dispatch of Samples to the Collector

The investigating officer must submit a report to the Collector of the District under Section 6A of the EC Act, stating:

  • The date on which sample bottles were dispatched to the RFSL
  • The mode of dispatch
  • The reference number or forwarding letter number
Section 6A Requirement

Section 6A of the EC Act requires that a report of seizure be made to the Collector "without unreasonable delay." The Collector may then direct production for inspection and order confiscation if satisfied of the contravention. This report and the dispatch information together establish the chain of custody for the sample.

9 Documents to Accompany the FIR

The FIR should be supported by the following documents wherever applicable:

  • Sample slip — prepared at the time of seizure
  • Seizure list — signed by witnesses, accused, and officer
  • Weighment chart — if weighment was conducted separately
  • Zimanama — the bond for custody of seized property, identifying the Zimadar responsible for custody
  • Forwarding letter to RFSL — showing date and mode of sample dispatch
  • Acknowledgement of one bottle given to accused
Documentation = Prosecution Strength

Each document is a link in the evidentiary chain. A missing document does not necessarily destroy the prosecution — but it creates a gap that defence counsel will exploit. Courts have quashed cases where the Zimanama was absent or the RFSL forwarding letter was not produced.

10 Recording Statement of the Person Concerned

The statement of the person from whose possession the commodity is seized must be recorded during investigation. The investigating officer must also determine and record:

  • Whether the transportation was done with the knowledge of the vehicle owner
  • The source of procurement of the PDS commodity (who sold it, at what price, with or without a bill)
  • Whether the person had any valid licence, permit, or authority to possess the commodity
Mandatory Reading Over

The statement must be read over and explained to the person concerned in their own language before their signature is obtained. Under Section 161 BNSS (formerly CrPC), any statement recorded without reading over is challengeable at trial.

11 Timely Completion of Investigation

After registration of the FIR, the investigating officer must:

  • Promptly conduct further investigation — record statements, collect documents, obtain FSL report
  • Submit a prosecution report (chargesheet) under Section 7 of the EC Act before the competent court — preferably within two months of the FIR
  • Record reasons for any unavoidable delay
Delay = Grounds for Relief

Unexplained delay in investigation is one of the five most common grounds on which courts grant bail and ultimately relief to the accused. If delay is unavoidable (e.g., awaiting FSL report), the investigating officer should file an interim chargesheet and update the court periodically.

12 Collection of Identification Details

The investigating officer must collect and record the following for every person involved in the case:

  • Accused / delinquent: mobile number, photo identity (Aadhaar / Voter ID / Driving Licence)
  • Owner of the vehicle: mobile number, RC book details, photo identity
  • Independent witnesses: mobile number, photo identity

These details must be reflected in both the FIR and the seizure list. Without contact and identity details, witnesses cannot be summoned for trial, and the prosecution case collapses.

Godown inspection — the seizure list must record every detail including witness names, commodity quantity, and time of each stage
Sample collection and FSL dispatch — the chain of custody from seizure to laboratory result is the spine of every EC Act prosecution
Part B

Part B — Five Common Investigation Mistakes That Lead to Quashing

The following five mistakes are the most frequently cited grounds in High Court petitions seeking quashing of EC Act prosecutions. Each is supported by reported judgments.

1 Failure to Cite the Specific Control Order in the FIR

High Courts — including the Bombay, Rajasthan, and Karnataka High Courts — have consistently quashed cases where the FIR does not mention the exact Control Order issued under Section 3 that has allegedly been violated.

An offence under Section 7 of the EC Act has no independent existence. It is triggered only by the contravention of a specific Control Order. If the FIR simply says "the accused was found in possession of kerosene" without citing the Control Order that prohibits such possession by that person, the prosecution is legally unsustainable.

Dhanraj v. State of Maharashtra · Bombay HC · 2016 ALLMR(Cri) 2208
FIR contained no reference to any order under Section 3. Court held proceedings not tenable — quashed as abuse of process.
Correct Practice

The FIR must specifically name the Control Order (e.g., "Kerala Rationing Order, 1966, Clause 5A" or "LPG (Regulation of Supply and Distribution) Order, 2000, Clause 6") and state how the accused's act violated a specific clause of that Order.

2 FIR Based Only on Seizure Without Proving Violation

Mere seizure of an essential commodity does not automatically constitute an offence under the EC Act. The Calcutta High Court quashed a conviction where the prosecution failed to establish mens rea and could not show that any specific prohibition had been contravened.

The investigation must actively establish:

  • How the commodity was diverted, stored illegally, or transported in violation of the Control Order
  • Whether the accused had any licence, permit, or other authority to possess the commodity
  • The source of procurement and the chain of supply
Calcutta High Court (Justice Subhendu Samanta) · November 2023
Conviction quashed — mere omission of licence number in cash memos did not constitute an offence; mens rea was not established. (Source: SCC Online Blog, November 23, 2023)

3 Improper Sampling Procedure

Failure to follow the mandatory sampling procedure — including any of the following — may result in the chemical analysis report being challenged and excluded from evidence:

  • Not preparing the sample slip at the spot
  • Not sealing the sample bottles properly in the presence of witnesses
  • Not giving one sample bottle to the accused at the time of seizure
  • Discrepancy between the number/quantity of bottles recorded in the seizure list and the FSL report
Punjab & Haryana HC · Justice Manjari Nehru Kaul · 2023
"Any search and seizure not conducted in accordance with law … would hold no weight during trial. … Owing to the defective recovery process, there would arise no possibility of imposition of penalty under Section 7 of the Essential Commodities Act." (Source: LiveLaw, December 27, 2023)

4 Break in Chain of Custody of Samples

If there is no clear, documented record of when and how the samples were sent from the seizure site to the RFSL, courts may doubt the authenticity of the sample tested. Any break in the chain of custody — missing forwarding letter, undated dispatch, unlabelled bottles — gives the defence an opening to argue that the sample tested was not the sample seized.

The chain of custody requires clear documentation of:

  • Seizure — who collected, what quantity, in what container
  • Storage — who had custody (Zimadar), where stored
  • Dispatch — date, mode, forwarding letter reference
  • Receipt at RFSL — acknowledgement from the laboratory
  • Analysis — result referencing the specific sample

5 Delay in Investigation and Filing of Prosecution Report

Unexplained delay in completing the investigation or filing the prosecution report before the court weakens the prosecution case and may lead courts to grant bail, subsequently acquit, or quash on grounds of violation of the accused's right to a speedy trial.

Delay is particularly damaging when:

  • The accused has been in custody during the delay
  • The delay cannot be attributed to any genuine cause (e.g., awaiting FSL report)
  • The delay is so long that witnesses' memories are unreliable
Best Practice

File an interim chargesheet within the statutory period (generally 60 days for offences punishable with more than 10 years; 90 days for others under BNSS). If the FSL report is pending, note this in the interim chargesheet and request the court to await the full report before framing charges.

🎯 Conclusion

Procedural compliance is not a technicality — it is the foundation of every successful prosecution under the Essential Commodities Act. High Courts across India have made clear, in judgment after judgment, that the Act's penal provisions can only be invoked where a specific Control Order has been identified, proper seizure and sampling procedure has been followed, and the investigation has been completed without unexplained delay.

Strict adherence to the twelve mandatory steps in Part A ensures transparency and fairness in enforcement, preserves the integrity of evidence, and significantly reduces the likelihood of criminal cases being quashed on procedural grounds. The five-point awareness in Part B equips enforcement officers to pre-empt the most common legal vulnerabilities before they are exploited in court.
