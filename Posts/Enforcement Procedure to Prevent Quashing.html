Enforcement Procedure to Prevent Quashing of EC Act Cases

Sarat Rout April 11, 2026 14 min read Essential Commodities Act
Proper seizure procedure is the cornerstone of a prosecution-proof EC Act case — a single procedural lapse can lead to quashing at the High Court

High Courts across India routinely quash prosecutions under the Essential Commodities Act, 1955 — not because the accused is innocent, but because the enforcement officer failed to follow mandatory procedural steps during seizure, sampling, and investigation. The Bombay High Court in Dhanraj v. State of Maharashtra (2016) is unambiguous: "In absence of it being shown that there was any order made under Section 3 of the said Act that had been contravened, the proceedings for an offence punishable under Section 7 … would not be tenable."

This guide consolidates the mandatory steps that Supply Inspectors and enforcement officers must follow during raids, seizures, and post-seizure investigation — together with the five most common procedural mistakes that allow defence counsel to seek quashing.

1. Legal Framework

Essential Commodities Act, 1955 — Key Provisions

Section 3: Empowers the Central/State Government to issue Control Orders regulating production, supply, distribution, and price of essential commodities. Every prosecution must cite the specific Control Order violated.

Section 7: Prescribes penalties for contravening any Control Order — minimum 3 months imprisonment, up to 7 years, plus fine. For a repeat conviction, the minimum rises to 6 months. Forfeiture of property and vehicles used in the offence may also be ordered by the court.

Section 10A: Every offence under the Act is cognizable — police may arrest without a warrant.

Section 11: No court shall take cognizance of any offence except on a written report by a public servant, an aggrieved person, or a recognised consumer association. A Collector's sanction is not required for prosecution under Section 7.

The EC Act requires a specific Control Order violation — mere seizure is not enough for prosecution under Section 7
High Courts routinely quash EC Act prosecutions where mandatory procedural steps have not been followed
Dhanraj s/o Anandrao Mohod v. State of Maharashtra
Criminal Application (APL) No. 728 of 2015 · Bombay High Court (Nagpur Bench) · March 16, 2016
Citation: 2016 ALLMR(Cri) 2208

"In absence of it being shown that there was any order made under Section 3 of the said Act that had been contravened, the proceedings for an offence punishable under Section 7 of the said Act would not be tenable. The continuation of these proceedings therefore would amount to an abuse of the process of law."
Part A

Part A — Mandatory Steps During Enforcement & Investigation

Every enforcement action under the EC Act — whether a raid on a godown, interception of a vehicle, or inspection of a distributor's premises — must follow a strict sequence of procedural steps. Each step below represents a requirement whose omission has, in reported cases, led to quashing of the prosecution.

1 Preparation of the Seizure List

a. Presence of Witnesses

The seizure list must be prepared in the presence of at least two independent witnesses. Witnesses must be genuinely independent — not colleagues of the enforcement officer or employees of the accused. Their full names, addresses, and contact details must be recorded.

b. Complete Address Details

The full address of all concerned persons — the person from whose possession the commodity is seized, the vehicle owner, the transporter — must be recorded in the seizure list. Merely recording a name or "C/o" (care of) is legally insufficient and opens the door to identity challenges at trial.

c. Time Gap Requirement

A clear and reasonable time gap must be maintained and recorded between each stage:

  • Time of interception / detection
  • Time of weighment
  • Time of formal seizure
  • Time of Zimanama (custody handover to Zimadar)

d. Seizure of Vehicles — LPG / SK Oil Cases

  • Where a vehicle has been used for transportation or illegal use of LPG or SK oil, it must be clearly stated in the seizure list that complete drainage of SK oil has been carried out before any weighment.
  • If weighment cannot be conducted at the spot, maintain a proper time gap and record the reason. Since the fuel has been fully drained, the vehicle must be towed to the police station using another vehicle — not driven on the seized fuel.
  • LPG cylinders must be detached from the vehicle before shifting.

e. Weighment Procedure — LPG

Weighment of LPG cylinders may be conducted at an authorised LPG distributor's premises, ensuring a proper and recorded time gap between seizure and weighment.

f. Vehicle Description in Seizure List

The seizure list must record: colour, make and model, and direction of movement at the time of interception.

g. Details in the Final Report

The final report / chargesheet submitted to the court must contain the engine number and chassis number of any seized vehicle.

h. Acknowledgement of Seizure

After preparation of the seizure list, a copy must be handed over to the person from whom the commodity was seized, and a signed acknowledgement of receipt must be obtained in the presence of witnesses.

2 Mention of Sample Quantity

The seizure list must clearly specify the number of sample bottles collected, the exact quantity of liquid in each bottle, and the description and quantity of all other seized items.

Why This Matters

Any mismatch between the quantity recorded in the seizure list and the quantity later mentioned in the FSL report gives defence counsel grounds to challenge the authenticity of the sample tested. Courts have quashed cases on this basis alone.

3 Consistency Between FIR and FSL Report

Investigating officers must ensure that the sample quantity recorded in the FIR and seizure list exactly matches the quantity mentioned in the FSL (Forensic Science Laboratory) report.

Classic Discrepancy — Leading to Quashing

Example: FIR states that 1.5 litres were collected in each bottle (2 bottles = 3 litres). FSL report arrives stating that three bottles were received, each containing 750 ml. The mismatch creates reasonable doubt and is routinely exploited by defence counsel to challenge the chain of custody.

4 Mandatory Chemical Analysis

Whenever suspected PDS kerosene oil or any petroleum product is seized from a person other than an authorised retailer or wholesaler, representative samples must be taken and sent for chemical analysis to the Regional Forensic Science Laboratory (RFSL).

Legal Position

Chemical analysis is the essential evidentiary link that establishes what the seized commodity actually is. Without a valid FSL report, the prosecution cannot prove that the substance seized was indeed the essential commodity alleged in the FIR.

5 Preparation of Sample Slip

A sample slip must be prepared at the time of seizure — not later at the office. It must contain: number of bottles, exact quantity per bottle, commodity description, signatures of the accused, at least two independent witnesses, Zimadar, date/time, and exact place.

Sealing Requirement

Sample bottles must be properly sealed in the presence of witnesses. The seal must remain intact until opened at the RFSL. Any broken seal at the laboratory will render the analysis results challengeable.

6 Delivery of One Sample Bottle to the Accused

Out of the sample bottles collected, one bottle must be delivered to the person from whose possession the commodity was seized. A signed receipt acknowledging delivery must be obtained, signed in the presence of witnesses, at the time of seizure.

Failure = Prosecution Risk

Failure to give one sample to the accused is a procedural irregularity that courts have used to cast doubt on the fairness of the sampling process and the accused's right to a fair trial.

7 Mention of Sample Delivery in the FIR

The FIR must expressly state that one sample bottle was handed over to the person from whose possession the commodity was seized, along with the person's name, acknowledgement of receipt, and presence of witnesses at the time of delivery.

8 Reporting Dispatch of Samples to the Collector

The investigating officer must submit a report to the Collector of the District under Section 6A of the EC Act, stating the date of dispatch to the RFSL, mode of dispatch, and forwarding letter reference number.

Section 6A Requirement

Section 6A requires that a report of seizure be made to the Collector "without unreasonable delay." The Collector may then direct production for inspection and order confiscation if satisfied of the contravention.

9 Documents to Accompany the FIR

The FIR should be supported by: sample slip, seizure list, weighment chart, Zimanama, forwarding letter to RFSL, and acknowledgement of one bottle given to accused.

Documentation = Prosecution Strength

Each document is a link in the evidentiary chain. Courts have quashed cases where the Zimanama was absent or the RFSL forwarding letter was not produced.

10 Recording Statement of the Person Concerned

The statement must be recorded and must determine: whether the vehicle owner had knowledge of the transportation, the source of procurement of the PDS commodity, and whether the person had any valid licence or permit.

Mandatory Reading Over

The statement must be read over and explained to the person concerned in their own language before their signature is obtained. Under Section 161 BNSS, any statement recorded without reading over is challengeable at trial.

11 Timely Completion of Investigation

Submit a prosecution report (chargesheet) under Section 7 of the EC Act before the competent court — preferably within two months of the FIR. Record reasons for any unavoidable delay.

Delay = Grounds for Relief

Unexplained delay is one of the five most common grounds on which courts grant bail. If delay is unavoidable, file an interim chargesheet and update the court periodically.

12 Collection of Identification Details

Record mobile number and photo identity (Aadhaar / Voter ID / Driving Licence) for the accused, vehicle owner, and all independent witnesses. Without contact and identity details, witnesses cannot be summoned for trial.

Godown inspection — the seizure list must record every detail including witness names, commodity quantity, and time of each stage
Sample collection and FSL dispatch — the chain of custody from seizure to laboratory result is the spine of every EC Act prosecution
Part B

Part B — Five Common Investigation Mistakes That Lead to Quashing

The following five mistakes are the most frequently cited grounds in High Court petitions seeking quashing of EC Act prosecutions.

1 Failure to Cite the Specific Control Order in the FIR

High Courts have consistently quashed cases where the FIR does not mention the exact Control Order issued under Section 3 that has allegedly been violated. An offence under Section 7 is triggered only by the contravention of a specific Control Order.

Dhanraj v. State of Maharashtra · Bombay HC · 2016 ALLMR(Cri) 2208
FIR contained no reference to any order under Section 3. Court held proceedings not tenable — quashed as abuse of process.
Correct Practice

The FIR must specifically name the Control Order and state how the accused's act violated a specific clause of that Order.

2 FIR Based Only on Seizure Without Proving Violation

Mere seizure of an essential commodity does not automatically constitute an offence. The investigation must actively establish how the commodity was diverted or stored illegally, whether the accused had any licence, and the source of procurement.

Calcutta High Court (Justice Subhendu Samanta) · November 2023
Conviction quashed — mere omission of licence number in cash memos did not constitute an offence; mens rea was not established.

3 Improper Sampling Procedure

Failure to follow the mandatory sampling procedure — not preparing the sample slip at the spot, not sealing bottles in the presence of witnesses, not giving one sample bottle to the accused, or discrepancy between seizure list and FSL report — may result in the chemical analysis being excluded from evidence.

Punjab & Haryana HC · Justice Manjari Nehru Kaul · 2023
"Any search and seizure not conducted in accordance with law … would hold no weight during trial. … Owing to the defective recovery process, there would arise no possibility of imposition of penalty under Section 7."

4 Break in Chain of Custody of Samples

If there is no clear, documented record of when and how the samples were sent from the seizure site to the RFSL, courts may doubt the authenticity of the sample tested. The chain of custody requires documentation of seizure, storage (Zimadar), dispatch (forwarding letter), RFSL receipt, and analysis result referencing the specific sample.

5 Delay in Investigation and Filing of Prosecution Report

Unexplained delay in completing the investigation or filing the prosecution report weakens the case and may lead courts to grant bail, acquit, or quash on grounds of violation of the right to a speedy trial.

Best Practice

File an interim chargesheet within the statutory period. If the FSL report is pending, note this in the interim chargesheet and request the court to await the full report before framing charges.

🎯 Conclusion

Procedural compliance is not a technicality — it is the foundation of every successful prosecution under the Essential Commodities Act. High Courts across India have made clear, in judgment after judgment, that the Act's penal provisions can only be invoked where a specific Control Order has been identified, proper seizure and sampling procedure has been followed, and the investigation has been completed without unexplained delay.

Strict adherence to the twelve mandatory steps in Part A ensures transparency and fairness in enforcement, preserves the integrity of evidence, and significantly reduces the likelihood of criminal cases being quashed on procedural grounds. The five-point awareness in Part B equips enforcement officers to pre-empt the most common legal vulnerabilities before they are exploited in court.

Annexure–J
General Guidelines for Enforcement Officers Guiding principles for all enforcement actions under the EC Act, 1955
  • Follow Due Legal Procedure
    Every enforcement action must be conducted strictly in accordance with the provisions of the Essential Commodities Act, 1955, the applicable Control Order, and the Bharatiya Nagarik Suraksha Sanhita, 2023 (BNSS). Any deviation from prescribed procedure is grounds for challenge in court.
  • Maintain Transparency
    All actions — from interception to the preparation of the chargesheet — must be conducted openly and in the presence of witnesses. No stage of the enforcement process should be conducted in private or in the absence of the person concerned.
  • Ensure Proper Documentation
    Complete and accurate documentation is the most important safeguard against quashing. Use standard templates (Annexures A–J). Record every detail — time, quantity, names, addresses — at the time and place of the event, not subsequently at the office.
  • Avoid Procedural Lapses
    The five common mistakes detailed in Part B of this guide — failure to cite the Control Order, improper sampling, missing Zimanama, broken chain of custody, and unexplained delay — are the most frequently exploited grounds for quashing. Every officer must be aware of and actively avoid these lapses.
  • Maintain the Chain of Custody
    From the moment a sample is collected to the receipt of the FSL report, every transfer of custody must be documented — who held the sample, where it was stored, when and how it was dispatched, and acknowledgement of receipt at the RFSL. A broken chain of custody can destroy an otherwise strong case.
  • Treat All Persons with Dignity
    Enforcement officers must not threaten, humiliate, or use force beyond what is permitted by law. Any allegation of misconduct — regardless of whether it is ultimately proved — weakens the prosecution case and may lead to adverse findings by the court.
  • File Reports Without Unreasonable Delay
    The seizure report to the Collector (Section 6A) and the prosecution report to the court (Section 11) must be filed promptly. Unexplained delay is a standalone ground for relief in bail applications and in writ petitions for quashing.
