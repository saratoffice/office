Recording of Confessions and Statements Under the Bharatiya Nagarik Suraksha Sanhita, 2023 (BNSS)

Overview of Section 183 BNSS

Section 183 of the Bharatiya Nagarik Suraksha Sanhita, 2023 (BNSS), governs the recording of confessions and statements made by an accused person or a victim, particularly in cases involving serious offences such as rape, gang rape, or acid attacks. This provision applies to statements recorded during investigation or at any time before the commencement of an inquiry or trial.

Corresponding to Section 164 of the Code of Criminal Procedure (CrPC), 1973

Any Magistrate within the district where the First Information Report (FIR) has been registered may record confessions or statements, regardless of whether they have jurisdiction over the case. This can occur during the investigation or thereafter, but prior to the start of judicial proceedings.

Key Provisions:

  • Confessions or statements may be recorded using audio-video electronic means, in the presence of the accused's advocate, wherever feasible.
  • A police officer granted powers of a Magistrate under any prevailing law cannot record a confession under this section.

Information of Rights

Inform the individual that they are not obligated to make the confession.

Consequences Explained

Clarify that any confession made may be used as evidence against them in court.

Voluntary Nature

Satisfy themselves, after direct questioning, that the confession is voluntary and genuine.

Unwilling to Confess

Cease recording if person expresses unwillingness; ensure they are not returned to police custody.

The confession must follow the procedure laid down in Section 316 BNSS for recording the examination of an accused person. It must be signed by the individual, and a memorandum must be prepared by the Magistrate.

Magistrate's Memorandum Format

"I have explained to (name) that he is not required to make a confession, and that any confession he chooses to make may be used against him as evidence. I believe this confession was made voluntarily. It was taken in my presence and audible to me, was read over to the individual, and acknowledged by him as accurate. It provides a complete and truthful account of his statement."

(Signed) A. B. Magistrate

üìù Recording of Non-Confessional Statements

Any non-confessional statement recorded under Section 183(1) must follow the standard process for evidence recording, as deemed suitable by the Magistrate. The Magistrate also has the authority to administer an oath to the person giving the statement.

  • Preferably, the statement should be recorded by a female Magistrate; if not available, a male Magistrate should record it in the presence of a woman.

Offences with 10+ Years Imprisonment

  • Life imprisonment cases
  • Death penalty cases
  • Statement of any witness produced by police must be recorded by Magistrate

Special Categories

  • Rape and gang rape (Sections 64-66 BNS)
  • Sexual offences against minors
  • Acid attacks (Section 124 BNS)
  • Preference for female Magistrate

ü§ù Statements by Persons with Disabilities

Safeguards for Persons with Disabilities

If the person making the statement has any temporary or permanent mental or physical disability, the Magistrate must:

  • Engage an interpreter or special educator during the recording.
  • Record the statement using audio-visual electronic means, preferably via mobile phone.

Evidentiary Value: Statements recorded in this manner are considered as examination-in-chief under Section 142 of the Bharatiya Sakshya Adhiniyam, 2023, meaning the person may be cross-examined on this statement during trial, without the need for re-recording.

üì® Transmission of Records

After recording a confession or statement, the Magistrate must send the documentation to the Magistrate presiding over the subsequent inquiry or trial, ensuring the record becomes part of the judicial process.

Constitutional Safeguards

The provisions of Section 183 BNSS must be read harmoniously with:

  • Article 20(3) of the Constitution - Right against self-incrimination
  • Article 21 - Right to life and personal liberty
  • Article 22(1) - Right to consult and be defended by a legal practitioner
