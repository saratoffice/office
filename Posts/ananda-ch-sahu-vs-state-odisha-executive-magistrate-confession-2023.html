Orissa High Court Rules Executive Magistrate Cannot Record Confession under Essential Commodities Act: A Landmark Verdict

Sarat Rout May 1, 2025 12 min read Criminal Law
Orissa High Court, Cuttack - Justice Sashikanta Mishra delivered landmark judgment on March 27, 2023

HIGH COURT OF ORISSA, CUTTACK

2023 LiveLaw (Ori) 48
Hon'ble Mr. Justice Sashikanta Mishra (Single Judge)

CRA No. 7 of 1995 | Judgment Delivered: March 27, 2023

Ananda Ch Sahu & Anr. v. State of Odisha

In a profoundly enlightening and legally consequential judgment, the Orissa High Court has set a powerful precedent by emphatically holding that an Executive Magistrate is not empowered to record confessions for offences under the Essential Commodities Act, 1955.

This judgment, authored by Hon'ble Mr Justice Sashikanta Mishra, forming part of a Single Judge Bench at Cuttack, is remarkable not only for its doctrinal clarity but also for its unwavering commitment to constitutional procedure and due process. It sets aside the conviction and sentence imposed by the trial court on the accused persons, thereby reaffirming the fundamental principles of criminal jurisprudence.

üìã Opening Observations and Background

Para 1

The Hon'ble Bench commences the judgment by stating that the appeal arises from a conviction judgment dated 23.12.1994 passed by the learned Additional Sessions Judge-cum-Special Judge, Jajpur, in 2(a) CC Case No. 198 of 1984 / 6 of 1993. The accused was convicted under Section 7 of the Essential Commodities Act and sentenced to three months of Rigorous Imprisonment and a fine of Rs. 100, with a default sentence of one month RI. Originally, two appellants had challenged the conviction, but one ‚Äì Paramananda Sahu ‚Äì passed away during the pendency of the appeal, causing it to abate against him.

Para 2

The Court sets out the facts: the prosecution was initiated based on a report dated 24.03.1984 by the Inspector of Supplies (Regulatory), Cuttack, who discovered that M/s Minati Stores at Kuakhia had 40.95 quintals of groundnut in the shell at two locations, which was not reflected in the stock records. The firm was allegedly operating beyond permissible limits and without a proper licence. It was also noted that no price or stock display board was found in the shop or godown. The alleged violations pertained to Clause 3 of the Orissa Declaration of Stocks and Prices of Essential Commodities Order, 1973.

The deceased appellant Paramananda Sahu was stated to be the husband of the firm's partners and was allegedly involved in the transactions alongside co-accused Ananda Ch. Sahu. Based on these allegations, the trial court took cognizance of the offence.

‚öñÔ∏è Trial Court Proceedings and Conviction

Para 3

The Hon'ble Court notes that the accused persons pleaded denial to all charges.

Para 4

The prosecution examined two witnesses ‚Äì P.W.1 (Executive Magistrate) and P.W.2 (complainant) ‚Äì and relied on eight documents. In defence, three witnesses were examined.

Para 5

The trial court placed reliance on the seizure of the groundnut stock and the confessional statement of the accused Paramananda (marked as Exhibit 3) to convict both accused.

üîç Critical Scrutiny of the Confession and Evidentiary Gaps

Para 9

The Court examines the nature and evidentiary value of Exhibit 3. P.W.1, the Executive Magistrate, admitted that the statement was written in his hand but not signed due to oversight. Importantly, he also admitted to not giving any certificate that the statement had been read over and explained to the accused. The trial court nonetheless treated it as a valid confession.

Para 10

The High Court questioned the admissibility of this so-called confession. The Bench observed that Exhibit 3 lacked any formal endorsement as to who recorded it. Though signed by the accused, there was no proof that the Executive Magistrate had the authority to record it, especially in light of his failure to even sign the document.

üìö Legal Framework: Section 164 CrPC and EC Act

Para 11

The Court addressed whether the Executive Magistrate was empowered to record such a confession. Section 10-A of the Essential Commodities Act makes offences under the Act cognizable. Section 11 stipulates that no court shall take cognizance unless a public servant, an aggrieved person, or a recognised consumer association files a written report. The Act itself does not lay down any specific procedural mechanism for trial or confession, bringing Section 4 of the CrPC into play.

Para 12-13

Section 4(2) of the CrPC provides that offences under any other law must be tried in accordance with CrPC provisions unless that special law prescribes a different procedure. Since the Essential Commodities Act lacks such a provision, CrPC procedures apply, including those under Section 164, which governs confessions. Section 164 empowers only Judicial Magistrates to record confessions, and this applies even to offences under special legislations like the Essential Commodities Act, unless otherwise provided.

üìú Legal Precedents and Analysis

Para 14

The Court referred to the landmark ruling in Asstt. CCE v. Duncan Agro Industries, (2000) 7 SCC 53, where the Supreme Court held that Section 164 CrPC applies only during investigations under Chapter XII of the Code and that confessions must be voluntary and made before an authorised Magistrate.

Asstt. CCE v. Duncan Agro Industries, (2000) 7 SCC 53

This position was reinforced by Mahavir v. State, (2001) 7 SCC 148, which clarified that non-confessional statements could not be recorded unless sponsored by the investigating officer.

Mahavir v. State, (2001) 7 SCC 148
Para 15

The Court found no provision under which the Executive Magistrate (P.W.1) could record a confession. Without any statutory authority, Exhibit 3 was inadmissible in law. It could, at best, be treated as an extra-judicial confession, which would still need to be proved as voluntary ‚Äì something the prosecution failed to establish. The confession, being the foundation of the trial court's verdict, rendered the judgment legally unsustainable.

üîó Lack of Linkage and Scanty Evidence

Para 16

The Court dissected the other evidence presented by the prosecution. Groundnuts were found in the houses of Madhab Sahoo and Raghunath Behera, not in the firm's shop. Crucially, no evidence linked the accused with these individuals or the groundnuts. As to the deceased appellant Paramananda Sahu, the prosecution failed to demonstrate his involvement beyond mere presence during the inspection. That alone was insufficient to establish guilt.

Para 17

The Court analyzed allegations that the accused operated without a licence or failed to display mandatory boards. The prosecution's case rested on entries in a stock register and some cash memos, which allegedly proved that more than 30 quintals of groundnuts were transacted. However, no evidence directly connected the accused to these transactions, nor were the items seized from the firm's premises. The Court held the evidence to be insufficient and speculative.

Para 18

The High Court found the conviction unsustainable in law and proceeded to set aside the impugned judgment.

Core Holdings of the Judgment

1

Executive Magistrate Cannot Record Confession

Executive Magistrates do not possess statutory authority to record confessions under the Essential Commodities Act.

2

Section 164 CrPC Applies

Section 164 CrPC applies even to special legislations unless expressly excluded.

3

Only Judicial Magistrates

Only Judicial Magistrates can record confessions under CrPC.

4

Confession Inadmissible

Any confession not recorded under due procedure is inadmissible and cannot form basis for conviction.

5

Mere Suspicion Insufficient

Mere suspicion or circumstantial presence does not equate to proof beyond reasonable doubt.

üéØ Significance of the Judgment

This judgment by the Orissa High Court is a watershed moment in reinforcing procedural integrity in criminal trials under special statutes. The Court has decisively clarified that procedural safeguards are not mere technicalities, but essential bulwarks of justice. It reaffirms that substantive justice must be rooted in procedural legality, ensuring fairness to the accused and preserving the sanctity of criminal law.

This case stands as a vital precedent and a beacon of judicial clarity for future cases involving the Essential Commodities Act and similar special legislations.
