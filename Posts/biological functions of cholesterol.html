01 Key Biological Functions

Key Biological Functions

Cholesterol and lipoproteins are far more than cardiovascular risk markers — they are central to membrane architecture, energy metabolism, hormone production, immune defence, and vitamin transport. The five major lipoprotein classes (chylomicrons, VLDL, IDL, LDL, HDL) each carry a distinct cargo of triglycerides, cholesterol esters, phospholipids, and apolipoproteins, functioning as the body's lipid logistics network (NCBI StatPearls, 2024).

LDL — Cholesterol Delivery

LDL carries approximately two-thirds of all plasma cholesterol and cholesterol esters to peripheral tissues via the apoB-100 / LDL-receptor pathway. Cells internalise LDL through receptor-mediated endocytosis in clathrin-coated pits, releasing free cholesterol for membrane synthesis, steroidogenesis, and cell repair. Oxidised or acetylated LDL forms are pro-atherogenic, driving foam-cell and plaque formation.

ApoB-100 LDL Receptor Endocytosis Pro-Atherogenic if Elevated

VLDL — Triglyceride & Energy Delivery

Synthesised in the liver, VLDL carries endogenous triglycerides and cholesterol esters to muscle and adipose tissue. Lipoprotein lipase (activated by ApoC-II) hydrolyses VLDL triglycerides at capillary walls, releasing free fatty acids for energy production or storage. The progressively delipidated VLDL remnant passes through IDL and is ultimately converted to LDL by hepatic lipase.

Triglycerides LPL Activation → IDL → LDL ApoB-100, ApoC-II, ApoE

HDL — Reverse Cholesterol Transport

HDL removes excess cholesterol from peripheral cells and macrophages via ABCA1 and ABCG1 transporters, esterifies it with LCAT, and returns it to the liver via SR-B1 receptors or indirect CETP-mediated transfer through VLDL/LDL. High HDL concentration is strongly inversely associated with atherosclerosis risk. HDL also carries paraoxonase-1 (PON1), an anti-oxidant enzyme that protects LDL from oxidation.

ApoA-I ABCA1 / ABCG1 LCAT Anti-Atherogenic

Antioxidant Protection

Cholesterol itself resists lipid peroxidation within membranes by ordering phospholipid packing. HDL-associated enzymes — paraoxonase-1 (PON1) and platelet-activating factor acetylhydrolase (PAF-AH) — neutralise oxidised phospholipids on LDL, reducing foam-cell formation. Vitamin E, carried by LDL, is the primary chain-breaking antioxidant protecting polyunsaturated fatty acids from free radical damage.

PON1 PAF-AH Vitamin E (α-tocopherol) Free Radical Neutralisation

Ubiquinol (CoQ10) — Mitochondrial Energy

Ubiquinol (the reduced form of Co-enzyme Q10) is an isoprenoid synthesised from the same mevalonate pathway as cholesterol. It is an essential electron carrier in the mitochondrial respiratory chain (Complex I–III), enabling ATP synthesis. As a lipid-soluble antioxidant it also regenerates vitamin E and protects mitochondrial membranes. Statin drugs, which inhibit the mevalonate pathway, reduce CoQ10 biosynthesis — a mechanism linked to statin-associated myopathy.

ATP Synthesis Mevalonate Pathway Complex I–III Statin-CoQ10 Interaction

Fat-Soluble Vitamin Transport (A, D, E, K)

Vitamins A, D, E, and K are hydrophobic and must be packaged within lipoprotein particles for circulation. Dietary absorption requires bile acid emulsification and incorporation into chylomicrons. LDL is the primary plasma carrier for vitamin E (α-tocopherol); HDL and LDL carry vitamin D metabolites to target tissues. Apolipoproteins serve as cofactors facilitating uptake of these vitamins by cells via lipoprotein receptors.

Vitamin A Vitamin D Vitamin E Vitamin K

Immune Defence & Pathogen Neutralisation

Lipoproteins transport and neutralise bacterial endotoxins (lipopolysaccharides, LPS) from sites of infection, preventing systemic inflammation. LDL and HDL bind LPS and deliver it to the liver for inactivation. HDL also modulates toll-like receptor (TLR4) signalling, reducing inflammatory cytokine release. Cholesterol-rich lipid rafts in cell membranes serve as signalling platforms for immune receptor assembly and activation.

LPS Binding TLR4 Modulation Lipid Rafts Cytokine Regulation

Cholesterol Esters — Storage & Transport Form

Free cholesterol is esterified with fatty acids by LCAT (in plasma) or ACAT (in cells) to form cholesterol esters. This esterification makes cholesterol more hydrophobic, allowing it to pack densely in the hydrophobic core of lipoprotein particles. Cholesterol esters constitute the majority of cholesterol stored in adrenal glands, liver, and atherosclerotic plaques. They are the primary substrate for steroidogenic cells.

LCAT (Plasma) ACAT (Cellular) Lipoprotein Core Steroid Substrate

Apoptosis, Cell Division & Malignancy

Cholesterol is indispensable for cell membrane fluidity and raft formation, which regulate death receptor clustering (Fas, TNFR1) for apoptosis signalling. Cholesterol depletion sensitises cells to apoptosis, while excess cellular cholesterol is linked to evasion of programmed death in cancer cells. Elevated LDL receptor expression in rapidly dividing cancer cells reflects their high demand for cholesterol for membrane biosynthesis.

Death Receptor Clustering Lipid Raft Signalling Cancer Cell Demand

Cell Membrane Repair & Integrity

Cholesterol maintains the structural fluidity and integrity of every cell membrane in the body — accounting for up to 30–40% of plasma membrane lipids. It fills gaps between phospholipids, modulates membrane permeability, and is critical for vesicle budding, endocytosis, and exocytosis. LDL delivers cholesterol to sites of membrane damage and rapidly dividing cells during repair and regeneration.

Membrane Fluidity 30–40% of Plasma Membrane Endocytosis / Exocytosis

Cell Signalling & Neurotransmission

Cholesterol-rich lipid rafts act as dynamic scaffolds clustering G-protein coupled receptors (GPCRs), ion channels, and signalling proteins. In neurons, cholesterol is essential for synapse formation, myelin sheath integrity, and the release and recycling of neurotransmitters. The brain contains ~25% of the body's cholesterol despite being only 2% of body weight, almost all synthesised locally by astrocytes via the mevalonate pathway.

Lipid Rafts GPCRs Myelin Sheath Synapse Formation

Steroid Hormone Precursor

Cholesterol is the universal steroid hormone precursor. In the adrenal cortex, ovaries, testes, and placenta, the enzyme CYP11A1 converts cholesterol to pregnenolone — the first committed step in steroidogenesis. All five classes of steroid hormones (glucocorticoids, mineralocorticoids, androgens, estrogens, progestogens) and bile acids derive from this pathway, regulating metabolism, stress response, reproduction, electrolyte balance, and digestion.

02 Lipoprotein Metabolism Pathway

Lipoprotein Metabolism: Endogenous Pathway

The endogenous pathway begins in the liver with the assembly of VLDL and terminates with LDL receptor-mediated uptake in peripheral cells. Each transformation step is enzyme-driven and tightly regulated to maintain lipid homeostasis. Disruption at any stage — from VLDL secretion to LDL receptor function — can elevate cardiovascular risk.

Liver → Tissues: The Endogenous Lipoprotein Cascade

Triglycerides and cholesterol esters assembled in hepatocytes with ApoB-100 are secreted as VLDL and progressively remodelled by vascular lipases into IDL, then LDL, for delivery to peripheral tissues.

03 Cholesterol & Vitamin D

Cholesterol & Vitamin D: A Special Relationship

Cholesterol and vitamin D share a four-ring steroid skeleton (cyclopentanoperhydrophenanthrene). Vitamin D₃ (cholecalciferol) is synthesised directly from 7-dehydrocholesterol, a cholesterol precursor, making this the most structurally intimate link between cholesterol and any vitamin. Both the liver and kidney are required to activate D₃ into its hormonal form, 1,25-dihydroxyvitamin D (calcitriol).

ℹ Clinical Note — Serum 25(OH)D₃ The circulating 25-hydroxyvitamin D (calcidiol) level is the accepted clinical marker of vitamin D status. Deficiency (<20 ng/mL) is associated with impaired calcium absorption, increased fracture risk, immune dysfunction, and increased incidence of some cancers. Because statin drugs reduce the mevalonate pathway, they can theoretically affect 7-dehydrocholesterol availability — though clinical evidence for statin-induced vitamin D deficiency remains inconclusive.
04 Steroid Hormone Synthesis

Cholesterol as Steroid Hormone Precursor

All five classes of steroid hormones share cholesterol as their obligate precursor. The rate-limiting step — conversion of cholesterol to pregnenolone by CYP11A1 (cytochrome P450 side-chain cleavage enzyme) — occurs on the inner mitochondrial membrane of steroidogenic cells. Steroidogenic Acute Regulatory protein (StAR) governs the transport of cholesterol to the inner membrane, making it the true physiological rate-controller.

⚠ Key Pathway Note Bile acids — synthesised from cholesterol in the liver by CYP7A1 (cholesterol 7α-hydroxylase) — are the major route of cholesterol excretion from the body. Approximately 500 mg of cholesterol is converted to bile acids daily. Statins reduce hepatic cholesterol synthesis, upregulating LDL receptors and reducing plasma LDL-C; bile acid sequestrants (resins) reduce enterohepatic recirculation of bile acids, forcing the liver to use more cholesterol for their resynthesis.

Key Takeaways

  • Lipoproteins as logistics: Five distinct classes (chylomicrons, VLDL, IDL, LDL, HDL) transport insoluble lipids in aqueous blood — each with unique apolipoprotein signals for receptor targeting
  • LDL dual role: Essential cholesterol delivery vehicle for membranes and steroidogenesis; pathological when oxidised or elevated, driving atherosclerotic plaque
  • HDL as scavenger: Reverse cholesterol transport protects arteries; HDL-associated PON1 and PAF-AH also prevent LDL oxidation
  • Vitamin D precursor: 7-dehydrocholesterol (a direct cholesterol derivative) is the skin substrate for UVB-driven vitamin D₃ synthesis; two further hydroxylations in liver and kidney yield the active hormone calcitriol
  • Steroid hub: All five steroid hormone classes plus bile acids derive from cholesterol via the CYP11A1 / StAR-controlled mitochondrial pathway
  • CoQ10 connection: Ubiquinol shares the mevalonate biosynthetic pathway with cholesterol; statins reduce both simultaneously, explaining statin-associated myopathy
