What is the Bureau of Indian Standards (BIS)?

The Bureau of Indian Standards (BIS) is the National Standards Body of India, operating under the Ministry of Consumer Affairs, Food & Public Distribution. Established under the BIS Act, 2016, it is the authoritative body for the harmonious development of standardisation, quality certification, marking of goods, and consumer protection across India.

BIS formulates Indian Standards, certifies products and management systems, manages the mandatory hallmarking of precious metals, and collaborates with global bodies like ISO and IEC to align Indian standards with international best practices.

1. Establishment of BIS — Historical Background

Historical Timeline

6 Jan 1947

The Indian Standards Institution (ISI) was established with the objective of harmonious development of standardisation activity in India. Initially focused on product standardisation and quality norms.

1955

ISI introduced the Certification Marks Scheme — the origin of the ISI Mark — to extend standardisation services to consumers and give visible proof of quality compliance.

1 Apr 1987

Bureau of Indian Standards was formally established under the BIS Act, 1986, succeeding ISI with an expanded mandate for standardisation, marking, and quality certification of goods.

22 Mar 2016

The Bureau of Indian Standards Act, 2016 received Presidential assent. It significantly expanded BIS's mandate — adding services standards, compulsory hallmarking powers, stronger penal provisions, and product recall authority.

12 Oct 2017

The BIS Act, 2016 came into force — BIS reconstituted under the new Act. The BIS Rules, 2017 were simultaneously notified, providing the operational framework. This is the effective date from which the current BIS operates.

Headquarters: Manak Bhavan, 9 Bahadur Shah Zafar Marg, New Delhi – 110002, India

2. Structure and Governance

Organisational Structure

BIS operates under the Ministry of Consumer Affairs, Food & Public Distribution. The governing council includes 25 members drawn from government departments, industry, research institutions, and consumer organisations. The Director General heads the professional secretariat.

  • Governing Council: Policy-making body chaired by the Minister for Consumer Affairs
  • Executive Committee: Oversees day-to-day administration
  • 14 Division Councils: Technical bodies overseeing standards formulation across economic sectors
  • Technical Committees: Specialist committees under each Division Council, comprising experts from industry, government, and academia
  • 8 Laboratories: Nationwide testing labs for chemical, food, electrical, and mechanical products — ~25,000 samples tested annually
  • NITS: National Institute of Training for Standardisation — set up in 1995, Noida — provides capacity building programmes
  • BIS Care App: Consumer-facing mobile app for verifying hallmarked jewellery (HUID) and lodging complaints about product quality or misuse of BIS marks

3. Key Activities of BIS

BIS is involved in a broad range of activities to ensure adherence to quality standards across industries in India. Its major activities span standardisation, certification, consumer protection, and international engagement:

1. Standards Formulation

BIS formulates Indian Standards (IS) for industrial goods, consumer products, services, and management systems through 14 Division Councils and specialist Technical Committees comprising experts from industry, government, and academia.

Sectors: Chemical, Civil, Electrotechnical, Food & Agriculture, Medical Equipment, Metallurgical, Petroleum, Transport, and more

2. Product Certification

BIS issues product certification to ensure products meet prescribed quality standards. Certified products bear the ISI MARK. The Compulsory Registration Scheme (CRS) mandates electronics and IT goods to be registered before market entry.

ISI Mark: Cement, steel, LPG cylinders, electrical goods, food products | CRS Mark: Electronics and IT goods

3. Management System Certification

Since 1991, BIS has provided certification for management systems including Quality Management (IS/ISO 9001), Environmental Management (IS/ISO 14001), and Occupational Health & Safety (IS/ISO 45001), verifying organisations meet international standards.

4. Hallmarking

BIS manages India's mandatory Hallmarking Scheme for gold and silver jewellery — ensuring purity and protecting consumers from adulteration and fraud. Launched in 2000 for gold, extended to silver in 2005.

Mandatory from 16 June 2021 — currently 380 districts covered. 60+ crore items hallmarked with HUID (as of March 2026)

5. International Collaboration

BIS represents India in the International Organization for Standardization (ISO) and the International Electrotechnical Commission (IEC), participating in global standards-setting and harmonising Indian Standards with international norms.

6. ECO Mark & Other Schemes

The ECO Mark is awarded to products meeting environmental criteria (soaps, paper, packaging, paints, lubricants). BIS also runs the Scheme of Declaration of Conformity (SDOC) for self-declaration of conformity by manufacturers for lower-risk products.

4. Standards Formulation — 14 Division Councils

How Indian Standards Are Formulated

Standards formulation is a core activity of BIS. It is carried out through 14 Division Councils representing diverse areas of the economy and technology. Each Division Council supervises specialist Technical Committees — comprising representatives from manufacturers, consumers, government bodies, testing laboratories, and R&D institutions. The process involves:

  • Drafting by Technical Committees based on industry need, technical data, and international standards
  • Wide circulation of the draft for public comment and stakeholder feedback
  • Revision based on comments and consensus — then final publication as an Indian Standard (IS)
  • Periodic review and revision to keep standards updated with technology changes

Division Council areas include: Chemical, Civil Engineering, Electrotechnical, Electronics & IT, Food & Agriculture, Mechanical Engineering, Medical Equipment & Hospital Planning, Metallurgical Engineering, Petroleum, Coal & Related Products, Textile, Transport Engineering, Water Resources, and more.

5. Product Certification — ISI Mark and Compulsory Registration Scheme

ISI Mark — Product Certification Scheme

The ISI Mark is India's most widely recognised quality certification symbol. It indicates that a product has been independently tested and found to conform to the relevant Indian Standard. BIS certification is basically voluntary in nature — but for a large number of products, compliance with Indian Standards and certification is made compulsory by the Central Government through Quality Control Orders (QCOs) under various considerations such as:

  • Public interest and consumer safety
  • Protection of human, animal, or plant health
  • Safety of the environment
  • Prevention of unfair trade practices
  • National security

Products requiring mandatory ISI Mark include: cement, household electrical appliances, LPG cylinders, steel, packaged drinking water, infant food products, automotive tyres, safety helmets, and many more.

Certification process duration (approximate): ISI Mark scheme — 30 to 90 days; CRS — 30 to 35 working days; FMCS — 90 to 120 days.

Post-certification, manufacturers are subject to regular surveillance — including factory inspections and product testing — to ensure continued conformity. Certificates are periodically renewed.

Compulsory Registration Scheme (CRS) — Electronics & IT Goods

The Compulsory Registration Scheme (CRS) was introduced by the Ministry of Electronics & Information Technology (MeitY) through the Electronics and IT Goods (Requirement for Compulsory Registration) Order, 2012. Under CRS:

  • No person shall manufacture, store for sale, import, sell, or distribute CRS-notified electronics goods that do not conform to the specified Indian Standard and do not bear the CRS Standard Mark
  • Products must be tested in BIS-recognised laboratories — the test report is submitted with the registration application via the BIS online portal
  • Products covered include: LED lamps, mobile phones, laptops, tablets, printers, power banks, CCTV cameras, lithium and nickel batteries, and many other electronic and IT products
  • Foreign manufacturers must appoint an Authorised Indian Representative (AIR) — an Indian resident — to act as agent between BIS and the manufacturer
  • Penalties for non-compliance: fines of up to ₹5 lakh or imprisonment, as well as product recall powers under the BIS Act, 2016

6. Hallmarking of Gold and Silver

BIS Mandatory Hallmarking Scheme

The BIS Hallmark guarantees the purity and fineness of gold and silver jewellery — protecting consumers from adulteration and fraud. Hallmarking of gold jewellery was initiated in 2000 and extended to silver jewellery in 2005. Mandatory hallmarking of gold was launched on 23 June 2021.

Hallmark components on jewellery include:

  • BIS logo (the triangular shape)
  • Purity/fineness grade (e.g., 916 for 22K gold, 750 for 18K gold)
  • HUID — Hallmark Unique Identification Number: a 6-digit alphanumeric code uniquely identifying each hallmarked article, enabling traceability and consumer verification
  • Assaying & Hallmarking Centre (AHC) identification mark

Gold caratages covered under mandatory hallmarking: 9K, 14K, 18K, 20K, 22K, 23K, and 24K (9K made mandatory from July 2025)

Consumer verification: Consumers can verify HUID authenticity using the "Verify HUID" feature on the BIS CARE App — which also enables lodging complaints about product quality, misuse of BIS marks, and deceptive advertisements.

7. Foreign Manufacturers Certification Scheme (FMCS)

FMCS — For Overseas Manufacturers Exporting to India

Launched in 2000, the Foreign Manufacturers Certification Scheme (FMCS) enables overseas manufacturers to obtain BIS certification and display the ISI Mark on products intended for the Indian market. Key features:

  • Foreign manufacturers must appoint an Authorised Indian Representative (AIR) — an Indian resident or company — to liaise with BIS
  • Products are tested at BIS-recognised laboratories in India; the test report must comply with the relevant Indian Standard
  • FMCS applies to products bearing the ISI Mark — it does not cover electronics/IT goods under CRS, which have a separate foreign manufacturer registration pathway
  • Process duration: approximately 90 to 120 days
  • Indian importers seeking the ISI Certification Mark may also apply for licence under the FMCS route

8. BIS Rules and Regulations 2017 — Key Provisions

The Bureau of Indian Standards Rules, 2017 provide the operational framework for the BIS Act, 2016. Key provisions:

1. Functions of BIS

  • Formulation, recognition, promotion, and maintenance of Indian Standards across sectors — food, electronics, textiles, civil engineering, automobiles, medical devices, and more
  • Product certification, management system certification, and hallmarking
  • Consumer awareness on the importance of product standards and the significance of quality marks
  • Monitoring, surveillance, and enforcement of BIS certifications

2. Certification Process

  • Manufacturers apply for certification by submitting products/systems for inspection and testing at BIS-recognised laboratories
  • If the product complies with the relevant Indian Standard, the company is granted a licence and allowed to use the appropriate BIS mark
  • Certified manufacturers undergo regular surveillance — including factory inspections and product testing from the market
  • Certification can be suspended, withdrawn, or cancelled if violations or deviations from standards are found
  • Any changes in the product or manufacturing process must be reported to BIS for re-evaluation

3. Penalties for Non-Compliance

The BIS Act, 2016 provides robust enforcement powers and penal provisions:

  • Using the ISI Mark or any BIS mark without certification or in a misleading manner is a punishable offence
  • Penalty: fine of up to ₹5 lakh or imprisonment, or both — for violations of mandatory certification requirements
  • BIS has the power to order product recall for products bearing the Standard Mark but not conforming to the relevant Indian Standard
  • Compounding of offences is also permitted under the Act
  • Products can also attract liability under the Consumer Protection Act, 2019 for defects and deceptive practices

4. International Cooperation

BIS collaborates with:

  • ISO (International Organization for Standardization) — BIS is the Indian member body; Indian Standards are harmonised with ISO standards wherever applicable
  • IEC (International Electrotechnical Commission) — India's representative for international electrotechnical standards
  • Mutual recognition agreements and technical cooperation with national standards bodies of trading partner countries
  • Active participation in standards-setting committees at ISO and IEC to influence global standards in India's interest

Legacy of ISI

BIS continues the 77-year legacy of ISI with a vastly expanded mandate — covering services, hallmarking, and digital consumer protection tools like BIS CARE App.

Global Alignment

Active participation in ISO and IEC ensures Indian standards stay aligned with global best practices, facilitating exports and reducing technical trade barriers.

Consumer Protection

Mandatory certification for essential products and compulsory hallmarking protects millions of consumers from substandard, counterfeit, and adulterated goods.

Industry Facilitation

Quality Control Orders and standardisation create a level playing field, reduce substandard imports, and drive manufacturing quality across Indian industry.

Conclusion

The Bureau of Indian Standards (BIS) is a vital institution for maintaining high-quality standards for products and services across India. The BIS Act, 2016, and the BIS Rules, 2017 provide a modern, robust framework — expanding BIS's mandate from the original ISI to encompass services, hallmarking, digital tools, and stronger enforcement.

From ensuring the purity of gold jewellery through mandatory hallmarking — now covering 380 districts with over 60 crore items bearing a unique HUID — to mandating safety standards for electronics through the Compulsory Registration Scheme, BIS touches the lives of every Indian consumer. Its alignment with global bodies like ISO and IEC ensures that India's standards keep pace with international developments, facilitating trade and promoting manufacturing quality.
