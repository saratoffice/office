Fuel Adulteration Detection – Density Method

Practical rules used by inspectors

📌 1. Basic Principle

Oil company provides standard density at 15°C (in Density Register). Inspector measures density on‑site, converts to 15°C, and compares.

📈 2. Allowed Variation (Industry Guideline)

ProductAllowed variationg/cm³ equivalent
Petrol (MS)±3 kg/m³±0.003 g/cm³
Diesel (HSD)±3 kg/m³±0.003 g/cm³

🧪 3. Example Calculation

Density register: 0.7350
Allowed range: 0.7320 – 0.7380

If measured density outside this range → suspicion of adulteration.

🔬 4. Field Testing Procedure

  • Collect a fuel sample in a clean Cylindrical glass jar.
  • Insert both the hydrometer and thermometer simultaneously, carefully into the sample.
  • Record both the observed density and temperature.
  • Convert the observed density to the standard density at 15°C using ASTM conversion tables.
  • Compare the converted density value with the density mentioned in the petrol pump density register.

    • Signs of adulteration:

  • Density difference greater than 0.003 g/cm³ from the recorded value.
  • Unusual or abnormal colour and smell of the fuel.
  • Unstable or fluctuating hydrometer reading.
  • Presence of water or visible impurities in the sample.

    • ⚖️ 5. Legal Provisions

    • Essential Commodities Act, 1955
    • Petroleum Rules, 2002
    • Legal Metrology Act, 2009

    Penalties: sealing of dispensing unit, licence suspension, prosecution.

    🧠 6. Inspector’s Tip

    Check three tanks randomly – adulteration is sometimes done in only one tank.
