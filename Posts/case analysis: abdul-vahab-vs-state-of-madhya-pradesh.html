Abdul Vahab vs. State of Madhya Pradesh (2022) — Vehicle Confiscation After Acquittal Violates Article 300A

Sarat Rout April 30, 2026 10 min read Case Analysis
Supreme Court of India — Criminal Appeal No. 340 of 2022 | Hrishikesh Roy, J. | A landmark ruling on the constitutional limits of vehicle confiscation after acquittal under the M.P. Prohibition of Cow Slaughter Act, 2004

Can the District Magistrate confiscate a vehicle owner's truck — after every accused person in the related criminal case has been acquitted by the trial court — simply because confiscation proceedings are legally independent of criminal prosecution? The Supreme Court's answer in Abdul Vahab vs. State of Madhya Pradesh is an emphatic no. The judgment draws a critical constitutional line: while confiscation and prosecution may run on parallel tracks, a criminal acquittal is not a fact that the confiscating authority can simply ignore. Doing so violates the right to property guaranteed under Article 300A of the Constitution of India.

Governing Framework

This case arises under the M.P. Prohibition of Cow Slaughter Act, 2004 and the M.P. Govansh Vadh Pratishedh Rules, 2012. The confiscation power is vested in the District Magistrate under Section 11(5) of the 2004 Act. The Supreme Court's ruling resolves three issues simultaneously: the availability of Section 482 CrPC jurisdiction, the proper scope of the confiscation authority's independence from criminal proceedings, and the constitutional requirement under Article 300A that property deprivation must be backed by lawful authority — not by ignoring an acquittal.

Three Legal Issues at the Heart of the Case

Issue 1

Did the MP High Court have jurisdiction to entertain a petition under Section 482 CrPC challenging the confiscation order under the 2004 Act?

Issue 2

Are confiscation proceedings before the District Magistrate truly independent of the parallel criminal prosecution — and can they proceed regardless of the criminal case's outcome?

Issue 3

When all accused are acquitted in the criminal case, can the District Magistrate still confiscate the vehicle — or does doing so violate Article 300A of the Constitution?

Facts — The Seizure and Acquittal

The Interception

The appellant Abdul Vahab's truck (bearing registration No. MP/09/GF/2159) was intercepted while carrying 17 cow progeny. The driver Surendra and another person Nazir, who was sitting in the truck, were arrested. Crime No. 102/2013 was registered at Police Station Kannad, District Agar Malwa, Madhya Pradesh.

The Charges

  • Offences under Sections 4 and 9 of the M.P. Prohibition of Cow Slaughter Act, 2004 — transportation and aiding in transportation of cow progeny for the purpose of slaughter
  • Offences under Section 11(d) of the Prevention of Cruelty to Animals Act, 1960
  • The truck owner (appellant) was additionally charged under provisions of the Motor Vehicles Act

All four accused — including the truck owner — were charge-sheeted and faced trial before the Judicial Magistrate, First Class, Agar Malwa.

The Trial and Acquittal

The trial court framed the central question: whether the accused, "with motive of slaughter of 17 bulls or with knowledge that the bulls will be slaughtered, transported or aided in transportation" of the same for slaughter outside Madhya Pradesh to Nasik.

Vide judgment dated 28 November 2016, the JMFC concluded that the prosecution had completely failed to establish the primary ingredient of the charge — that the cow progeny was being transported "for the purpose of its slaughter." All four accused were acquitted of all charges under the 2004 Act, the 1960 Act, and the Motor Vehicles Act.

What the Acquittal Evidence Showed

The acquittal was not a mere procedural technicality — it was founded on a complete absence of incriminating evidence. The prosecution witnesses, including the Investigating Officer and the key witnesses PW1 and PW2, did not testify about the accused's involvement in any intended slaughtering. More tellingly, the veterinary doctor (PW4) examined the animals and stated that the animals were healthy and fit for agricultural purpose — directly contradicting the slaughter narrative. The trial court explicitly noted that the case of proposed slaughter was not supported by any medical evidence.

The High Court's Error — Wrong Jurisdictional Bar Applied

The MP High Court rejected the Section 482 CrPC petition on a fundamental legal error: it applied jurisdictional bar cases from the Indian Forest Act, 1927 and the M.P. Van Upaj (Vyaapar Viniyam) Adhiniyam, 1969 to the 2004 Cow Slaughter Act — even though the two statutory regimes are fundamentally different in structure.

Cases Wrongly Applied by the High Court

  • State of M.P. vs. Smt. Kallo Bai (2017) 14 SCC 502 — Under Section 15-C of the 1969 Adhiniyam, a jurisdictional bar on courts and tribunals is specifically provided. Section 15 was held to give independent confiscation power even before guilt is fully established.
  • State of M.P. vs. Uday Singh (2020) 12 SCC 733 — Section 52-C(1) of the Forest Act (MP Amendment) contains a non-obstante clause ousting criminal court jurisdiction once confiscation proceedings are initiated. The HC's Section 482 CrPC direction to the Magistrate was held contrary to law.

Forest Act / 1969 Adhiniyam

  • Contains explicit non-obstante clause — Section 52-C(1) of Forest Act (MP Amendment) bars jurisdiction of all courts/tribunals
  • Section 15-C of 1969 Adhiniyam provides specific jurisdictional bar on courts
  • Criminal court jurisdiction expressly ousted once confiscation proceedings initiated
  • Magistrate's Section 451/482 CrPC powers unavailable

MP Cow Slaughter Act, 2004

  • No non-obstante clause — no jurisdictional bar on criminal courts
  • Section 11(4) of the 2004 Act specifically applies CrPC provisions for search and seizure
  • Section 11A(4) empowers the Appellate Authority to release the vehicle at interim stage
  • Rules 5 and 6 of 2012 Rules reference Section 100 CrPC for seizure — CrPC is integral, not excluded
SC's Finding on HC Jurisdiction

Since the 2004 Act contains no non-obstante clause barring criminal court jurisdiction and actually incorporates CrPC provisions, the provisions of the Forest Act cases were not pari materia to the 2004 Act. The High Court's conclusion that it lacked jurisdiction under Section 482 CrPC was therefore erroneous. The HC was fully competent to entertain the petition — and should have done so on merits.

Issue 1 — HC Jurisdiction Under Section 482 CrPC

The Supreme Court held that the High Court of Madhya Pradesh did have jurisdiction under Section 482 CrPC to entertain the truck owner's petition challenging the confiscation order under the 2004 Act.

The court drew support from State of M.P. vs. Madhukar Rao (2008) 14 SCC 624 — which dealt with the Wildlife (Protection) Act, 1972. There, the SC had held that the Magistrate's power to order interim release of a confiscated vehicle under Section 451 CrPC was not affected, and that withdrawal of the power of interim release from Authorities under Section 50(2) of the WPA could not be construed as a bar on the Magistrate's Section 451 CrPC powers. The 2004 Act is structurally closer to the WPA (no ousting clause) than to the Forest Act (explicit ousting clause).

Issue 2 — Independence of Confiscation Proceedings

Confiscation and Prosecution — Parallel But Not Blind

The Correct Position

The Supreme Court confirmed that confiscation proceedings before the District Magistrate and criminal prosecution are legally separate tracks that may run simultaneously. The District Magistrate has independent power to adjudicate violations under Sections 4, 5, 6, 6A, and 6B of the 2004 Act and pass orders of confiscation. The two proceedings are not mutually dependent — they serve different purposes and proceed before different fora.

The Critical Qualification

However, independence does not mean wilful blindness. Where a criminal trial on the same set of facts has concluded in an acquittal, the District Magistrate is not free to ignore that outcome. The judgment of the criminal trial court is a material fact that the confiscating authority must factor in when deciding the confiscation proceeding. The two proceedings can run simultaneously — but their outcomes must inform each other where they overlap on the same factual question.

Why This Matters

The objective of the 2004 Act's confiscation power is punitive and deterrent — to deprive persons who use vehicles for committing offences of those means. But where the criminal trial has concluded that no offence was committed — with specific findings that the animals were not being transported for slaughter and that the prosecution case was unsupported by evidence — the foundation for deterrent confiscation simply disappears.

Issue 3 — Confiscation After Acquittal and Article 300A

This is the constitutional core of the judgment. The Supreme Court held that confiscating a vehicle after all accused were acquitted in the criminal prosecution amounts to arbitrary deprivation of property — and violates the right guaranteed under Article 300A of the Constitution of India.

Article 300A — The Constitutional Right to Property

Article 300A provides that no person shall be deprived of his property save by authority of law. This right requires not merely a statutory provision authorising deprivation, but that the deprivation be — in the words of the Court — legally justified. To deprive a person of their property, the State must establish that the property was illegally obtained, is part of the proceeds of crime, or the deprivation is warranted for public purpose or public interest.

Why the DM's Order Failed This Test

  • The confiscation order was premised entirely on the criminal proceedings — the same proceedings that ended in acquittal
  • The DM passed the confiscation order despite being informed of the acquittal — he did not factor the acquittal into his reasoning at all
  • The acquittal itself recorded that prosecution witnesses failed to testify on the intended slaughter, and the veterinary evidence affirmatively showed the animals were healthy and fit for agriculture
  • There was no independent evidence before the DM to justify confiscation separate from the criminal case's evidentiary record
  • The State did not even project a case that the truck was likely to be used for a similar offence in the future
Article 300A — Constitution of India

No person shall be deprived of his property save by authority of law.

The Supreme Court in this case held that "by authority of law" requires the deprivation to be substantively justified — not merely formally authorised by a statutory provision. Confiscating a truck on the basis of criminal proceedings that have ended in acquittal lacks the substantive legal basis that Article 300A demands.

The Burden of Proof Issue — Section 13A of the 2004 Act

The State argued that Section 13A of the 2004 Act shifts the burden of proof to the accused — so the truck owner had to prove the truck was innocent. The Supreme Court rejected this squarely: Section 13A operates in the context of criminal prosecution, not confiscation proceedings. The State cannot use the burden-shifting provision applicable to prosecution to avoid its obligation to legally justify a confiscation order. In confiscation proceedings, the burden to justify deprivation remains on the State.

Precedents — Applied, Distinguished, and Relied Upon

Cases Considered by the Supreme Court

RELIED ON

State of M.P. vs. Madhukar Rao
(2008) 14 SCC 624
Wildlife Protection Act — Magistrate's Section 451 CrPC power to order interim release of confiscated vehicle not ousted merely because the Act gives Authorities interim release power. Applied here to confirm HC's Section 482 CrPC jurisdiction, since the 2004 Act similarly incorporates CrPC provisions without an ousting clause.

RELIED ON

State of W.B. vs. Sujit Kumar Rana
(2004) 4 SCC 129
West Bengal Forest Act — Held that confiscation orders should not be passed automatically; "commission of an offence" is a requisite ingredient. The owner of the vehicle must be given notice and an opportunity to show the vehicle was used without his knowledge. The SC applied this principle to hold that confiscation cannot survive when the criminal trial itself found no offence was committed.

RELIED ON

Nitesh s/o Dhannalal vs. State of M.P.
(2016) SCC Online MP 7622
Coordinate Bench of the MP High Court — interpreted various provisions of the 2004 Act to hold that unless a criminal offence is committed, seizure and confiscation of the vehicle involved would be unwarranted. The appellant's counsel relied on this decision; the Supreme Court's reasoning is consistent with its conclusion.

DISTINGUISHED

State of M.P. vs. Smt. Kallo Bai
(2017) 14 SCC 502
Indian Forest Act and MP Van Upaj Adhiniyam 1969 — Section 15-C of the 1969 Adhiniyam creates an explicit jurisdictional bar on courts. Section 15 gives independent confiscation power even before guilt is established. Distinguished: the 2004 Act has no such bar or non-obstante clause, making these observations inapplicable.

DISTINGUISHED

State of M.P. vs. Uday Singh
(2020) 12 SCC 733
Forest Act, 1927 (MP Amendment by Act 25 of 1983) — Section 52-C(1) has a non-obstante clause expressly ousting criminal court jurisdiction. HC direction under Section 482 CrPC to Magistrate to release vehicle held contrary to law. Distinguished: the 2004 Act contains no such non-obstante clause; CrPC is specifically incorporated, not excluded.

Court's Ruling and Final Order

The Supreme Court allowed the appeal. Its key holdings were:

  • The High Court erred in holding it lacked Section 482 CrPC jurisdiction — the Forest Act/1969 Adhiniyam jurisdictional bar cases were not pari materia to the 2004 Act, which incorporates CrPC provisions and has no non-obstante clause
  • While confiscation proceedings and criminal prosecution are legally independent and may run simultaneously, the District Magistrate is required to factor in the acquittal judgment of the criminal trial court when deciding confiscation
  • The DM's confiscation order was premised entirely on the criminal proceedings and was passed by ignoring the trial court's acquittal — this rendered it arbitrary
  • Confiscating the truck after acquittal amounts to arbitrary deprivation of property in violation of Article 300A of the Constitution
  • Section 13A of the 2004 Act (burden of proof) applies to criminal prosecution — it cannot be used to shift the State's burden to justify confiscation in confiscation proceedings
  • The confiscation order of the District Magistrate was declared unsustainable and set aside; the High Court's decision was overruled
The Settled Principle — Abdul Vahab (2022)

When all accused persons are acquitted in a criminal case, the confiscating authority (District Magistrate) cannot ignore the acquittal and proceed to confiscate the vehicle. The acquittal must be factored in. Confiscation premised solely on criminal proceedings that have ended in acquittal amounts to arbitrary deprivation of property under Article 300A of the Constitution of India. The State bears the burden to justify confiscation — this burden cannot be shifted to the vehicle owner by invoking Section 13A of the 2004 Act.

Practical Significance

For Vehicle Owners

  • If acquitted in the criminal case arising from the same set of facts, vehicle owners have a strong constitutional argument against confiscation — the DM cannot ignore the acquittal
  • The High Court has Section 482 CrPC jurisdiction to interfere with confiscation orders under the 2004 Act — the jurisdictional bar argument based on Forest Act cases will not succeed
  • The State cannot invoke Section 13A of the 2004 Act (burden of proof in prosecution) to make the vehicle owner justify himself in confiscation proceedings
  • This ruling — cited as the key precedent in the Anup Kumar Saria litigation — confirms that acquittal of all accused (not just the owner) is the relevant threshold for compelling vehicle release

For Enforcement Authorities

  • Confiscation and prosecution may run simultaneously — the DM is not required to await the criminal trial outcome before initiating confiscation proceedings
  • However, once a criminal acquittal is recorded, the DM must factor it into the confiscation decision — it cannot be ignored or treated as irrelevant
  • An independent evidentiary basis — separate from the criminal case's failed evidence — is required to sustain confiscation after acquittal
  • The non-obstante/jurisdictional bar argument (Forest Act style) is only available where the applicable statute explicitly provides for such a bar — it cannot be implied or borrowed from other statutes
Connection to the Anup Kumar Saria Case

This judgment is the Supreme Court precedent that was pressed — and ultimately distinguished — in the Anup Kumar Saria vs. State of Jharkhand litigation (LPA No. 351 of 2023). In Abdul Vahab, all accused including the owner were acquitted. In Anup Kumar Saria, the Jharkhand HC distinguished it because only the owner's criminal case was dropped (not the driver, helper, and lessee) — showing that the Abdul Vahab principle requires acquittal of all accused, not just the vehicle owner, to compel release of a confiscated vehicle.

🎯 Summary

Abdul Vahab vs. State of Madhya Pradesh (2022) is a landmark Supreme Court judgment on three fronts simultaneously. First, it confirms that the High Court retains Section 482 CrPC jurisdiction over confiscation orders under the 2004 Act — unlike the Forest Act regime where jurisdiction is explicitly ousted. Second, while confiscation proceedings may run parallel to criminal prosecution, an acquittal is not a fact the confiscating authority can ignore. Third, and most significantly, confiscating a truck after all accused are acquitted — without any independent evidentiary basis — is arbitrary deprivation of property in violation of Article 300A of the Constitution.

The judgment also clarifies that Section 13A's burden-shifting applies to prosecution, not confiscation — the State cannot use a prosecutorial presumption to avoid justifying its confiscation order. The ruling has wide relevance across all State legislation creating confiscation powers tied to criminal proceedings — particularly under ECA, wildlife, and animal welfare statutes.
