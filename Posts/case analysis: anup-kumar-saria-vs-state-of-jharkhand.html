Anup Kumar Saria vs. State of Jharkhand — Tanker Confiscation Under the ECA 1955: A Two-Round Battle

Sarat Rout April 30, 2026 10 min read Case Analysis
Jharkhand High Court — W.P.(C) No. 3059 of 2017 & LPA No. 351 of 2023 | A decade-long litigation over one tanker carrying 20,000 litres of blue kerosene illustrates the uneasy relationship between criminal acquittal and civil confiscation under the ECA, 1955

Can a vehicle owner whose criminal case has been dropped demand the return of a tanker that has already been validly confiscated? And when only the vehicle's driver and helper are acquitted — but not the owner himself — does that acquittal travel back to undo the confiscation order? These two questions, separated by five years of litigation, form the core of the Anup Kumar Saria saga before the Jharkhand High Court — a case that wound through two writ petitions, two confiscation orders, and ultimately a Letters Patent Appeal before a Division Bench.

Overview — Two Rounds, One Tanker

Why This Case Is Unusual

This post analyses two separate but directly connected judgments involving the same petitioner, the same tanker, and the same underlying seizure. The first (2018) resulted in a favourable remand for the owner; the second (2024) confirmed that a favourable criminal outcome for the driver and helper does not automatically translate into relief for the owner in confiscation proceedings. Reading both together gives a complete picture of how ECA confiscation orders interact with criminal outcomes.

Round 1 — 2018

W.P.(C) No. 3059 of 2017
Criminal case against owner dropped by Chief Judicial Magistrate (CJM) HC remands to Deputy Commissioner (DC) Dhanbad to reconsider release — a partial win for the petitioner.

Round 2 — 2024

LPA No. 351 of 2023
Fresh confiscation order upheld. Abdul Vahab distinguished. Writ dismissed. Division Bench hears LPA with delay condoned.

Common Facts — The Seizure (2015)

The factual foundation is the same for both rounds of litigation. In 2015, a tanker bearing registration number JH-17D-8720, owned by Anup Kumar Saria (proprietor, Anup Automobiles Petrol Pump, Ratanpur, Govindpur, Dhanbad), was intercepted by police and brought to Chirkunda (Maithan) Police Station, Dhanbad.

What Was Found

  • 20,000 litres of blue kerosene oil was found in the tanker on inspection
  • The driver and helper of the tanker could produce no documents relating to the purchase or authorised transportation of the kerosene
  • Blue kerosene is subsidised PDS kerosene — it is not permitted for free sale in the open market. Its storage and transportation can only be carried out by a licence holder under the applicable control orders

Proceedings Initiated

  • The tanker and kerosene were seized and forwarded to the Deputy Commissioner, Dhanbad through the Sub-Divisional Officer for initiation of a proceeding under Section 6-A of the Essential Commodities Act, 1955
  • An FIR — Chirkunda (Maithan) P.S. Case No. 252 of 2015 — was registered under Section 7 of the ECA against Anup Kumar Saria (owner), Gunjan Singh (driver), and Suraj Rawani (helper)
Key Context — Blue Kerosene and PDS Diversion

Blue-dyed kerosene is supplied through the Public Distribution System (PDS) at subsidised rates for domestic cooking and lighting. Its diversion into commercial channels — particularly as cheap fuel for vehicles — is a major enforcement concern under the ECA. Tankers found transporting blue kerosene without valid licences or documentation are routinely seized and confiscated. 20,000 litres represents a large-scale diversion, making this a serious case for the authorities.

Confiscation Proceedings and Appeal

Following seizure, Confiscation Case No. 06 of 2015 was registered before the Deputy Commissioner, Dhanbad. After due hearing, the DC passed an order dated 16 December 2016 ordering confiscation of the tanker under Section 6-A of the ECA.

The petitioner challenged this before the Additional Sessions Judge-VIII, Dhanbad in Confiscation Appeal No. 01 of 2017. The appellate court, vide order dated 29 March 2017, upheld the confiscation in its entirety.

The petitioner then filed the present writ petition before the Jharkhand High Court seeking quashing of both orders and release of the tanker.

The Turning Point — Criminal Case Dropped Against the Owner

The Changed Circumstance

The Big Change: CJM Order — 5 May 2017

After the tanker was originally taken away by the authorities, a new and important event happened. On May 5, 2017, a Judge (the Chief Judicial Magistrate) looked at the criminal case involving the tanker’s owner, Anup Kumar Saria..

The Judge noted that the police officer in charge of the investigation did not find enough evidence to put the owner on trial. Because of this, the Judge officially dropped the criminal chargesagainst him.

The Petitioner's Argument

The owner (the Petitioner) argued that since the police found no proof of wrongdoing and the criminal case was closed, there was no point in keeping the tanker seized.

He believes that because he was cleared of the crime, the authorities should at least look at this new information and consider giving the vehicle back. Essentially, if there is no crime, there shouldn't be a penalty.

The State's Counterargument

The Government (the State) disagreed. They argued that dropping the criminal case does not make the seizure of the tanker illegal.

Their logic is that taking the vehicle (confiscation) and punishing the person (criminal trial) are two separate paths that run side-by-side. According to the State, even if one path stops (the criminal case), the other path (the seizure) can still stand on its own because they are independent of each other.

HC Order — Remand to DC Dhanbad (2018)

The High Court (Rajesh Shankar, J.) acknowledged the limited but important submission of the petitioner: that irrespective of the validity of the confiscation orders themselves, the confiscating authority should at least consider the changed circumstance — i.e., that the owner had not been sent up for trial — in the ends of justice.

  • The court noted that the confiscation orders by the Deputy Commissioner and the Additional Sessions Judge were passed prior to the CJM's order dropping the criminal case against the petitioner
  • The petitioner, as undisputed owner of the tanker, was the person most directly affected
  • The court therefore remanded the matter to Respondent No. 2 (DC, Dhanbad) with a direction to hear the matter afresh and decide expeditiously on the question of release of the tanker
  • Crucially, the court directed that the DC shall not be prejudiced or influenced by his earlier order dated 16.12.2016 or the ASJ's appellate order dated 29.03.2017 when reconsidering the matter
Round 1 — HC Direction (31 July 2018)

The writ petition was disposed of with a direction remanding the confiscation matter to the DC, Dhanbad for fresh hearing on the question of release in light of the CJM's order dropping the criminal case against the owner. The DC was directed not to be influenced by his earlier confiscation order or the appellate order while reconsidering.

Fresh Confiscation Order (2019) and the Second Writ Petition

Following the HC's 2018 remand, the matter went back before the Collector-cum-Deputy Commissioner, Dhanbad. In the fresh hearing conducted in Miscellaneous Case No. 02 of 2018, the DC again passed a confiscation order dated 15 February 2019 — this time a fresh order after reconsidering all facts, including the dropping of the criminal case against the owner.

The petitioner appealed this order. The appellate court — the Sessions Judge, Dhanbad — upheld the confiscation in Criminal Appeal No. 52 of 2019 vide order dated 7 May 2019.

The petitioner then filed a second writ petition — WP(C) No. 1210 of 2021 — before the Jharkhand High Court challenging both the fresh confiscation order and the appellate order.

The Abdul Vahab Argument — Raised and Rejected

In the second writ petition, the petitioner pressed a stronger legal argument based on the Supreme Court's decision in Abdul Vahab vs. State of Madhya Pradesh, (2022) 13 SCC 310.

What Abdul Vahab Held

In Abdul Vahab, the Supreme Court had held that where the accused persons in the criminal case (including the vehicle owner) have been acquitted, the vehicle seized/confiscated ought to be released. The rationale was that an acquittal in the criminal case on the same set of facts undermines the foundation of the confiscation order — particularly when the owner himself is acquitted.

Why the Writ Court Distinguished It

The Single Judge in WP(C) No. 1210 of 2021 refused to apply Abdul Vahab on the facts and held as under:

Writ Court's Reasoning — WP(C) No. 1210 of 2021 (Order dated 3 April 2023)

"The facts and situation of the present case is completely different... In the present case, the final form [was] submitted in favour of the petitioner primarily due to the reason that at the relevant point of time, the vehicle was leased by him to someone else for plying it. However, it has not been pleaded by the petitioner that the criminal case against other accused persons i.e., the driver, helper and the lessee has also ended in their acquittal and thus the proposition laid down in the case of Abdul Vahab (supra.) will not be applicable in the case of the petitioner."

Owner's Position

  • Not sent up for trial by the IO (final form in his favour)
  • Reason: vehicle was leased to someone else at the time — distancing him from direct involvement
  • Argued this was equivalent to acquittal, triggering Abdul Vahab

Driver, Helper & Lessee

  • These persons — who were actually in possession and control of the tanker during the seizure — were not acquitted
  • The petitioner never pleaded that their criminal cases also ended in acquittal
  • The HC: Abdul Vahab requires acquittal of the accused — dropping of owner's case alone is not sufficient

Section 6-C(2) — Commodity vs. Vehicle

The writ court also examined Section 6-C(2) of the ECA (Bihar Amendment), applicable in Jharkhand, which provides for release of the confiscated commodity in certain circumstances upon appeal being allowed. The court held that this provision refers only to the release of the essential commodity — not the vehicle seized — and therefore could not independently support the petitioner's prayer for return of the tanker.

The writ court also declined to interfere with the concurrent findings of fact by the DC and the Sessions Judge, holding that no perversity, jurisdictional error, or excess of jurisdiction had been demonstrated — the standard for invoking Articles 226/227 of the Constitution against concurrent factual findings.

Round 2 Writ Court Outcome

The Single Judge dismissed the writ petition (WP(C) No. 1210 of 2021) on 3 April 2023. The confiscation of the tanker (order dated 15.02.2019) and the appellate order (dated 07.05.2019) were upheld. The petitioner thereafter preferred the Letters Patent Appeal — LPA No. 351 of 2023 — before the Division Bench.

LPA Before the Division Bench (6 February 2024)

Delay Condonation — I.A. No. 872 of 2024

The Division Bench first addressed a preliminary application under Section 5 of the Limitation Act seeking condonation of a delay of 15 days in preferring the LPA. The court recorded that it was satisfied with the cause shown by the appellant and accordingly condoned the delay, allowing I.A. No. 872 of 2024.

Hearing on Merits — LPA No. 351 of 2023

The Division Bench took up the Letters Patent Appeal on merits, with the appellant challenging the Single Judge's order dated 3 April 2023. The Division Bench noted the writ court's reasoning — particularly the distinction drawn between the owner's criminal case being dropped versus a full acquittal of all accused, and the inapplicability of Abdul Vahab on the facts. The LPA record as available does not indicate a reversal of the Single Judge's conclusion; the Division Bench's detailed order on merits would govern the final outcome of this round.

Note on LPA Outcome

The LPA No. 351 of 2023 was at the stage of hearing before the Division Bench on 6 February 2024. The bench condoned delay and entertained the appeal. The final merits order of the Division Bench on the LPA would constitute the definitive judicial conclusion of Round 2 in this case.

Statutory Framework

Section 6-A — Essential Commodities Act, 1955 (Confiscation)

Where any essential commodity is seized in pursuance of an order made under Section 3, the Collector may order confiscation of — (a) the essential commodity; (b) any package or receptacle in which it is found; and (c) any animal, vehicle, vessel or other conveyance used in carrying such essential commodity. The second proviso gives the vehicle owner an option to pay a fine in lieu of confiscation.

Section 6-C — Appeal Against Confiscation

Section 6-C provides for appeals against confiscation orders. Sub-section (2) of Section 6-C (Bihar Amendment, applicable in Jharkhand) deals with the release of the commodity confiscated where an appeal is allowed — the courts in this case confirmed that this provision does not extend to cover release of the vehicle seized.

Section 6-D — Confiscation Does Not Bar Prosecution

An order of confiscation under Section 6-A shall not prevent the infliction of any punishment to which the person is liable under the Act. This provision makes confiscation and criminal prosecution under Section 7 independent parallel tracks — outcomes in one do not automatically control the other.

Key Cases Referred

RELIED ON

Abdul Vahab vs. State of Madhya Pradesh
(2022) 13 SCC 310
Supreme Court — held that where the accused in a criminal case (including the vehicle owner) are acquitted, the confiscated vehicle ought to be released. The Jharkhand HC in Round 2 distinguished this ruling on facts: in Abdul Vahab, the owner himself was acquitted; here, only the driver and helper were out of the criminal net, while the driver, helper, and lessee remained in the criminal proceedings.

DISTINGUISHED

Application of Abdul Vahab — Rejected
In WP(C) No. 1210 of 2021
The writ court found that the facts did not attract Abdul Vahab because: (i) the petitioner's criminal case was dropped due to the vehicle being leased at the relevant time — not on merits; (ii) the criminal cases against the driver, helper, and lessee were not shown to have ended in acquittal. Both conditions for Abdul Vahab relief were absent.

Practical Significance

For Vehicle Owners

  • Leasing out a vehicle does not fully insulate the owner from confiscation — the vehicle remains liable regardless of who was in control at the time of seizure
  • A criminal case being dropped against the owner alone — especially when the driver, helper, or lessee remain in the criminal proceedings — is not sufficient to compel release under Abdul Vahab
  • To invoke Abdul Vahab effectively, the owner must demonstrate that all accused in the criminal case were acquitted — not merely that the owner's own case was closed
  • Changed circumstances (e.g., criminal case dropped) should be brought to the confiscating authority's attention promptly, as the 2018 remand shows this can succeed at the right stage

For Enforcement Authorities

  • Confiscation under Section 6-A and prosecution under Section 7 are independent tracks — the dropping of a criminal case against the owner does not automatically invalidate a confiscation order
  • When a matter is remanded for fresh consideration, the confiscating authority must hear the party without being influenced by the earlier confiscation order
  • Section 6-C(2) (Bihar Amendment) on release of confiscated goods does not extend to vehicles — the vehicle and the commodity are treated separately under the ECA framework
  • Concurrent findings of fact by the DC and the appellate court are given substantial weight by High Courts — they will not be interfered with unless perversity or jurisdictional error is shown

🎯 Summary

The Anup Kumar Saria litigation is a textbook illustration of the ECA's dual-track architecture — confiscation under Section 6-A and prosecution under Section 7 are independent proceedings, and outcomes in one do not automatically control the other. In Round 1, the HC correctly directed the DC to take note of the changed circumstance (criminal case dropped against the owner) before finalising confiscation — a significant procedural safeguard for vehicle owners. But in Round 2, the DC re-examined and still ordered confiscation; the HC upheld it, finding that Abdul Vahab did not apply because the owner was not acquitted on merits and the criminal cases against those actually in possession of the tanker were never closed.

The key takeaway: to compel release of a confiscated vehicle on the strength of a criminal acquittal, all accused persons — owner, driver, and any other persons directly involved — must be acquitted. A final form in favour of the owner alone, especially where the vehicle was leased to others who remain in the criminal net, will not meet that threshold.
