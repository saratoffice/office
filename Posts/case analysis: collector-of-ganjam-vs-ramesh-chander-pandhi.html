Collector of Ganjam vs. Ramesh Chander Pandhi — Fine in Lieu of Vehicle Confiscation Under ECA 1955

Sarat Rout April 30, 2026 8 min read Case Analysis
Supreme Court of India — Civil Appeal No. 755 of 2009 | The bench settled a long-standing ambiguity in Section 6-A(1) of the Essential Commodities Act, 1955 regarding the measure of fine for vehicle confiscation

When a vehicle is seized while transporting an essential commodity in violation of a control order, the law gives its owner an option — pay a fine and get it back. But how is that fine calculated? Is it capped by the market price of the seized commodity, or the market price of the vehicle itself? This deceptively simple question created a significant legal controversy across Indian courts. The Supreme Court's answer in Collector of Ganjam & Anr. vs. Ramesh Chander Pandhi is the definitive resolution.

1. Case Identity and Background

This Supreme Court civil appeal arose from a judgment of a learned Single Judge of the Orissa High Court, which had set aside part of a confiscation order passed by the Collector, Ganjam, under Section 6-A of the Essential Commodities Act, 1955 (ECA). The Orissa High Court had accepted the vehicle owner's contention that the fine imposed in lieu of confiscation should be limited to the market price of the essential commodity seized — not the vehicle.

The Collector of Ganjam challenged this reading before the Supreme Court, arguing that the High Court had misread the second proviso to Section 6-A(1). The Supreme Court agreed and used the occasion to settle the law definitively by applying its earlier ruling in Deputy Commissioner, Dakshina Kannada District vs. Rudolph Fernandes (2000).

2. Facts of the Case

  • 1
    21 December 2004 — Seizure at Bus Stand, Jaganathprasad Block

    A Marketing Inspector accompanying the Sub-Collector, Bhanjanagar on a tour discovered a bus (registration No. OIG-185) parked at the bus stand, with kerosene oil being filled into its fuel tank. On spotting the officials, both the driver and conductor fled.

  • 2
    Seizure of Vehicle and Kerosene

    The Inspector drained 42 litres of kerosene from the bus's fuel tank and collected a 2-litre sample for chemical examination. Both the bus and the kerosene were seized, and EME Proceeding No. 37 of 2004 was initiated under Section 6-A of the ECA.

  • 3
    Contravention Alleged

    The proceeding was initiated for contravention of Clause 8 of the Orissa Kerosene Control Order, 1962 read with Clause 3 of the Kerosene Control (Restriction on Use and Fixation of Ceiling Price) Order, 1993 — essentially, illegal diversion of subsidised PDS kerosene for use as fuel in a commercial vehicle.

  • 4
    Collector's Order (29 May 2006)

    The respondent (bus owner) applied for release of the vehicle. The Collector, Ganjam concluded the Section 6-A proceeding, ordered confiscation of the bus, but gave the owner an option to pay a fine of Rs. 20,000 in lieu of confiscation.

  • 5
    High Court Writ Petition

    The bus owner challenged the order, arguing that the fine must not exceed the market price of the essential commodity seized (42 litres of kerosene — worth far less than Rs. 20,000). The Orissa High Court accepted this reading and set aside part of the order, directing a fresh calculation. The Collector of Ganjam then appealed to the Supreme Court.

Context — Why This Mattered

Subsidised kerosene under the Public Distribution System (PDS) was — and continues to be — a target of large-scale diversion in India. Trucks and buses were frequently found using cheap PDS kerosene as fuel, depriving intended beneficiaries. Strict confiscation powers under the ECA were the primary deterrent, making the calculation of fines a matter of significant practical importance for enforcement authorities.

3. The Legal Question

The Core Legal Issue

The Question Before the Court

Under the second proviso to Section 6-A(1) of the Essential Commodities Act, 1955, when a vehicle used for carrying an essential commodity is liable to confiscation and the owner is given an option to pay a fine instead — is the maximum ceiling on that fine to be calculated with reference to:

  • (A) The market price of the seized essential commodity (here, 42 litres of kerosene), or
  • (B) The market price of the confiscated vehicle (here, the bus) at the date of seizure?

Why the Ambiguity Existed

The statutory text itself was awkwardly worded — it stated the fine "shall not exceed the market price at the date of seizure of the essential commodity sought to be carried." A literal reading suggested the commodity's price was the benchmark. But this led to absurd results: a bus worth lakhs could be released by paying a token fine equivalent to a few litres of kerosene.

4. Statutory Framework — Section 6-A of the ECA 1955

Section 6-A(1) — Essential Commodities Act, 1955 (with Second Proviso)

Where any essential commodity is seized in pursuance of an order made under Section 3... the Collector may... order confiscation of — (a) the essential commodity so seized; (b) any package, covering or receptacle in which such essential commodity is found; and (c) any animal, vehicle, vessel or other conveyance used in carrying such essential commodity:

Provided further that in the case of any animal, vehicle, vessel or other conveyance used for the carriage of goods or passengers for hire, the owner of such animal, vehicle, vessel or other conveyance shall be given an option to pay, in lieu of its confiscation, a fine not exceeding the market price at the date of seizure of the essential commodity sought to be carried by such animal, vehicle, vessel or other conveyance.

Fine in lieu of vehicle confiscation under the Essential Commodities Act, 1955 — monetary penalty imposed as an alternative to confiscation by competent authority

Literal Reading

The phrase "market price... of the essential commodity sought to be carried" appears to set the commodity's price as the ceiling — leading the High Court and the bus owner to argue that fine ≤ price of 42 litres of kerosene.

Purposive Reading (SC's View)

What is being confiscated is the vehicle, not the commodity. The fine is in lieu of its (the vehicle's) confiscation. Logically, the fine's measure must relate to the market price of the vehicle — otherwise the penalty is meaninglessly trivial.

The Supreme Court also drew support from Section 7 of the ECA, which separately empowers a criminal court to order forfeiture of both the commodity and the vehicle. This parallel structure confirmed that the vehicle's value, not the commodity's value, was the appropriate benchmark under Section 6-A(1).

5. Court's Reasoning and Ruling

Key Planks of the Court's Reasoning

  • The object of the confiscation provision is deterrence — both against the person illegally dealing in an essential commodity and against vehicle owners who facilitate such illegal activity. A fine capped at the price of 42 litres of kerosene is no deterrent whatsoever for a commercial bus operator.
  • The second proviso explicitly contemplates confiscation of the vehicle — the fine is offered "in lieu of its confiscation." The natural measure of a substitute for confiscation of a thing is the value of that thing.
  • The phrase "market price at the date of seizure of the essential commodity sought to be carried" qualifies the date of valuation (the date of seizure of the commodity), not the subject of valuation (which remains the vehicle).
  • This interpretation is consistent with Section 7 of the ECA, where the court in criminal proceedings can forfeit the vehicle in addition to the commodity, confirming the legislature treated them as independently valuable items.
  • The competent authority retains discretion to fix a reasonable fine within the ceiling (i.e., up to but not exceeding the vehicle's market price), considering the facts and circumstances of each case.
The Settled Rule

The maximum fine that may be imposed in lieu of confiscation of a vehicle under the second proviso to Section 6-A(1) of the ECA, 1955 is the market price of the vehicle at the date of seizure of the essential commodity — not the market price of the essential commodity itself. The Collector retains discretion to impose a lesser amount based on the facts of each case.

6. Precedents Relied Upon

Cases Cited by the Supreme Court

RELIED ON

Deputy Commissioner, Dakshina Kannada District vs. Rudolph Fernandes
2000 (3) SCC 306
The primary precedent. This case had already settled that the fine is relatable to the market price of the vehicle, not the commodity. The Supreme Court found that the Orissa High Court's Single Judge had failed to consider this binding precedent — the sole reason the matter was remitted.

RELIED ON

Shambhu Dayal Agarwala vs. State of West Bengal
1990 (3) SCC 569
Earlier Supreme Court decision which observed that the second proviso to Section 6-A(1) permits the grant of an option to pay, in lieu of confiscation of any vehicle, a fine equal to its market price at the date of seizure — explicitly treating the vehicle's price as the benchmark.

Note on the High Court Error

The Orissa High Court's Single Judge had accepted the vehicle owner's argument that the fine ceiling was the market price of the seized kerosene. The Supreme Court noted that this reading had been squarely rejected in Rudolph Fernandes (2000) and that the High Court simply had not applied the binding precedent. The matter was therefore remitted rather than decided afresh, as the Rudolph Fernandes ruling itself was clear and conclusive.

7. Outcome and Order

  • The Supreme Court allowed the appeal of the Collector of Ganjam.
  • The impugned judgment of the Orissa High Court's Single Judge was set aside.
  • The matter was remitted to the High Court to consider it afresh in light of the law as settled in Deputy Commissioner, Dakshina Kannada District vs. Rudolph Fernandes (2000 (3) SCC 306).
  • The original fine of Rs. 20,000 imposed by the Collector for release of the bus was consequently not reinstated by the Supreme Court at this stage — the High Court was directed to reconsider.

8. Practical Significance

For Enforcement Authorities

  • Collectors and competent authorities under the ECA can impose fines up to the full market value of the seized vehicle — giving the provision genuine deterrent teeth
  • The fine must be fixed at the vehicle's market price as on the date of seizure of the essential commodity
  • The authority retains discretion to set a lower fine based on the facts and circumstances of the case

For Vehicle Owners

  • Vehicle owners cannot expect token fines when their vehicles are used — knowingly or unknowingly — to transport illegally diverted essential commodities
  • The fine ceiling is the vehicle's own market price — which for commercial vehicles, trucks, or buses could be substantial
  • Separate prosecution under Section 7 of the ECA remains possible even after payment of the fine and release of the vehicle
Important — Confiscation Does Not Bar Prosecution

Section 6-D of the ECA explicitly states that an order of confiscation — or the payment of fine in lieu thereof — does not prevent prosecution of the person responsible for the contravention. A vehicle owner who pays the fine and recovers the vehicle can still face criminal proceedings and punishment under Section 7 of the Act.

🎯 Summary

The Supreme Court's ruling in Collector of Ganjam vs. Ramesh Chander Pandhi resolves a textual ambiguity in the second proviso to Section 6-A(1) of the Essential Commodities Act, 1955. The fine to be imposed in lieu of vehicle confiscation is not capped by the trifling value of the seized essential commodity — it is capped by the market price of the vehicle itself at the date of seizure. This interpretation aligns with the deterrent purpose of the ECA, avoids absurd outcomes, and is consistent with the parallel forfeiture power under Section 7.

The case is also a reminder that binding precedents must be applied by High Courts — the entire re-litigation could have been avoided had the Orissa High Court applied the 2000 Rudolph Fernandes ruling.
