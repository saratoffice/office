Deonath Prasad & Another v. State of Bihar — Essential Commodities Act: Key Legal Principles on Proof, Investigation, and Benefit of Doubt

A case law analysis covering conviction under Section 7 of the Essential Commodities Act, 1955 — investigator bias, hostile witnesses, summary trial procedural irregularities, and the benefit of doubt doctrine.

Patna High Court Decided Post-1988 12 min read Criminal Law
The Patna High Court set aside the conviction, holding that where the complainant also conducts the investigation, the fairness of the entire prosecution case becomes suspect — a principle consistently upheld by the Supreme Court.

In Deonath Prasad & Another v. State of Bihar, the Patna High Court (Mrs. Indu Prabha Singh, J.) acquitted both appellants who had been convicted under Section 7 of the Essential Commodities Act, 1955 and sentenced to three months' rigorous imprisonment with a fine of ₹2,500. The judgment is a instructive illustration of several interlocking principles of criminal law: the rule against a complainant-cum-investigator, the effect of hostile independent witnesses, the standard of proof beyond reasonable doubt, and procedural requirements of summary trial under the Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973.

Governing Legal Framework

Essential Commodities Act, 1955 — Section 7: Creates penal liability for contravening any order made under Section 3 controlling production, supply, and distribution of essential commodities. Punishment ranges up to seven years' imprisonment or fine, or both.

Indian Penal Code, 1860 — Section 414: Voluntarily assisting in concealment or disposal of stolen property — maximum punishment: three years' imprisonment, or fine, or both. The accused were acquitted of this charge at the trial stage itself.

Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973 — Summary Trials (Sections 260–265): Prescribes a condensed procedure for minor offences — only the substance of the witness's evidence is recorded. The judgment must be delivered by the same officer who recorded the evidence.

Facts and Prosecution Case

On 1 December 1988, the Officer-in-Charge (OC) of Islampur Police Station received confidential information that the joint partnership flour mill of Deonath Prasad and Mahendra Ram was being used to store wheat belonging to the Food Corporation of India (FCI) — a controlled essential commodity. Acting on this information, the OC drew a Special Diary (S.D.) entry, organised a raiding party, and raided the mill premises the following morning at 7:00 AM in the presence of two independent witnesses and both accused persons.

Seizure

25 bags of wheat were recovered — five of which bore the FCI mark, indicating they were government-controlled stock. Neither accused produced any licence, permit, or documentation authorising them to possess or trade in wheat.

FIR and Investigation

A formal FIR (Ext. 5) was registered at Islampur P.S. at 9:00 AM the same day. Critically, the same OC who conducted the raid and lodged the complaint also took up investigation of the case — a procedural irregularity that became central to the appellate outcome.

Charges and Trial Outcome

Cognizance was taken for offences under Section 414 IPC and Section 7 ECA. The trial court acquitted the accused under Section 414 IPC (receiving stolen property) but convicted them under Section 7 ECA — three months RI + ₹2,500 fine each. The Patna High Court reversed the ECA conviction as well.

2

Section 7, ECA, 1955 — The Charge Explained

The Essential Commodities Act, 1955 is a central legislation enacted to ensure equitable distribution and availability of essential commodities to consumers. Section 7 is the principal penal provision.

Section 7, Essential Commodities Act, 1955 — Penalties

Any person who contravenes any order made under Section 3 of the Act shall be punishable —

  • In the case of an order made with reference to an essential commodity specified in sub-clause (ii) of clause (a) of Section 2: with imprisonment for a term which shall not be less than three months but which may extend to seven years, and shall also be liable to fine.
  • In any other case: with imprisonment for a term which may extend to seven years, or with fine, or with both.

Wheat is an essential commodity under the Schedule to the Act and is regulated by orders made under Section 3, such as the Wheat and Wheat Products (Price Control) Order and State-level Storage Limit Orders. Possession of FCI-marked wheat by unlicensed traders without supporting documentation — particularly when discovered during a raid — raises a strong presumption of contravention of such orders.

Presumption Under the ECA

Under Section 7(1A) (as amended), the burden of proving compliance with any order made under Section 3 lies on the accused — the prosecution need only establish possession of the controlled commodity without a valid licence or permit. However, this presumption does not dispense with the requirement that the prosecution's case must be credible and free from procedural infirmities.

3

The Complainant-cum-Investigator Problem

The most significant legal infirmity identified by the Patna High Court was that P.W. 3 — the OC of Islampur P.S. — was simultaneously the raiding officer, the complainant (lodger of FIR), and the Investigating Officer. This is a well-recognised procedural irregularity that adversely affects the credibility and fairness of the prosecution case.

Supreme Court — Recognised Principle
"The officer who himself lodged the complaint should not take up the investigation himself so that there may not be any occasion to suspect the fair and impartial investigation."
— Applied in Deonath Prasad v. State of Bihar; principle affirmed by the Supreme Court of India
Why This Matters — The Bias Risk

When the same officer who formed a belief of guilt, conducted the raid, and registered the FIR also controls the investigation, there is an inherent risk that the investigation will be shaped to support the predetermined conclusion rather than to objectively determine the truth. Witnesses may be influenced, evidence collection may be selective, and exculpatory material may be overlooked.

CrPC Position — Investigation by Designated Officer

Under Section 156 of the CrPC, 1973, any officer in charge of a police station may investigate a cognisable offence. The law does not explicitly prohibit the informant from also being the investigator. However, the Supreme Court has repeatedly held that such a practice affects the reliability of the investigation and must be scrutinised carefully by the trial court, and that an accused is entitled to raise this as a ground for casting reasonable doubt.

Supreme Court

Mohan Lal v. State of Punjab (2018) 17 SCC 627

The Supreme Court (3-judge bench) held that where the complainant and the investigating officer are the same person — particularly in NDPS cases — it vitiates the trial. The case underscores the importance of the complainant-investigator issue.

Supreme Court

Bhagwan Singh v. State of Rajasthan AIR 1976 SC 135

The Supreme Court observed that it is undesirable for the officer who lodges the FIR to also conduct the investigation, as it deprives the accused of the safeguard of independent objective inquiry.

Principle

The Independence Requirement

The separation of the roles of complainant and investigator serves as an important check in the criminal justice system — the investigator's objectivity is a constitutional safeguard under Articles 20 and 21 of the Constitution.

Practice

Effect on Admissibility and Weight

The investigation conducted by the complainant-officer is not automatically vitiated, but courts are required to scrutinise such evidence with much greater care, and the credibility of the raiding party's testimony is significantly weakened as a result.

Deonath Prasad v. State of Bihar highlights the critical need for a fair investigation, establishing that an informant generally should not also be the investigating officer to avoid suspicion of bias.
Under the Essential Commodities Act, if the recovery of commodities is disputed by independent witnesses, the benefit of the doubt may be given to the accused, requiring the prosecution to prove intent to violate a control order.
4

Effect of Hostile and Unsupportive Independent Witnesses

Of the eight prosecution witnesses, three were declared hostile (P.W. 1, P.W. 5, P.W. 6) and one (P.W. 7) did not fully support the prosecution case. The independent witnesses, who were cited as having witnessed the raid, did not corroborate the prosecution's version in court. The only witnesses who fully supported the prosecution were members of the raiding party itself.

Legal Significance of Hostile Witnesses

When a witness is declared hostile and cross-examined by the party who called them, their evidence does not automatically become worthless — but the court is required to carefully examine what portions, if any, still support the prosecution case. Where the very witnesses cited in the seizure list (the independent witnesses to the raid) resile from the prosecution's case in court, the credibility of the seizure itself is gravely undermined.

Raiding Party — Interested Witnesses

Police officers who are members of the raiding party that conducted the search and seizure are categorised as interested witnesses — they have a stake in the success of the prosecution since their professional actions are under scrutiny. Their evidence, while admissible, requires corroboration by independent witnesses for it to be safely relied upon to secure a conviction.

Signatures on Seizure List — What They Prove

The signatures of the accused and the witnesses on the seizure list (proved by P.W. 2, P.W. 3, and P.W. 4) prove that the document existed and was signed — they do not independently corroborate that the seizure actually occurred as described, particularly when the signatories either turn hostile or do not fully support the prosecution's narrative at trial.

Supply Inspector P.W. 8 — Disbelieved by Trial Court

P.W. 8, the Supply Inspector, stated that 20–25 bags were FCI wheat — a damning piece of evidence, but the trial court itself disbelieved it, which is why the accused were acquitted under Section 414 IPC (receiving stolen property) at the trial stage. This creates an internal inconsistency: the same seizure cannot simultaneously be insufficient for S. 414 IPC but sufficient for S. 7 ECA.

5

Summary Trial — Procedural Safeguards

The High Court identified a further procedural infirmity: in a summary trial, the evidence of witnesses had been recorded by different presiding officers and the judgment was pronounced by yet another officer — a violation of the procedural requirements of a summary trial under the CrPC.

Summary Trial — CrPC 1973, Sections 260–265

  • Section 260: Magistrates may try summarily certain offences (petty cases) — those punishable with imprisonment up to two years are eligible. Offences under the ECA may fall within this power if specifically directed.
  • Section 263: In a summary trial, the presiding Magistrate is required to record the substance of the evidence. Verbatim recording is not required, but the substance must be correctly captured.
  • Section 265: The Magistrate must record the findings and sentence — and this must be done by the same presiding officer who conducted the trial and recorded the evidence.
  • Key Rule: Evidence recorded by one officer and judgment pronounced by a different officer is a serious procedural lapse that violates the fair trial guarantee and can be a ground for setting aside the conviction.
Consequence of Procedural Irregularity

Courts have consistently held that where evidence is recorded by one presiding officer and the judgment is delivered by another in a summary trial, the trial is vitiated — the officer pronouncing judgment had no opportunity to observe the demeanour of witnesses, which is critical to assessing credibility. This is not a mere technical defect; it goes to the root of fair trial.

The officer who himself lodged the complaint should not take up the investigation himself so that there may not be any occasion to suspect the fair and impartial investigation.
Seizure of illegally stored or diverted wheat highlights the importance of strict monitoring, transparent supply chains, and enforcement against violations in the public distribution system.

Standard of Proof — Beyond Reasonable Doubt

The golden thread running through criminal jurisprudence in India (as in all common law systems) is that the prosecution must prove its case beyond all reasonable doubt. The accused carry no burden of proof — any reasonable doubt arising from the evidence, or from the manner in which it was collected, must be resolved in their favour.

Classic Statement — Supreme Court of India
"Doubts would be called reasonable if they are free from a zest for acquittal. It is the quality of doubt that matters and not the quantity. The doubt must not be imaginary, trivial or merely possible doubt, but a fair doubt based upon reason and common sense."
— State of U.P. v. Krishna Gopal, AIR 1988 SC 2154
Cumulative Effect of Infirmities

No single infirmity in this case — the complainant-investigator, the hostile witnesses, the procedural lapse in summary trial — may individually have been sufficient to acquit. But courts apply the doctrine of cumulative effect: where multiple reasonable doubts arise from the prosecution's evidence and procedure, taken together they make it unsafe to convict. The High Court applied this principle.

Presumption of Innocence

The accused are presumed innocent until proven guilty — this presumption does not shift to the accused merely because an FCI-marked commodity was found on their premises. The prosecution must additionally establish that its recovery was proper, witnessed impartially, and investigated fairly. When it cannot do so, the presumption of innocence prevails.

7

Acquittal and Benefit of Doubt — Legal Basis

The Patna High Court allowed the appeal, set aside the conviction and sentence, and directed that both appellants be set at liberty. The acquittal rested on three independently supportable grounds which, taken cumulatively, made the conviction unsustainable:

Ground 1 — Complainant-cum-Investigator

The infirmity that P.W. 3 simultaneously raided, complained, and investigated the case casts a shadow over the entire prosecution case. In line with the principle affirmed by the Apex Court, this created reasonable doubt as to whether the investigation was fair and impartial.

Ground 2 — Independent Witnesses Did Not Support the Case

P.W.s 1, 5, 6, and 7 — the independent witnesses — failed to fully corroborate the prosecution case. With only members of the raiding party supporting the prosecution, the evidence fell short of the standard needed to sustain a criminal conviction.

Ground 3 — Summary Trial Procedural Violation

Evidence was recorded by different presiding officers and judgment was delivered by yet another — a violation of the requirement that the same presiding officer must both record evidence and pronounce the judgment in a summary trial. This procedural lacuna was an independent ground to set aside the conviction.

Holding

The prosecution could not prove its case beyond all reasonable doubt. The appellants were entitled to the benefit of doubt. The order of conviction and sentence was set aside. The appeal was allowed and the appellants were acquitted of all charges.

8

Key Takeaways for Prosecutors and Defence Counsel

For Prosecutors

Separate the Complainant and Investigator

Ensure that the officer who lodges the FIR or complaint does not take up investigation. Assign investigation to a different officer at the outset to preserve the fairness and credibility of the case.

For Prosecutors

Ensure Independent Witness Reliability

Before the raid, assess the willingness and reliability of independent witnesses. Their failure to support the prosecution in court is one of the most common causes of acquittal in ECA and seizure-based cases.

For Courts

Summary Trial Integrity

Ensure that the same presiding officer who records the evidence also pronounces the judgment. Transfers mid-trial must be managed in a way that does not violate the accused's right to fair trial.

For Defence

Challenge the Investigation's Fairness

Where the complainant and the investigator are the same, challenge this as a ground for reasonable doubt at both trial and appellate stage. Cite the Supreme Court's principle affirmed in this and related cases.

Broader Relevance — ECA Prosecutions

This case remains relevant across ECA prosecutions involving raids on storage premises — whether for wheat, sugar, edible oil, or other controlled commodities. The principles on investigator bias, independent witness corroboration, and the benefit of doubt are universally applicable. PESO enforcement officers and state supply department officers conducting similar raids would do well to ensure procedural compliance to avoid similar outcomes.

⚖️ Conclusion — When Procedure is as Important as Substance

Deonath Prasad v. State of Bihar is a reminder that in criminal law, the manner in which a case is investigated, tried, and proved is as important as the underlying facts. A seizure of FCI-marked wheat — prima facie strong evidence of an ECA violation — was rendered legally insufficient because the investigation was tainted by the complainant-investigator problem, independent witnesses turned hostile, and the summary trial was procedurally flawed. The benefit of doubt is not a technicality — it is the constitutional guarantee that no person shall be deprived of liberty except through a fair and lawful process.
