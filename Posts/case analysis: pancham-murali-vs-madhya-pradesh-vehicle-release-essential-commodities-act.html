Case Analysis: Pancham Murali vs. State of Madhya Pradesh

Vehicle Seizure & Release under the Essential Commodities Act — High Court Judgment Analysis

Background of the Case

The present case concerns the applicant, Pancham Murali, the registered owner of vehicle No. MP-51-G-0446. The vehicle was seized by the police on the allegation that it was being used to transport 90 bags of DAP (Di-ammonium Phosphate) fertilizer — an essential commodity — in alleged contravention of an order made under Section 3 of the Essential Commodities Act, 1955. A criminal case was registered as Crime No. 306/2018 under Sections 3 and 7 of the Act.

Critical Fact

Although the criminal case was registered, the applicant (vehicle owner) was not named as an accused in the proceedings. His only connection to the matter was his status as the registered owner of the seized vehicle.

Following seizure, the applicant filed an application before the Collector seeking release of the vehicle and the fertilizers under Sections 451 and 457 of the Code of Criminal Procedure (Cr.P.C.), which govern custody and disposal of property pending trial.

Rejection by the Collector and the Appellate Court

22.06.2019
Collector's Order — Jabalpur District

The Collector dismissed the application for release of the vehicle. Critically, in the same composite order, the Collector also directed the confiscation of both the vehicle and the 90 bags of DAP fertilizer. This combined order was the subject of legal challenge before the Sessions Judge, Mandla.

06.11.2019
Appellate Court — Sessions Judge, Mandla

The Sessions Judge upheld the Collector's order and dismissed the appeal, affirming both the refusal of release and the confiscation. The applicant then approached the High Court of Madhya Pradesh in a Criminal Revision petition, challenging both orders.

Court's Observations on Procedural Irregularities

High Court Finding

The High Court found the Collector's order to be fundamentally flawed on two grounds: (1) the Collector had no competence to pass a composite order simultaneously rejecting a Cr.P.C. release application and ordering confiscation under the EC Act; and (2) the order of confiscation was passed in flagrant violation of the mandatory procedural safeguards prescribed by Section 6A of the Act.

The High Court clarified the jurisdictional limits of the Collector when dealing with an application under Sections 451/457 Cr.P.C.: the Collector's role at that stage is limited to deciding whether the property may be released on interim custody. Confiscation is a substantive order affecting property rights and can only be passed after strictly following the prescribed procedure under Sections 6A and 6B of the Essential Commodities Act, 1955.

Violation of Section 6A — Mandatory Statutory Safeguards

Section 6A(1) of the Essential Commodities Act, 1955

"Where any essential commodity is seized in pursuance of an order made under section 3 in relation thereto, a report of such seizure shall, without unreasonable delay, be made to the Collector of the district in which such essential commodity is seized and whether or not a prosecution is instituted for the contravention of such order, the Collector may, if he thinks it expedient so to do, direct the essential commodity so seized to be produced for inspection before him, and if he is satisfied that there has been a contravention of the order, may order confiscation of — (a) the essential commodity so seized; (b) any package, covering or receptacle in which such essential commodity is found; and (c) any animal, vehicle, vessel or other conveyance used in carrying such essential commodity."
Proviso to Section 6A(1) — Protection for Vehicle Owners (Hire Vehicles)

"Provided further that in the case of any animal, vehicle, vessel or other conveyance used for the carriage of goods or passengers for hire, the owner of such animal, vehicle, vessel or other conveyance shall be given an option to pay, in lieu of its confiscation, a fine not exceeding the market price at the date of seizure of the essential commodity sought to be carried by such animal, vehicle, vessel or other conveyance."

Mandatory Requirements Before Confiscation of a Vehicle under Section 6A

Read together with Section 6B of the Act, the following steps are mandatory before the Collector can pass an order of confiscation of a vehicle:

  • A show cause notice must be issued to the person whose property is proposed to be confiscated, informing them of the ground of confiscation
  • The owner must be given a reasonable opportunity to make a representation in writing within such time as specified in the notice
  • The owner must be given a reasonable opportunity of being heard in person
  • In the case of a hired vehicle, the owner must be offered the option to pay a fine in lieu of confiscation — the fine cannot exceed the market price of the essential commodity at the date of seizure
  • Under Section 6B(2), no vehicle shall be confiscated if the owner proves the vehicle was used without his knowledge or connivance and that all reasonable precautions were taken
Collector's Failure

In this case, the Collector failed to issue any show cause notice to the vehicle owner before passing the confiscation order. The applicant was never offered the option to pay a fine in lieu of confiscation, as mandated by the proviso to Section 6A(1). The High Court held that the confiscation order was therefore passed without jurisdiction, in violation of the statutory mandate, and was a result of non-application of mind.

Section 6E — Bar of Jurisdiction & Why It Did Not Apply

Section 6E of the Essential Commodities Act, 1955

"Whenever any essential commodity is seized… or any animal, vehicle, vessel or other conveyance used in carrying such essential commodity is seized pending confiscation under Section 6A, the Collector… shall have jurisdiction… and any court, tribunal or other authority shall not have jurisdiction to make orders with regard to the possession, delivery, disposal, release or distribution of such essential commodity, package, covering, receptacle, animal, vehicle, vessel or other conveyance."
Key Legal Point

The bar of jurisdiction under Section 6E applies only when the vehicle is seized pending confiscation under Section 6A, meaning when confiscation proceedings have actually been initiated in accordance with law. Since the Collector had not followed the mandatory procedure under Section 6A (no notice, no hearing, no offer of fine), the confiscation order itself was void. The High Court could therefore entertain the revision petition and order release on supurdginama.

Applicant's Status and Entitlement to Release

High Court's Key Findings on Ownership & Entitlement

  • The applicant was the registered and undisputed owner of vehicle MP-51-G-0446
  • He was not named as an accused in Crime No. 306/2018 — the criminal proceedings were against the person(s) found transporting the DAP fertilizer
  • No other person had laid any claim to the vehicle or the fertilizers during the proceedings
  • The vehicle had been detained in police custody for a prolonged period without any lawful order of confiscation following due process

The Court held that in the absence of a valid confiscation order, the refusal to release the vehicle to its lawful owner was unjustified. Mere ownership was sufficient to entitle the applicant to seek interim release of the vehicle, particularly where the mandatory procedural safeguards for confiscation had not been observed.

High Court's Order — Release on Supurdginama

Revision Allowed

The High Court allowed the Criminal Revision petition and set aside both the Collector's order dated 22.06.2019 and the Appellate Court's order dated 06.11.2019. The vehicle was directed to be released to the applicant on supurdginama (interim custody) subject to the following conditions:

Release Conditions (Supurdginama Bond)

  • Furnish a surety bond and surety, each worth ₹5,00,000, to the satisfaction of the trial court
  • Undertake to produce the vehicle before the court or any authority as and when required
  • Undertake not to alienate, transfer, or encumber the vehicle in any manner during the pendency of proceedings
  • Undertake not to use the vehicle for any unlawful purpose
  • Undertake not to alter or modify the vehicle's machinery or structure
  • Undertake that the vehicle shall not be used in any further offence or anti-social activity
Important Caveat

The High Court made it explicit that if a confiscation order is ultimately passed by the competent authority after following the mandatory procedure under Sections 6A and 6B of the EC Act, the applicant must produce and surrender the vehicle accordingly. The release is interim in nature and does not foreclose lawful confiscation after due process.

Conclusion & Significance

This judgment reaffirms the settled legal position that the power of confiscation under Section 6A of the Essential Commodities Act is not automatic upon seizure. It must be exercised in compliance with the mandatory procedural safeguards prescribed by Sections 6A and 6B — namely, the issuance of a show cause notice, an opportunity for the owner to be heard, and, in the case of hired vehicles, the offer of a fine in lieu of confiscation.

A Collector who simultaneously rejects a Cr.P.C. application for release and orders confiscation — without following the separate and distinct procedure mandated for confiscation — acts outside his jurisdiction. Such an order amounts to a non-application of mind and is liable to be set aside in revision.

The case is also significant for vehicle owners who are not accused in EC Act proceedings. Mere ownership entitles such persons to seek interim release, and detention of the vehicle beyond what is reasonably necessary for the purposes of the investigation or confiscation proceedings is unjustified.
