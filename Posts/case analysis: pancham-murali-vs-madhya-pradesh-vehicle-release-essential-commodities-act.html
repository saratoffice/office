Pancham Murali v. State of M.P. — Illegal Confiscation Order Set Aside

Vehicle Seizure & Release under the Essential Commodities Act — Sections 6A, 6B & Supurdginama | Madhya Pradesh High Court — Criminal Revision

Sarat Rout February 17, 2025 10 min read Legal Case Analysis
Madhya Pradesh High Court — Collector's composite confiscation order quashed; vehicle released on supurdginama with ₹5 lakh bond

This Criminal Revision before the High Court of Madhya Pradesh arose from the illegal confiscation of a vehicle transporting DAP fertilizer. The Collector — instead of simply deciding whether to release the vehicle on interim custody — passed a composite order simultaneously rejecting the release application and ordering confiscation, without following the mandatory notice and hearing procedure prescribed under Section 6B of the Essential Commodities Act, 1955. The High Court set aside both the Collector's order and the Appellate Court's confirmation, and directed release of the vehicle on supurdginama subject to conditions.

1. Background — Facts of the Case

Seizure of Vehicle MP-51-G-0446 — DAP Fertilizer

The petitioner-applicant Pancham Murali is the registered owner of vehicle bearing Registration No. MP-51-G-0446. Police seized this vehicle on the allegation that it was being used to transport 90 bags of DAP (Di-Ammonium Phosphate) fertilizer in alleged violation of the Essential Commodities Act, 1955 — specifically Sections 3 and 7.

DAP fertilizer is classified as an essential commodity under the Essential Commodities Act. Its production, supply, distribution, and transportation are regulated under Control Orders issued under Section 3 of the Act. Unauthorised transportation — i.e., transport without a valid permit, bill, or delivery order as required by the applicable Control Order — can constitute an offence under Section 7.

Critical Fact — Applicant Not Named as Accused

Although Crime No. 306/2018 was registered, the applicant Pancham Murali was not named as an accused in the criminal proceedings. He was the vehicle's registered owner but was not the person allegedly transporting the fertilizer unlawfully. This distinction became central to his entitlement to seek interim custody of the vehicle.

Following the seizure, the applicant filed an application before the Collector seeking release of the vehicle and the fertilizers under Sections 451 and 457 of the Code of Criminal Procedure (CrPC), which govern interim custody and disposal of property seized by police during investigation.

2. Rejection of Release Application and Confiscation Order

Timeline of Orders Challenged

22.06.2019

The Collector passed a composite order simultaneously: (a) dismissing the applicant's release application; and (b) ordering confiscation of both the vehicle and the 90 bags of DAP fertilizer. No prior show cause notice was issued to the applicant before the confiscation order was passed.

06.11.2019

The applicant appealed to the Sessions Judge, Mandla, under Section 6C of the EC Act. The Appellate Court upheld the Collector's order and dismissed the appeal.

High Court

The applicant filed a Criminal Revision petition before the High Court of Madhya Pradesh challenging both the Collector's order (Annexure-1) and the Appellate Court's order (Annexure-4).

3. Legal Framework — Sections 6A and 6B of the EC Act

The Statutory Framework for Seizure and Confiscation

Two provisions of the Essential Commodities Act, 1955 govern the post-seizure process and were central to the High Court's analysis:

Section 6A — Power of Collector to Confiscate

Section 6A empowers the Collector, upon receiving a report of seizure under the EC Act, to confiscate any essential commodity, package, covering, receptacle, animal, vehicle, vessel or other conveyance used in connection with the contravention of any Control Order. The Collector may confiscate if he is satisfied, after enquiry, that a contravention has occurred.

Importantly, confiscatory proceedings under Section 6A are independent of the criminal prosecution under Section 7 — both proceedings may run simultaneously.

Section 6B — Mandatory Pre-Confiscation Safeguards

Section 6B lays down the procedure that must be followed before any confiscation order is passed. It provides that no order confiscating any animal, vehicle, vessel or other conveyance shall be made under Section 6A unless the owner or the person from whom it is seized:

  • Is given a notice in writing specifying the grounds on which confiscation is proposed
  • Is given an opportunity to make a written representation within a reasonable time specified in the notice
  • Is given a reasonable opportunity of being heard in the matter

Additionally, Section 6B(2) provides that no vehicle shall be ordered to be confiscated if the owner proves to the satisfaction of the Collector that it was used in carrying the essential commodity without the knowledge or connivance of the owner, his agent, or the person in charge of the vehicle — and that each of them had taken all reasonable precautions against such use.

4. Procedural Irregularity — The Composite Order Problem

What the Collector Did Wrong

The High Court found that the Collector's order dated 22.06.2019 was fundamentally flawed in two respects:

  • Wrong jurisdiction for confiscation: When the Collector was seized of the applicant's petition under Sections 451/457 CrPC, his jurisdiction was limited to deciding whether to grant or refuse interim custody of the seized property pending trial. He had no authority in that proceeding to simultaneously pass a final order of confiscation.
  • No notice issued under Section 6B: Confiscation — which permanently deprives the owner of both ownership and possession of the vehicle — is a substantive order affecting property rights. It can only be passed after following the mandatory procedure under Section 6B: issuing a written show cause notice, providing a reasonable time for written representation, and granting a personal hearing. None of these steps were taken before the Collector passed the confiscation order.

The confiscation order was therefore held to be illegal and a result of non-application of mind — the Collector had merged two distinct proceedings into one order without following the procedure applicable to either correctly.

Seizure vs Confiscation — A Critical Distinction

Under the EC Act, seizure and confiscation are legally distinct. Upon seizure, possession transfers to the State but ownership remains with the original owner. Upon confiscation under Section 6A, both ownership and possession vest in the Government. Because confiscation permanently extinguishes the owner's property right, the procedural safeguards under Section 6B are mandatory and non-derogable — their omission vitiates any confiscation order entirely.

5. Interim Custody — Sections 451 and 457 CrPC

The Applicant's Legal Route — Sections 451 and 457 CrPC

Section 451 CrPC empowers a court to make orders for the custody and disposal of property — including vehicles — pending inquiry or trial. It allows the court (or the authority to which the matter is reported) to grant interim custody of seized property to its lawful owner, subject to conditions it deems fit, so that the property is preserved and available when needed.

Section 457 CrPC is specifically applicable where property is seized by a police officer and no Magistrate has taken cognizance of the offence. It allows the police officer reporting the seizure, or the Magistrate, to order delivery of the property to any person claiming to be entitled to possession — subject to such conditions as may be imposed.

Scope of the Collector's Power Under These Sections

When the Collector acts on an application under Sections 451/457 CrPC in an EC Act case (where he has been given powers analogous to a police officer for search and seizure purposes), his jurisdiction is limited to deciding interim custody — he may grant or refuse interim release, and impose conditions. He cannot, in the same order and without notice, pass a final confiscation order under Section 6A. The two proceedings — interim custody and confiscation — are separate and must be decided under their respective statutory frameworks.

6. Applicant's Status — Owner but Not Accused

Why the Applicant Was Entitled to Seek Release

The High Court noted three facts that together made the refusal to release the vehicle unjustified:

  • The applicant was not named as an accused in Crime No. 306/2018 — the criminal proceedings were directed at others
  • He was the registered owner of vehicle MP-51-G-0446 — the undisputed lawful owner of the property sought to be confiscated
  • No other person claimed ownership of the vehicle or the fertilizers during any stage of the proceedings
Section 6B(2) — Owner's Defence

Section 6B(2) of the EC Act specifically provides a defence for vehicle owners who can prove that their vehicle was used in carrying an essential commodity without their knowledge or connivance and that they had taken all reasonable precautions against such use. Since the applicant was not accused of any offence in the FIR, the Collector had a heightened obligation to follow Section 6B procedure and examine this defence — none of which was done.

The court held that mere ownership entitles the applicant to seek release of the vehicle, especially when the mandatory procedural safeguards for confiscation had not been followed and no conviction had been recorded in the criminal proceedings.

7. High Court's Decision — Revision Allowed

Criminal Revision Allowed — Both Orders Set Aside

Having found both impugned orders legally unsustainable, the High Court allowed the Criminal Revision petition and:

  • Set aside the Collector's composite order dated 22.06.2019 (Annexure-1) — confiscation without Section 6B notice was illegal
  • Set aside the Appellate Court's (Sessions Judge, Mandla) order dated 06.11.2019 (Annexure-4) — which had erroneously affirmed the illegal order
  • Directed the release of the vehicle to the applicant on supurdginama (interim custody) subject to conditions
  • Made clear that a fresh confiscation order may be passed by the competent authority after following the due procedure under Sections 6A and 6B of the EC Act — the High Court's order left open the possibility of future confiscation proceedings conducted lawfully

8. Release on Supurdginama — Conditions Imposed

What is a Supurdginama?

A supurdginama (Hindi/Urdu: سپردگی نامہ) is a custody bond or undertaking executed by a person who is given interim custody of seized property during the pendency of legal proceedings. The person receiving the property (the Supurdar or Zimadar) undertakes to keep it safe and produce it before the court or authority as and when required. It is essentially an interim custody arrangement — the property is returned to its owner for practical use, but the owner is legally bound by the undertaking.

Conditions for Release — Imposed by the High Court

  • Furnish a supurdginama bond of ₹5,00,000 and a surety of equal amount (₹5,00,000) to the satisfaction of the trial court
  • Undertaking to produce the vehicle as and when required by the court or any competent authority
  • Undertaking not to alienate (sell, transfer, mortgage, or otherwise dispose of) the vehicle
  • Undertaking not to use the vehicle for any unlawful purposes
  • Undertaking not to alter or modify the vehicle's machinery, body, or nature
  • Undertaking to ensure the vehicle is not used in any further offences or anti-social activities
Future Confiscation Still Possible

The High Court made clear that if a confiscation order is ultimately passed by the competent authority after following the proper procedure under Sections 6A and 6B, the applicant must produce the vehicle accordingly. The supurdginama release does not permanently immunise the vehicle from confiscation — it simply ensures the applicant has use of the vehicle during the pendency of proceedings, subject to the bond and conditions.

10. Key Legal Principles and Practical Implications

Confiscation Requires Prior Notice under Section 6B

No confiscation order under Section 6A EC Act can be passed without first issuing a written notice, allowing a written representation, and granting a personal hearing. Skipping any of these three steps vitiates the entire confiscation order.

Interim Custody and Confiscation Are Separate Proceedings

A Collector deciding a release application under Sections 451/457 CrPC cannot simultaneously order confiscation. The two proceedings have distinct jurisdictional bases and different procedural requirements.

Owner-Not-Accused Is Entitled to Interim Release

A vehicle owner who is not named as an accused in the FIR has a strong claim to interim custody of the seized vehicle under Sections 451/457 CrPC, particularly when no conviction has been recorded and no other person claims ownership.

Section 6B(2) — Knowledge and Connivance Defence

If the vehicle owner can prove the commodity was transported without their knowledge or connivance, and that they took all reasonable precautions, no confiscation order shall be passed — even if the driver is found guilty of an EC Act offence.

Confiscation Proceedings Are Independent of Criminal Trial

Confiscatory proceedings under Section 6A EC Act can run simultaneously with criminal proceedings under Section 7. They are separate in nature — but the outcome of the criminal trial (especially acquittal) may affect the sustainability of a confiscation order.

Supurdginama Is Not Immunity from Future Confiscation

Interim release on supurdginama does not prevent the competent authority from later ordering confiscation after following due process. The bond and conditions ensure the vehicle remains traceable and available throughout proceedings.

⚖️ Conclusion

The Pancham Murali judgment is a clear reaffirmation of the mandatory procedural safeguards built into the Essential Commodities Act before a vehicle can be permanently deprived from its owner through confiscation. The Collector's failure to issue a Section 6B notice before passing the confiscation order — and the error of merging two jurisdictionally distinct proceedings into a single composite order — rendered both the Collector's order and the Appellate Court's confirmation legally unsustainable.

For enforcement officers, the case underscores that confiscation proceedings under Section 6A must strictly follow the Section 6B procedure — notice, written representation, and hearing — regardless of the strength of the underlying seizure case. The vehicle owner's entitlement to interim custody under Sections 451/457 CrPC is a separate question from confiscation and must be decided on its own merits. Merging the two — as happened here — invites reversal at the High Court level.
