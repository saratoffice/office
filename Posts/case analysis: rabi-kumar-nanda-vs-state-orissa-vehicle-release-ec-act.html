📋 Introduction

pg. 1 The Petitioner seeks to challenge the order dated 06.01.2024 passed by the learned J.M.F.C. Barbil in C.M.C. No. 187 of 2023 (arising out of G.R. Case No. 768 of 2023; corresponding to Barbil P.S. Case No.252 of 2023) rejecting the petition filed under Section 457 of the Cr.P.C. for release of a Mahindra Bolero Power+ SLX bearing Regd. No. OD-02-BD-8384; seized by the Barbil police in connection to theft and smuggling of diesel.

I. CASE OF THE PROSECUTION

pg. 2 2. Succinctly put, the case of the prosecution is as follows:

12/13.09.2023
Barada Prassan Sahu, Sub-Inspector of Police at Barbil Police Station, filed a written FIR with the In-Charge of Barbil Police Station detailing an incident.
13.09.2023 (4:30 AM)
Acting on instructions, Sahu and other police personnel were conducting night patrols in Bhadrasahi area. Received credible information that a Bolero vehicle with registration OD-02-BD-8384 was en route transporting illegally obtained diesel towards Durby Chowk from Kiriburi Chowk.
During Chase
When police approached, the driver drove recklessly, leading to the Bolero overturning near Durby Chowk. The accused and two others managed to escape.
Seizure
Search revealed 21 (twenty-one) diesel-filled gallons, each containing 20 liters. Fifteen gallons were full, while six were damaged during overturning, resulting in spilled diesel on the road.

pg. 3 Based on the allegations, the In-Charge of Barbil Police Station registered PS Case No. 252 of 2023 under Sections 379, 279, and 34 of the IPC, along with Section 7 of the Essential Commodities Act, 1955 ("EC Act") against unidentified individuals.

Subsequently, the police seized the vehicle and apprehended the accused parties. The petitioner, who is the owner of the vehicle, filed a petition before the J.M.F.C., Barbil, under Section 457 of the Cr.P.C. seeking interim release of the vehicle.

pg. 3 The lower court rejected the aforesaid petition citing Section 6E of the Essential Commodities Act, 1955 which bars the jurisdiction of the court to grant the custody of the vehicle.

II. SUBMISSIONS

A. On behalf of the Petitioner

pg. 3-4 Mr. P.R. Singh, learned counsel for the Petitioner urged the following submissions:

  • Upon filing the petition before the lower court and requesting a report from the local police, it became evident from the order sheet that no confiscation proceedings had been initiated against the vehicle. The Appellant has not received any notice regarding registration or initiation of confiscation proceedings under Section 6-E of the EC Act. Consequently, Section 6-E would not be applicable.
  • The petitioner being the owner of the vehicle, the right to claim the vehicle is always with him and keeping the vehicle inside the police station will not help the prosecution in any manner. The bar created by Section 6-E of the EC Act is not absolute.
  • The case is pending before the J.M.F.C. Barbil and a bare perusal of the petition and the order of the learned J.M.F.C. reveals that the confiscation proceeding by the Collector has not yet started.

B. On behalf of the Opp. Party-State

pg. 4-5 Mr. G.R. Mohapatra, learned Additional Standing Counsel for the State urged:

  • Section 6-E completely ousts the jurisdiction of all Courts, Tribunal and Authority. Only the Collector or judicial authority would have jurisdiction to the exclusion of all others to pass orders with regard to disposal of essential commodity and other articles.
  • The scheme of confiscation in Sections 6-A to 6-E of the EC Act is comprehensive and a complete code in itself. It is a special enactment and supersedes general law such as the Cr.P.C.
  • Any essential commodity or other articles once seized under allegation of contravention of orders made under Section 3, from that stage itself, jurisdiction of all other Courts stands abrogated. The Magistrate will not have any jurisdiction to pass any order under Section 457 Cr.P.C.

C. Legal Arguments

pg. 5-7 The learned counsel for the Petitioner submitted detailed legal arguments:

  • Two parts of Section 6-A:
    • First part - production of essential commodity for inspection
    • Second part - if Collector is satisfied of contravention, he may direct confiscation
    After inspection, the Collector may or may not order confiscation.
  • Section 6-A gives power of confiscation on satisfaction that there has been contravention.
  • Section 6-B prescribes procedure for confiscation - confiscation cannot be made unless owner is intimated vide a notice and heard. Unless any primary notice is issued, the contemplated satisfaction in the mind of the authority cannot be presumed.
  • Section 6-E - when read carefully, if the confiscation order has been passed, then the judicial authority under Section 6-C shall have power to order delivery or disposal. If confiscation order is under challenge, judicial authority can grant custody by way of interim order. Section 6-E does not create an absolute bar on judicial authority for grant of custody.
  • When a charge sheet is pending before the judicial court, by application of Cr.P.C., normally will have jurisdiction. Section 6E only takes away jurisdiction when confiscation proceeding has been commenced by the Collector.

III. EXAMINATION OF THE LEGAL MATRIX

pg. 7-8 The Essential Commodities Act of 1955 serves the interests of the general public by controlling production, supply, distribution, and trade of specific commodities. The primary objective is to ensure essential goods reach common citizens without unnecessary obstacles and against illegal market practices.

pg. 8 The Act contains special provisions with regard to confiscation of essential commodities, package, covering, receptacle, animal, vehicle, vessel or other conveyance used in carrying such essential commodities. Sections 6-A to 6-E constitute a self-contained code dealing with confiscation, powers and authority, and procedure.

Section 6-A of the EC Act, 1955

Section 6-A

"(1) Where any essential commodity is seized in pursuance of an order made under section 3 in relation thereto, a report of such seizure shall, without unreasonable delay, be made to the Collector of the district or the Presidency town in which such essential commodity is seized and whether or not a prosecution is instituted for the contravention of such order, the Collector may, if he thinks it expedient so to do, direct the essential commodity so seized to be produced for inspection before him, and if he is satisfied that there has been a contravention of the order may order confiscation of -

(a) the essential commodity so seized;

(b) any package, covering or receptacle in which such essential commodity is found, and

(c) any animal, vehicle, vessel or other conveyance used in carrying such essential commodity."

Section 6-B – Procedure for Confiscation

Section 6-B

(1) No order confiscating any essential commodity, package, covering or receptacle, animal, vehicle, vessel or other conveyance shall be made under section 6A unless the owner of such essential commodity, package, covering, receptacle, animal, vehicle, vessel or other conveyance or the person from whom it is seized—

(a) is given a notice in writing informing him of the grounds on which it is proposed to confiscate;

(b) is given an opportunity of making a presentation in writing within such reasonable time as may be specified in the notice against the grounds of confiscation; and

(c) is given a reasonable opportunity of being heard in the matter."

pg. 11 This section incorporates the principles of natural justice to ensure that the owner has the fullest opportunity to satisfy the Collector against passing a confiscation order under Section 6-A.

Section 6-E – Bar of Jurisdiction

Section 6-E

"Whenever any essential commodity is seized in pursuance of an order made under section 3 in relation thereto, or any package, covering or receptacle in which such essential commodity is found, or any animal, vehicle, vessel or other conveyance used in carrying such essential commodity is seized pending confiscation under section 6A, the Collector, or, as the case may be, the State Government concerned under section 6C shall have, and, notwithstanding anything to the contrary contained in any other law for the time being in force, any court, tribunal or other authority shall not have, jurisdiction to make orders with regard to the possession, delivery, disposal, release or distribution of such essential commodity, package, covering, receptacle, animal, vehicle, vessel or other conveyance."

Shambhu Dayal Agarwala v. State of West Bengal (1990) 3 SCC 549

pg. 12-13 The Supreme Court examined the comprehensive plan of Section 6-E:

"Section 6E was first enacted to debar courts from making any order with regard to the possession, delivery, disposal or distribution of any essential commodity seized... By the substituted Section 6E as it presently stands the scope of the provision has been enlarged by extending the bar of jurisdiction to the release, etc., of packages, coverings or receptacles as well as animals, vehicles, vessels or other conveyances also."

"While debarring courts, tribunals and other authorities from exercising power in relation to the seized commodity, power is conferred on the Collector or the State Government concerned under Section 6C, to make orders with regard to the possession, delivery, disposal, release or distribution of such commodity, etc. This power can be exercised pending confiscation."

pg. 13 Key Interpretation by the Court:

However, as long as confiscation proceedings are not initiated by issuance of notice under Section 6-B of the EC Act, confiscation proceedings cannot be said to be pending. Referring to the words "pending confiscation under Section 6-A" unveils a legislative scheme which calls for the ouster of jurisdiction of Criminal Court only with commencement of confiscation proceedings in the manner provided under Section 6-B.

Merely upon seizure, as contemplated under Section 6-A or even till the stage of production and inspection before the Collector, ouster of jurisdiction does not take place. It is only when the Collector decides to initiate proceedings by issuance of show cause notice under Section 6-B, that Section 6-E steps in to confer exclusive jurisdiction on the Collector.

Therefore, if the prerequisites of Section 6-B are not met, Section 6-E cannot be effectively implemented.

State of M.P. v. Rameshwar Rathod (AIR 1990 SC 1849)

pg. 14 The Supreme Court held that the ouster of jurisdiction of the criminal courts cannot be inferred ordinarily. The power to grant custody of any vehicle, goods, encompasses under the Cr.P.C., and when the prosecution is pending, unless any specific bar is created for operations of any provisions of Cr.P.C., the criminal court will have the jurisdiction to deal with it.

IV. COURT'S ANALYSIS AND REASONS

pg. 14 15. The principle of due process is a fundamental aspect of legal systems. It is a constitutional guarantee that ensures fairness, justice, and liberty in legal proceedings. By ensuring equal weightage and opportunity to all parties, the balance of power in legal proceedings is ensured which prevents miscarriage of justice.

pg. 14-15 16-19. Section 6-B provides a safeguard against arbitrary confiscation by mandating a show-cause notice, promotes transparency and fairness, and upholds principles of natural justice.

Deputy Commr., Dakshina Kannada District v. Rudolph Fernandes (2000) 3 SCC 306

pg. 15 The Supreme Court earmarked the importance of Section 6-B:

"Further, it is required to be noted that under Section 6B(2) no order confiscating vehicle or other conveyance can be passed if the owner proves to the satisfaction of the competent authority that it was used in carrying the essential commodity without his knowledge or connivance."

pg. 15-16 21. The stipulations in Section 6-B are indispensable for confiscation of a vehicle. This section ensures due process, including issuing a show-cause notice before any confiscation action. This provision is crucial as it provides the vehicle owner an opportunity to present their case and contest the confiscation. Therefore, adherence to the procedures in Section 6-B is a sine qua non (essential condition) for lawful confiscation.

Dommaraju Vinay v. State of A.P.

pg. 16 The Andhra Pradesh High Court held:

"As seen from the proceedings of the Collector which was signed on 09.03.2022, the seized stock is ordered to be confiscated to the Government. Learned counsel for the petitioners contends that no notice under Section 6B of the Essential Commodities Act was issued to the first petitioner before passing the orders confiscating the seized stock. The said proceedings also do not show that any such notice is issued to the first petitioner...

In view of the facts and circumstances, the notice under Section 6B of the Essential Commodities Act was not issued to the first petitioner, the proceeding which was signed on 09.03.2022 confiscating the stock to the Government is set aside."

pg. 16-17 23. It is undisputed that confiscation proceeding under the EC Act has not been initiated yet as the petitioner was not furnished a show cause notice concerning confiscation of his vehicle and not given an opportunity of fair hearing.

pg. 17 24. Ergo, two findings flow out of the discussion above:

  1. In the present case, due to non-fulfillment of the requisites of Section 6-B of the EC Act, Section 6-E was not triggered and the trial court erred in its judgement by holding otherwise.
  2. The confiscation proceedings were never initiated sans fulfilment of Section 6-B and the petitioner can appeal for the interim release of the confiscated vehicle.

V. CONCLUSION

pg. 17-18 25. In the facts and circumstances of the present case, as discussed above, as the vehicle is still lying in the custody of Police. Following the law laid down by the Supreme Court in General Insurance Council v. State of A.P. and Sunderbhai Ambalal Desai v. State of Gujarat, the orders of rejection of application for interim custody cannot be allowed to sustain. Consequently applying the said principles, it is directed that the vehicle be released in favour of the petitioner by way of interim measure.

pg. 17-18 26. The Opposite Party is directed to get the subject vehicle valued by the concerned Motor Vehicle Inspector in the presence of the petitioner after serving notice on him and the petitioner is directed to furnish a third party immovable property security by executing a bond in favour of the Opposite Party strictly adhering to the Stamps and Registration Laws, as per the value fixed by the Motor Vehicle Inspector and on production of such third party immovable property as security, the respondent is directed to release the subject vehicle bearing registration as interim custody to the petitioner in accordance with law.

pg. 18 27. It is also made clear that the petitioner shall not alienate or change the nature of the vehicle during the pendency of the enquiry.

pg. 18 28. Accordingly, the CRLREV is disposed of being allowed. There shall be no order as to costs.

📌 Key Legal Principles Established

Principle Holding
Initiation of Confiscation Confiscation proceedings are initiated only when notice under Section 6-B is issued, not merely upon seizure.
Bar under Section 6-E Section 6-E ouster applies only when confiscation proceedings are pending. No bar before initiation of Section 6-B proceedings.
Natural Justice Section 6-B incorporates principles of natural justice – notice and opportunity of hearing mandatory before confiscation.
Criminal Court Jurisdiction Criminal courts retain jurisdiction under Section 457 Cr.P.C. until confiscation proceedings are validly initiated.
Owner's Rights Vehicle owner entitled to interim custody pending enquiry, subject to security conditions.

📝 Summary & Takeaways

  • Confiscation proceedings under EC Act are not automatic upon seizure – they require initiation by notice under Section 6-B.
  • Section 6-E bar applies only "pending confiscation" – i.e., after Section 6-B notice is issued.
  • Natural justice is paramount – owner must be given notice and opportunity of hearing before confiscation.
  • Criminal courts retain jurisdiction under Section 457 Cr.P.C. for interim release until confiscation proceedings are validly commenced.
  • Vehicle owner entitled to interim custody on furnishing security, with condition not to alienate or change nature of vehicle.

This judgment clarifies that the bar under Section 6-E of the Essential Commodities Act is not absolute and only operates once confiscation proceedings are properly initiated under Section 6-B with due notice to the owner. Mere seizure of a vehicle does not oust the jurisdiction of criminal courts to grant interim custody under Section 457 Cr.P.C.
