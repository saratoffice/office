Shri Sitaram Sugar Company Limited & Anr. vs. Union of India & Ors.

A plain-language analysis of the landmark five-judge Supreme Court judgment upholding zonal levy sugar price fixation under s.3(3-C) of the Essential Commodities Act, 1955 — with authoritative rulings on judicial review of economic policy and the legislative character of price fixation orders

Sarat Rout July 1, 2025 14 min read Constitutional & Administrative Law
The Supreme Court of India, New Delhi — a five-judge bench delivered this judgment on 13 March 1990, settling foundational principles on judicial review of government price fixation under the Essential Commodities Act, 1955

When the Central Government fixes the price at which sugar mills must sell their levy quota to the State, can courts second-guess that price? Can a sugar mill whose profits are squeezed by the zonal pricing formula demand unit-wise price fixation? Is price fixation a legislative act, or must individual producers be heard before a price is determined? In a landmark judgment delivered on 13 March 1990, a five-judge bench of the Supreme Court of India comprehensively answered all these questions — upholding the constitutional validity of zonal levy sugar price fixation under Section 3(3-C) of the Essential Commodities Act, 1955, and setting enduring boundaries on judicial review of economic policy.

Governing Legal Framework

Essential Commodities Act, 1955 — the Central law governing control over production, supply, distribution, and price of essential commodities including sugar.

Section 3(3-C) — the key provision inserted in 1967, empowering the Central Government to determine the "price of sugar" — having regard to four statutory factors — at which amounts are payable to producers required to sell sugar under compulsory levy orders.

Sugarcane (Control) Order, 1966 — the Central delegated legislation fixing the Statutory Minimum Price for sugarcane, one of the four factors under s.3(3-C)(a).

Article 14, Constitution of India — the equality guarantee, invoked by the petitioner mills to challenge the alleged arbitrariness and discriminatory effect of geographical zoning.

Background — The Levy Sugar System and the Dispute

Under the partial control policy for the sugar industry, sugar mills are required to sell approximately 60% of their output (the "levy quota") to the government at government-determined prices for distribution through the public distribution system, while the remaining 40% is free to be sold in the open market at market rates.

The price at which the levy quota must be sold — the "levy sugar price" — is fixed by the Central Government under Section 3(3-C) of the Essential Commodities Act, 1955. For this purpose, the country is divided into zones (16 zones as of the impugned orders), with different prices for different grades of sugar produced in different zones. Each State generally constitutes a separate zone; Uttar Pradesh — with its enormous and varied sugar industry — is divided into three sub-zones: West, Central, and East.

The petitioners owned sugar mills in the West and East Zones of U.P. They challenged the notifications of 28 November 1974 and 11 July 1975, which fixed the levy sugar price for the 1974–75 production season, as ultra vires the Act and violative of their fundamental rights.

The Partial Control Policy — Context

The dual pricing system — compulsory levy at government price + free market sale — was designed to balance three competing interests simultaneously: protecting the sugarcane farmer (through the sugarcane minimum price), protecting the consumer (through subsidised levy sugar), and giving mills a market-linked price on the free-sale quota to incentivise higher production and modernisation.

Sugarcane — the primary input in sugar manufacturing — accounts for approximately 70% of the total cost of sugar production, making its price the dominant factor in s.3(3-C) price calculations
2

Section 3(3-C) — Anatomy of the Price Fixation Provision

Sub-section (3-C) — inserted into the Essential Commodities Act in 1967 — is the cornerstone of the entire controversy. The Court's analysis of its structure is foundational.

Section 3(3-C) — Essential Commodities Act, 1955 (as amended)

Where any producer is required by an order made with reference to clause (f) of sub-section (2) to sell any kind of sugar, and either no notification under sub-section (3-A) has been issued or any such notification has ceased to remain in force, then — notwithstanding anything contained in sub-section (3) — there shall be paid to that producer an amount calculated with reference to such price of sugar as the Central Government may, by order, determine, having regard to —

  • (a) the minimum price, if any, fixed for sugarcane by the Central Government under this section;
  • (b) the manufacturing cost of sugar;
  • (c) the duty or tax, if any, paid or payable thereon; and
  • (d) the securing of a reasonable return on the capital employed in the business of manufacturing sugar.

Different prices may be determined from time to time for different areas, different factories, or different kinds of sugar.

"Price of Sugar" vs. "Amount" — A Critical Distinction

The Court identified a vital two-tier structure: (1) the Central Government determines the "price of sugar" — a rate of general application, legislative in character; (2) each individual producer is then paid an "amount" calculated with reference to that price. The price is set for the industry cross-section; the amount is calculated for each producer individually. These are legally distinct acts with different characters.

Power to Differentiate — Zones, Factories, Grades

The sub-section expressly authorises different prices for different areas, different factories, or different kinds of sugar. Whether to group by geography, cost-similarity, or factory size is a policy discretion entirely within the province of the Central Government — not the courts.

Publication and Parliamentary Oversight

Every order determining the price of sugar must be (a) notified in the Official Gazette (s.3(5)) and (b) laid before both Houses of Parliament as soon as may be (s.3(6)). This mandatory parliamentary oversight is itself a marker of the legislative character of the price-fixing exercise.

3

The Petitioners' Arguments

The petitioner sugar mills raised several interlocking arguments, each targeting a different aspect of the zonal price fixation system.

Geographical Zoning is Irrational

The cost of manufacturing sugar depends on recovery from sugarcane, duration of the crushing season, crushing capacity, sugarcane price paid, and capital employed — factors that vary factory to factory, not zone to zone. Grouping all factories in a zone and averaging their costs conceals wide cost disparities, giving large efficient mills an undue advantage over small high-cost mills like the petitioners.

Reliance BICP (1976 Report) — recommending cost-characteristic based grouping, irrespective of location

Unit-Wise Price Fixation Required

The intervener contended that s.3(3-C)'s requirement to "determine" price and "have regard to" specific factors means the Government must examine clauses (a) to (d) with reference to each individual producer — not a cross-section average. Any grouping, they argued, fails to satisfy the statutory mandate.

Argument "determine" = quasi-judicial act requiring individual consideration

"Having Regard To" is Mandatory

Clauses (a) to (d) are conditions precedent to valid price determination. The Government must strictly comply with each factor for each producer. The phrase "having regard to" imposes a mandatory obligation, not a directory guideline.

Argument Non-compliance with all four factors vitiates the price order

Violation of Article 14

Clubbing the petitioners' low-capacity, high-cost mills with large, efficient mills in the same zone for price fixation constitutes arbitrary and discriminatory classification without intelligible differentia, violating the constitutional guarantee of equality under Article 14.

Argument Zonal grouping lacks rational nexus with price fixation objective

4

"Having Regard To" — Mandatory or Directory?

The Court devoted careful analysis to the phrase "having regard to" in s.3(3-C) — a phrase whose interpretation directly determined whether the Government's price orders were legally sufficient.

Not "Having Regard Only To" — The Textual Key

The Court emphasised that the statute uses the phrase "having regard to" — not "having regard only to." This distinction is textually decisive: the four factors in clauses (a) to (d) are relevant considerations the Government must address, not an exhaustive and exclusive checklist that bars consideration of anything else. The Government remains free to take into account other relevant matters after considering the four specified factors.

General Guidance, Not a Fetter

Citing the Judicial Committee in Commissioner of Income Tax v. Williamson Diamonds Ltd. [L.R. 1958 A.C. 41] and this Court's decision in Saraswati Industrial Syndicate Ltd. v. Union of India [1975 (1) SCR 956], the Court held that the words "having regard to" are "legislative instruction for the general guidance of the Government" — they are "not strictly mandatory, but in essence directory." They are a guide, not a fetter.

The Sufficiency Standard

Sufficient compliance with s.3(3-C) is established if the Government has addressed its mind to the factors in clauses (a) to (d) — among other relevant factors — and has reached a conclusion that any reasonable person placed in the Government's position would have reached. The Court will not strictly scrutinise the extent to which each factor was considered; it asks only whether the Government's mind was applied to the right questions.

No Data Furnished — A Fatal Gap in the Petitioners' Case

The Court noted with particular emphasis that the petitioners had not furnished any data to show that the prices determined by the Government would have been different had the ingredients of clauses (a) to (d) been examined with reference to each individual producer instead of a representative cross-section. Without such evidence, the challenge remained a theoretical objection unsupported by any concrete showing of prejudice or alternate outcome.

5

Legislative Character of Price Fixation Orders

One of the most doctrinally significant aspects of this judgment is its treatment of whether price fixation orders under s.3(3-C) are legislative, administrative, or quasi-judicial in character — a classification with major practical consequences for procedural rights and the scope of judicial review.

The Legislative vs. Adjudicative Distinction

Citing Wade's Administrative Law (6th ed.) and Davis's Administrative Law Text (3rd ed.), the Court reiterated that what distinguishes legislation from adjudication is that legislation affects the rights of individuals in the abstract — applicable to all members of a broadly identifiable class — and must be applied in a further proceeding before any particular individual's position is definitively touched. Adjudication, by contrast, operates concretely upon individuals in their individual capacity.

The "Price of Sugar" Order is Legislative

The impugned orders — notified in the Official Gazette, applicable without exception to all producers falling within well-defined zones, laid before both Houses of Parliament — satisfy every test of legislative character. They determine the rate; they do not decide individual cases. The Court held: "These orders are required by sub-section (6) to be laid before both Houses of Parliament. The notified prices are applicable without exception to all persons falling within well defined groups. They can be legitimately characterised as legislative."

But the "Amount" Order is Non-Legislative

The order calculating the specific "amount" payable to each individual producer — applying the predetermined price to that producer's actual sales — is a non-legislative, administrative act. The principle of audi alteram partem is not applicable to the legislative price-determination order. However, the calculation of the individual amount must still be done reasonably and with fair play — emanations of natural justice — even though a formal hearing is not required.

Clarifying Cynamide — An Apparent Inconsistency Resolved

The petitioners relied on an observation of Chinnappa Reddy, J. in Union of India v. Cynamide India Ltd. [1987 (2) SCC 720] suggesting that an order under s.3(3-C) is non-legislative. The Court in Sitaram Sugar distinguished this: Chinnappa Reddy, J. was speaking of the individual amount-calculation order applicable to a specific producer — which is indeed non-legislative — not the price-determination order of general application, which is legislative. The two must not be conflated.

6

Judicial Review of Economic Policy — Limits Defined

The Court's articulation of the limits of judicial review in matters of economic policy — particularly price fixation — is one of the most frequently cited passages from this judgment.

Courts Do Not Substitute Their Judgment on Economic Policy

Quoting Justice Frankfurter of the U.S. Supreme Court in Railroad Commission of Texas v. Rowan & Nichols Oil Co. [311 US 570], the Court held that judicial review is not concerned with matters of economic policy. Courts do not supplant the "feel of the expert" with their own views. When the legislature delegates price-fixing power to an expert agency after due deliberation, the court does not re-examine the correctness of the factual basis of the resulting orders.

Judicial Review is Exhausted at "Rational Basis"

The judicial function in reviewing a price fixation order is exhausted when the court finds that the order has "warrant in the record" and a "rational basis in law." If the findings of fact by the expert authority are reasonably based on evidence and consistent with the laws of the land, judicial review ends — even if the court might have reached a different conclusion as a trier of fact.

When Courts CAN Intervene

Any act of the repository of power — whether legislative, administrative, or quasi-judicial — remains open to challenge if it: (i) conflicts with the Constitution or the governing Act; (ii) conflicts with the general principles of the law of the land; or (iii) is so arbitrary or unreasonable that no fair-minded authority could ever have made it. These are the outer limits; within them, the expert authority's judgment is final.

Article 14 Applies to All State Action — Including Legislative

Even price fixation orders — characterised as legislative — cannot be arbitrary. The principle of equality under Article 14 must guide every State action. As held in E.P. Royappa v. State of Tamil Nadu [1974 (2) SCR 348]: "equality and arbitrariness are sworn enemies." Any classification must have an intelligible differentia with a rational nexus to the object sought to be achieved.

7

The Zonal System — Upheld on Expert Foundations

Having established the doctrinal framework, the Court applied it to the specific zonal price fixation system for levy sugar — relying on a chain of expert committee recommendations and its own prior Constitution Bench decisions.

Precedent — Anakapalle & Panipat (1973)

The zonal system had already been comprehensively examined and upheld by a Constitution Bench of the Supreme Court in two landmark 1973 decisions — Anakapalle Co-operative Agricultural & Industrial Society Ltd. v. Union of India [1973 (2) SCR 882] and The Panipat Cooperative Sugar Mills v. Union of India [1973 (2) SCR 860]. The bench in Sitaram Sugar found no grounds to reconsider those decisions.

Tariff Commission Recommendations

The zonal classification was based on the Tariff Commission's 1969 and 1973 Reports — comprehensive expert studies recommending 15–16 zones on geographical-cum-agro-economic grounds. Factors such as sugarcane availability, quality, labour conditions, climate, and infrastructure — which are broadly similar within a zone but vary between zones — justified the regional grouping.

Unit-Wise Fixation — Rejected as Impractical

The Court reaffirmed Anakapalle's rejection of unit-wise price fixation as impractical and contrary to the object of price control. If price were fixed at the actual cost of each individual producer, inefficient and mismanaged mills would be subsidised while consumer interests would be sacrificed. A "cost-plus" basis for each unit "perpetuates inefficiency and is against the long-term interest of the country" — as held in the 1965 Sugar Enquiry Commission Report.

Intelligible Differentia — Rational Nexus Established

Geographical-cum-agro-economic classification of zones has a clear and rational nexus with the object of s.3(3-C): determining a fair price of sugar at which the compulsory levy can be sustained while ensuring a reasonable return to a representatively efficient producer. This is not a discriminatory classification; it is an evidence-based economic methodology.

On Losses Attributed to Zoning — The Court's Response

The petitioners claimed losses attributable to the zonal system. The Court's response was incisive: if a mill incurs losses, those losses need not be caused by geographical zoning at all. They may equally be caused by the condition of plant and machinery, quality of management, investment policy, labour relations, and financial decisions — matters entirely within the control of the mill owners, on which the petitioners had furnished no data. Even in a free market without price control, uneconomic high-cost units would face competitive disadvantage. Price control does not immunise inefficiency.

8

Final Holdings and Significance

The Five-Judge Bench Held Unanimously —

  • The impugned notifications are intra vires s.3(3-C) of the Essential Commodities Act, 1955. There is no merit in the constitutional challenge.
  • "Having regard to" in s.3(3-C) is directory, not strictly mandatory. It is legislative guidance — not a fetter — requiring the Government to address its mind to the four specified factors among other relevant considerations. The Government need not examine each factor unit-wise.
  • Price-determination orders under s.3(3-C) — notified in the Official Gazette and laid before Parliament — are legislative in character. No rule of natural justice (including audi alteram partem) applies to them.
  • Individual amount-calculation orders are administrative (non-legislative) and must satisfy the requirements of reasonableness and fair play.
  • Geographical-cum-agro-economic zoning is a rational, expert-based policy classification with a clear nexus to the objective of fair price determination. It does not violate Article 14.
  • Judicial review of economic policy is limited. Courts do not substitute their judgment for that of the expert authority. The judicial function is exhausted when a rational basis for the order is found in the record.
  • Decisions in Anakapalle (1973) and Panipat (1973) require no reconsideration. The writ petitions are dismissed. No order as to costs.

Why This Judgment Continues to Matter

Sitaram Sugar (1990) remains a foundational citation in three distinct areas of Indian law: (1) the doctrine of judicial review of economic policy — courts do not supplant the expert's judgment; (2) the legislative character of price fixation orders — no hearing required at the price-determination stage; and (3) the interpretation of "having regard to" as directory guidance rather than a mandatory exhaustive condition. It has been cited with approval by the Supreme Court in multiple subsequent decisions across regulatory and constitutional law.

⚖️ Verdict — Zonal Pricing Upheld. Judicial Review Has Limits.

The Sitaram Sugar Company judgment draws a clear constitutional map for the regulation of essential commodity prices in India. The Central Government's power to fix levy sugar prices on a zonal basis — grounded in expert committee recommendations and parliamentary oversight — is a legitimate exercise of delegated legislative power. Courts enter this domain only to check arbitrariness and illegality, not to re-determine what price is "correct."

For practitioners dealing with essential commodity regulation, price fixation challenges, or delegated legislative power, this judgment is an indispensable reference on the outer limits of Article 32 jurisdiction over executive price orders.
