A Fair Price Shop, 249 kg of Unrecorded Rice, and a Prosecution That Couldn't Prove Its Case
Introduction — What This Case Is About

On an April afternoon in 2007, a team of enforcement officers from Delhi's Food and Supplies Department walked into a Fair Price Shop on Nathu Pura Road and found something suspicious: 249 kilograms of loose rice stacked in the premises — but the stock register showed nil. On its face, this looks like a clear case of PDS misappropriation. The rice isn't on the books. Where did it come from?

Thirteen years later, a Metropolitan Magistrate at Tis Hazari Courts, Delhi, acquitted both the salesman and the shopkeeper. Not because the rice wasn't there — it was. But because the prosecution, despite having nine witnesses and years to build its case, failed to answer the most fundamental question: Was that rice the government's rice?

This case is a study in how evidentiary protocols — sampling, independent witnessing, account verification — are not bureaucratic formalities. When they are skipped, even a seemingly strong case collapses in court. And this is precisely what happened at FPS No. 9097, M/s Mohit Store, Nathu Pura, Delhi.

The Public Distribution System (PDS) in Delhi — The Legal Background

The Public Distribution System in Delhi is administered by the Food & Supplies Department of the GNCT of Delhi under the National Food Security Act, 2013 (NFSA) and earlier under the Targeted Public Distribution System (TPDS) framework. Fair Price Shops (FPS) are licensed outlets through which subsidised foodgrains — rice, wheat, sugar — are distributed to APL (Above Poverty Line), BPL (Below Poverty Line), and AAY (Antyodaya Anna Yojana) cardholders. FPS operators receive stock from the government and are required to maintain meticulous records — stock registers, sale registers, and cash memos — to account for every kilogram received and distributed. Violation of the PDS (Control) Order, 2001 or the Delhi Specified Articles (Regulation of Distribution) Order, 1981 attracts prosecution under Section 7 of the Essential Commodities Act, 1955, with imprisonment up to seven years.

The Laws Invoked — Section 7, EC Act and the PDS Control Orders
Understanding the Legal Framework Before the Facts
Section 7, Essential Commodities Act, 1955 — The Penal Provision

Any person who contravenes any order made under Section 3 of the Act shall be punishable with imprisonment for a term which may extend to seven years, or with fine, or with both. The section covers violations of control orders relating to production, supply, distribution, and trade of essential commodities — including foodgrains, edible oils, sugar, LPG, and fertilisers.

The prosecution alleged violations of two subordinate control orders issued under the EC Act:

PDS (Control) Order, 2001

Governs the functioning of Fair Price Shops under the Targeted Public Distribution System across India. Mandates proper maintenance of stock registers, sale registers, and cash memos. Prohibits diversion, misappropriation, or sale of PDS commodities (rice, wheat, sugar, kerosene) outside the authorised beneficiary list. FPS operators must account for every unit of government-supplied stock received and distributed.

Delhi Specified Articles (Regulation of Distribution) Order, 1981

A Delhi-specific order issued under the EC Act by the GNCT of Delhi that regulates the distribution of Specified Food Articles (SFA) — rice, wheat, and sugar — through licensed FPS dealers in the NCT. Non-SFA items such as atta (wheat flour) are not covered by this order. Violations attract prosecution under Section 7 of the EC Act, 1955.

The Four Core Legal Questions
Issues for Determination Before the Court
I

Whether the 2.49 quintals of rice found at FPS No. 9097 was PDS-supplied stock (i.e., APL rice issued by the Food & Supplies Department, GNCT of Delhi) and therefore capable of constituting misappropriation under the EC Act?

II

Whether the prosecution proved a discrepancy or irregularity in the accounts — stock register, sale registers, cash memos — maintained by the accused at the FPS?

III

Whether the failure to take a sample of the seized rice for comparison with the department-supplied variety is fatal to the prosecution case?

IV

Whether the non-examination of independent public witnesses — despite their manifest availability at the time of the daytime inspection — creates reasonable doubt entitling the accused to acquittal?

Five Independent Grounds for Acquittal
Why the Prosecution's Case Failed — Comprehensively

The court found not one but five independent and cumulative grounds on which the prosecution failed. Any one of them, standing alone, would have been sufficient to create reasonable doubt. Together, they made conviction impossible.

1
No Sample Taken
No separate rice sample preserved for comparison with department-supplied stock. Identity of rice as PDS material remains purely inferential and unproven.
2
No Account Discrepancy
PW-1, PW-2, and PW-4 admitted accounts were correct. Nil register entry consistent with no April 2007 supply — admitted by all witnesses.
3
Atta Not an SFA
PW-2, PW-3, PW-4 all admitted atta is not a Specified Food Article and was not supplied by the department. That charge collapsed entirely.
4
No Cardholder Complaints
PW-1 and PW-2 admitted no ration cardholder complained about supply from January 2007 to inspection date — suggesting FPS was distributing entitlements correctly.
5
No Independent Witnesses
Inspection at 1:00 pm on a busy commercial road. No attempt to join public witnesses. IO arrived at 7:30–8:00 pm — hours after the inspection. Multiple SC precedents applied.
Why Sampling Was the Most Critical Lapse

The prosecution's entire case rested on the proposition that the 2.49 quintals of rice found at FPS No. 9097 was government-issued APL rice that had been misappropriated — i.e., not distributed to cardholders as required. Without a preserved sample and a scientific/comparative analysis showing that this rice was of the same variety, grade, and lot as the government-supplied stock, the foundational factual premise of the charge was unproven.

PW-1, PW-3, and PW-4 all admitted no sample was taken. In PDS enforcement practice, this is a mandatory protocol — the Food & Supplies Department's own inspection guidelines require that a sample of any seized commodity be separately preserved and sent for testing. The failure here was not incidental but systemic.

The Prosecution's Own Witnesses Destroyed Its Case — Accounts Were Clean

PW-1 admitted the sale register tallied with cash memos — no discrepancy in accounts. PW-2 admitted cash memo entries matched stock register and sale register correctly. PW-4 admitted accounts were correct upto 31.03.2007. All three confirmed the FPS had received no SFA in April 2007 until the inspection — meaning the nil stock entry was accurate and legitimate. When the inspecting officers themselves confirm clean books, the prosecution is left without an evidentiary foundation.

7
The Independent Witness Problem — A Recurring National Failure
How the Law on Non-Examination of Public Witnesses Has Developed

The inspection at FPS No. 9097 was conducted at 1:00 pm on a busy commercial road in Nathu Pura, Delhi — broad daylight, a populated area. The inspecting team had multiple officers. The Investigating Officer (PW-9) admitted he arrived at the spot at 7:30–8:00 pm — hours after the inspection — and that no independent witness was ever joined at any stage. No explanation was offered for this failure.

The court relied on an extensive body of Supreme Court jurisprudence affirming that this omission — when unexplained — is seriously prejudicial to the prosecution:

Hem Raj v. State of Haryana — AIR 2005 SC 2110

Unexplained omission to examine available independent witnesses is a serious infirmity, especially where prosecution evidence raises doubts about the presence of witnesses at the time of the occurrence.

Before conducting a search the concerned police officer is required to call upon some independent and respectable people of the locality to witness the search… if it is found that no attempt was even made by the concerned police officer to join with him some persons of the locality who were admittedly available to witness the recovery, it would affect the weight of evidence of the Police Officer.

— Supreme Court in Sahib Singh v. State of Punjab, AIR 1997 SC 2417
D. V. Shanmugham v. State of A.P. — AIR 1997 SC 2583

When independent witnesses were available but not examined, and only interested/departmental witnesses testified, the prosecution case must be scrutinised with greater care and caution.

Roop Chand v. State of Haryana — CC Cases 3 (HC)

Where police failed to join independent witnesses despite availability and failed to record names of those who refused, the explanation for non-joining is an afterthought and liable to be rejected.

Pawan Kumar v. The Delhi Administration — 1989 Cr.LJ 127 (Delhi)

At minimum, the IO should have deposed that an attempt was made to join public witnesses or that they refused. Absence of even this attempt throws doubt on the recovery.

Gurbel Singh v. State of Punjab — 1991 Crl. Rev. No. 504 (P&H) & Dhanpat v. State of Punjab — 2000 CC Cases HC 52

Non-joining of independent witnesses has been held fatal to the prosecution case where no satisfactory explanation is given — accused entitled to benefit of doubt.

Massa Singh v. State of Punjab — 2000 (2) CC Cases HC 11

The IO was obliged to take assistance of independent witnesses at a public place to lend authenticity to the investigation conducted. The conviction was set aside on this ground.

The Verdict — Acquittal on All Grounds
The Operative Order of MM-02 (Central), Tis Hazari Courts, Delhi

  Operative Order — Ms. Shilpi Jain, MM-02 (Central), Tis Hazari Courts, Delhi

Having appreciated the totality of evidence, the court found that the prosecution had failed to prove the guilt of the accused persons beyond reasonable doubt. The benefit of doubt was accordingly extended to both accused.

The court's cumulative reasoning (Para 40):

  • No discrepancy found in records and accounts maintained at FPS No. 9097
  • No sample of seized rice taken to establish its identity with department-supplied stock
  • No complaint lodged by any ration cardholder against the accused from January 2007 to inspection date
  • No independent public witnesses examined despite availability at the inspection site
  • Atta allegation abandoned — admitted by prosecution witnesses not to be a department-supplied SFA
  • FPS received no SFA in April 2007 till inspection — nil register entry was correct and consistent

Both accused — Satish Kumar and Vijaya Kumari — are acquitted of all charges under Section 7 of the Essential Commodities Act, 1955. Bail bonds with surety under Section 437A Cr.P.C. accepted for a period of six months from the date of judgment.

Critical Analysis — Five Observations
What This Case Tells Us About PDS Enforcement in Practice
1
The Sampling Lacuna — A Structural Flaw in PDS Enforcement

The failure to take a sample of the seized rice is the most significant lapse in this case — and it is not unique to it. In PDS enforcement cases, the identity of the commodity is the foundational factual question. Without a preserved sample and forensic comparison, the allegation that found rice was government-supplied stock is purely speculative. This is not a technical deficiency — it goes to whether any offence was committed at all. The Food & Supplies Department, GNCT of Delhi, like enforcement agencies across India, must treat mandatory sampling as a non-negotiable, first-step protocol in every inspection that results in seizure. The National Food Security Act, 2013 and the PDS (Control) Order, 2001 both require accountability — but that accountability must be backed by forensic evidence.

2
Prosecution Witnesses Who Supported the Defence — An Unusual and Damaging Feature

It is a remarkable and highly damaging feature of this case that the prosecution's own witnesses — the inspecting officers of the Food & Supplies Department — categorically admitted in cross-examination that the FPS accounts showed no discrepancy and that no ration cardholder had ever complained. These are not neutral admissions; they actively support the defence's position that the shop was operating lawfully. When the very officials who conducted the raid acknowledge clean books and satisfied beneficiaries, the prosecution faces an evidentiary challenge it fundamentally cannot overcome. The Magistrate correctly drew from these admissions the inference that the accused had been distributing PDS entitlements correctly to cardholders.

3
Non-Examination of Independent Witnesses — A Recurring Institutional Problem in Delhi

The failure to join independent public witnesses to enforcement proceedings is not an aberration in this case — it is a systemic problem in PDS and EC Act enforcement across India. The Supreme Court has consistently, across decades, affirmed that this failure — when unexplained — is seriously prejudicial or even fatal to the prosecution. The inspection in this case was at 1:00 pm on Main 25 Foota Road, Nathu Pura — a busy commercial road in Delhi in broad daylight. The team comprised five enforcement officials and three CVC representatives. Eight people present, none found to join even one local independent witness. The court's reliance on seven separate precedents on this point underscores how entrenched — and how systematically unaddressed — this problem remains in enforcement practice, particularly in the national capital.

4
Thirteen Years — Trial Delay as an Institutional Failure

The case was registered in February 2008 for an offence committed in April 2007. Judgment was delivered in February 2020 — nearly thirteen years after institution. This delay is not incidental to the outcome — it shaped it. PW-3 repeatedly stated "I do not remember due to lapse of time" — a direct product of the delay. Documents lose context. Institutional memory fades. The deterrent effect of EC Act enforcement is entirely negated when cases take over a decade to reach judgment. The Supreme Court has repeatedly affirmed speedy trial as a fundamental right under Article 21, Constitution of India. EC Act cases, being economic offences with time-sensitive public interest dimensions, should be prioritised for fast-track or summary trial procedures where available.

5
Reasonable Doubt vs. Suspicion — The Court's Nuanced Position

It is important to note what this acquittal does not mean. The court did not find the accused innocent. The presence of 249 kg of unrecorded rice at a Fair Price Shop with a nil stock register is, as the court itself acknowledged, an inherently suspicious circumstance. The defence explanation — private purchase from Vinod General Store — collapsed entirely under cross-examination. The rice could have been privately sourced; or it could have been diverted from a prior supply cycle not captured in available April 2007 records. The court correctly found that reasonable doubt existed — but the underlying facts raise legitimate questions that better evidence could have answered definitively. This is precisely why sampling and independent witnessing are so critical: they convert suspicious circumstances into provable facts.

Legal Significance

Key Takeaways & Broader Implications

🧪
Mandatory Sampling
In all EC Act and PDS enforcement cases, seized food articles must be sampled and separately preserved for comparison with department-supplied stock. Without a sample, the prosecution cannot establish that the seized commodity was government stock — and the entire charge fails at its foundation.
👥
Independent Witnesses — Non-Negotiable
Inspections, searches, and recoveries conducted in public or commercial premises must be witnessed by independent local persons. The IO must make a genuine, documented attempt to join such witnesses and record names of those who refuse. A bare assertion of unavailability is treated as an afterthought by courts.
📒
Account Verification at the Spot
Enforcement officers must independently reconcile account books at the time of inspection and document any discrepancy in the seizure memo. Relying on physical excess of stock alone — while confirming clean accounts — is an evidentiary contradiction that courts will exploit to favour the accused.
⏱️
Trial Delay — A Constitutional Issue
A 13-year trial lag defeats the purpose of EC Act enforcement. Speedy trial is a constitutional right under Article 21. EC Act cases, being time-sensitive economic offences, must be prioritised. Departments should actively follow up on pending prosecutions and push for timely disposal.
📋
Know Your SFA Scope
Enforcement officers and prosecutors must know precisely which commodities are Specified Food Articles under applicable orders. Including atta (wheat flour) — a non-SFA — in the charge damaged the prosecution's credibility and was entirely abandoned through its own witnesses.
⚖️
Proof Beyond Reasonable Doubt
EC Act offences carry imprisonment up to seven years — they are serious criminal charges. Courts apply the full constitutional standard of proof. Enforcement agencies must investigate and document with the same rigour expected in any serious criminal prosecution, not with a quasi-administrative approach.
Conclusion

Concluding Remarks

The acquittal of Satish Kumar and Vijaya Kumari in this case is a textbook illustration of how even a facially suspicious situation — a Fair Price Shop salesman found with 249 kg of unrecorded rice on a register showing nil — can result in acquittal when the investigative apparatus fails to follow basic evidentiary protocols. The prosecution's failure on five independent grounds left Metropolitan Magistrate Ms. Shilpi Jain with no viable path to conviction without violating the foundational criminal law standard of proof beyond reasonable doubt.

This case is not an indictment of the accused — the court itself noted the suspicious underlying circumstances. It is an indictment of the enforcement and investigative process of the Food & Supplies Department, GNCT of Delhi. The Essential Commodities Act was enacted to protect the food security of India's poorest citizens — the very cardholders who depend on the Public Distribution System for their daily grain. Its purpose is defeated not only by dishonest FPS operators who divert subsidised rice to the black market, but equally by prosecutions assembled without the evidentiary discipline necessary to secure convictions in court.

The Enduring Institutional Lesson

The lessons from this case — mandatory sampling, independent witnessing, on-the-spot account reconciliation, SFA scope awareness, and trial timeline management — are not legal technicalities. They are the building blocks of a prosecution that actually delivers justice for the ration cardholder who was deprived of their entitlement. Enforcement agencies and the Food & Supplies Department, GNCT of Delhi, would do well to institutionalise these protocols into every inspection procedure as non-negotiable steps, documented in every seizure memo, signed by every officer present.
