State of Haryana vs. Ch. Bhajan Lal (1990) — The Seven-Category Test for Quashing FIRs Under Section 482 CrPC

Sarat Rout April 30, 2026 13 min read Landmark Judgment
Supreme Court of India — S.R. Pandian & K. Jayachandra Reddy, JJ. | 21 November 1990 | The judgment that defined when courts may quash an FIR — and when they must step back and let the police investigate

Few Supreme Court judgments have been cited as frequently — or relied upon as universally — as State of Haryana vs. Ch. Bhajan Lal. Decided on 21 November 1990, it remains the foundational authority on when the High Court may exercise its extraordinary powers under Article 226 of the Constitution or its inherent jurisdiction under Section 482 of the Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973 to quash an FIR or criminal proceedings. The seven categories laid down in this judgment — universally known as the "Bhajan Lal categories" — are cited in virtually every FIR-quashing petition before Indian High Courts to this day. At the same time, the judgment makes equally clear the limits of judicial interference with police investigation — a balance as important as the categories themselves.

Why This Judgment Matters

Every year, thousands of FIR-quashing petitions are filed before India's High Courts. The threshold question — when can a court quash an FIR? — is answered by the seven categories in this judgment. It is equally cited for what it prohibits: courts may not stifle or impinge upon a legitimate investigation simply because the accused is powerful, the allegations are denied, or the complaint may be politically motivated. The balance between individual liberty and the state's investigative function was never stated more clearly.

Background — The Complaint Against Bhajan Lal

On 12 November 1987, a complaint was presented before the Chief Minister of Haryana containing serious allegations against the First Respondent — Ch. Bhajan Lal, who had served as Chief Minister of Haryana and was at the relevant time a Union Minister in the Central Government.

The Allegations

The complaint alleged that Bhajan Lal had accumulated huge properties worth crores of rupees in the names of his family members, relatives, and close associates by:

  • Misusing his power and position as a public servant
  • Undervaluing market prices in various property transactions
  • Engineering benami transactions — properties registered in others' names but beneficially owned by him
  • Acquiring buildings, land, shares, and ornaments far in excess of his known legal means

The complainant urged that these accumulations being disproportionate to his legal income, an investigation should be directed under the Prevention of Corruption Act, 1947.

Registration of FIR

The complaint was routed from the Chief Minister's Secretariat → Director General of Police → Superintendent of Police → SHO. On the direction of the SP, the SHO registered a case on the very first day of receiving the complaint from the DGP (after only 9 days in the DGP's office) under:

  • Sections 161 and 165 of the IPC (public servant taking gratification)
  • Section 5(2) of the Prevention of Corruption Act, 1947 (criminal misconduct)

The Respondent's Counter

Bhajan Lal filed a writ petition before the Punjab and Haryana High Court for quashing the FIR, alleging that the incoming Chief Minister of Haryana had used the complainant — his political stooge — to file false and scurrilous allegations driven entirely by deep-rooted political animosity and rivalry.

The SP's "Over-Enthusiasm" — A Note From the Court

The Supreme Court notably observed that the SP appeared to have exhibited "unprecedented over-enthusiasm, presumably to please someone" by directing the SHO to register the case and investigate it on the very first day of receiving the complaint — without disclosing any reasons for departing from the normal rule of assigning the investigation to a designated officer. While the Court found this conduct shocking to one's sense of justice, it explicitly held that this conduct could not be a ground for quashing the FIR itself.

The High Court's Error

The High Court quashed the entire criminal proceedings, holding that the allegations did not constitute a cognizable offence and therefore no lawful investigation could commence.

The High Court's reasoning was flawed on multiple levels:

  • It held that since no written counter-affidavit was filed by the State in reply to Bhajan Lal's writ petition, the averments of the respondent must be treated as having disproved the entire crimination alleged in the FIR — the Supreme Court held this neither conceivable nor comprehensible
  • It drew upon a historical anecdote to suggest that an incoming government should not rake up the past — the SC held this observation unwarranted and the anecdote out of context and inappropriate
  • It substituted its own assessment of the merits of the allegations at the FIR stage — a function that belongs exclusively to the investigating agencies and, thereafter, the trial court

The Supreme Court set aside the High Court's order, restored the FIR, but quashed the investigation on the distinct ground that the SHO lacked valid authority under Section 5A(1) of the Prevention of Corruption Act.

Section 154 CrPC — The Mandatory Duty to Register an FIR

Section 154(1) — Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973

Every information relating to the commission of a cognizable offence, if given orally to an officer in charge of a police station, shall be reduced to writing by him or under his direction, and be read over to the informant; and every such information, whether given in writing or reduced to writing as aforesaid, shall be signed by the person giving it, and the substance thereof shall be entered in a book to be kept by such officer in such form as the State Government may prescribe.

The Supreme Court held unequivocally that if any information disclosing a cognizable offence is laid before an officer-in-charge of a police station satisfying the requirements of Section 154(1), the police officer has no other option except to enter the substance thereof in the prescribed form — that is, to register a case. This is a mandatory, non-discretionary duty.

The Court reinforced that a police officer cannot investigate a non-cognizable offence without a Magistrate's order under Section 155(2). However, under Section 155(4), where a case relates to two or more offences of which at least one is cognizable, the entire case is deemed cognizable and the police officer may investigate all offences with the same powers as in a cognizable case.

Lalita Kumari — Later Reinforcement (2014)

The mandatory nature of Section 154(1) was further reinforced and expanded by the Supreme Court's Constitution Bench in Lalita Kumari vs. Government of Uttar Pradesh (2014) 2 SCC 1, which held that registration of an FIR is mandatory upon receipt of information disclosing a cognizable offence — and a preliminary inquiry before registration is permissible only in specific categories of cases (matrimonial, commercial, medical negligence, corruption, and abnormally delayed cases). Bhajan Lal and Lalita Kumari together form the complete FIR-registration framework.

The Meaning of "Reason to Suspect"

The expression "reason to suspect the commission of an offence" under Section 154(1) CrPC — and in the broader investigation framework of Sections 156–159 — was carefully defined by the Court.

The Court held that "reason to suspect" means the sagacity of rationally inferring the commission of a cognizable offence based on the specific articulate facts mentioned in the FIR, including any annexures and attending circumstances — even though these may not amount to proof.

  • The expression is governed and dictated by the facts and circumstances of each case
  • At the stage of registration, the question of adequate proof does not arise — mere rational inference from stated facts is sufficient
  • The investigating officer must subjectively satisfy himself that there is sufficient ground for entering on an investigation under the proviso to Section 157(1)(b)

Investigation as the Exclusive Domain of Police

The Field of Investigation — Courts Must Stay Back

The Core Principle

The Supreme Court held that the investigation of any cognizable offence is exclusively within the domain of the investigating agencies. Courts cannot exercise control over this domain and have no power to stifle or impinge upon the proceedings in an investigation so long as it proceeds in compliance with the provisions of Chapter XII of the CrPC.

The Magistrate's Role During Investigation

A Magistrate is kept in the picture at all stages of police investigation — but he is not authorised to interfere with the actual investigation or to direct the police how that investigation is to be conducted. If the police decide not to investigate, only then can the Magistrate intervene: either directing an investigation, or proceeding himself, or deputing a subordinate Magistrate to hold a preliminary inquiry.

When Courts May Intervene

The Court carved out a narrow but important exception: if a police officer transgresses the circumscribed limits and improperly or illegally exercises investigatory powers in breach of a statutory provision, thereby causing serious prejudice to the personal liberty and property of a citizen, the Court — on being approached by the aggrieved person — must consider the nature and extent of the breach and pass appropriate orders. No one can claim absolute immunity or unlimited powers.

The Seven Categories — When Courts May Quash an FIR

This is the most-cited part of the entire judgment. The Supreme Court held that in the exercise of extraordinary power under Article 226 or the inherent power under Section 482 CrPC, the following categories of cases illustrate when such power may be exercised to prevent abuse of the process of any court or to secure the ends of justice. The Court emphasised that these are illustrative, not exhaustive — no precise, inflexible guide-lines can be laid down given the myriad kinds of cases that may arise.

A
Allegations Do Not Constitute Any Offence

Where the allegations made in the FIR or the complaint, even if taken at face value and accepted in their entirety, do not prima facie constitute any offence or make out a case against the accused.

B
No Cognizable Offence Disclosed — No Basis for Police Investigation

Where the allegations in the FIR and accompanying materials do not disclose a cognizable offence, justifying an investigation by police under Section 156(1) CrPC — except under a Magistrate's order within the scope of Section 155(2).

C
Uncontroverted Allegations + Evidence Do Not Disclose Any Offence

Where the uncontroverted allegations in the FIR or complaint and the evidence collected in support thereof do not disclose the commission of any offence and do not make out a case against the accused.

D
Only Non-Cognizable Offence Made Out — No Police Investigation Without Magistrate's Order

Where the allegations in the FIR constitute only a non-cognizable offence — no investigation is permitted by a police officer without a Magistrate's order under Section 155(2) CrPC.

E
Allegations Absurd and Inherently Improbable

Where the allegations in the FIR or complaint are so absurd and inherently improbable on their face that no prudent person could ever reach a just conclusion that there is sufficient ground for proceeding against the accused.

F
Express Legal Bar or Specific Statutory Remedy Available

Where there is an express legal bar engrafted in any provision of the Code or the concerned Act to the institution and continuance of the proceedings — and/or where there is a specific provision providing efficacious redress for the grievance of the aggrieved party.

G
Manifestly Mala Fide or Maliciously Instituted Proceedings

Where a criminal proceeding is manifestly attended with mala fide and/or where the proceeding is maliciously instituted with an ulterior motive for wreaking vengeance on the accused and with a view to spite him due to private and personal grudge.

Critical Caution — These Categories Are Not Freely Applicable

The Supreme Court has repeatedly cautioned against the liberal use of these categories. In particular, category (g) — mala fide — is very difficult to establish at the FIR stage when investigation has not yet concluded. The mere fact that a complaint may have a political motive, or that the complainant has personal animosity, does not by itself bring a case within category (g) if the allegations themselves are serious and require investigation. The categories are meant to prevent clear abuse of process — not to give accused persons an early exit from legitimate investigations.

Mala Fide Complaint — Cannot Quash at Preliminary Stage

Bhajan Lal's primary argument was that the complaint was filed due to deep-rooted political animosity of the incoming Chief Minister who had used the complainant as a stooge. The Supreme Court rejected this as a ground for quashing the FIR, holding:

  • The matter was at a premature stage — the investigation had not proceeded beyond some preliminary effort on the date of registration
  • Evidence must be gathered after thorough investigation before the Court can arrive at any conclusion on the plea of mala fides
  • If the allegations are bereft of truth and made maliciously, the investigation itself will say so
  • At the stage where there are only allegations and recriminations but no evidence, the Court cannot anticipate the result of investigation and render a finding on mala fides
  • Even assuming the complainant filed the complaint due to personal animosity, that by itself is not a ground to discard a complaint containing serious allegations that must be tested after evidence is collected

The Court also clarified the dominant purpose of an FIR and follow-up investigation: only to investigate the allegations and, if sufficient evidence supports them, present a case before the court. The purpose is not to make a character assassination of the person complained against.

Section 5A, Prevention of Corruption Act — Mandatory Safeguard

While the FIR itself was upheld, the Supreme Court quashed the investigation on a distinct ground: the SHO who had taken up the investigation was not a designated officer under Section 5A(1) of the Prevention of Corruption Act, 1947, and no valid order authorising him to investigate had been passed.

What Section 5A(1) Required

Section 5A of the 1947 Act — the precursor to the modern Prevention of Corruption Act's investigation provisions — provided that offences under Section 5(1)(e) (disproportionate assets / criminal misconduct) shall not be investigated by any police officer below a designated rank except:

  • First Proviso: A police officer not below the rank of Inspector, if authorised by the State Government, could investigate — but only on a separate and independent order of an officer not below the rank of Superintendent of Police
  • Second Proviso: Strict compliance with the SP's order was an additional legal requirement — the order must, on its face, disclose the reasons for authorising the lower-ranked officer

The Rule and the Exception

Investigation by designated officers is the rule; investigation by an officer of lower rank is the exception — and that exception must be justified with adequate reasons disclosed on the face of the order.

The Main Object of Section 5A — As Held by the Court

The main object of Section 5A is to protect the public servant against harassment and victimisation. Section 5A is mandatory and not directory. The investigation carried on by a non-designated officer in contravention of Section 5A is an illegality — though that illegality does not affect the competence and jurisdiction of the court for trial once cognizance has been taken and the trial has proceeded to conclusion, unless a miscarriage of justice has been caused thereby.

Why the SP's Order Was Fatally Defective

In this case, the SP gave absolutely no reason in directing the SHO to investigate. There was no disclosure of why the normal rule was departed from. The SHO was therefore not clothed with requisite legal authority under the second proviso to Section 5A(1). The Court found it an inexplicable departure — why would the SP hastily direct the SHO to investigate serious allegations against a former Chief Minister and Union Minister without recording a single reason? The investigation was accordingly quashed, while leaving the State free to re-initiate investigation through a properly authorised officer.

Valid Investigation — Requirements

  • Must be conducted by a police officer of designated rank under Section 5A(1)
  • If lower-ranked officer is authorised — SP-level order required, with reasons disclosed on the face of the order
  • SP must genuinely satisfy himself that good and sufficient reasons exist for departing from the normal rule
  • Both the first and second provisos must be strictly complied with — they are cumulative, not alternative

What Makes Investigation Illegal

  • Investigation by an SHO without a valid authorising order from SP or above
  • SP's order that gives no reasons for departing from the designated-officer rule
  • Hasty registration and investigation driven by considerations beyond statutory compliance
  • However — illegality of investigation does not void the FIR or the trial, unless it causes miscarriage of justice

The Final Verdict — FIR Upheld, Investigation Quashed

FIR — Upheld

The High Court's order quashing the FIR was set aside. The allegations in the complaint, taken at face value, clearly constituted a cognizable offence under the Prevention of Corruption Act warranting investigation. The High Court had no justification in quashing the FIR at this premature stage. The FIR was restored.

Investigation — Quashed

The commencement and entirety of the investigation — in so far as carried on by the SHO — was quashed on the ground that the SHO was not clothed with valid legal authority under the second proviso to Section 5A(1) of the Prevention of Corruption Act. The State remained free to re-investigate through a properly authorised officer.

Enduring Significance

More than three decades after it was decided, Bhajan Lal remains the single most authoritative pronouncement on FIR-quashing in Indian law. Its significance operates on multiple levels:

For Accused Persons and Their Counsel

  • The seven categories provide a structured framework for testing whether a quashing petition is maintainable — counsel must identify which specific category is attracted before approaching the High Court
  • Category (a) — allegations do not constitute any offence even if fully accepted — is the most straightforwardly applied; categories (e) and (g) require the highest threshold
  • Political motivation or personal animosity of the complainant alone will not suffice unless the allegations themselves are substantively deficient

For Enforcement Authorities and Prosecutors

  • The mandatory nature of FIR registration under Section 154(1) is absolute — any refusal to register an FIR disclosing a cognizable offence is itself illegal
  • Investigation under special statutes (like the Prevention of Corruption Act) must strictly follow the designated-officer requirement — short-cutting through lower-ranked officers without proper authorisation and recorded reasons will render the investigation vulnerable to quashing
  • The investigation itself and the FIR are independently assessable — the FIR may stand even when the investigation is quashed

For Courts

  • The power to quash is extraordinary and must be exercised sparingly — it is not an appeal against the decision to register an FIR
  • Non-filing of a counter-affidavit by the State does not amount to admission of the accused's allegations — courts cannot treat silence as concession at the FIR stage
  • The question of mala fides is for the trial court to determine after evidence is led — not for the High Court to decide on untested affidavits at the pre-investigation stage
Later Developments — The Bhajan Lal Framework Applied and Refined

The Bhajan Lal categories have been applied and refined in numerous subsequent Supreme Court judgments. Notably, in Vineet Narain vs. Union of India (1998) 1 SCC 226 (the Hawala Case), the Court reinforced that investigating agencies must be free from executive interference. In Amit Kapila vs. State and other recent decisions, courts have underscored that the Bhajan Lal framework does not permit pre-emptive quashing of investigations into serious offences merely because the accused disputes the allegations — the investigation must be allowed to conclude before the court can properly assess the merits.

🎯 Summary

State of Haryana vs. Ch. Bhajan Lal (1990) is one of India's most consequential criminal law judgments. On the FIR-quashing question, it laid down seven categories — (a) through (g) — that remain the definitive test used by every High Court in India. On the investigation question, it reaffirmed that the investigation of cognizable offences is the exclusive domain of the police, and courts may only intervene when there is a clear statutory violation causing serious prejudice. On the Prevention of Corruption Act question, it declared Section 5A(1) mandatory — investigation by a non-designated officer without proper authorisation and recorded reasons is an illegality that vitiates the investigation (though not necessarily the trial).

The outcome itself was carefully calibrated: the FIR was restored (serious allegations warranted investigation) but the investigation was quashed (the SHO had no valid authority). The State was free to begin again — but this time, through the right officer, with the right authorisation, and with recorded reasons.
