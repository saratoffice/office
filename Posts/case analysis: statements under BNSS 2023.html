Case Analysis: Recording of Confessions and Statements Under the Bharatiya Nagarik Suraksha Sanhita, 2023 — Section 183

Sarat Rout March 15, 2025 14 min read Criminal Law
Section 183 BNSS 2023 — governing recording of confessions and statements before a Magistrate

The Bharatiya Nagarik Suraksha Sanhita, 2023 (BNSS) replaced the Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973 (CrPC) as India's principal statute governing criminal procedure. Among its most significant provisions is Section 183, which modernises the framework for recording confessions and statements before a Magistrate. Building upon its predecessor — Section 164 of the CrPC — Section 183 introduces digital recording mandates, strengthened protections for victims of sexual offences, robust safeguards for persons with disabilities, and clarified rules on evidentiary value. This article provides a comprehensive analysis of every sub-section of Section 183, with procedural nuances, constitutional context, and key judicial precedents.

1. Overview and Legislative Context

  Bharatiya Nagarik Suraksha Sanhita, 2023

Section 183 — Recording of Confessions and Statements

Corresponding to: Section 164 of the Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973 (CrPC)

Governs the recording of confessions and statements made by an accused, a witness, or a victim to a Judicial Magistrate — during investigation or at any time before the commencement of an inquiry or trial.

In force from 1 July 2024 Audio-video recording mandated Enhanced victim protections Disability-inclusive provisions

Section 183 BNSS is the cornerstone of judicially supervised statement-recording in Indian criminal procedure. Its purpose is threefold: to ensure that confessions are genuinely voluntary and free from police influence; to create an admissible, tamper-proof record of statements made before the commencement of formal judicial proceedings; and to protect the dignity and rights of vulnerable persons — particularly victims of sexual violence and individuals with disabilities — during the most sensitive stages of a criminal investigation.

Why a Magistrate — Not the Police? Confessions made to police officers are expressly inadmissible under Section 23 of the Bharatiya Sakshya Adhiniyam, 2023 (BSA) — the successor to the Indian Evidence Act. By routing confessions through an independent judicial officer, Section 183 BNSS ensures that the record carries presumptive reliability and is not tainted by custodial pressure.

2. Authority of the Magistrate — Section 183(1)

Who Can Record — and Who Cannot

Any Magistrate of the district in which the FIR has been registered may record any confession or statement made during an investigation — or at any time afterwards, but before the commencement of an inquiry or trial. Crucially, this jurisdiction is district-wide and not case-specific: the Magistrate need not have jurisdiction over the case being investigated.

  • Audio-video electronic means: Confessions and statements may be recorded using audio-video electronic means in the presence of the accused's advocate, wherever feasible.
  • Police officers excluded: A police officer on whom the powers of a Magistrate have been conferred under any law is expressly prohibited from recording a confession under this section. The prohibition is absolute and admits of no exceptions.
Jurisdictional Scope: Unlike Section 164 CrPC, which allowed any Judicial Magistrate to record confessions regardless of district, Section 183 BNSS localises jurisdiction to the district where the FIR was registered. This prevents forum-shopping and reduces jurisdictional anomalies, but also means a victim or accused cannot approach a Magistrate in a different district even if it is more convenient for them.

3. Safeguards Before Recording a Confession — Sections 183(2) & (3)

Before recording any confession, the Magistrate must satisfy two independent obligations — an explanatory duty and a verification duty. These are not merely procedural formalities; they are constitutional safeguards embedded in the Code.

1. Inform of Right to Silence

The Magistrate must clearly inform the person that they are not bound to make a confession — that the making of a confession is entirely voluntary and no adverse inference will be drawn from silence alone.

2. Warn of Consequences

The person must be told that if they do confess, the confession may be used as evidence against them in the subsequent inquiry or trial. The warning must be clear, not cursory.

3. Verify Voluntariness

The Magistrate must satisfy themselves — through direct questioning — that the confession is genuinely voluntary and not made under any inducement, threat, or promise from police or any other person.

4. No Police Custody if Unwilling

If, at any point before the confession is recorded, the person expresses unwillingness to confess, the Magistrate must not authorise their detention in police custody. This is an absolute prohibition — not a discretionary one.

5. Time for Reflection

The Magistrate is expected to give the person reasonable time for reflection before recording the confession — particularly where the person has been in police custody. The sooner a confession is recorded, the better, but it must not be rushed.

6. Remove Police Influence

Courts have consistently held that the Magistrate must take active precautions to remove any police influence before recording the confession — including ensuring that no police officer is present during recording unless absolutely necessary.

4. Procedure for Recording a Confession — Section 183(4)

Once the Magistrate is satisfied of the voluntariness of the confession, the recording must follow the procedure prescribed in Section 316 BNSS for recording the examination of an accused person. The following steps apply:

  1. The confession is recorded in the Magistrate's own handwriting or through audio-video electronic means — question by question if necessary, in a narrative form otherwise.
  2. Each portion of the statement is read back to the person in a language they understand. Where necessary, a certified interpreter assists.
  3. The person is given an opportunity to make corrections. Any corrections are incorporated and initialled.
  4. The person making the confession must sign the written record or provide biometric authentication where recording is electronic.
  5. The Magistrate appends the mandatory memorandum (see Section 5 below) at the foot of the record.
  6. Where audio-video recording is used, the electronic file is secured, timestamped, and forms part of the official record forwarded to the trial court.

5. The Magistrate's Memorandum

At the conclusion of the record, the Magistrate must append a signed memorandum in the following prescribed form. This memorandum is the Magistrate's personal certification of voluntariness — and its absence or deficiency can render the confession invalid.

  Mandatory Memorandum — Section 183(4) BNSS

"I have explained to [name of person] that he is not bound to make a confession and that, if he does so, any confession he may make may be used as evidence against him; and I believe that this confession was voluntarily made. It was taken in my presence and hearing, and was read over to the person making it and admitted by him to be correct, and it contains a full and true account of the statement made by him."

(Signed) A.B., Magistrate
Significance of the Memorandum: Courts have held that the memorandum is not a mere formality — it is a judicial certificate of voluntariness. Its presence creates a presumption of compliance with Section 183 safeguards. Its absence or material deviation raises a serious doubt about the admissibility of the confession. The Magistrate who signs it takes personal responsibility for its accuracy.

6. Non-Confessional Statements — Section 183(5)

Statements Other Than Confessions

Any statement made under Section 183(1) that is not a confession — for example, a witness statement, a victim's narration, or any statement that does not amount to an admission of guilt — is recorded in a manner the Magistrate considers best fitted to the circumstances of the case. The strict procedure for confessions does not apply.

The Magistrate has the authority to administer an oath to the person making the statement. This is a significant departure from police-recorded statements under Section 175 BNSS (successor to Section 161 CrPC), where no oath can be administered.

Evidentiary distinction: A non-confessional statement recorded under Section 183 can be used to corroborate or contradict the witness's testimony in court under Section 145 of the Bharatiya Sakshya Adhiniyam, 2023. It is not substantive evidence by itself, but its value in cross-examination is significant.

7. Special Provisions for Sexual Offences — Section 183(6)(a)

Sections 64–79 and 124 of the Bharatiya Nyaya Sanhita, 2023

In cases involving offences punishable under Sections 64–79 and 124 of the BNS — encompassing rape, gang rape, sexual assault, sexual offences against minors, stalking, voyeurism, and acid attacks — the following mandatory provisions apply:

  • The Magistrate must record the victim's statement as soon as the offence is reported to the police. There is no discretion to defer this recording.
  • The statement should be recorded by a female Magistrate wherever feasible. If no female Magistrate is available, a male Magistrate must record the statement in the presence of a woman.
  • In cases involving offences punishable with ten or more years' imprisonment, life imprisonment, or the death penalty, the Magistrate is required to record the statement of any witness produced by the police officer.

8. Statements of Persons with Disabilities — Section 183(6)(b)

Inclusive Safeguards for Persons with Disabilities

Where the person making the statement has any temporary or permanent mental or physical disability, the Magistrate must:

  • Engage an interpreter or a special educator to assist in communication during the recording process
  • Record the statement using audio-visual electronic means — preferably via mobile phone — rather than the previously mandated videography requirement under the CrPC
  • Ensure that the interpreter or special educator is qualified and impartial
Evidentiary Value — Section 142 BSA 2023: Statements recorded under this provision are treated as examination-in-chief within the meaning of Section 142 of the Bharatiya Sakshya Adhiniyam, 2023. This means the person may be cross-examined by the opposite party on this statement at trial — without the need to re-record the statement. This is a significant protection: the disabled person need not relive the trauma of giving evidence in open court in conventional examination.
Practical Improvement over CrPC: Section 164 CrPC required videography for disability-related statements — a resource-intensive process not uniformly available across India. Section 183 BNSS substitutes this with audio-visual recording via mobile phone, making the provision practically implementable even in remote districts and under-resourced courts.

9. Transmission of Records — Section 183(7)

Once a confession or statement has been recorded under Section 183, the Magistrate who recorded it must forward the entire record — including any audio-video file — to the Magistrate by whom the case is to be inquired into or tried. This ensures that the recorded statement becomes an integral part of the judicial record and is available to the trial court from the outset.

Re-recording — Not a Routine Power: The Allahabad High Court (Division Bench, Justice Rajiv Gupta and Justice Achal Sachdev, 2026) clarified that a direction for re-recording a victim's statement under Section 183 BNSS is not a routine power — it can only be exercised in exceptional circumstances by the High Court or the Supreme Court to prevent miscarriage of justice. The section contemplates a single, reliable, voluntary recording. There is no statutory mandate for multiple recordings of the same person's statement.

10. Constitutional Safeguards

Section 183 BNSS and the Constitution of India

  • Article 20(3): Right against self-incrimination — no person accused of an offence shall be compelled to be a witness against themselves. Section 183's voluntariness requirement is a direct legislative embodiment of this guarantee. A coerced confession violates Article 20(3) and is void.
  • Article 21: Right to life and personal liberty — the prohibition on returning an unwilling confessor to police custody directly protects the accused's personal liberty from arbitrary executive action.
  • Article 22(1): Right to consult and be defended by a legal practitioner — Section 183(1)'s proviso for the presence of the accused's advocate during audio-video recording directly operationalises this constitutional right.
  • Article 14: Right to equality — the disability-inclusive provisions under Section 183(6)(b) reflect the equality mandate, ensuring that persons with disabilities have meaningful, not merely formal, access to the confession-recording process.

11. Key Judicial Precedents

Dagdu & Ors. v. State of Maharashtra
Supreme Court of India — 1977

The Judicial Magistrate must strictly follow the procedure while recording a confession. Confessions must be voluntary, free from any form of coercion. The Magistrate must take active precautions to remove police influence before recording, and the sooner the confession is recorded after separation from police custody, the better.

Pyare Lal Bhargava v. State of Rajasthan
Supreme Court of India — 1963

Confessions must be made voluntarily and with full understanding of their consequences. The Magistrate's duty to explain the right to silence and the evidentiary use of a confession is not merely directory — it is a mandatory precondition to a valid recording.

State of Punjab v. Gurdeep Singh
Supreme Court of India — AIR 1999 SC 2080

A confession obtained by threat, inducement, or promise loses its entire evidentiary value. Voluntariness is the cornerstone of a valid judicial confession. This principle is now codified in the BNSS framework with added transparency through electronic recording.

State (NCT of Delhi) v. Navjot Sandhu
Supreme Court of India — 2005 (Parliament Attack Case)

Electronic and recorded evidence can be used to prove the voluntariness and accuracy of a statement if the recording is done transparently. This ruling provided the judicial foundation for Section 183 BNSS's audio-video recording mandate.

Mahabir Singh v. State of Haryana
Supreme Court of India — 2001

Recording of statements is critical for establishing a victim's narrative in cases of serious offences. The principle supports Section 183(6)(a)'s mandate for immediate statement recording in sexual offence cases.

Allahabad HC — Re-Recording Case
Allahabad High Court — 2026 (Justice Rajiv Gupta & Justice Achal Sachdev)

Held that directing a fresh recording of a victim's statement under Section 183 BNSS is not a routine power. Section 183 contemplates a single, reliable, voluntary statement. Re-recording may only be directed in exceptional circumstances by the High Court or Supreme Court to prevent miscarriage of justice.

Section 183 BNSS represents a significant modernisation of India's confession-recording framework — marrying constitutional safeguards with technological accountability, while extending meaningful protection to the most vulnerable participants in the criminal justice process.

In essence: Section 183 of the BNSS 2023 is not merely a procedural provision — it is a rights-based framework that operationalises Article 20(3)'s protection against self-incrimination, Article 21's guarantee of personal liberty, and Article 22(1)'s right to legal counsel, all within a single judicially supervised mechanism. Its introduction of audio-video recording, immediate victim statement-taking, interpreter-assisted disability provisions, and examination-in-chief status for disability statements marks a qualitative leap from the CrPC era toward a criminal procedure regime that is transparent, inclusive, and technologically accountable.
