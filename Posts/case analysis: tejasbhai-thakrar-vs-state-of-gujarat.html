1
A Trader on the Highway — and a Sudden Seizure
Setting the Scene — Who is Tejasbhai, and What Happened on 31 October 2012?

Picture a trader moving his goods on a highway in Gujarat. His business involves trading in essential commodities — the everyday items that keep households and businesses running: foodgrains, edible oils, sugar, and similar goods regulated under Indian law. On 31 October 2012, his goods in transit were intercepted by enforcement officers. Allegations of illegalities and irregularities under the Essential Commodities Act, 1955 were made against him. The goods were seized on the spot.

This is not an unusual scene in India. Enforcement agencies frequently intercept vehicles carrying essential commodities to check for hoarding, black-marketing, or violations of storage and distribution orders. When a violation is found — or suspected — the authorities have the power to confiscate the goods. But that power is not absolute. It comes with strict procedural safeguards that the law demands be followed to the letter. And in Tejasbhai's case, one critical safeguard was skipped entirely — with consequences that would reach all the way to the Gujarat High Court.

What Are "Essential Commodities" Under Indian Law?

The Essential Commodities Act, 1955 is a central legislation that gives the government wide powers to control the production, supply, distribution, and trade of commodities declared "essential" — including foodgrains, edible oils, sugar, kerosene, LPG, drugs, and fertilisers. The Act aims to prevent hoarding, black-marketing, and profiteering in these goods, especially during scarcity. The power to seize and confiscate is one of its strongest enforcement tools — and precisely because it is so drastic, the law has built-in procedural protections before confiscation can be ordered.

4
Understanding the Law — Sections 6A and 6B Explained Simply
The Confiscation Power and Its Mandatory Three-Step Safeguard

To understand why this case is important, we first need to understand what Sections 6A and 6B of the Essential Commodities Act actually say — and why they are designed the way they are.

Section 6A gives the Collector or any authorised officer a very powerful tool: the ability to confiscate any essential commodity, vehicle, vessel, animal, or conveyance used in violating any order made under the Act. Confiscation means you permanently lose your goods — the state keeps them. This is an extremely drastic consequence, especially for a trader whose business depends on those goods.

Because confiscation is so harsh, Parliament built in Section 6B — a set of mandatory procedural safeguards that the confiscating authority must follow before passing any confiscation order. These safeguards are not optional niceties. They are statutory requirements, and the Court must insist on strict compliance.

Section 6B(1), Essential Commodities Act, 1955 — The Three Mandatory Steps
6B(1)(a) The owner or person from whom the goods are seized must be given a notice in writing informing them of the grounds on which confiscation is proposed.
6B(1)(b) The person must be given an opportunity of making a written presentation within a reasonable time specified in the notice, against the grounds of proposed confiscation.
6B(1)(c) The person must be given a reasonable opportunity of being heard in the matter — i.e., a personal oral hearing before the confiscating authority before any order is passed.

These three steps are cumulative and mandatory — not alternative. Each must be separately and genuinely complied with. The law does not permit the authority to skip step (c) on the grounds that steps (a) and (b) were already done. All three are required.

The Appellate Court's Fundamental Error

The Appellate Court dismissed Tejasbhai's appeal on the ground that since clauses (a) and (b) were complied with, "proper opportunity" was given. This reasoning represents a fundamental misreading of Section 6B(1). The three clauses are conjunctive — connected by "and" — not disjunctive. Two out of three is not good enough. As Justice Udhwani observed, the Appellate Court "fell into jurisdictional error by misreading Section 6B(1) of the Act." When a court misreads the plain text of a statutory provision, it commits an error of jurisdiction — not merely of fact or discretion — which is correctable by the High Court in revision.

6
The Latin Principle Behind It All — Audi Alteram Partem
Why the Right to Be Heard Is Sacred in Law

Section 6B(1)(c) is not a random legislative choice. It is the codification of one of the oldest and most fundamental principles in law — the Latin maxim "audi alteram partem", which means "hear the other side." This principle is a cornerstone of natural justice — the basic idea that a person should never be condemned without being given a genuine opportunity to defend themselves in person.

Natural Justice — The Two Core Rules

Rule 1 — Nemo judex in causa sua: No one should be a judge in their own cause. The decision-maker must be impartial and free from bias.

Rule 2 — Audi alteram partem: Hear the other side. Before any decision adversely affecting a person is made, that person must be given a fair opportunity to present their case — not just in writing, but in person if they so wish.

The Supreme Court has held in multiple cases — including Maneka Gandhi v. Union of India, (1978) 1 SCC 248 — that natural justice is embedded in Articles 14 and 21 of the Constitution. Any procedure that violates natural justice is not "procedure established by law" within the meaning of Article 21.

Why does a personal hearing matter when the trader has already submitted a written reply? Because writing and speaking are fundamentally different forms of advocacy. In a personal hearing, the trader can:

What a Personal Hearing Allows That a Written Reply Cannot

Respond immediately to the authority's questions or doubts about the written reply; clarify ambiguities in the documents produced; present additional oral explanations or context that a written submission may fail to capture; demonstrate the genuineness of the transaction through direct interaction; and gauge — and respond to — the authority's concerns in real time, rather than guessing what objections remain unanswered after a written reply.

A written reply and a personal hearing are complementary safeguards, not substitutes for each other. Parliament knew this when it drafted Section 6B(1) with three separate, sequential requirements.

7
What the High Court Said — The Judgment Explained
Justice G.R. Udhwani's Sharp, Concise Reasoning

The Gujarat High Court's judgment in this case is admirably concise — just seven paragraphs. But it packs a strong legal punch. Justice G.R. Udhwani identified the issue precisely and resolved it without ambiguity.

Confiscation of essential commodities is a harsh act, and therefore, with an intent to give full opportunity to the person concerned, appropriate provision in Section 6B(1) has been made. Therefore, the said provision needs to be complied with in its letter and spirit.

— Justice G.R. Udhwani, Gujarat High Court (Para 5)

The Court's reasoning proceeded in three clear steps:

First — it acknowledged the three-fold nature of the obligation under Section 6B(1): (A) show cause notice; (B) written presentation opportunity; and (C) oral hearing. These are three separate, cumulative obligations on the confiscating authority.

Second — it found that while (A) and (B) were complied with, (C) was not. The APP for the State could not point to anything in the impugned orders showing that a personal hearing was ever given. The silence of the orders on this point was itself telling.

Third — it held that the Appellate Court had also erred by treating compliance with (A) and (B) as sufficient. This was a misreading of the plain language of Section 6B(1), which uses "and" to connect all three clauses — meaning all three must be complied with. The error was not merely factual but jurisdictional — the Appellate Court applied the wrong legal standard.

The Court's Operative Direction

Both orders — the Collector's confiscation order and the Appellate Court's order affirming it — were quashed and set aside. The matter was remanded back to the Collector with a specific direction to pass a fresh order after giving Tejasbhai a proper opportunity of being heard as contemplated in Section 6B(1)(c). The Rule was made absolute. No order as to costs.

8
The Verdict in Full
Summary of the High Court's Final Order

  Operative Order — Gujarat High Court

Having examined the provisions of Section 6B(1) of the Essential Commodities Act, 1955, and finding that Clause (c) was not complied with by the confiscating authority, the Court held:

  • Section 6B(1) creates three cumulative, mandatory obligations on the confiscating authority — all three must be fulfilled before a confiscation order can be passed.
  • The Collector's confiscation order, passed without affording the petitioner a personal oral hearing, cannot be sustained and is quashed.
  • The Appellate Court's order affirming the confiscation is equally vitiated — it committed jurisdictional error by misreading Section 6B(1) as requiring only two out of three compliances.
  • Both orders are quashed and set aside.
  • The matter is remanded to the Collector to pass a fresh order after giving the petitioner a proper opportunity of being heard under Section 6B(1)(c).

Rule made absolute. No order as to costs.

Why This Judgment Matters — Critical Analysis
Four Observations on the Deeper Significance of This Case
1
"Letter and Spirit" — The Court Refuses a Mechanical Compliance Defence

The phrase "in its letter and spirit" in the judgment is significant. It signals that the Court is not interested in technical compliance that hollows out the protection the provision was designed to give. The Collector here might argue that he did comply "in spirit" — he read the written reply, examined the documents, and applied his mind. But the Court rejected this reasoning. Statutory procedural protections for citizens facing drastic government action must be followed strictly — not just substantially. This is especially true in quasi-judicial proceedings where someone's property rights are at stake.

2
Jurisdictional Error vs. Factual Error — A Critical Distinction

The High Court described the Appellate Court's mistake as a "jurisdictional error" — not merely an error of fact or discretion. This distinction matters enormously for revisional jurisdiction. A High Court in revision does not ordinarily re-examine facts already found by lower courts. But when a lower court applies the wrong legal standard — misreads a statutory provision — it steps outside its jurisdiction. That error is reviewable by the High Court as of right. By framing the Appellate Court's misreading of Section 6B(1) as a jurisdictional error, Justice Udhwani gave himself the cleanest possible ground to intervene and correct the mistake.

3
Remand — Not Acquittal — A Balanced Outcome

Notice that the High Court did not declare Tejasbhai innocent or direct the return of his goods. It quashed the procedurally defective orders and sent the matter back to the Collector for a fresh start — this time with proper process. This is the legally correct approach. The confiscation proceedings under the EC Act serve a legitimate public interest purpose; the problem here is not that the Collector lacked power or evidence, but that he skipped a mandatory step. Remand preserves the state's ability to proceed afresh on the merits — but this time, Tejasbhai gets to stand in front of the Collector and speak for himself. The outcome of the fresh proceedings remains open.

4
A Small Case, A Big Principle — Natural Justice in EC Act Enforcement

This is a short, seven-paragraph oral judgment — but it carries weight far beyond its length. Thousands of traders across India have their goods seized and confiscated under the EC Act and allied orders every year. In many of these cases, enforcement officers — under pressure to act quickly — skip the formal oral hearing, treating the written reply as sufficient. This judgment authoritatively says: that is not enough. The personal hearing under Section 6B(1)(c) is non-negotiable. Every confiscation order passed without a proper personal hearing is legally vulnerable — and this case is the authority to challenge it.

Legal Significance

Key Takeaways

📋
Three-Step Compliance
All three clauses of Section 6B(1) — written notice, written presentation opportunity, and personal hearing — are cumulative and mandatory. Compliance with two is not compliance with the law. Every confiscation order must reflect all three.
👂
Personal Hearing is Non-Negotiable
A written reply does not substitute for an oral hearing. Section 6B(1)(c) requires a separate, genuine opportunity to appear and be heard in person. Silence of the order on this point is damning evidence of non-compliance.
⚖️
Audi Alteram Partem — Codified
Section 6B(1)(c) is Parliament's specific codification of the natural justice principle "hear the other side." Courts will insist on its strict observance, especially where the consequence — confiscation — is described by the High Court itself as a "harsh act."
🔍
Jurisdictional Error Reviewable
A lower court or authority that misreads the plain text of a statutory provision commits a jurisdictional error — correctable by the High Court in revision. Practitioners should frame such arguments in revision petitions challenging EC Act orders.
🏛️
Confiscation is a Quasi-Judicial Act
The Collector exercising confiscation powers under Section 6A is not acting as an executive officer but as a quasi-judicial authority — bound by the rules of natural justice and the procedural safeguards of Section 6B(1). This distinction shapes the standard of review.
🔄
Remand Preserves State's Rights
Quashing on procedural grounds does not mean the trader wins on merits. The Collector can pass a fresh order after giving the required hearing — and may still confiscate if the facts warrant it. The judgment protects process, not outcome.
Conclusion

The Story's Lesson — Procedure Is Not a Technicality

Tejasbhai Thakrar's case is, on its surface, a small dispute about a trader's goods seized on a Gujarat highway. But the principle it establishes is large. When the government uses its power to take away a person's property — whether through confiscation, cancellation of a licence, or any other drastic action — the law demands that the person be genuinely heard before that decision is made. Not just on paper, not just through a written reply, but in person, with a real opportunity to speak and be listened to.

The Collector in this case perhaps believed he had done enough — he had issued a notice, received a reply, and applied his mind to the documents. But Section 6B(1)(c) asked for one more step: bring the person before you and hear them out. That step was not taken. And when a government authority skips a mandatory procedural safeguard, the resulting order — however justified on the facts — cannot stand in a court of law.

The Enduring Lesson

Natural justice is not a technicality that lawyers use to frustrate enforcement. It is a fundamental value — the idea that no person should lose their property, their liberty, or their livelihood without being genuinely heard. Section 6B(1) of the Essential Commodities Act is a parliamentary expression of that value. Courts will enforce it in letter and spirit — and officers who skip the personal hearing step do so at the cost of their orders being quashed.
