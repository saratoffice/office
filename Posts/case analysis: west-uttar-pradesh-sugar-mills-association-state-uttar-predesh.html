Can a State Government fix a sugarcane price higher than the minimum price set by the Central Government? Does that amount to a conflict — or repugnancy — under the Indian Constitution? This landmark five-judge Supreme Court bench answered both questions with a decisive yes and no respectively, settling a long-standing controversy between sugar mills and cane growers in Uttar Pradesh.

Background: The Dispute in Simple Terms

Sugarcane is the lifeline of millions of small farmers across Uttar Pradesh. Every year, they supply their cane to large sugar mills under government-supervised arrangements. To protect farmers from being underpaid by powerful mill owners, the U.P. Government has long fixed a State Advised Price — a price higher than the Central Government's minimum.

Sugar mills challenged this practice before the Supreme Court. Their main argument was that the Central Government already has the exclusive power to fix the price of sugarcane under the Essential Commodities Act. The State's separate price fixation, they said, was unconstitutional and conflicted with Central law.

Sugarcane farmers in U.P. — the SAP mechanism exists to protect small growers who lack bargaining power.

A Brief Legislative History: How We Got Here

To fully understand the judgment, it is important to trace the evolution of sugarcane price regulation in India — from the British era to independent India.

The 1932 Sugar Industry Act fostered sugarcane cultivation; the 1934 Sugarcane Act empowered provinces to fix minimum prices. Later, the Essential Commodities Act 1955 gave the Centre control. The 1966 Sugarcane (Control) Order deliberately omitted the word "price" and only retained the power to fix a "minimum price" — leaving scope for State-advised higher prices.

Key Legislative Milestones

1932: Sugar Industry Act — promoted cultivation. 1934: Sugarcane Act — empowered provinces to fix minimum prices. 1955: Essential Commodities Act — Central control. 1966: Sugarcane (Control) Order — omits "price", retains only "minimum price", leaving room for SAP.

The Supreme Court of India, New Delhi — a five-judge Constitution Bench resolved the reference on 22 April 2020.

The Alleged Conflict: Tika Ramji vs. U.P. Cane Unions Federations

A three-judge bench that originally heard the case felt there was a genuine conflict between two Constitution Bench decisions and referred it to a larger bench. Understanding the alleged conflict is the heart of this judgment.

Tika Ramji (1956) — Core Finding

The U.P. Act of 1953 contained no provision for price fixation. U.P. had never fixed a price, so there was no question of repugnancy with Central law.

U.P. Cane Unions Federations (2004)

After the 1966 Order, the Centre fixes only a "minimum price". The State can fix a higher "advised price". Both laws can be obeyed simultaneously — therefore no repugnancy arises.

Central Authority (SMP)

Sets a statutory minimum price — the absolute floor below which no payment can be made. Derived from the Essential Commodities Act 1955 and the 1966 Control Order.

State Authority (SAP)

Sets an advised price always above the SMP. Protects farmers who lack bargaining power. Found constitutionally valid by the 2020 bench.

How the 2020 Court Resolved the Conflict

The 2020 bench, authored by Justice M.R. Shah, concluded there was no real conflict between the two earlier Constitution Bench decisions. Different legal orders were under consideration in each case — the 1955 Order in Tika Ramji and the 1966 Order in the 2004 decision.

The deliberate deletion of the word "price" from the 1966 Order shows Parliament's clear intent to vacate the field above the minimum, expressly leaving room for State intervention. Repugnancy under Article 254 arises only when two laws are completely inconsistent — impossible to obey both simultaneously. A state SAP that is simply higher than the Central SMP creates no such inconsistency.

Article 254 — Repugnancy Test

Repugnancy arises only when (i) both Parliament and State legislate on a Concurrent List subject, and (ii) it is impossible to obey both simultaneously. Where a State law merely provides a higher standard, no repugnancy exists — both the SMP and SAP can be complied with by paying the SAP, which automatically satisfies the SMP.

Why the SAP Matters: Protecting the Weaker Party

The Court emphasised that sugarcane growers cannot negotiate on equal terms with large, organised sugar mills. State intervention is therefore not merely permissible — it is constitutionally necessary to give effect to the directive principles of state policy under Part IV of the Constitution.

U.P. fixes SAP based on multiple protective factors — cost of cultivation including input costs, transport and headload charges, reasonable return on capital, current market demand, and the need to prevent diversion of cane to gur/khandsari units. The SAP is therefore a guided, non-arbitrary exercise of State price-fixing power.

Cost of Cultivation

Includes seed, fertiliser, irrigation, labour and input costs, ensuring farmers recover production expenses.

Transport Charges

Headload and cartage from field to mill gate are factored into the SAP calculation to protect remote growers.

Reasonable Return

Farmers are entitled to a fair profit margin beyond mere cost recovery — recognised as a fundamental fairness principle.

Market Demand

Current demand conditions and competing uses (gur, khandsari) are assessed to prevent suppression of cane price.

Final Conclusions of the Court

The Constitution Bench held unanimously —

  1. Both Centre and State can fix sugarcane price under List III entries. However, the State can only fix an "advised price" higher than the Central minimum — not lower.
  2. Section 16 of the 1953 U.P. Act read with the 1966 Order empowers the State Government to fix a remunerative SAP above the Central SMP.
  3. No repugnancy exists between the U.P. Act and Central laws. The SAP merely fixes a price above the SMP; both laws can be complied with simultaneously.
  4. No conflict between Tika Ramji (1956) and the 2004 decision — they dealt with different factual and legislative contexts and are fully reconcilable.
  5. No reference to a seven-judge bench is warranted. The SAP mechanism remains constitutionally valid and enforceable.

The Five-Judge Constitution Bench

Justice Arun Mishra Justice Indira Banerjee Justice Vineet Saran Justice M. R. Shah Justice Aniruddha Bose

This judgment reaffirms India's cooperative federalism: the Central law sets the floor, and State law builds above it to protect farmers. The ruling protects millions of sugarcane growers in U.P. and validates SAP as a constitutional instrument of agricultural welfare, one that cannot be challenged simply because it exceeds the Central minimum.
