West U.P. Sugar Mills Association & Ors. vs. The State of Uttar Pradesh & Ors.

A plain-language analysis of the landmark five-judge Constitution Bench judgment on the validity of the State Advised Price (SAP) for sugarcane — Article 254 repugnancy examined and rejected

Sarat Rout June 1, 2025 12 min read Constitutional Law
The Supreme Court of India, New Delhi — a five-judge Constitution Bench resolved the reference on 22 April 2020, upholding the constitutional validity of Uttar Pradesh's State Advised Price (SAP) for sugarcane

Can a State Government fix a sugarcane purchase price higher than the minimum price already set by the Central Government? Does that constitute a constitutional conflict — a "repugnancy" — under Article 254 of the Constitution of India? A five-judge Constitution Bench of the Supreme Court, in its judgment dated 22 April 2020, answered both questions with a decisive yes and no respectively — settling a decades-long controversy between U.P. sugar mills and cane growers, and reaffirming cooperative federalism in the domain of agricultural price regulation.

Governing Legal Framework

Petroleum [read: Sugarcane] (Control) Order, 1966 — the Central delegated legislation under the Essential Commodities Act, 1955, which fixes only a minimum price (Statutory Minimum Price / SMP) and deliberately omits the power to fix a ceiling or exclusive price.

U.P. Sugarcane (Regulation of Supply and Purchase) Act, 1953 — the State law under which the U.P. Government fixes the State Advised Price (SAP) annually, always at a level higher than the Central SMP.

Essential Commodities Act, 1955 — Central legislation under List III (Concurrent List), under which the Sugarcane (Control) Order, 1966 is made.

Article 254, Constitution of India — the provision on repugnancy between Central and State laws on Concurrent List subjects; State law yields to Central law only to the extent of the inconsistency.

Background — The Dispute in Simple Terms

Sugarcane is the livelihood of millions of small and marginal farmers in Uttar Pradesh. Every season, they supply their cane to large, corporately owned sugar mills under government-supervised price arrangements. The State Advised Price (SAP) is the minimum price that any mill must pay a cane farmer — the State Government of U.P. fixes it annually, and it is consistently higher than the Central Government's Statutory Minimum Price (SMP).

Sugar mills challenged this practice before the Supreme Court. Their principal argument was that the Central Government, acting under the Essential Commodities Act, 1955 and the Sugarcane (Control) Order, 1966, had already occupied the field of sugarcane price fixation. The State's separate price — being higher — was, they contended, repugnant to Central law under Article 254 of the Constitution and therefore void.

Why This Matters

U.P. accounts for roughly half of India's total sugar production. The SAP mechanism directly governs the income of an estimated 45 lakh sugarcane farmers across the State. Its invalidation would have eliminated their statutory price protection, leaving them to negotiate individually against large industrial buyers.

2

Legislative History — How We Got Here

The evolution of sugarcane price regulation in India spans nearly a century and is key to understanding why the SAP does not conflict with Central law.

1932 — Sugar Industry Act (British Era)

The colonial government's first intervention to encourage sugarcane cultivation and regulate the relationship between mills and growers. Established the foundational framework of regulated supply and minimum pricing.

1934 — Sugarcane Act

Empowered provincial governments to fix minimum prices for sugarcane, recognising that provinces were better positioned to understand local market conditions and farmer needs.

1953 — U.P. Sugarcane (Regulation of Supply and Purchase) Act

Enacted by independent India's U.P. Legislature. Section 16 of this Act empowers the State Government to fix a remunerative price for sugarcane. This is the State law at the heart of the dispute.

1955 — Essential Commodities Act

Gave the Central Government sweeping power to regulate essential commodities — including sugarcane and sugar — under the Concurrent List. The Sugarcane (Control) Order was issued under this Act.

1966 — Sugarcane (Control) Order — The Critical Pivot

This Order replaced an earlier 1955 Order. Crucially, when drafting the 1966 Order, the Central Government deliberately deleted the word "price" from the power to regulate, retaining only the power to fix a "minimum price." The Court held that this deletion was Parliament's intentional act of vacating the field above the minimum — creating the constitutional space for States to fix an advised price higher than the Central floor.

The Significance of "Minimum Price" vs. "Price"

The 1966 Order empowers the Central Government to fix a minimum price only — it does not fix a maximum or a single exclusive price. A State SAP that is higher than the minimum neither contradicts nor makes the Central order impossible to comply with. Both obligations — pay not less than the SMP (Central) and pay not less than the SAP (State) — can be satisfied simultaneously by paying the SAP, which is always higher.

The Alleged Conflict: Tika Ramji vs. U.P. Cane Unions Federations

A three-judge bench that originally heard the matter felt there was an apparent conflict between two earlier Constitution Bench decisions and referred the matter to a larger (five-judge) bench. Understanding the alleged conflict is central to appreciating the 2020 judgment.

Tika Ramji & Ors. vs. State of U.P. (1956)

A five-judge bench examined the constitutional validity of the U.P. Sugarcane (Regulation of Supply and Purchase) Act, 1953. At that time, the U.P. Act contained no provision for price fixation and the State had never in practice fixed a price for sugarcane. The Court found no repugnancy — there was nothing to conflict with.

Key observation No price fixed → no conflict possible

U.P. Cooperative Cane Unions Federations vs. West U.P. Sugar Mills Assn. (2004)

A two-judge bench held that after the 1966 Order came into force, the Central Government fixed only a "minimum price" — leaving the State free to advise a higher price. Both laws could be obeyed simultaneously. Therefore, the U.P. SAP was not repugnant to the Central order.

Key observation SMP is floor, SAP is higher → no conflict

Why the Referral? The Perceived Tension

The three-judge referring bench thought that Tika Ramji (1956) had held repugnancy was not possible in principle in this subject area — which would conflict with the 2004 bench's nuanced analysis based on the 1966 Order. The 2020 bench cleared this up: Tika Ramji was decided under a factual scenario (no price fixation by U.P. at all) that was entirely different from the scenario after the 1966 Order was promulgated.

4

How the 2020 Court Resolved the Conflict

Justice M.R. Shah, authoring the unanimous judgment for all five judges, concluded there was no genuine conflict between the two earlier decisions. They were examining different regulatory contexts — the 1955 Order (in Tika Ramji) versus the 1966 Order (in 2004 and in the present case). Different legal instruments lead to different analyses.

Repugnancy Requires Impossible Simultaneous Compliance

Under Article 254, repugnancy arises only when two laws are so irreconcilably contradictory that it is impossible to comply with both at the same time. A sugarcane mill that pays the SAP (which is higher than the SMP) is simultaneously complying with the Central minimum and exceeding it — there is nothing impossible about this.

Parliament Deliberately Left the Field Above the Minimum Open

By using the phrase "minimum price" and deleting "price" in the 1966 Order, Parliament intentionally declined to fix a single, exclusive price for sugarcane. That deliberate choice cannot be overridden by reading the Order as impliedly occupying the entire field. The State's power to advise a higher price is constitutionally preserved.

Cooperative Federalism — Centre Sets Floor, State Builds Above It

The constitutional architecture of List III (Concurrent List) is not one of Central supremacy but of coordination. The 1966 Order and Section 16 of the U.P. Act operate in a complementary manner — neither displaces the other. This reflects cooperative federalism in action.

The Test for Repugnancy — Applied

The Court applied the settled three-part test: (a) both laws cover the same subject matter; (b) they are so contradictory that compliance with one entails violation of the other; or (c) Parliament intended to "cover the field" exclusively. On all three — particularly (b) and (c) — the answer was negative. Paying the SAP satisfies the SMP requirement; and Parliament in 1966 did not intend exclusivity.

5

SAP — Factors and Farmer Protection

The Court emphasised that the SAP is not an arbitrary figure — it is a guided, reasoned price fixed after consideration of multiple statutory factors. This further reinforces its constitutional validity.

Cost of Cultivation

SAP is fixed with reference to the actual cost of producing sugarcane — inputs, labour, irrigation — ensuring farmers at least recover their production costs.

Transportation Cost

The cost of transporting cut cane from field to mill — which deteriorates rapidly after harvest — is factored into the SAP calculation.

Reasonable Return

Farmers are entitled to a reasonable profit on their labour and capital beyond mere cost recovery — the SAP is set to ensure viability of cane cultivation.

Diversion to Gur / Khandsari

If mill prices are too low, farmers divert cane to unregulated gur/khandsari producers. SAP prevents this diversion by making mill sales economically attractive, ensuring regulated supply chains are maintained.

Unequal Bargaining — Why State Intervention is Necessary

Individual cane growers — mostly small and marginal farmers — cannot negotiate on equal terms with large, corporately financed sugar mills. The SAP mechanism substitutes State-regulated price discovery for individual bargaining, correcting a structural power imbalance. The Court affirmed this as constitutionally necessary, not merely desirable.

6

Final Conclusions and Significance

The five-judge Constitution Bench delivered a unanimous verdict, reversing the referral and definitively settling the constitutional status of the U.P. SAP.

The Constitution Bench Held Unanimously —

  • Both Centre and State can regulate sugarcane prices under List III entries. The State may fix a "State Advised Price" higher than the Central statutory minimum price — this is constitutionally permissible.
  • Section 16 of the U.P. Act, 1953, read with the Sugarcane (Control) Order, 1966, empowers the State Government to fix a remunerative SAP. The power has a clear statutory basis.
  • No repugnancy exists between the U.P. Act and the Central laws. Complying with the SAP also satisfies the SMP — both obligations are met simultaneously.
  • No conflict between Tika Ramji (1956) and the 2004 decision — they dealt with different factual and legal contexts (the 1955 Order vs. the 1966 Order), and there is no irreconcilable difference between them.
  • No reference to a seven-judge bench is warranted. The SAP mechanism is constitutionally valid and the matter is definitively closed.

Significance of the Judgment

This ruling reaffirms India's model of cooperative federalism: Central law sets the regulatory floor; State law builds above it in the interest of local stakeholders. The judgment protects the income of millions of sugarcane farmers in U.P., validates the constitutional instrument of SAP, and clarifies that "minimum price" legislation does not impliedly pre-empt State price protection for agricultural producers.

⚖️ Verdict — SAP is Constitutional, SAP is Settled

The West U.P. Sugar Mills judgment is a foundational ruling at the intersection of constitutional law, agricultural policy, and cooperative federalism. It affirms that the Central SMP and the State SAP are complementary instruments — not conflicting ones — and that State governments retain the constitutional power to protect their farming communities above and beyond the Central floor price.

For practitioners, policy makers, and industry stakeholders working in regulated agricultural commodities, this judgment is essential reading on the doctrine of repugnancy under Article 254 in a concurrent-list context.
