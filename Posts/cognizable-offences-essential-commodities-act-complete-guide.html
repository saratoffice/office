1. Cognizable Offences: Definition & Key Features

A cognizable offence is one in which a police officer is empowered to arrest the accused without a warrant, investigate without the prior permission of a Magistrate, and register a First Information Report (FIR) on its own motion — as provided under the Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973 (CrPC) and now continued under the Bharatiya Nagarik Suraksha Sanhita, 2023 (BNSS).

Key Features

Arrest Without Warrant

The police may arrest without prior magistrate sanction where the offence is grave enough to demand immediate action for public safety.

Mandatory FIR Registration

Under Section 154 CrPC (Section 173 BNSS), the officer in charge of a police station must register an FIR on receipt of information about a cognizable offence — the Supreme Court affirmed this in Lalita Kumari v. Govt. of U.P. (2014).

Investigation Without Permission

Under Section 156 CrPC (Section 175 BNSS), police may investigate a cognizable offence without a Magistrate's order, enabling swift evidence collection.

Statutory Definition (CrPC § 2(c))

Section 2(c) CrPC defines a cognizable offence as one "for which, and cognizable case as one in which, a police officer may, in accordance with the First Schedule or under any other law for the time being in force, arrest without warrant."

2. Contrast with Non-Cognizable Offences

Cognizable Offences

  • Police can arrest without warrant
  • Investigation may begin without Magistrate's order
  • FIR must be registered immediately
  • Serious crimes: murder, rape, robbery, hoarding
  • Bail generally not a matter of right

Non-Cognizable Offences

  • Police cannot arrest without warrant
  • Magistrate's permission required to investigate
  • Complaint filed; police record "NCR" only
  • Minor offences: defamation, simple hurt, cheating below threshold
  • Bail generally available as of right

3. Offences Under the Essential Commodities Act, 1955

The Essential Commodities Act, 1955 (ECA) was enacted to ensure equitable distribution and availability at fair prices of goods declared "essential" by the Central Government. It regulates the production, supply, and distribution of such commodities, and provides stringent penal provisions against practices that distort supply chains.

ECA § 7 r/w Schedule — First Schedule CrPC

Cognizability of ECA Offences

Offences under the ECA are classified as cognizable and non-bailable by virtue of Section 7 read with the First Schedule to the CrPC (now Schedule II of the BNSS). This classification empowers law enforcement to act decisively — arresting offenders and seizing contraband without waiting for a Magistrate's authorisation, thereby curbing practices that harm the public.

Common Offences Covered

Hoarding

Stockpiling essential goods in excess of permitted limits to create artificial scarcity and drive up prices — violating Control Orders issued under Section 3.

Black Marketing

Selling essential commodities at prices above the maximum price fixed, or through unlicensed channels, in contravention of Section 3 orders.

Adulteration

Mixing inferior or harmful substances into essential goods (e.g., food grains, edible oil) to defraud consumers — also covered by the Food Safety and Standards Act, 2006.

Other violations include: dealing without a valid licence, contravening price-fixation orders, refusing to sell at controlled prices, and obstructing authorised inspectors during inspection or search.

4. Enforcement & Police Powers

Powers Conferred on Enforcement Agencies

Because ECA offences are cognizable, police officers — and authorised officers under Section 10 of the Act — possess the following powers:

Arrest Without Warrant

Any police officer, or an officer empowered under the Act, may arrest the accused person without obtaining a warrant from a Magistrate. This enables immediate prevention of activities that could worsen commodity scarcity.

Search and Seizure

Under Section 10A of the ECA, authorised officers may enter and search any premises, vehicle, or vessel, and seize essential commodities stored or transported in contravention of an order made under Section 3.

Registration of FIR

The police are obligated to register an FIR immediately upon receiving credible information about an ECA violation. The Lalita Kumari (2014) Supreme Court ruling makes this non-discretionary for cognizable offences.

5. Section 7 – Penalties for Violations

Section 7, Essential Commodities Act, 1955 — Any person who contravenes an order made under Section 3 shall be punishable with imprisonment for a term which may extend to seven years, or with fine, or with both, and the court may also direct forfeiture of the essential commodity involved.

Offence Maximum Sentence Nature
Hoarding / Stock Limit Violations Up to 7 years imprisonment + fine + forfeiture Cognizable
Black Marketing / Price Violation Up to 7 years imprisonment + fine + forfeiture Cognizable
Adulteration of Essential Commodities Up to 7 years imprisonment + fine Cognizable
Dealing Without Licence Up to 7 years imprisonment + fine Cognizable
Obstruction of Inspector Up to 7 years imprisonment + fine Cognizable

The Essential Commodities (Amendment) Act, 2020 removed several commodities from the "essential" list (cereals, pulses, oilseeds, edible oils, onion, potato) except during extraordinary circumstances such as war, calamity, or extraordinary price rise — limiting the routine application of stock limits to those commodities.

7. Conclusion

Key Takeaways

The Essential Commodities Act, 1955 classifies its principal offences as cognizable and non-bailable, granting enforcement agencies the authority to act swiftly against hoarding, black-marketing, adulteration, and unlicensed dealings. Police officers and authorised inspectors may arrest without a warrant, register an FIR immediately, and seize contraband goods — all without prior Magistrate sanction.

The penal framework under Section 7 prescribes imprisonment up to seven years, fines, and forfeiture of the offending commodity. Together with the procedural machinery of the CrPC (now BNSS), this makes the ECA a powerful legislative instrument for protecting consumers and maintaining market equilibrium during supply disruptions.

Note: The Bharatiya Nagarik Suraksha Sanhita, 2023 (BNSS) replaced the CrPC with effect from 1 July 2024. References to CrPC sections remain valid for offences that arose before that date; the BNSS equivalents apply going forward.
