⚖️ Order

pg. 1 This Writ Petition is filed questioning the seizure of 160 bags of raw rice along with lorry bearing registration No.AP 03 TB 2444 under seizure panchanama dated 11.02.2022, the order of the second respondent dated 09.03.2022 directing the third respondent to dispose of the seized stock in public auction and the direction to the second petitioner to deposit an amount of Rs.2,13,750/- for release of the seized lorry as arbitrary and illegal.

📋 Case of the Petitioners

pg. 1 The case of the petitioners 1 and 2 is that they are the owners of the seized rice and lorry respectively; first petitioner is dealer in food grains and he purchased paddy and converted it into rice; while he was transporting the same with e-way bills through the subject lorry the same was seized along with lorry on 11.02.2022 alleging that the subject rice is PDS rice; petitioners filed a petition on 14.02.2022 seeking release of the seized rice along with lorry by enclosing all the necessary documents; without taking the same into consideration, the second respondent passed an order on 09.03.2022 ordering confiscation of the seized stock to the Government and directing the Tahsildar to dispose of the seized stock through public auction; he also issued an endorsement on the same day directing the second petitioner to deposit an amount of Rs.2,13,750/- in favour of the Collector (CS) for release of the seized vehicle; questioning the said two orders, the present Writ Petition is filed.

pg. 1-2 Heard: Learned counsel for the petitioners and learned Government Pleader for Civil Supplies.

🔍 Critical Finding – Violation of Section 6B

pg. 2 As seen from the proceedings of the Collector which was signed on 09.03.2022, the seized stock is ordered to be confiscated to the Government. Learned counsel for the petitioners contends that no notice under Section 6B of the Essential Commodities Act was issued to the first petitioner before passing the orders confiscating the seized stock. The said proceedings also do not show that any such notice was issued to the first petitioner.

When the matter was listed on 16.03.2022, learned Government Pleader was directed to get instructions with regard to notice that is issued to the petitioner. But, today, when the matter came up for hearing, learned Government Pleader submits that no notice was issued to the first petitioner before passing the confiscation orders.

Section 6B – Essential Commodities Act, 1955

"(1) No order confiscating any essential commodity, package, covering or receptacle, animal, vehicle, vessel or other conveyance shall be made under section 6A unless the owner of such essential commodity, package, covering, receptacle, animal, vehicle, vessel or other conveyance or the person from whom it is seized—

(a) is given a notice in writing informing him of the grounds on which it is proposed to confiscate;

(b) is given an opportunity of making a presentation in writing within such reasonable time as may be specified in the notice against the grounds of confiscation; and

(c) is given a reasonable opportunity of being heard in the matter."

pg. 2 Principles of Natural Justice: Section 6B incorporates the principles of natural justice – notice and opportunity of hearing are mandatory before any confiscation order can be passed. The Collector cannot order confiscation without first issuing a show-cause notice to the owner.

⚖️ Court's Order on Confiscation of Rice Stock

pg. 2 In view of the facts and circumstances, as the notice under Section 6B of the Essential Commodities Act was not issued to the first petitioner, the proceeding which was signed on 09.03.2022 confiscating the stock to the Government is set aside.

As the learned counsel for the petitioners submits that an application has been made by the first petitioner seeking release of the seized stock, the respondents are directed to pass appropriate orders on the application filed by the first petitioner seeking release of the seized stock as expeditiously as possible, preferably within a period of ten (10) days from the date of receipt of a copy of this order.

Till orders are passed on the application made by the first petitioner seeking release of the seized stock, the respondents are directed not to sell the seized stock.

However, this order shall not prevent the respondents from issuing notice to the first petitioner and passing appropriate orders in 6A proceedings.

🚛 Vehicle Release Order

pg. 2-3 Learned counsel for the petitioners submits that the District Supply Officer also issued an endorsement on 09.03.2022 directing the second petitioner to deposit an amount of Rs.2,13,750/- in favour of the Collector (CS) for release of the seized lorry and the second petitioner is unable to furnish fixed deposit for such amount.

pg. 3 In view of the facts and circumstances of the case, the respondents are directed to get the subject vehicle valued by the concerned Motor Vehicle Inspector in the presence of the second petitioner after serving notice on her and the second petitioner is directed to furnish the third party immovable property security by executing a bond in favour of the second respondent strictly adhering to the Stamps and Registration Laws, as per the value fixed by the Motor Vehicle Inspector and on production of such third party immovable property as security, the second respondent is directed to release the subject vehicle bearing registration No.AP 03 TB 2444 as interim custody to the second petitioner in accordance with law.

It is also made clear that the second petitioner shall not alienate or change the nature of the vehicle during the pendency of the enquiry.

📋 Final Judgment

Writ Petition DISPOSED OF

pg. 3 1. Confiscation of Rice Stock: The proceeding dated 09.03.2022 confiscating the stock to the Government is set aside due to violation of Section 6B (no notice issued).

2. Release Application: Respondents directed to pass orders on petitioner's application for release of seized stock within 10 days.

3. Interim Protection: Respondents directed not to sell the seized stock till orders are passed on release application.

4. Vehicle Release: Vehicle to be valued by Motor Vehicle Inspector; petitioner to furnish third party immovable property security as per value fixed; vehicle to be released as interim custody.

5. Restriction: Second petitioner shall not alienate or change the nature of the vehicle during pendency of enquiry.

6. Liberty: This order does not prevent respondents from issuing notice and passing appropriate orders in 6A proceedings.

No order as to costs.

As a sequel thereto, the miscellaneous petitions, if any, pending in this Writ Petition shall stand closed.

📌 Key Legal Principles Established

Principle Holding
Notice under Section 6B is Mandatory No confiscation order can be passed without issuing notice under Section 6B. Violation renders confiscation order void.
Principles of Natural Justice Owner must be given opportunity of hearing before confiscation. Government's admission of no notice fatal to proceedings.
Interim Release of Vehicle Vehicle can be released on third party immovable property security after valuation by Motor Vehicle Inspector.
Protection of Seized Stock Court can direct respondents not to sell seized stock pending decision on release application.
Fresh Proceedings Permitted Setting aside confiscation order does not prevent authorities from issuing proper notice and initiating fresh proceedings under Section 6A.

📚 Consistency with Earlier Judgments

This judgment is consistent with the principles laid down in earlier cases:

  • Dommaraju Vinay v. State of A.P. – Andhra Pradesh High Court set aside confiscation order where no notice under Section 6B was issued.
  • Rabi Kumar Nanda v. State of Orissa (CRLREV No. 38 of 2024) – Orissa High Court held that confiscation proceedings are initiated only when notice under Section 6B is issued.
  • Shambhu Dayal Agarwala v. State of West Bengal (1990) 3 SCC 549 – Supreme Court examined the scheme of Sections 6A-6E and emphasized procedural safeguards.

The common thread in all these judgments is that adherence to Section 6B is a sine qua non (essential condition) for lawful confiscation under the Essential Commodities Act.

📝 Summary & Takeaways

  • Notice under Section 6B is mandatory before any confiscation order can be passed under the Essential Commodities Act. The Collector cannot order confiscation without first issuing a show-cause notice to the owner.
  • Admission of no notice by Government – When the Government Pleader admitted that no notice was issued, the confiscation order became indefensible.
  • Confiscation order set aside – The proceeding dated 09.03.2022 confiscating 160 bags of rice was quashed.
  • Vehicle release on security – Instead of demanding Rs.2,13,750/- deposit, the Court directed release on third party immovable property security after valuation by Motor Vehicle Inspector.
  • Expeditious disposal – Respondents directed to decide release application within 10 days, with interim direction not to sell seized stock.
  • Fresh proceedings permitted – Authorities can initiate fresh proceedings by issuing proper notice under Section 6B.

This judgment reinforces the fundamental principle that confiscation proceedings must follow due process – notice and opportunity of hearing are not mere formalities but essential prerequisites for any action that deprives a person of their property. The admission by the Government that no notice was issued made the confiscation order liable to be set aside.
