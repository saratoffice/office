Systematic and Detailed Overview: Crime, Mens Rea, Actus Reus, and Legal Doctrines

A comprehensive analysis under the Bharatiya Nyaya Sanhita, 2023 (BNS) and the Indian Penal Code, 1860 — with landmark case laws

Sarat Rout February 17, 2025 14 min read Criminal Law
Understanding the fundamentals of criminal liability under BNS, 2023 and IPC, 1860

Criminal law rests on a deceptively simple foundation: no one should be punished for an act unless they possessed both a guilty act and a guilty mind. This foundational principle — encoded in the Latin maxim actus non facit reum nisi mens sit rea — continues to govern criminal liability in India under the Bharatiya Nyaya Sanhita, 2023 (BNS), which replaced the Indian Penal Code, 1860 (IPC) with effect from 1 July 2024. The BNS preserves the classical doctrines while adapting them to contemporary realities — adding new offences for cybercrime, organised crime, and terrorism. This article provides a rigorous, updated analysis of these foundational principles with landmark Supreme Court judgments.

1. Definition of Crime — IPC and BNS

What is a Crime?

A crime is a public wrong — an act or omission forbidden by law under pain of punishment, committed against the State and society as a whole, warranting prosecution by the State rather than private individuals. Lord Macaulay, who drafted the IPC, conceived crime as "an act punishable by law" — a formulation that emphasises the State's role in defining and prosecuting criminal conduct.

The Bharatiya Nyaya Sanhita, 2023 (BNS) — enacted on 25 December 2023 and in force from 1 July 2024 — is India's new criminal code. Section 1(3) of BNS provides that every person shall be liable to punishment for every act or omission contrary to its provisions. The BNS comprises 358 sections and 20 chapters, adding new categories of offences while retaining the classical structure of criminal liability.

Animals and Corporate Entities as "Persons"

Animals cannot form mens rea and are therefore incapable of committing crimes. Humans who commit crimes against animals (e.g., under the Prevention of Cruelty to Animals Act, 1960) are themselves punishable.

Importantly, Section 2(26) of BNS defines "person" to include a company, an association, or a body of persons — whether incorporated or not. This recognises both natural and artificial persons as capable of criminal liability, relevant for corporate offences and vicarious liability under regulatory statutes.

Statutory References: BNS, 2023 — Section 1(3), Section 2(26) | IPC, 1860 — Section 40 | Prevention of Cruelty to Animals Act, 1960

2. Essential Elements of a Crime

For criminal liability to arise, four elements must coexist:

  1. Human Being: The act must be committed by a human being capable of understanding legal obligations. BNS extends this to companies and associations for certain offences.
  2. Mens Rea (Guilty Mind): The mental element — intention, knowledge, recklessness, or criminal negligence — that renders the act blameworthy.
  3. Actus Reus (Guilty Act): The external, physical component — a voluntary act, omission, or the bringing about of a prohibited consequence.
  4. Injury: Under Section 44 IPC (mirrored in BNS), "injury" means any harm caused to a person illegally — in mind, body, reputation, or property. However, some offences (e.g., driving without a licence) are complete without actual injury to anyone.

3. Mens Rea — The Guilty Mind

Actus non facit reum nisi mens sit rea "The act does not make a person guilty unless the mind is also guilty" — Lord Kenyon in Fowler v. Padget (1798)

Mens Rea — "Guilty Mind"

Definition: The mental element of a crime — the blameworthy state of mind that accompanies the prohibited act. It is the moral ingredient that justifies criminal punishment.

The BNS does not use the phrase "mens rea" explicitly, but incorporates the concept through qualifying words such as intentionally, voluntarily, dishonestly, fraudulently, knowingly, rashly, negligently, maliciously, corruptly — spread throughout its provisions.

Supreme Court: In Nathulal v. State of Madhya Pradesh (AIR 1966 SC 43), the Court held that mens rea is an essential ingredient of a criminal offence unless the statute expressly or by necessary implication excludes it.

Actus Reus — "Guilty Act"

Definition: The external, physical component of a crime — the prohibited act, omission, or consequence. The term was coined by Professor Kenny. It is the manifestation of mens rea in the external world.

Under Sections 32–33 of IPC (mirrored in BNS), "act" includes both a single act and a series of acts; "omission" covers illegal omissions. An act must be voluntary — the result of a deliberate exercise of will power.

Key principle: Actus reus is not limited to positive acts — omissions can constitute actus reus when there is a legal duty to act (e.g., a parent who fails to feed a child, or a doctor who withholds emergency care).

4. Degrees of Mens Rea

Mens rea exists in varying degrees — from the highest (intention) to the lowest (negligence). The degree of culpability affects both the classification of the offence and the severity of punishment:

1st — Highest
Intention

The deliberate purpose or design to bring about a specific result. Always accompanied by knowledge. Highest degree of culpability.

2nd
Knowledge

Awareness that a particular result is substantially certain to follow from one's act. Lower culpability than intention, but still serious.

3rd
Recklessness

Conscious disregard of a known, obvious risk. The actor is aware of the risk but proceeds regardless.

4th — Lowest
Criminal Negligence

Failure to take precautions that a reasonable person would take. Must be gross negligence for criminal liability — not mere error of judgment.

Motive vs. Intention: Legal scholar Austin distinguished these as follows: motive is the "spring of action" (the underlying reason), while intention is the "aim of the act." Motive is not an element of mens rea — a person may be convicted even if their motive was good (e.g., stealing food to feed starving children). However, motive can be relevant in sentencing and in cases relying on circumstantial evidence.

5. Actus Reus — The Guilty Act in Detail

Actus reus takes several forms in Indian criminal law, going well beyond simple positive conduct:

Forms of Actus Reus

  • Positive Acts: A direct physical action constituting the offence — assault, theft, forgery. The most straightforward form.
  • Omissions: A failure to act when the law imposes a legal duty. BNS (Section 40) imposes a duty to assist a person in danger when one can do so without risk. A parent who deliberately withholds food from a child commits an omission-based offence. (Om Prakash v. State of Punjab — husband convicted for attempting to kill wife by starving her.)
  • Consequences: In some offences, the actus reus is a prohibited result — e.g., death in homicide. In others (treason, forgery, perjury, inchoate offences), no actual harm need result.
  • Circumstances: Certain offences require specific circumstances — e.g., criminal trespass requires the act to occur at a specific place (Sections 329–334 BNS, formerly Sections 441–462 IPC).
  • Creation of Dangerous Situation: If someone creates a dangerous situation, they acquire a duty to act to prevent resulting harm. (R v. Miller — accused who started a fire had a duty to take steps to prevent its spread.)
Statutory Basis: IPC Sections 32–33 (mirrored in BNS) — "act" includes single and series of acts; "omission" includes illegal omissions.

6. Concurrence of Mens Rea and Actus Reus

It is not enough that both elements exist independently — they must coincide in time. A person who decides to kill and later accidentally causes a death through unrelated conduct is not guilty of murder for that act. Conversely, a person who accidentally causes harm and then decides to let it worsen acquires liability from the point of that decision onward.

"The act itself does not make a man guilty unless his intention was also guilty. Guilty intention and guilty act together constitute a crime — thinking of committing a crime alone is never enough." — Drishti Judiciary (citing foundational criminal law principle)

7. Strict Liability and Absolute Liability

Strict Liability — No Mens Rea Required

In strict liability offences, the prosecution need not prove mens rea — the mere commission of the prohibited act is sufficient for conviction. This exception applies primarily to regulatory offences where public safety, health, and welfare are paramount.

Rationale: Mens rea under IPC/BNS is not an absolute rule — it is a strong presumption that can be rebutted by the express words of a statute or by necessary implication from its legislative intent. The Supreme Court in Ravule Hariprasada Rao v. State (1951) confirmed this: unless a statute clearly rules out mens rea, guilt requires a guilty mind.

IPC exceptions — five offences require no mens rea: waging war (Section 121 IPC), sedition (Section 124A IPC — now modified in BNS), kidnapping and abduction (Sections 359, 363 IPC), and counterfeiting coins (Section 232 IPC).

Key Cases: Gammon (Hong Kong) Ltd. v. Attorney-General [1985] AC 1 | State of Maharashtra v. Mayer Hans George (1965) — gold smuggling conviction upheld without mens rea | Nathulal v. State of M.P. (AIR 1966 SC 43) — EC Act conviction set aside as no mens rea proved

Absolute Liability — No Exceptions Whatsoever

Going further than strict liability, the Supreme Court in M.C. Mehta v. Union of India (1987) — arising from the Oleum Gas Leak case — introduced the doctrine of absolute liability for enterprises engaged in inherently hazardous activities. Under absolute liability:

  • There are no defences available — not even due diligence, force majeure, or act of God
  • The enterprise is strictly liable for all harm resulting from the hazardous activity
  • This applies primarily to industrial and environmental disasters — Bhopal Gas tragedy aftermath, chemical plant leaks, toxic waste disposal
  • Unlike strict liability (which follows English law), absolute liability is India's own indigenous development — stricter and more victim-protective
Foundational Case: M.C. Mehta v. Union of India, AIR 1987 SC 1086 — Oleum Gas Leak, Delhi. Justice P.N. Bhagwati held that enterprises dealing with hazardous substances bear an absolute non-delegable duty to the community.

8. Burden of Proof — Beyond Reasonable Doubt

Golden Thread of Criminal Law

The prosecution bears the entire burden of establishing the accused's guilt beyond a reasonable doubt. The accused begins with a presumption of innocence — a constitutional guarantee under Article 20 of the Constitution — and this presumption does not shift merely because the prosecution has made out a prima facie case.

The defence only needs to raise a reasonable doubt in the mind of the court to secure an acquittal. It does not need to prove innocence beyond reasonable doubt.

Foundational Case: Woolmington v. Director of Public Prosecutions [1935] AC 462 — Viscount Sankey called the presumption of innocence the "golden thread" running through the web of English criminal law. Indian courts have consistently adopted and applied this principle.

9. Ignorance Defences — Mistake of Fact vs. Mistake of Law

DoctrineLatin MaximMeaningEffectExample
Ignorance of Fact Ignorantia Facti Excusat Ignorance or mistake of fact is a valid defence — negates mens rea May excuse criminal liability where the mistake is honest and reasonable A buys goods unaware they are stolen — no dishonest intent, no theft
Ignorance of Law Ignorantia Juris Non Excusat Ignorance of law is no excuse — everyone is presumed to know the law Cannot be used as a defence to criminal charges Selling a newly prohibited substance while unaware of the ban — still punishable
Important Limitation: The ignorance of fact defence is not available in strict liability offences, where the legislature has intentionally excluded mens rea. In R v. Prince (1875), the accused took a girl below 16 believing she was 18 — the court held the mistake of fact defence unavailable because taking a girl from parental custody was independently unlawful regardless of age. Indian courts have applied the same reasoning in kidnapping cases.

10. Stages of a Crime

Criminal law recognises four progressive stages in the commission of an offence. Liability varies across these stages:

  1. Intention (Mental Stage): The first stage — purely internal. Mere intention to commit an offence is not punishable (the law cannot read minds and proof is impossible). No offence arises at this stage alone.
  2. Preparation: Taking steps to equip oneself for the commission of a crime (acquiring tools, surveilling the target). Generally not punishable except in specific cases — preparing to wage war (Section 121A IPC / BNS), making counterfeit currency plates, etc.
  3. Attempt: A direct, proximate act toward the commission of the offence that goes beyond mere preparation. Section 109 BNS (formerly Section 307 IPC) — attempt to commit murder. The Supreme Court in Abhayanand Mishra v. State of Bihar held that an attempt is any act done after preparation that moves directly toward the offence.
  4. Accomplishment (Commission): The complete commission of the offence — actus reus and mens rea both fully realised. Full criminal liability attaches.

11. Special Categories — Negligence, Theft, and Cybercrime

A. Criminal Negligence

Criminal negligence arises when someone fails to act with the standard of care expected of a reasonable person, causing harm. Crucially, the negligence must be gross — far beyond a mere error of judgment. Section 106 BNS (formerly Section 304A IPC) criminalises causing death by a negligent act.

Key Cases: Jacob Mathew v. State of Punjab (2005) 6 SCC 1 — Supreme Court: doctors can only be held criminally liable for negligence that is gross or of a very high degree, not mere error of clinical judgment. | R v. Adomako [1995] 1 AC 171 — anaesthetist failed to notice a disconnected oxygen tube; convicted of gross negligence manslaughter. | Prabhakaran v. State of Kerala (2007) — SC clarified the high threshold for criminal negligence under Indian law.

B. Theft

Theft under Section 302 BNS (formerly Section 378 IPC) requires the dishonest intention to take moveable property out of the possession of any person without their consent. The mens rea element is explicit: dishonest intention to permanently deprive the owner.

In Malhan K.A. v. Kora Bibi Kutti (1996 SCC 281), the Supreme Court held that a financier who seized a vehicle from a defaulting borrower could not be convicted of theft because the element of mens rea (dishonest intent) was totally wanting — he had a bona fide claim of right over the vehicle.

References: BNS Section 302 (Theft) | IPC Section 378 | Malhan K.A. v. Kora Bibi Kutti (1996)

C. Cybercrime — New Provisions in BNS

The BNS, 2023 introduces dedicated provisions for cybercrime — going beyond the Information Technology Act, 2000. Offences include cyber terrorism, identity theft, hacking, and digital impersonation. Section 2(39) of BNS incorporates definitions from the IT Act, 2000 by reference.

Due to the deliberate, technical nature of cyber offences, mens rea (criminal intent) is typically present — accidental cybercrime is rare. In Prabhat Kumar Singh v. State of Bihar (2021), the Supreme Court discussed the application of mens rea in cybercrime cases, emphasising that criminal intent remains crucial even in digital offences.

References: BNS, 2023 — Sections on Cyber Terrorism and Electronic Offences | Information Technology Act, 2000 | Prabhat Kumar Singh v. State of Bihar (2021)

12. Treatment of Minors under BNS and Juvenile Justice Act

Under 7 Years

Absolute Immunity — Doli Incapax

Section 20 BNS (formerly Section 82 IPC): "Nothing is an offence which is done by a child under seven years of age."

Derived from the common law maxim doli incapax — incapable of evil. A child below seven is conclusively presumed incapable of forming mens rea.

No FIR can be registered; no arrest can be made. Police are legally bound not to take any criminal action against a child under seven.

Note: BNS Section 20 retains Section 82 IPC without substantive change.

7 to 18 Years

Juvenile Justice Act, 2015

Children aged 7–18 are governed by the Juvenile Justice (Care and Protection of Children) Act, 2015. The emphasis is on rehabilitation, education, skill development, and reformation — not punishment.

Under Section 21 BNS (formerly Section 83 IPC): a child between 7 and 12 years is liable only if they have sufficient maturity of understanding to judge the nature and consequences of the act.

For heinous offences by children aged 16–18: The JJB may, after assessment, direct trial as an adult — maximum confinement 3 years in a special home (Section 15, JJ Act, 2015).

13. Key Judicial Precedents

Nathulal v. State of Madhya Pradesh
Supreme Court of India — AIR 1966 SC 43

Held that mens rea is an essential ingredient of every criminal offence unless the statute expressly or by necessary implication excludes it. A food grain dealer who stored grain without licence after his application was neither granted nor rejected was acquitted — no mens rea established.

Ravule Hariprasada Rao v. State
Supreme Court of India — 1951

Ruled that a person should not be held guilty of an offence unless they had a guilty mind at the time of commission of the act, unless the statute clearly or by necessary implication rules out mens rea.

State of Maharashtra v. Mayer Hans George
Supreme Court of India — 1965

A German smuggler brought gold into India unaware of an RBI notification prohibiting it. SC upheld conviction — the offence involved national economic interest, and ignorance of law is no excuse. Confirmed that mens rea may be excluded by statute or necessary implication.

M.C. Mehta v. Union of India
Supreme Court of India — AIR 1987 SC 1086

Introduced the doctrine of absolute liability — enterprises engaged in inherently hazardous activities are absolutely liable for resulting harm with no exceptions. India's indigenous evolution beyond strict liability, developed in the context of the Oleum Gas Leak disaster.

Virsa Singh v. State of Punjab
Supreme Court of India — AIR 1958 SC 465

If the accused intentionally inflicted a bodily injury and that injury was sufficient to cause death in the ordinary course of nature, the offence is murder. The focus is on intention to inflict that injury — not necessarily the intention to kill.

Jacob Mathew v. State of Punjab
Supreme Court of India — (2005) 6 SCC 1

Laid down guidelines for criminal negligence of doctors. Mere error of clinical judgment does not constitute criminal negligence — the negligence must be gross and of a very high degree, demonstrating a complete disregard for the life and safety of patients.

Woolmington v. Director of Public Prosecutions
House of Lords, England — [1935] AC 462

Established the "golden thread" principle — the presumption of innocence runs through the web of criminal law. The prosecution must prove guilt; the accused need only raise a reasonable doubt. Adopted and consistently applied by Indian courts.

Om Prakash v. State of Punjab
Supreme Court of India

Husband convicted for attempt to murder his wife by deliberately withholding food. Established that criminal omissions — failure to act where there is a legal duty to do so — constitute actus reus. Applied Section 307 IPC read with the duty arising from the marital relationship.

In summary: The foundational principles of criminal law — mens rea, actus reus, strict liability, absolute liability, and the presumption of innocence — form a coherent system designed to ensure that punishment is inflicted only on those who are both morally and legally culpable. The Bharatiya Nyaya Sanhita, 2023 preserves these classical doctrines while adding new offences for organised crime, cybercrime, and terrorism. Understanding these principles is essential not only for legal practitioners and students but for any citizen seeking to understand how the law distinguishes between accidents and crimes, and why intent matters as much as act in the Indian criminal justice system.
