Criminal Revision Application — Petitioners vs. State of Gujarat

LPG Confiscation Case Analysis under the Essential Commodities Act, 1955

Sarat Rout February 17, 2025 10 min read Legal Case Analysis
High Court of Gujarat — Criminal Revision jurisdiction under Section 397 CrPC

This case analysis examines a Criminal Revision Application filed before the High Court of Gujarat, challenging an order of the Additional Sessions Judge, Banaskantha at Palanpur in Appeal No. 19 of 2000. The case raises important questions about the scope and applicability of Clause 9 of the Liquified Petroleum Gas (Regulation of Supply and Distribution) Order, 1993 to LPG traders who are not Public Distribution System (PDS) dealers, the proper basis for modifying confiscation orders under Section 6A of the Essential Commodities Act, 1955, and the duty of appellate courts to give clear, consistent, and reasoned orders.

2. Legal Background — EC Act, LPG Control Order 1993, and Section 6A

The Regulatory Framework

Liquified Petroleum Gas (LPG) is an essential commodity under the Essential Commodities Act, 1955 (EC Act). Under Section 3 EC Act, the Central Government is empowered to issue Control Orders regulating the production, supply, distribution, and trade in essential commodities.

The Liquified Petroleum Gas (Regulation of Supply and Distribution) Order, 1993 was issued under this power. Clause 9 of the 1993 Order specifically governs the maintenance of registers, account books, and submission of returns by a distributor appointed under the public distribution system (PDS). It does not apply universally to all persons dealing in LPG.

Section 6A EC Act empowers the Collector to confiscate an essential commodity seized pursuant to a violation of a Section 3 Control Order — along with its packaging and any conveyance used. The power extends to partial or total confiscation, but requires satisfaction of a contravention, proper notice under Section 6B, and a reasoned order.

Key Distinction: Under the 1993 LPG Control Order, different obligations apply to different categories of LPG handlers — distributors under the PDS, parallel marketeers, transporters, and others. Clause 9 specifically targets PDS distributors — its applicability to others must be expressly established. This distinction became the central legal issue in the present case.

3. Facts — Seizure and the Collector's Order

The petitioners were engaged in the business of refilling Liquified Petroleum Gas (LPG). They were alleged to have purchased LPG without proper bills or supporting documentation — a violation the authorities attributed to Clause 9 of the LPG (Regulation of Supply and Distribution) Order, 1993, read with Section 3 of the Essential Commodities Act, 1955.

Collector's Confiscation Order

The LPG in question was intercepted and seized by the authorities while it was being transported to a weighing machine. The first petitioner faced logistical difficulties and the LPG was being transported with a bill in the name of the second petitioner. Part of the gas was to be used by the second petitioner, and the remainder was en route to a weighing machine for proper record verification. The seizure occurred during this transit.

The Collector, believing this to constitute a violation of the LPG Control Order, issued a show-cause notice under Section 6A of the EC Act, asking the petitioners to show cause why the seized LPG and the vehicle used in transportation should not be confiscated.

The petitioners submitted their reply, which the Collector found unsatisfactory. The Collector then passed an order for full confiscation of both the LPG stock and the vehicle.

4. Appeal before the Sessions Court — Appeal No. 19 of 2000

Additional Sessions Judge, Banaskantha at Palanpur — Appeal No. 19 of 2000

Aggrieved by the Collector's wholesale confiscation order, the petitioners filed Appeal No. 19 of 2000 before the Additional Sessions Judge, Banaskantha at Palanpur under Section 6C of the EC Act, which provides a right of appeal against confiscation orders to the judicial authority appointed by the State Government.

After hearing the parties, the Additional Sessions Judge passed a modified order on 7th March, 2006, partially reducing the confiscation:

The Sessions Court thus reduced the LPG confiscation from 100% to 50%, and instead of ordering full confiscation of the vehicle, directed that 25% of its market value be paid as a fine in lieu of confiscation — a course of action available under the second proviso to Section 6A(1) for hire vehicles.

5. Criminal Revision before the High Court — Arguments Advanced

Criminal Revision Application — High Court of Gujarat

Still aggrieved by the Sessions Court's modified order, the petitioners filed a Criminal Revision Application before the High Court of Gujarat under Section 397 of the CrPC. Two distinct legal grounds were raised:

Ground 1 — Inapplicability of Clause 9 to Non-PDS Traders: The petitioners argued that Clause 9 of the LPG (Regulation of Supply and Distribution) Order, 1993 specifically regulates the conduct of distributors appointed under the public distribution system (PDS). Since the petitioners were LPG refilling businessmen and had never been appointed as PDS distributors, Clause 9 simply did not apply to them. The invocation of this clause as the basis for confiscation was therefore legally misconceived and without jurisdiction.
Ground 2 — Factual and Procedural Justification for the Transport: The LPG was being transported legitimately in the name of the second petitioner due to logistical constraints faced by the first petitioner. Part of the consignment was intended for the second petitioner's own use, and the remainder was being taken to a weighing machine for proper documentation and record-keeping — not for illicit diversion or black-marketing. The seizure occurred during this regular business activity.

6. Government's Response and its Significance

State's Response before the High Court

The learned counsel representing the State of Gujarat was unable to produce any proof that the petitioners were distributors appointed under the Public Distribution System. More critically, the government's advocate did not even argue that Clause 9 of the LPG Control Order 1993 was applicable to persons outside the PDS framework.

This failure had significant consequences: it meant that the foundational basis for invoking Clause 9 against the petitioners — the very legal provision upon which both the Collector's confiscation order and the Sessions Court's modified order rested — was left without legal justification before the High Court.

Burden of Proof Issue: Under the EC Act, the prosecution bears the initial burden of establishing that the relevant Control Order provision is applicable to the accused. The State's failure to demonstrate that Clause 9 applied to non-PDS LPG traders was a significant concession that effectively undermined the entire confiscation proceeding.

7. Court's Observation — Inconsistency in the Sessions Court's Order

Internal Contradiction in the Sessions Court's Reasoning

The High Court identified a fundamental inconsistency in the Sessions Court's order of 7th March, 2006. The two operative paragraphs of the order directly contradicted each other, rendering the appellate court's reasoning unintelligible and legally unsustainable:

Paragraph 9

The Sessions Court concluded that there was no violation of the relevant Control Order by the petitioners.

Paragraph 10

The Sessions Court then stated that there was a "technical flaw" — without identifying what that flaw was, or how it differed from the "no violation" finding in Paragraph 9 — and on this opaque basis modified the confiscation order from 100% to 50% LPG and 25% vehicle value.

The contradiction was stark: if there was no violation (Paragraph 9), there was no lawful basis for any confiscation at all. If there was a technical flaw (Paragraph 10), the court was obliged to specify what it was, why it amounted to a partial rather than complete exoneration, and how the proportionate reduction (50% / 25%) was arrived at. The Sessions Court did neither.

Issues That Required Fresh Examination

The High Court noted that neither the Collector nor the Sessions Court had specifically addressed the crucial threshold question: whether Clause 9 of the LPG Control Order 1993 was actually applicable to the petitioners in the first place. This was the foundational jurisdictional question, and its omission meant that the entire confiscation proceeding rested on an unexamined assumption.

Further, if Clause 9 were applicable, the Sessions Court had failed to articulate what the "technical flaw" consisted of and why it justified partial confiscation rather than either full confiscation (if a clear violation) or full release (if no violation).

8. High Court's Direction on Remand

Given the inconsistent and incomplete reasoning of the Sessions Court's order, the High Court held that justice would not be served by deciding the revision on its merits at that stage. The appropriate course was to remand the matter to the Sessions Court for a fresh, properly reasoned decision. The High Court issued clear directions for the re-hearing:

Direction 1: The Sessions Court must specifically examine and determine whether Clause 9 of the LPG (Regulation of Supply and Distribution) Order, 1993 is applicable to the facts of the present case — i.e., whether the petitioners, as LPG refilling businessmen, fall within the definition of "distributor appointed under the public distribution system" as contemplated by the clause.
Direction 2: If the Sessions Court finds that a "technical flaw" exists, it must clearly identify and explain what that flaw is, how it is distinct from a full-scale contravention, and whether the flaw justifies partial confiscation, complete confiscation, or release of the goods and vehicle. The proportionate reduction must be reasoned, not arbitrary.
Direction 3: The Sessions Court must ensure that its order does not contain internal contradictions — if it finds no violation, it cannot simultaneously order confiscation (partial or full); if it finds a violation or technical flaw, it must explain the evidentiary and legal basis for that finding.

9. Final Judgment

Criminal Revision Application — ALLOWED

  • The judgment of the Additional Sessions Judge, Banaskantha at Palanpur dated 7th March, 2006 (Appeal No. 19 of 2000) was set aside in its entirety.
  • The case was remanded to the Sessions Court, Banaskantha at Palanpur for fresh consideration and re-decision in accordance with the directions issued by the High Court.
  • The High Court directed that there would be no order as to costs — neither party would bear the legal expenses of the other in the revision proceedings.
Effect of Remand: The matter returns to the Sessions Court as if the appeal (No. 19 of 2000) were pending for fresh hearing. The Sessions Court must now: (1) determine Clause 9 applicability, (2) resolve the internal contradiction in its earlier reasoning, and (3) pass a fresh, reasoned, legally consistent order. The High Court's directions are binding on the Sessions Court in the re-hearing.

Case Significance

This case is an important illustration of the interplay between jurisdictional applicability of Control Orders, the duty to give reasoned appellate orders under Section 6C of the EC Act, and the High Court's supervisory revisional jurisdiction to correct fundamentally flawed quasi-judicial and judicial decisions. Several principles of lasting significance emerge:

  • Clause-specific applicability matters: Control Orders under the EC Act are precise regulatory instruments — each clause applies only to the category of persons it expressly addresses. Clause 9 of the 1993 LPG Order targets PDS distributors; applying it to other LPG traders without proof of their PDS status is legally impermissible.
  • No confiscation without clear violation: A finding of "no violation" and an order of partial confiscation cannot coexist in the same decision. The contradiction is not merely a procedural lapse — it goes to the root of the order's legality.
  • Reasoned orders are not optional: Under Section 6C EC Act, the judicial authority must produce a reasoned order explaining the factual and legal basis for any confiscation it affirms, modifies, or sets aside. Vague references to "technical flaws" without explanation are not adequate.
  • State's failure of proof is conclusive: When the State cannot establish the applicability of the very provision it relies upon for confiscation, the entire proceeding is rendered legally vulnerable — regardless of how many courts have already upheld it.
