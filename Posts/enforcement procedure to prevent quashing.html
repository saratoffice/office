Enforcement Procedure to Prevent Quashing of EC Act Cases

High Courts across India routinely quash prosecutions under the Essential Commodities Act — not because the accused is innocent, but because enforcement officers failed to follow mandatory procedural steps during seizure, sampling, and investigation. The Supreme Court's landmark February 2026 ruling in Manoj v. State of Maharashtra (2026 INSC 152) reaffirmed that a subsisting, valid Control Order is a jurisdictional prerequisite for every Section 7 prosecution. This guide covers the 12 mandatory steps, the 5 most common mistakes, and Annexures A–J providing ready-to-use field templates.

01 Legal Framework

Legal Framework — EC Act Key Provisions

High Courts across India have repeatedly emphasised that the Essential Commodities Act's penal machinery is not self-activating. The Bombay High Court in Dhanraj v. State of Maharashtra (2016) made clear that the absence of a cited Control Order renders a prosecution untenable. More recently, the Supreme Court of India in Manoj v. State of Maharashtra, 2026 INSC 152 (Justice B.V. Nagarathna and Justice R. Mahadevan, decided February 2026) set aside convictions arising from a 1994 cement incident in Aurangabad. The Court held that since the Cement Control Order 1967 had been effectively rescinded through 1989 amendments before the date of the alleged offence, no subsisting statutory order under Section 3 existed — making prosecution under Section 7 legally impermissible. The case confirms that a valid, subsisting Control Order is a jurisdictional prerequisite, not merely a procedural requirement, for any EC Act prosecution.

Essential Commodities Act, 1955 — Key Provisions

Section 3

Empowers the Central/State Government to issue Control Orders regulating production, supply, distribution, and price of essential commodities. Every prosecution must cite the specific Control Order violated — and that order must have been in force on the date of the alleged offence. A lapsed or decontrolled order provides no basis for prosecution.

Section 7

Prescribes penalties for contravening any order under Section 3 — minimum 3 months imprisonment, up to 7 years, plus fine. Repeat conviction minimum rises to 6 months. Forfeiture of property and vehicles may also be ordered by the court. Section 7 has no independent existence — it operates only through a specific, subsisting, in-force Control Order (SC: 2026 INSC 152).

Section 10A

Every offence under the Act is cognizable — police may arrest without a warrant. For search of premises, BNSS 2023 Section 100 (which replaced CrPC Section 100) requires at least two independent witnesses to be present. Non-compliance vitiates the entire search and seizure.

Section 11

No court shall take cognizance of any offence except on a written report by a public servant, an aggrieved person, or a recognised consumer association. A Collector's sanction is not required for prosecution — but the Collector must be notified under Section 6A without unreasonable delay after seizure.

Part A
02 Mandatory Steps

Part A — 12 Mandatory Steps During Enforcement & Investigation

Every enforcement action under the EC Act — whether a raid on a godown, interception of a vehicle, or inspection of a distributor's premises — must follow a strict sequence of procedural steps. Each step represents a requirement whose omission has, in reported cases, led to quashing of the prosecution. These are not bureaucratic formalities — they are the evidentiary backbone of every successful prosecution.

Mandatory Field Procedure — EC Act 1955 · BNSS 2023
Steps 01 – 12
01 of 12
Preparation of the Seizure List At the Spot
a. Presence of Independent Witnesses

The seizure list must be prepared in the presence of at least two independent witnesses as required by BNSS 2023 Section 100. Witnesses must be genuinely independent — not colleagues of the enforcement officer, not employees of the accused. Their full names, full residential addresses (with PIN code), and mobile numbers must be recorded on every page.

b. Complete Address Details

The full postal address of all concerned persons — the person in possession, the vehicle owner, the transporter — must be recorded with PIN code and landmark. Merely recording a name, a village name, or "C/o" (care of) is legally insufficient — it prevents effective service of summons and witness identity can be challenged at trial.

c. Time Gap Requirement

A clear, reasonable, and recorded time gap must exist between each procedural stage:

  • Time of interception / detection
  • Time of weighment
  • Time of formal seizure
  • Time of Zimanama (custody handover to Zimadar)

Gaps that are too short raise credibility concerns; unexplained gaps raise chain-of-custody questions.

d. Seizure of Vehicles — LPG / SK Oil Cases
  • The seizure list must explicitly state that complete drainage of SK oil has been carried out before any weighment.
  • If weighment cannot be conducted at the spot, record the reason in the seizure list. Since fuel has been drained, the vehicle must be towed — not driven on seized fuel.
  • LPG cylinders must be detached from the vehicle before shifting.
e. Weighment Procedure — LPG

Weighment of LPG cylinders may be conducted at an authorised LPG distributor's premises using certified weighing scales, with the time gap between seizure and weighment recorded and explained.

f. Vehicle Description

The seizure list must record the vehicle's colour, make, model, registration number, and direction of movement at the time of interception.

g. Engine and Chassis Numbers in Final Report

The prosecution report / chargesheet must contain the engine number and chassis number of any seized vehicle. Without these identifiers, vehicle identity cannot be conclusively established at trial.

h. Acknowledgement of Seizure

After preparation of the seizure list, a copy must be handed over to the person concerned, and a signed acknowledgement of receipt must be obtained in the presence of both witnesses.

02 of 12
Mention of Sample Quantity Accuracy Critical

The seizure list must clearly specify the number of sample bottles collected, the exact quantity of liquid in each bottle (in ml or litres), and the description and quantity of all other seized items — with batch/serial numbers where available.

Why This Matters

Any mismatch between the quantity recorded in the seizure list and the quantity later mentioned in the FSL report gives defence counsel grounds to challenge the authenticity and identity of the sample tested. Courts have quashed cases on this basis alone.

03 of 12
Consistency Between FIR and FSL Report No Discrepancy

Investigating officers must ensure that the sample quantity recorded in the FIR and seizure list exactly matches the quantity, number of bottles, and description in the FSL (Forensic Science Laboratory) report. Any discrepancy — including seal condition or commodity description — undermines the evidentiary value of the chemical analysis and will be exploited by defence counsel.

Classic Discrepancy — Leading to Quashing

Example: FIR records 1.5 litres per bottle (2 bottles = 3 litres total). FSL report arrives stating three bottles received, each containing 750 ml. The mismatch in both the number of bottles and volume per bottle creates reasonable doubt that the sample at the RFSL is the sample that was seized.

04 of 12
Mandatory Chemical Analysis RFSL Dispatch

Whenever suspected PDS kerosene, adulterated fuel, or any petroleum product is seized from a person other than an authorised retailer or wholesaler, representative samples must be taken and dispatched to the Regional Forensic Science Laboratory (RFSL) for chemical analysis. The analysis must establish the product's identity and, for adulterated products, its deviation from BIS / applicable specifications.

Legal Position

Chemical analysis is the essential evidentiary link that establishes what the seized commodity actually is. Without a valid RFSL report identifying the substance as the essential commodity specified in the Control Order, the prosecution cannot prove the foundational element of the offence.

05 of 12
Preparation of Sample Slip at the Spot On-Site Only

A sample slip must be prepared at the time and place of seizure — not later at the office. It must contain: number of bottles, exact quantity per bottle, commodity description, seal type and seal number on each bottle, signatures of the accused, at least two independent witnesses, and the Zimadar — with the exact date, time, and place of preparation.

Sealing Requirement

All sample bottles must be properly sealed in the presence of witnesses — using cloth seal, wax seal, or tamper-evident tape as per departmental procedure. The seal description and seal number must be recorded in the sample slip. A broken seal at the RFSL will render the analysis legally challengeable.

06 of 12
Delivery of One Sample Bottle to the Accused Mandatory Right

Out of the sample bottles collected, one bottle must be delivered to the person from whose possession the commodity was seized, at the time of seizure — not at the police station later. A signed receipt acknowledging delivery must be obtained in the presence of both witnesses.

Failure = Prosecution Risk

Failure to give one sample to the accused is a procedural irregularity that courts have used to cast doubt on the fairness of the entire sampling process. The Punjab & Haryana High Court (Justice Manjari Nehru Kaul, 2023) held that a defective recovery and sampling process leaves "no possibility of imposition of penalty under Section 7."

07 of 12
Mention of Sample Delivery in the FIR Record in Writing

The FIR must expressly state: (a) that one sample bottle was handed over to the person from whose possession the commodity was seized; (b) the person's name and the fact of acknowledgement of receipt; and (c) that both witnesses were present at the time of delivery. If not reflected in the FIR itself, the fact of delivery cannot be proven at trial even if it actually occurred — secondary records alone will not suffice.

08 of 12
Reporting Dispatch of Samples to the Collector Section 6A

Under Section 6A of the EC Act, the investigating officer must submit a report to the Collector of the District without unreasonable delay, stating: the date on which sample bottles were dispatched to the RFSL, the mode of dispatch, and the forwarding letter reference number. This report establishes the chain of custody at the official level and is separate from the FIR.

Section 6A Requirement

Section 6A(1) requires that a report of seizure be made to the Collector "without unreasonable delay." The Collector may then inspect the commodity and, if satisfied of a contravention, order confiscation of the commodity, packaging, and vehicle used to transport it. Failure to report to the Collector is independently challengeable, and some High Courts have held that prosecution without this report is irregular.

09 of 12
Documents to Accompany the FIR Complete Bundle

The FIR should be supported by all of the following documents at the time of filing:

  • Sample slip (Annexure-D) — prepared at the spot
  • Seizure list (Annexure-E) — signed by witnesses, accused, and officer
  • Weighment chart (Annexure-C) — where weighment was conducted separately
  • Zimanama / custody bond (Annexure-F) — identifying the Zimadar and goods
  • Forwarding letter to RFSL with RFSL acknowledgement of receipt
  • Signed acknowledgement from accused of receipt of one sample bottle
  • Photo ID copies of accused, vehicle owner, and all witnesses
Documentation = Prosecution Strength

Each document is a link in the evidentiary chain. A missing document does not necessarily destroy the prosecution — but it creates a gap that defence counsel will exploit. Courts have quashed cases where the Zimanama was absent or the RFSL forwarding letter was not produced.

10 of 12
Recording Statement of the Person Concerned BNSS S.161

The statement of the person from whose possession the commodity is seized must be recorded during investigation — covering: whether transportation was done with the knowledge of the vehicle owner; the source of procurement of the commodity and the price paid; and whether the person held any valid licence, permit, or authorisation under the applicable Control Order. All three elements are relevant to establishing both the act and mens rea.

BNSS 2023 — Section 161 Compliance

Under Section 161 of the Bharatiya Nagarik Suraksha Sanhita (BNSS) 2023, the statement must be read over and explained to the person in their own language before their signature is obtained. Any statement recorded without this step is challengeable at trial as having been made involuntarily or without understanding.

11 of 12
Timely Completion of Investigation ~2 Months

After registration of the FIR, the investigating officer must submit a prosecution report under Section 7 of the EC Act preferably within two months. If the RFSL report is awaited, file an interim chargesheet noting this and request the Magistrate to await the full report before framing charges. Record reasons for every cause of delay contemporaneously in the case diary — not retrospectively.

Delay = Grounds for Relief

Unexplained delay is one of the five most common grounds for bail and, in extreme cases, quashing of proceedings. The Supreme Court's observation in Manoj v. State of Maharashtra (2026 INSC 152) — that criminal proceedings had continued for over three decades for an act that was not an offence — underscores the systemic harm of investigative lapses and failure to revisit the legal basis of a prosecution promptly.

12 of 12
Collection of Identification Details All Parties

The investigating officer must collect and record mobile numbers and government photo identity (Aadhaar / Voter ID / Driving Licence) for the accused, the vehicle owner, and all independent witnesses. Make copies of all IDs and attach to the case file at the time of seizure. Without contact details, witnesses cannot be summoned for trial. Without photo ID, identity can be disputed.

Part B
03 Critical Mistakes

Part B — Five Common Mistakes That Lead to Quashing

The following five mistakes are the most frequently cited grounds in High Court petitions seeking quashing of EC Act prosecutions. Each is supported by reported judgments from 2016–2026.

Grounds for Quashing — High Courts & Supreme Court of India
Mistakes I – V

Each mistake below has featured as the operative ground in at least one reported quashing order between 2016 and 2026. Read these before every enforcement action — knowledge of these failure points is the first line of prosecution defence.

I
Quashing Ground

Failure to Cite the Specific Control Order in the FIR — or Citing a Lapsed Order

High Courts — including the Bombay, M.P., Rajasthan, Karnataka, and Punjab & Haryana High Courts — have consistently quashed cases where the FIR does not name the exact Control Order issued under Section 3 that was allegedly violated. An offence under Section 7 has no independent existence — it is triggered only by contravention of a specific, subsisting Control Order.

The Supreme Court's February 2026 ruling in Manoj v. State of Maharashtra (2026 INSC 152) goes further: even where a Control Order is named, if it was not in force on the date of the alleged offence (because the commodity had been decontrolled), the prosecution under Section 7 is legally impermissible — and the conviction may be set aside even decades later. The investigating agency's failure to check whether the cited Control Order was actually operative on the date of the offence has been characterised by the Supreme Court as a "lapse at the door of the investigating agency."

Dhanraj v. State of Maharashtra · Bombay HC · 2016 ALLMR(Cri) 2208 — Quashed: FIR did not reference any Section 3 order.

Manoj v. State of Maharashtra · 2026 INSC 152 · SC of India — Conviction set aside: Control Order had lapsed before the date of the incident. "Prosecution under Section 7 is legally impermissible in the absence of a subsisting statutory order under Section 3 on the date of the alleged occurrence."

MP HC (Justice R.K. Shrivastava) · March 2022 — Quashed: FIR did not indicate which Control Order was violated by the petitioner.
Correct Practice

The FIR must specifically name the Control Order, confirm it was in force on the date of the offence (citing the Gazette notification), state the specific clause violated, and describe the act or omission. Consult the district Public Prosecutor or legal cell to confirm current applicability of the Control Order before filing the FIR.

II
Quashing Ground

FIR Based Only on Seizure Without Proving Violation or Mens Rea

Mere seizure of an essential commodity does not automatically constitute an offence under the EC Act. The investigation must actively establish how the commodity was diverted, whether the accused held any valid licence under the applicable Control Order, the source of procurement, and whether the Control Order prohibited the specific conduct in question. The Supreme Court in Nathulal v. State of Madhya Pradesh (1965) affirmed that mens rea — guilty mind — is an essential element of a Section 7 offence, even under a regulatory statute.

Calcutta High Court (Justice Subhendu Samanta) · November 2023
Conviction quashed — mere omission of a licence number in cash memos did not constitute an offence under the EC Act; mens rea was not established. Seizure alone was held insufficient to sustain conviction.
III
Quashing Ground

Improper Sampling Procedure

Failure to follow the mandatory sampling procedure — not preparing the sample slip at the spot; not sealing bottles properly in the presence of witnesses; not giving one bottle to the accused at the time of seizure; a discrepancy between the seizure list and the RFSL report; or broken seals at the laboratory — may result in the chemical analysis report being challenged and excluded from evidence.

Punjab & Haryana HC · Justice Manjari Nehru Kaul · 2023
"Any search and seizure not conducted in accordance with law … would hold no weight during trial. … Owing to the defective recovery process, there would arise no possibility of imposition of penalty under Section 7 of the Essential Commodities Act."
IV
Quashing Ground

Break in Chain of Custody of Samples

If there is no clear documented record of when and how samples were transferred from the seizure site to the RFSL — missing forwarding letter, undated dispatch, unlabelled bottles, no RFSL acknowledgement on file — courts may doubt the authenticity of the sample tested and reject the analysis report. The chain of custody must be documented without a single gap: seizure → sealed sample slip → Zimadar's custody → dispatch to RFSL (with forwarding letter) → RFSL receipt acknowledgement → analysis → report. Any undocumented break in this chain is a prosecution vulnerability.

V
Quashing Ground

Delay in Investigation and Filing of Prosecution Report

Unexplained delay in completing investigation or filing the prosecution report weakens the case — particularly where the accused is in custody. Courts view such delay unfavourably and may grant bail or, in extreme cases, quash proceedings as abuse of process. The Supreme Court's 2026 ruling in Manoj v. State of Maharashtra also illustrated how investigative lapses — including failure to verify whether the correct law applied — can haunt a prosecution for over three decades.

Best Practice

File an interim chargesheet within the statutory period if the RFSL report is awaited. Inform the Magistrate of the pending analysis and request the court to await the full report before framing charges. Record all causes of delay in the case diary contemporaneously — not retrospectively.

Criminal Appeal No. 1630 of 2015 · Neutral Citation: 2026 INSC 152
Justice B.V. Nagarathna & Justice R. Mahadevan · Supreme Court of India · February 2026

The accused were convicted under Sections 3 and 7 of the EC Act for alleged possession of 365 bags of PWD-diverted cement seized in Aurangabad on 24.03.1994. Both the Special Judge and the Bombay High Court affirmed conviction. The Supreme Court allowed the appeals and set aside the conviction, holding: "in the absence of any subsisting statutory order under Section 3 of the E.C. Act on the date of the alleged occurrence, a conviction under Section 7 thereof is legally impermissible." The Cement Control Order 1967 had been rescinded by 1989 amendments — no operative order existed in 1994. The Court further noted that the investigating agency ought to have invoked IPC provisions (diversion of government property, dishonest retention) — "The lapse therefore lies squarely at the door of the investigating agency."
Dhanraj s/o Anandrao Mohod v. State of Maharashtra
Criminal Application (APL) No. 728 of 2015 · Bombay High Court (Nagpur Bench) · 2016 ALLMR(Cri) 2208

"In absence of it being shown that there was any order made under Section 3 of the said Act that had been contravened, the proceedings for an offence punishable under Section 7 … would not be tenable. The continuation of these proceedings therefore would amount to an abuse of the process of law."
MP High Court (Justice R.K. Shrivastava) — Section 482 CrPC Application, Gwalior Bench, March 2022

"The impugned FIR does not indicate which Control Order has been violated by the petitioner … prosecution launched by the police against the petitioner is not in accordance with law as they registered the case at their own instance without mentioning the Control Order violated." — FIR and further proceedings quashed.

Annexure-J — General Guidelines for Enforcement Officers

Standing instructions for all Supply Inspectors and enforcement officers conducting raids and inspections under the EC Act 1955. Read before every enforcement action.

  • Cite the specific Control Order and confirm it is in force. A prosecution under Section 7 is legally impermissible in the absence of a subsisting statutory order under Section 3 on the date of the alleged occurrence — Supreme Court (2026 INSC 152, Manoj v. State of Maharashtra). Confirm the Order is currently operative before seizing and before filing the FIR.
  • Follow due legal procedure at all times. Ensure BNSS 2023 Section 100 compliance during searches — minimum two independent witnesses. Unlawful search or seizure vitiates the entire case.
  • Prepare all documents at the spot — never retrospectively. Seizure list, sample slip, weighment chart, and Zimanama must all be prepared at the time and place of the enforcement action, not later at the police station.
  • Avoid the five procedural mistakes in Part B. These are the actual grounds on which courts quash EC Act cases — read Part B before every enforcement action.
  • Maintain and document the chain of custody without a single gap. Seizure → sealed sample slip → Zimadar's custody → dispatch to RFSL (forwarding letter) → RFSL receipt → analysis report. Every link must be documented with date, time, and signature.
  • Respect the legal rights of the accused. Hand over one sample bottle to the accused at the spot. Provide a copy of the seizure list. Read over and explain the statement in the accused's own language before obtaining signature. These are legal requirements under the BNSS 2023, not courtesies.
  • Complete investigation without unreasonable delay. File the prosecution report preferably within two months. If the RFSL report is awaited, file an interim chargesheet and inform the court. Record all causes of delay contemporaneously in the case diary.
  • If the investigation reveals the commodity was decontrolled or the Control Order had lapsed — do not file a Section 7 chargesheet. Instead, examine whether IPC provisions (diversion of government property, dishonest retention, criminal breach of trust) apply. The Supreme Court in 2026 INSC 152 expressly noted that the investigating agency's failure to invoke IPC provisions was itself a lapse that deprived the prosecution of an alternative basis for conviction.
  • Update your knowledge of currently applicable Control Orders. The EC Act, BNSS 2023, and State Control Orders are amended periodically. Verify with your district legal cell or Public Prosecutor when in doubt about whether a Control Order remains operative.
The Core Principle

The purpose of enforcement is to protect public welfare — not to create paperwork. A prosecution built on correct procedure protects both the public and the enforcement officer. A prosecution built on shoddy procedure allows guilty persons to walk free and may expose the investigating officer to professional consequences. The Supreme Court (2026 INSC 152) noted that accused persons faced criminal proceedings for over three decades for an act that turned out not to be an offence under the statute cited — "The lapse therefore lies squarely at the door of the investigating agency."

Conclusion

Procedural compliance is not a technicality — it is the foundation of every successful prosecution under the Essential Commodities Act. The Supreme Court's February 2026 ruling in Manoj v. State of Maharashtra (2026 INSC 152) has added a new first-order requirement to the established checklist: the investigating officer must now affirmatively verify — and record in the FIR — that the cited Control Order was in force on the date of the alleged offence. A prosecution based on a decontrolled or lapsed order cannot stand, regardless of the weight of other evidence.

Strict adherence to the 12 mandatory steps in Part A ensures transparency and fairness in enforcement, preserves the integrity of evidence, and significantly reduces the likelihood of cases being quashed on procedural grounds.
