Amendment in Essential Commodities Act: A Gift to Farmers

Sarat Rout March 9, 2021 6 min read Agriculture
Essential Commodities (Amendment) Act, 2020 – empowering farmers with freedom

The Essential Commodities (Amendment) Act, 2020, often described as a watershed moment for Indian agriculture, fundamentally alters the colonial-era Essential Commodities Act of 1955. For decades, this act empowered the government to regulate production, supply, and distribution of essential commodities, including foodstuffs. The amendment removes these stringent controls and places the farmer at the centre of agricultural trade. It is, without exaggeration, a gift that liberates the annadata from unnecessary licensing, stock limits, and bureaucratic oversight.

Before the amendment, the government could impose stock limits on commodities like cereals, pulses, oilseeds, onions, and potatoes under the notion of maintaining supplies and preventing hoarding. In practice, this discouraged private investment and created an atmosphere of uncertainty. Traders and food processors could not hold large inventories, which limited their ability to purchase from farmers at remunerative prices. The farmer often had no option but to sell at the farm gate to local traders at below-MSP rates. The amendment sweeps away these restrictions entirely.

A new era of deregulation

The amended Act stipulates that stock limits can now only be imposed under extraordinary circumstances—such as war, famine, or severe price rise—and even then, only if there is no automated system to track prices. Agricultural foodstuffs, including cereals, pulses, and onions, are now exempt from stock limits unless prices rise sky-high (50% increase over the previous year's average for perishables, 100% for non-perishables). This rational approach creates a predictable policy environment.

What does this mean for a farmer in Punjab or Madhya Pradesh? It means that a large processor or exporter can now purchase his entire crop without worrying about stock caps. It encourages the setting up of godowns, cold storages, and supply chains close to farms. The farmer is no longer forced to sell only during the harvest glut when prices are lowest. He can choose to sell to a consolidator who stores the produce and sells later. This bargaining power was unthinkable under the old regime.

“The amendment liberates farmers by allowing them to engage with buyers who can now hold inventory without fear of sudden government action. It is a structural reform that encourages competition and investment.” — Policy analyst

Why it's a gift to farmers

The removal of licensing requirements for storage and trade of foodstuffs further reduces the inspection raj. Earlier, even wholesalers and large retailers needed licences, which bred corruption and harassment. Now, any entity can store and sell essential commodities freely. This opens the door for big retail chains, exporters, and food processing companies to build direct procurement links with farmers. Contract farming and direct marketing become viable at scale.

Moreover, the amendment encourages futures trading and commodity markets to mature. When large players can hold stocks, they hedge in futures markets, which leads to better price discovery. Farmers can eventually benefit from price signals and plan their sowing accordingly. The act is part of a trilogy of farm reforms—alongside the Farmers' Produce Trade and Commerce Act and the Farmers (Empowerment and Protection) Agreement Act—that together dismantle the monopsony of mandis.

Impact on pulses and onions

Take the case of pulses. India imports a significant quantity of pulses because domestic production is volatile. With the amendment, private traders can now hold large stocks of pulses, allowing them to import in bulk and store for lean periods. This stabilises prices and ensures availability. Similarly, for onions—a politically sensitive commodity—the fear of sudden stock limits often led to massive wastage. Now, cold storage and supply chain players can confidently invest in onion storage, reducing post-harvest losses and allowing farmers to sell over a longer period.

Critics argue that the amendment could lead to hoarding and price manipulation. However, the built-in safeguards—triggering stock limits only when prices rise abnormally—address that concern. The government retains the power to intervene in genuine emergencies. In normal times, market forces determine prices. This balances the interests of consumers and producers.

Empowering the small farmer

Small and marginal farmers, who constitute the majority, gain the most. They often lack storage facilities and are forced to sell at harvest time. With more buyers entering the market due to relaxed norms, even a small farmer near a town can find multiple buyers—local millers, retailers, or aggregators—who can now store legally. This competition pushes up farmgate prices. The amendment thus directly addresses price realisation, the single biggest pain point of Indian agriculture.

A long‑awaited structural shift

The Essential Commodities (Amendment) Act, 2020, is not an isolated tweak; it signals a paradigm shift from control to facilitation. For decades, agriculture was treated as a subsistence activity requiring tight regulation. The new approach views farming as an enterprise that needs investment, technology, and markets. By freeing up storage and movement, the amendment encourages private capital to build the rural infrastructure that the government alone could never fund. Siloes, warehouses, and cold chains will emerge, creating jobs and reducing wastage.

Opponents claim that the amendment erodes the public distribution system (PDS) or that it will eventually lead to MSP dismantling. These fears are unfounded. MSP operations continue; the government remains the largest procurer. What changes is that farmers now have additional options beyond government procurement. They can sell to private players at competitive prices without moving out of their villages. The amendment also explicitly allows farmers to avail themselves of MSP even if they sell to private traders—there's no exclusivity clause.

Conclusion

The amendment to the Essential Commodities Act is indeed a gift to farmers—a gift of choice, dignity, and opportunity. It removes the invisible fences that confined farmers to local mandis and limited their buyers. By trusting farmers and traders rather than suspecting them, the law paves the way for a modern, market‑oriented agricultural economy. As with any reform, the true test lies in implementation and in ensuring that farmers are aware of their new freedoms. Extension services and awareness campaigns must complement the legal change. But the direction is clear: Indian agriculture is finally breaking free from the clutches of an archaic act. For the farmer, this is a new dawn.
