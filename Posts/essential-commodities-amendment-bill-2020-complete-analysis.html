Essential Commodities (Amendment) Bill, 2020 – Full Overview & Analysis

Sarat Rout February 17, 2025 9 min read Legal & Policy
Agricultural reform: Transforming India's food supply chain

Introduction

Rajya Sabha: 22 Sept 2020 Lok Sabha: 15 Sept 2020 Ordinance: 5 June 2020

The Rajya Sabha passed the Essential Commodities (Amendment) Bill, 2020 on 22nd September 2020. The Bill seeks to deregulate cereals, pulses, oilseeds, edible oils, onions, and potatoes from the list of essential commodities.

The Bill was initially introduced in the Lok Sabha on 14th September 2020 by Shri Danve Raosaheb Dadarao, the Minister of State for Consumer Affairs, Food & Public Distribution, to replace ordinances promulgated earlier on 5th June 2020. It was passed by the Lok Sabha on 15th September 2020.

The Bill Aims To:

Reduce excessive regulatory interference

Allow free production, storage, movement, distribution

Attract private & FDI in agriculture

Expand cold storage facilities

Modernize food supply chain

Responding to discussions in Rajya Sabha, Minister Danve Raosaheb Dadarao stated that the amendment is crucial to:

  • Reduce agri-produce wastage due to lack of storage
  • Create a favorable environment for farmers, consumers, and investors
  • Contribute towards making India self-sufficient
  • Fulfill the government's promise to double farmers' income by encouraging investment

Source: PRS Legislative Research

Background

The Progress

India has transitioned from a food-deficient to a food-surplus country.

Farmers, however, continue to struggle to fetch fair prices due to:

  • Poor investment in cold storage
  • Lack of warehouses, processing facilities, and export mechanisms

ECA 1955 Context

Under the Essential Commodities Act, 1955 (ECA 1955):

  • States could impose stock limits and restrict movement of essential goods
  • Governments were required to impose stock limits on pulses, edible oils, vegetables, etc., to prevent hoarding

Economic Survey 2019-20 Findings

According to the Economic Survey 2019-20 (Government of India, Volume I, Chapter 7):

  • Frequent stock limit impositions did not control inflation or price volatility
  • It enabled rent-seeking behavior and harassment of traders
  • Examples where stock limits worsened price volatility:
    • Dal (2006)
    • Sugar (2009)
    • Onions (September 2019)

OECD-ICRIER Report 2018

A 2018 OECD-ICRIER Agricultural Policies Report highlighted that restrictive export policies under ECA kept Indian farmers poor by preventing them from benefiting when global crop prices surged.

Thus, reforming ECA is expected to:

Boost investment in cold storage

Strengthen food supply chain

Benefit farmers and consumers

Stabilize market prices

Key Features of the Bill

1

Regulation of Food Items

The Central Government can designate commodities as essential and regulate their production, supply, distribution, trade, and commerce.

The Bill restricts regulation of cereals, pulses, potatoes, onions, edible oilseeds, and oils only under exceptional circumstances such as:

War Famine Extraordinary price rise Natural calamities of severe nature
2

Stock Limitations

Stock limits on agricultural produce can only be imposed if:

Horticulture produce: Retail price rise by 100%

Non-perishable items: Retail price rise by 50%

Price increase calculated based on: Average retail price for previous 12 months, OR average of past 5 years, whichever is lower.

3

Exceptions for Processors & Exporters

Stock limits will not apply if:

  • Stocks are within the installed capacity of processors
  • Stocks meet the export demands for exporters
4

Exemption for PDS

Any government order related to Public Distribution System (PDS) or Targeted Public Distribution System (TPDS) will be exempted from stock restriction rules.

5

Consumer Protection

While liberalizing the regulatory framework, consumer interests will be safeguarded to prevent hoarding and inflation.

Expected Benefits

Freedom to produce, hold, move, distribute, and supply

Realization of economies of scale in agriculture

Increased private and foreign investment

Rise in cold storage investments

Modernization of supply chains

Reduced agri-produce wastage

Price stabilization for farmers and consumers

Issues and Criticisms

Centralization Concerns

Critics argue that the Bill overly centralizes power, limiting state governments' ability to regulate hoarding and black-marketing.

Risk of Hoarding and Inflation

Relaxation of stock limits could lead to illicit market practices and hoarding by large traders. Potential for monopoly control over essential commodities, leading to price spikes.

Conclusion

India's agricultural sector needs reform that matches its transition from scarcity to surplus production.

The Essential Commodities (Amendment) Bill, 2020 is a significant step toward:

Doubling farmers' incomes

Improving ease of doing business

Attracting investments & infrastructure

However, to fully realize its potential, it is crucial to carefully monitor and enforce regulations to prevent hoarding and exploitation while protecting small farmers and consumers.
