Excel Trick: Keeping the "Abstract" Row at the Bottom of Your Data Range When Sorting

Genus Power February 17, 2025 4 min read Excel Tips
Keep your summary row fixed at the bottom while sorting data

The Problem: Abstract Row Gets Mixed Up

If you work with large datasets in Excel, it's common to have an "Abstract" or summary row at the bottom of your data, which you want to stay fixed at the bottom of the table even when sorting your data. However, if you sort the entire range of data, the "Abstract" row can get mixed up within the data, causing confusion.

The Solution: Convert the Abstract Row to a Table (Without Header)

Here's a simple Excel trick to fix this issue and ensure your "Abstract" row remains at the bottom while sorting the data.

Step-by-Step Guide:

1

Select Your Data Range

First, highlight the entire data range of the "Abstract" row, which is located at the bottom of your dataset.

Click & Drag to select the entire abstract row
2

Convert to a Table

Press Ctrl + T or go to the "Insert" tab and click on "Table".

A window will pop up asking: "Does your table have headers?"

Important: Since your "Abstract" row is not part of the main data and does not have headers, leave the box unchecked and click "OK".
✓ Headers: UNCHECKED
3

Remove Header from the Abstract Table

After converting the abstract row to a table, you will see it has a header row automatically added.

How to remove: Right-click on the header of the new table and choose "Table" → "Convert to Range" OR:

  1. Click anywhere in the table
  2. Go to the "Table Design" tab
  3. Uncheck "Header Row" in the Table Style Options group

Table Design → Header Row (uncheck)

4

Delete the Blank Row

After removing the header, a blank row appears above the abstract row. Delete this blank row to clean up your spreadsheet.

Right-click on the row number → Delete
5

Move the Abstract Table (if needed)

If necessary, move the abstract row table below your main dataset to ensure it's always at the bottom. You can do this by cutting the table (Ctrl + X) and pasting it (Ctrl + V) in the desired location.

6

Sort Your Data

Now, when you sort the rest of your data, the abstract row will stay fixed at the bottom.

Excel recognizes that the abstract row is part of a separate table and will not include it in the sort range.

Why It Works

By converting the abstract row into its own table and removing the header, you tell Excel to treat it as a non-sortable range. This ensures that it will remain unaffected by any sorting actions on the main dataset, keeping it exactly where you need it.

Bonus Tip: Visual Enhancement

You can also format the abstract row with different colors or borders to visually distinguish it from the rest of your data, making it even easier to spot.

Conclusion

This simple Excel trick allows you to keep the "Abstract" or summary row at the bottom of your data range, even when sorting the rest of the dataset. By converting only the abstract row into a table format without headers, you can prevent it from getting mixed up with your data, improving the readability and organization of your spreadsheet.
