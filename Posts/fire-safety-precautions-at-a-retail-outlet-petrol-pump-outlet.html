Fire Safety Precautions at a Retail Outlet (Petrol Pump)

A statutory compliance guide under the Petroleum Act 1934, Petroleum Rules 2002, OISD-STD-225, and Gas Cylinders Rules 2016 retail outlet operators

Sarat Rout April 23, 2026 14 min read Regulatory Compliance
Fire safety at petrol pump retail outlets is a non-negotiable statutory requirement under PESO oversight — non-compliance can lead to suspension or cancellation of the Form XIV licence

A petroleum retail outlet — commonly called a petrol pump — handles Class A petroleum (motor spirit / petrol, flash point below 23°C) and Class B petroleum (High Speed Diesel, flash point 23°C–65°C). Both are highly flammable hydrocarbons whose storage, handling, and dispensing are strictly regulated under the Petroleum Act, 1934 and the Petroleum Rules, 2002 (as amended through 2024), with technical standards set by the Oil Industry Safety Directorate (OISD) — particularly OISD-STD-225. Regulatory oversight and licensing rests with the Petroleum and Explosives Safety Organisation (PESO). Outlets additionally handling CNG or Auto-LPG are governed by the Gas Cylinders Rules, 2016.

Governing Statutory Framework

Petroleum Act, 1934 — foundational law governing import, production, refining, storage, transport, and distribution of petroleum products in India.

Petroleum Rules, 2002 (amended 2024) — prescribes technical requirements for retail outlets, licensing conditions (Form XIV), UST standards, safety distances, earthing, electrical fittings, fire-fighting equipment, and road tanker safety.

OISD-STD-225 — Oil Industry Safety Directorate standard specifically for storage, handling, and dispensing of petroleum products at retail outlets. Referenced in the Petroleum Rules 2002 as an approved specification under Rule 2(ii)(a).

Gas Cylinders Rules, 2016 — governs CNG dispensing stations (licensed under Form G) and Auto-LPG facilities co-located at retail outlets.

Licensing and Administrative Compliance

Every petroleum retail outlet must operate under a valid licence in Form XIV issued by PESO under the Petroleum Rules, 2002, authorising the storage of petroleum in underground tanks for fuelling motor conveyances. The licence must be prominently displayed on the premises at all times.

PESO Form XIV — Key Administrative Requirements

  • Licence validity and renewal: Applications for renewal must reach the licensing authority on or before the date of expiry. Renewal is applied for through the NSWS portal at peso.gov.in.
  • Staff training records: Records of periodic safety training, including mock fire drills, must be maintained and available for inspection.
  • Equipment certification: All fire extinguishers must bear a valid ISI/BIS mark, current pressure test tag, and annual refilling record.
  • Hazard signage: "No Smoking," "Switch Off Engine," "No Mobile Phone Use," and emergency telephone numbers must be displayed prominently in local, Hindi, and English languages.
  • Explosives Rules extract: An extract of the applicable Petroleum Rules must be displayed at the outlet as per the OISD-STD-225 daily checklist.
Non-Compliance Consequence

Operating without a valid Form XIV licence, or in violation of licence conditions, constitutes an offence under the Petroleum Act, 1934. PESO has the authority to suspend or cancel the licence.

Underground Storage Tanks — Design and Infrastructure

Underground Storage Tanks (USTs) at retail outlets store Class A and Class B petroleum. Their design, installation, testing, and maintenance are among the most critical fire and explosion prevention elements at a retail outlet.

Tank Design and Leak Prevention

USTs must be leak-proof, constructed to approved specifications, and periodically tested for integrity. Fill pipes must have a standard thread size of 75 mm diameter. The diprod must be in good working condition and used regularly for manual stock verification.

Vent Pipes and Flame Arrestors

Wire mesh / flame arrestors must be fitted to all vent pipe outlets — the wire gauge must not be clogged or missing. Vent pipe locations must conform to the PESO-approved layout drawing. Blocked vent pipe wire mesh is a specific item verified in the OISD-STD-225 daily checklist.

Earthing and Static Electricity Control

All tanks, pipelines, dispensing units, and pump pedestals must be properly earthed to prevent static electricity build-up. Electrodes must be accessible for testing. Pump pedestals must be maintained with dry sand filled in the gap below.

Safety Distances

Safety distances between retail outlet facilities and surrounding buildings, roads, and sensitive locations must comply with the conditions specified in Form XV of the Petroleum Rules, 2002 and the applicable licence conditions.

3

Electrical Safety and Hazardous Area Classification

Petroleum retail outlets handle Class A petroleum vapour which, when mixed with air in certain proportions, forms a highly flammable vapour-air mixture. The dispensing area is classified as a hazardous zone under OISD-STD-113, incorporated by reference in the Petroleum Rules, 2002.

Flameproof Electrical Fittings

All electrical fittings — wiring, switches, motors, junction boxes, light fittings — within the designated hazardous zone must be of the explosion-proof (flameproof) type, conforming to relevant IEC/IS standards. Tamper-proof junction boxes must be kept properly closed at all times.

Emergency Stop / Master Switch

A prominent "Master Switch" capable of cutting power to all dispensing units simultaneously must be installed at an accessible, clearly marked location to enable rapid isolation of all dispensing activities in an emergency.

OISD-STD-225 Daily Checklist Item

The daily checklist specifically requires verification that: all dispensing unit connections and tank farm piping are leak-free with no sweating at joints; there is no loose wiring in the pump; tamper-proof boxes are closed properly; and dry sand is filled in the gap below the pump pedestal.

4

Control of Ignition Sources

Ignition source control is the first line of fire prevention at a retail outlet. Every possible source of spark, heat, or flame must be rigorously excluded from the vicinity of dispensing and storage areas.

No Smoking or Open Flames

Smoking, use of lighters, matchsticks, and any open flame is strictly prohibited within the licensed area. This must be marked with clear signage and enforced without exception.

Engine Off During Fuelling

All vehicle engines must be switched off before fuelling begins. A running engine presents a heat and spark source that can ignite spilled petroleum vapour. Attendants must enforce this for every vehicle.

Mobile Phone Restriction

Mobile phones must not be used near dispensing points — primarily to keep attendants focused on the fuelling operation and eliminate distraction, the leading cause of fuel spills and overflow.

No Unauthorised Hot Work

Welding, grinding, cutting, or any hot work requires prior authorisation, a formal permit-to-work, gas-free certification, and a dedicated fire watch throughout the entire operation.

5

Mandatory Fire-Fighting Equipment

Every licensed retail outlet must maintain specified fire-fighting equipment — both in type and in quantity — as prescribed by OISD-STD-225 and referenced under the Petroleum Rules, 2002. All extinguishers must conform to their respective Indian Standards and bear valid ISI/BIS marks.

🧯

DCP Extinguishers (Dry Chemical Powder)

Effective against Class B (flammable liquid) and Class C (electrical) fires — the primary hazard type at a petrol pump.

  • OISD-STD-225 — 2 nos. of 10 kg DCP type per outlet dispensing pump
  • Conforming to IS:2171 bearing valid ISI mark
  • Maximum top surface height: 1.5 m above floor/ground level
  • Maximum running distance to locate any extinguisher: 15 metres
  • Rain protection must be provided; refilled annually or after use
🪣

Sand Buckets

Dry sand smothers small liquid fuel fires by cutting off oxygen and absorbing spilled fuel.

  • OISD-STD-225 — 8-litre capacity, round bottom, maximum 10 nos.
  • Sand must be fine, dry, and free from oil, water, or rubbish
  • Rain protection must be provided; last verification date on record
⚡

CO₂ Extinguishers (Carbon Dioxide)

Essential for electrical fires — CO₂ leaves no residue and is safe for use on live electrical equipment.

  • Mandatory near electrical panels, main switchboards, DG rooms, and sub-stations
  • As per OISD-STD-118: 1 No. 4.5 kg CO₂ extinguisher per 25 m² of switch room
  • Conforming to IS:2878 bearing valid ISI mark
🫧

Foam Extinguishers

Foam creates a blanket over burning fuel, cutting off oxygen and cooling the fuel surface.

  • Required near tank lorry unloading points and underground tank areas
  • Foam compound must conform to IS:4989 and be periodically tested per OISD-STD-142
  • Foam concentrates of different types must not be mixed
Inspection Point — OISD-STD-225 Daily Checklist

The daily checklist specifically verifies: fire extinguishers in position, charged, and within validity; sand buckets containing dry sand (not oil-contaminated or water-logged); and extinguishers/buckets positioned near the tank lorry during unloading. Mandatory items in any enforcement inspection.

Tanker Unloading — Safety Precautions

Fuel tanker (tank lorry / TL) unloading is the highest-risk operation at a retail outlet — it involves the transfer of large volumes of Class A or Class B petroleum under pressure, with significant vapour release. The Petroleum Rules, 2002 and OISD-STD-225 prescribe a strict protocol for this operation.

Bonding Cable — Static Electricity Discharge

Before decanting begins, the tank lorry must be connected to a dedicated bonding (earthing) cable to discharge any static electricity accumulated during transit. The OISD-STD-225 checklist asks: "Is bonding wire connected while decanting the tank lorry?" — failure to bond is a critical safety lapse that must be flagged immediately.

Engine Switched Off During Discharge

The engine of the tank lorry must be switched off throughout the decanting operation. A running tanker engine is both a heat source and a potential ignition source for petroleum vapour released during transfer.

Authorised Personnel Only

Only trained and authorised personnel — the tank lorry driver and the designated outlet Delivery Station Manager (DSM) — should be present and active during unloading. Bystanders must be kept away from the decanting area.

No Dispensing During Unloading

No fuel should be dispensed to vehicles during active tanker unloading. This prevents vapour pressure disturbances in the underground tank system that can increase spill risk.

Spillage Management

Any spillage during unloading must be immediately absorbed using dry sand or approved absorbents. Spilled petroleum must never be washed into the drainage system — it must be collected and safely disposed of.

Pre-Unloading Verification — OISD Checklist

Before authorising tanker unloading verify: (a) bonding cable connected; (b) fire extinguishers and sand buckets positioned near the TL discharge point; (c) tanker engine off; (d) discharge points distinctively painted; (e) dispensing to vehicles suspended; (f) lorry discharge points and dip pipes securely closed after completion.

7

Operational Safety Practices

Day-to-day operations must be carried out by trained personnel following established safe operating procedures. The OISD-STD-225 specifies both a daily checklist and a periodic checklist covering all safety elements of the outlet.

Housekeeping

Provision of dustbins, garbage disposal, and regular cleaning of drainage channels must be maintained. Spilled fuel must be immediately mopped up. No flammable materials (LPG cylinders, cardboard cartons) should be stored in the dispensing area.

Dispensing Unit Maintenance

All dispensing units must be kept externally and internally clean. The sand below each dispensing unit must be free of product spillage and inflammable waste.

Drainage Channels

Parapets (drainage channels) must be clean, not leaking, and maintain a proper gradient to allow drainage of any spilled fuel away from the dispensing and storage areas.

Only Trained Staff on Duty

All operations — fuelling, unloading, stock measurement — must be performed by trained outlet staff familiar with the applicable safety rules and emergency procedures.

8

CNG and Auto-LPG Dispensing — Additional Requirements

Retail outlets that also dispense Compressed Natural Gas (CNG) or Auto-LPG are subject to the additional requirements of the Gas Cylinders Rules, 2016, with CNG station licences issued in Form G. PESO's guidelines for CNG stations (March 2025) prescribe the following mandatory requirements:

Passengers Must Disembark Before Filling

As per the Gas Cylinders Rules, 2016 and PESO guidelines: "There shall not be any passenger while refueling." Every passenger must disembark from the vehicle before CNG or Auto-LPG filling begins. No exceptions.

Engine Off During Filling

"The engine shall be in switch off mode while refueling" at CNG/Auto-LPG dispensing points. The vehicle must not be left unattended while refuelling is in progress.

Compliance Plate — Hydro-Test Validity

CNG cylinders must undergo periodic hydrostatic testing at recognised testing stations. Attendants must verify that the vehicle's CNG cylinder is within its valid hydro-test period before dispensing. An expired hydro-test date is grounds to refuse filling.

Never Exceed Rated Maximum Working Pressure

CNG cylinders must never be filled beyond their rated maximum working pressure (MWP) stamped on the cylinder. Any leaking CNG cylinder valve must be moved to an open area away from ignition sources and the filler (city gas distribution company) must be immediately informed.

CNG Hose Testing Requirement

As per PESO guidelines (March 2025), CNG dispensing hoses must be tested per the Gas Cylinders Rules, 2016 at prescribed intervals. Hose condition — for cracks, bulges, or connector corrosion — must be checked regularly. A damaged CNG hose under operating pressure is an extreme fire and explosion hazard.

9

Emergency Preparedness and Reporting

A written Emergency Action Plan (EAP) specific to the retail outlet must be prepared, approved, and kept updated. This plan covers actions to be taken in the event of a fire, fuel spill, or gas leak; evacuation routes and assembly points; responsibility assignments; and the procedure for alerting external emergency services.

Emergency Contact Display

Emergency telephone numbers — local fire brigade, police, nearest hospital, PESO regional office — must be displayed prominently at multiple locations. The OISD-STD-225 checklist specifically asks: "Are safety messages like Tel. No., Police, Fire Brigade, Hospital and No Smoking displayed?"

Mock Drills and Safety Audits

Regular mock fire drills must be conducted and records maintained. All staff must be trained on how to operate each type of fire extinguisher, use sand buckets effectively, follow evacuation procedures, and safely deal with a fuel spill or CNG leak.

Mandatory Reporting — Any Accident, Fire, or Leakage

Any accident, fire, explosion, or leakage must be immediately reported to PESO and to local authorities (police, fire brigade). Failure to report is an independent violation of the Petroleum Rules. The outlet must cooperate fully with any subsequent investigation.

2025 Update — EV Charging at Retail Outlets

PESO has issued guidelines in 2025 for the installation of Electric Vehicle (EV) charging/battery swapping facilities at petroleum retail outlets. Outlets adding EV facilities must obtain prior approval from PESO and comply with the specific safety separation distances and electrical safety requirements before commissioning any EV charging infrastructure.

🔥 Conclusion — Safety is Statutory, Not Optional

Fire safety at a petroleum retail outlet is not a matter of best practice — it is a mandatory statutory requirement embedded in the Petroleum Act 1934, Petroleum Rules 2002 (amended 2024), OISD-STD-225, and the Gas Cylinders Rules 2016. Every element — from the earthing of USTs to the hydro-test verification of CNG cylinders — has a specific legal basis and can be verified during an enforcement inspection by PESO officers or by officials empowered under the Petroleum Rules.

Non-compliance with any mandatory requirement must be documented and referred to the appropriate authority for action under the Petroleum Act, 1934.
