Fire Safety Precautions at an LPG Godown

Sarat Rout April 15, 2026 10 min read LPG Safety
LPG godown safety is governed by the Gas Cylinders Rules, 2016 and SMPV Rules under the Explosives Act, 1884 — compliance is a legal mandate and a critical public safety measure

Safety precautions at an LPG (Liquefied Petroleum Gas) godown are governed strictly by the Gas Cylinders Rules, 2016 and the Static and Mobile Pressure Vessels (Unfired) Rules, 2016 (SMPV Rules) under the Explosives Act, 1884. Compliance is not just a regulatory necessity — it is a critical measure to prevent catastrophic fire or pressure-related accidents that can endanger workers, neighbouring buildings, and the public.

Governing Framework

Gas Cylinders Rules, 2016 and SMPV Rules, 2016 under the Explosives Act, 1884 set the complete framework for LPG godown safety. All requirements below — from licensing to fire equipment — flow from these rules. The Petroleum and Explosives Safety Organisation (PESO) is the enforcement authority.

A massive fire accident in an LPG godown is not just an isolated incident—it is usually the direct result of ignored safety protocols, poor supervision, or violation of statutory norms.

1. Licensing and Approval Requirements

An LPG godown must operate only with a valid licence issued by the Petroleum and Explosives Safety Organisation (PESO) under the SMPV Rules. The storage capacity, layout, and construction must strictly conform to the approved plan.

  • Any modification — expansion, structural change, or repositioning of storage areas — requires prior approval from the licensing authority before work commences
  • Unauthorized storage or excess stock beyond the licensed capacity is strictly prohibited
  • Violations are punishable under the Explosives Act, 1884 and may result in cancellation of the distributor's agreement with the Oil Marketing Company (OMC)

2. Location and Structural Safety

The godown must be situated in a safe location maintaining prescribed separation distances from residential buildings, public roads, schools, hospitals, and other hazardous installations. Access routes must allow easy movement of emergency vehicles at all times.

Structural Requirements

Construction

  • Single-story, non-combustible structure — no wood, thatch, or combustible cladding
  • Adequate ventilation at both high and low levels — LPG is heavier than air and settles at floor level
  • Non-sparking flooring (bitumen, heavy-duty stone) to prevent ignition if a cylinder is dropped

Safety Zone

  • A clear "Safety Zone" or "Licensed Area" must be maintained around the godown
  • Free from any unauthorized buildings, dry vegetation, or combustible material
  • Surrounding area should be free from congestion

3. Storage and Stacking Standards

  • Cylinders must be stored in an upright position with their valves protected by safety caps at all times
  • Stacks must have clear gangways (aisles) for easy inspection and rapid removal during an emergency
  • Empty and filled cylinders must be stored in separate, clearly marked sections — never mixed
  • It is strictly prohibited to stack cylinders more than two high for 14.2 kg domestic cylinders unless specifically permitted by the licence conditions
  • Overcrowding must be avoided; stacking height must remain within prescribed limits at all times

Cylinder Handling

Cylinders must be handled carefully to prevent damage to valves. They should never be rolled, dropped, or dragged. Cylinders must always be moved using hand trolleys with rubber-lined cradles to prevent metal-to-metal contact and sparking.

4. Fire Prevention and Firefighting Equipment

The godown must be a strict "No Smoking" zone. Matches, lighters, mobile phones, and unapproved electrical devices are not permitted inside. All electrical fittings — lights, switches, fans, and wiring — must be of Flame-proof (FLP) or Explosion-proof variety.

Mandatory Signage

"No Smoking" and "No Naked Flame" signs must be prominently displayed in English, Hindi, and the local language (Odia) at all entry points and within the storage area.

Firefighting Equipment Required

  • Dry Chemical Powder (DCP) fire extinguishers — primary frontline defence for Class C (gas) fires
  • Sand buckets filled with clean, dry sand — at all designated points
  • Water storage tank with a fire pump system — mandatory for higher-capacity godowns as specified by the licence

4.1 Mechanism of Water Storage Tank & Fire Pump System

In an LPG godown, the water system follows a "Cooling and Control" philosophy — it does not extinguish the fire directly; it manages the thermal environment to prevent a catastrophic explosion.

How the Water System Works

Heat Absorption — Preventing BLEVE

Water absorbs thermal energy as it turns into steam, keeping the internal pressure of surrounding LPG cylinders below the critical threshold. This prevents a BLEVE (Boiling Liquid Expanding Vapor Explosion) — the most catastrophic failure mode in LPG fires.

Water Curtain Effect

High-pressure nozzles create a thermal shield that blocks radiant heat from reaching adjacent cylinders. The water curtain also helps dilute small gas leaks below their Lower Explosive Limit (LEL).

Structural Integrity Maintenance

Continuous cooling prevents structural collapse of the godown, keeping the "envelope" intact and containing the incident.

System Components

  • Reservoir: Dedicated static water tank ensuring 1–4 hours of continuous spraying
  • Pump House: Main electric pump plus a diesel-engine driven standby pump — the standby pump is critical during power failure when the risk is highest
  • Hydrant Network: Loop piping (Ring Main) maintaining standardised pressure (usually 7 kg/cm²) for drenching
Crucial Safety Warning — Water on LPG Fires

Do not spray water directly into a large LPG flame to extinguish it. If the flame is extinguished but the gas leak continues, gas will accumulate and could lead to a massive secondary explosion. The goal of the water system is to cool the containers and allow the gas to burn out safely under controlled, cooled conditions.

4.2 Mechanism of Dry Chemical Powder (DCP) Extinguishers

DCP extinguishers are the standard frontline defence for LPG fires (Class C fires). The most common powders used are Mono-Ammonium Phosphate or Sodium Bicarbonate.

How DCP Works

  • Chemical Chain Reaction Inhibition (Main Mechanism): DCP particles interfere with free radicals (H⁺, OH⁻) in the flame, breaking the combustion chain reaction instantly — called chemical flame quenching
  • Heat Absorption: Solid particles undergo endothermic decomposition, locally reducing temperature below the ignition point
  • Smothering: The powder cloud creates a physical barrier between leaking LPG and oxygen
  • Non-Conductivity: Safe for use near electrical installations

Application Technique

  • Sweep at the base of the flame — do not aim at the top
  • For a jet fire, apply the powder to cut the flame off from the leak source
  • Approach from an upwind position
  • Use short, sweeping bursts — do not discharge entire canister at once
Critical Rule — When NOT to Use DCP

If the gas leak cannot be shut off, it is often safer to let the gas burn while cooling the surroundings with water rather than extinguishing the flame with DCP. Extinguishing the flame while gas continues to leak creates a massive unburned gas cloud — which, when reignited, causes a catastrophic explosion.

5. Operational Safety and Handling

  • Only trained and authorised personnel should enter the licensed premises
  • Every cylinder must be checked for leaks using a self-leak test or soap water solution before being accepted into the godown or dispatched for delivery
  • The "O" ring and valve seal integrity are paramount to preventing slow accumulation of gas in the godown atmosphere
  • Use of tools or practices that may cause sparks — grinding, welding, hammering on metal — is strictly prohibited within the premises
  • Leaking cylinders must be immediately isolated, moved to a designated open-air safe zone, and handled as per the emergency protocol issued by the OMC

6. Security and Access Control

The godown must be properly fenced with controlled entry and exit points. Unauthorized persons must not be allowed inside under any circumstances. Adequate supervision must be maintained whenever the godown is open for operations.

A stock register recording all cylinder movements (filled and empty) must be maintained and updated daily. This ensures the licensed storage capacity is never exceeded and provides accountability in case of inspection by PESO or district administration.

7. Signage and Emergency Preparedness

Mandatory Signage

  • Prominent "Highly Inflammable" and "No Smoking" signs in English, Hindi, and the local language (Odia)
  • Licence details and stock capacity displayed at the entrance
  • Safety instructions for workers posted at multiple points inside

Emergency Management Plan

An Emergency Management Plan (EMP) must be posted at the godown, detailing:

  • Contact information for the nearest Fire Station
  • Contact for local Police Station
  • Contact for the Oil Marketing Company (OMC) Sales Officer / Area Manager
  • Contact for PESO (Regional Office)
  • Evacuation route and assembly point

Reporting Obligations

Any accident, fire, or leakage — however minor — must be immediately reported to PESO and local authorities. Failure to report is itself a statutory violation. Staff must be trained periodically in emergency response procedures through drills.

8. Periodic Inspection and Compliance

Regular inspection and testing of cylinders, valves, and all safety equipment must be carried out as per prescribed intervals under the Gas Cylinders Rules, 2016. Authorities from PESO or district administration may conduct unannounced inspections; the distributor is required to comply with all observations and safety recommendations within the time limit specified.

🎯 Summary

LPG godown safety is not a formality — it is the primary defence against fire, explosion, and public harm. The framework under the Gas Cylinders Rules, 2016 and SMPV Rules covers every aspect from licensing and structural design to cylinder handling, fire suppression, and emergency response. Two mechanisms deserve special attention: the water system's role is to cool, not extinguish — and the DCP extinguisher's greatest risk is being used when gas is still flowing. Mastering this distinction is what separates safe godown operation from catastrophe.

Regular drills, maintained equipment, updated stock registers, and a clear emergency plan are the practical pillars on which statutory compliance rests.
