Fuel Adulteration Detection Guide

This practical guide is designed for field officers, petrol pump dealers, and enforcement teams to detect possible adulteration using visual checks, density testing, and basic field kits.

1️⃣ Common Types of Adulteration

Petrol (MS) Adulteration

  • Mixing solvents/naphtha with petrol
  • Mixing industrial solvents with MS
  • Unauthorized blending of low-grade fuels

Diesel (HSD) Adulteration

  • Mixing kerosene with diesel
  • Presence of water contamination
  • Unauthorized blending

2️⃣ Visual & Sensory Inspection Preliminary Check

✔ Colour Check

  • Petrol (MS): Light, transparent (may have slight tint as per OMC marking)
  • Diesel (HSD): Light yellow to light brown

If fuel appears cloudy, darker than usual, or has suspended particles → suspicion

✔ Smell Test

  • Petrol has a distinct sharp odour
  • Diesel has a heavier oily smell
  • Unusual solvent-like or pungent smell may indicate adulteration

⚠ Always avoid prolonged inhalation

3️⃣ Water Detection Test

Method:

  1. Apply water-finding paste to dip rod
  2. Insert into tank bottom
  3. Paste changes colour if water is present

✔ Even small water layers can be detected

✔ Record depth of water if found

4️⃣ Density Test Most Important Field Indicator

Hydrometer
Density Testing at Pump
Glass Measuring Cylinder
Professional Hydrometer Set

Equipment Required:

  • Calibrated Hydrometer
  • Thermometer
  • Glass Measuring Cylinder
  • ASTM Conversion Chart

Procedure:

  1. Draw fuel sample in measuring cylinder
  2. Insert hydrometer gently
  3. Record density at observed temperature
  4. Correct to 15°C using ASTM chart
  5. Compare with density mentioned in invoice/density register

Warning Signs:

  • Density variation beyond permissible tolerance
  • Significant deviation from supply document

5️⃣ Field Test Kits OMC / Mobile Labs

Oil Marketing Companies provide field kits for:

  • Marker detection (for SKO mixing)
  • Solvent detection
  • Colour reaction tests

✔ Useful for preliminary suspicion

✔ Laboratory confirmation required for prosecution

6️⃣ Signs of Diesel Adulteration with Kerosene

  • Lower flash point
  • Reduced viscosity
  • Density drop
  • Engine knocking complaints
  • Increased smoke

7️⃣ Signs of Petrol Adulteration

  • Reduced mileage
  • Engine misfiring
  • Hard starting
  • Spark plug deposits
  • Density variation

8️⃣ Documentation During Suspicion

If adulteration suspected:

☐ Record density and temperature
☐ Draw sealed samples (3-part sampling recommended)
☐ Prepare Panchnama
☐ Record seal numbers
☐ Send sample to authorized laboratory
☐ Mention invoice density & tank density comparison

9️⃣ Sample Drawing Procedure Best Practice

  • Draw sample from nozzle and tank
  • Use clean, dry container
  • Fill minimum required quantity
  • Seal with paper seal and wax
  • Obtain signatures of dealer & witnesses
  • Maintain chain of custody

🔟 Red Flags for Field Officers

Sudden increase in sales volume
Frequent density fluctuations
Complaints from consumers
Tank lorry irregularities
Seal tampering

📌 Important Precautions

  • Never rely only on colour
  • Temperature correction is mandatory
  • Use calibrated instruments
  • Maintain neutrality during inspection
  • Follow due procedure before seizure

📊 Density Reference (at 15°C)

Fuel Type Density Range (kg/m³) Adulteration Indicator
Petrol (MS) 715–780 Drop below 715 indicates lighter solvents
Diesel (HSD) 820–845 Drop below 820 indicates kerosene mixing
Kerosene 775–840 When mixed with diesel, lowers density

📝 Summary

This guide provides field officers and enforcement teams with practical tools to detect fuel adulteration:

  • Visual inspection – colour, smell, clarity
  • Water detection – using water-finding paste
  • Density testing – most reliable field indicator with temperature correction
  • Field kits – preliminary screening for kerosene markers
  • Documentation – proper sampling, sealing, and chain of custody for legal proceedings

Remember: Laboratory confirmation is essential for prosecution. All field tests are preliminary indicators that guide further action. Follow due process, maintain neutrality, and ensure all instruments are calibrated.
