⛽ Fuel Adulteration Detection Guide: Motor Spirit (MS) & High Speed Diesel (HSD) — Field Testing Manual

Sarat Rout February 17, 2025 12 min read Fuel Quality Control
Fuel quality inspection at a retail outlet — field testing provides preliminary screening; laboratory confirmation is mandatory for prosecution

Fuel adulteration is one of the most economically motivated offences in India's petroleum sector. The price differential between heavily taxed petrol/diesel and subsidised kerosene creates a financial incentive to mix cheaper products into higher-grade fuels — typically at levels of 10–30% by volume, where detection by consumers is difficult. This guide provides practical field-testing methods for Supply Inspectors, petroleum enforcement officers, and petrol pump dealers to identify suspected adulteration — along with the correct documentation and sampling procedure for further legal action.

1. Regulatory Standards — BIS Specifications

Governing Standards

Motor Spirit (MS / Petrol): IS 2796 / IS 17586 / IS 17021 (BS VI specification). Density range: 720–775 kg/m³ at 15°C.

High Speed Diesel (HSD): IS 1460 / IS 16531. HSD is a middle distillate with boiling range 160–400°C, comprising C₁₃–C₁₇ hydrocarbons. Density range: 820–845 kg/m³ at 15°C (PPAC / IS 1460).

PDS Kerosene (SKO): IS 1459. Density: 790–800 kg/m³ at 15°C. Mandatorily doped with blue dye to distinguish it from HSD in India. Density overlap with HSD makes purely density-based detection unreliable for low-level kerosene mixing.

Testing authority: IOC Marketing Discipline Guidelines 2024 specify that quality tests on MS/HSD must conform to the latest IS specifications as amended by the Bureau of Indian Standards.

MS (petrol) and HSD (diesel) quality at retail outlets is governed by BIS IS 2796 and IS 1460 respectively

2. Common Types of Adulteration

Petrol (MS) Adulteration

  • Mixing naphtha or light naphtha — lower cost, similar appearance but lower octane rating
  • Mixing industrial solvents — reduces octane and increases engine deposit formation
  • Mixing kerosene — less volatile, leaves residue; causes misfiring and hard starting
  • Unauthorised blending of low-grade refinery streams

Diesel (HSD) Adulteration

  • Mixing PDS kerosene (SKO) — the most common adulterant in India; subsidised price differential makes it financially attractive
  • Mixing water — increases apparent volume; causes engine damage and fuel injection corrosion
  • Mixing light diesel oil (LDO) or lubricating oil recycled streams
  • Adding high boiling solvents to increase volume
Scale of Adulteration

Research published in peer-reviewed journals indicates that the typical adulteration level in India ranges from 10% to 30% by volume. Below 10%, the financial benefit is insufficient; above 30%, the vehicle performance degradation becomes noticeable to consumers. This range is specifically designed to evade easy detection.

3. Visual & Sensory Inspection

Colour Check

  • Petrol (MS): Light, transparent liquid. OMCs add a dye marker — typically orange/red — for product identification. Visible colour change under UV spectrophotometry.
  • Diesel (HSD): Light yellow to pale amber in colour.
  • PDS Kerosene: Mandatorily doped with blue dye in India under PPAC/MoPNG guidelines to prevent misuse as an HSD adulterant. Visual detection of blue tinge in diesel is therefore a direct indicator of kerosene adulteration.
Colour Alone Is Unreliable

Colour is a preliminary indicator only. Adulterants like naphtha and light solvents are colourless and indistinguishable visually. Never rely solely on colour to confirm adulteration or clear a suspect sample.

Smell Test

  • Petrol has a distinct sharp, volatile aromatic odour
  • Diesel has a heavier, oily odour
  • A solvent-like, pungent, or unfamiliar smell may indicate adulteration with industrial solvents
  • A distinctly kerosene-like smell in diesel is suspicious
Safety Precaution

Never conduct prolonged inhalation. Brief, momentary olfactory assessment only. Keep the container at arm's length and do not insert face near the opening.

4. Filter Paper Evaporation Test (MS/Petrol)

This is one of the most reliable and simple field tests for petrol adulteration — used by Regional Forensic Science Laboratories and described in the CUTM Petroleum Testing Manual.

1

Place two drops of the suspect petrol sample on a clean, dry filter paper

2

Allow to stand for approximately 2–3 minutes at ambient temperature

3

Observe the filter paper for any residue, stain, or oily patch

Unadulterated Petrol

Pure petrol is highly volatile. The drops evaporate completely within 2–3 minutes, leaving no residue or mark on the filter paper.

Adulterated Petrol

Kerosene, diesel, or solvents are far less volatile. Adulterated petrol leaves an oily patch or yellowish stain after petrol evaporates. The size and darkness of the stain correlates roughly with adulterant percentage.

5. Water Detection Test

Water contamination is a direct indicator of either adulteration or improper storage. Water in fuel causes corrosion, microbial growth, and fuel injection system damage.

Method A — Water Finding Paste (Dip Rod)

1

Apply a thin, even layer of water-finding paste to the lower portion of the dip rod or dipstick

2

Insert the dip rod slowly to the bottom of the storage tank

3

Withdraw and observe immediately — the paste changes from its original colour (yellow) to red/purple in the presence of water

4

Record the depth of colour change — this indicates the depth of the water layer at the bottom of the tank

Method B — Visual Separation

Draw a sample into a clear glass cylinder. Water, being denser than fuel, settles at the bottom. A visible interface line between a lower clear/cloudy layer (water) and the fuel above it confirms water contamination. Cloudy or hazy fuel — especially diesel — is a strong indicator.

6. Density Test — Most Important Field Indicator

Equipment Required

  • Calibrated hydrometer (IS 7324 / ASTM D1298)
  • Thermometer (0–50°C range)
  • Glass measuring cylinder (appropriate diameter for hydrometer)
  • ASTM D1250 / IP 200 petroleum measurement tables (for temperature correction)

Procedure (ASTM D1298 — Hydrometer Method)

1

Collect a representative sample in a clean, dry glass cylinder. The cylinder must be wide enough for the hydrometer to float freely without touching the sides

2

Record the temperature of the sample using the thermometer

3

Carefully lower the hydrometer into the sample — avoid touching the walls. Allow it to settle and read the scale at the bottom of the meniscus

4

Record the observed density at the observed temperature (e.g., 835 kg/m³ at 32°C)

5

Apply temperature correction to convert to density at 15°C using ASTM D1250 tables. For every 1°C above 15°C, diesel density reduces by approximately 0.65 kg/m³

6

Compare the corrected density with the invoice density and the BIS specification range. A significant deviation from the invoice density or the BIS range is suspicious

Mandatory — Temperature Correction

Never record or compare density without temperature correction. Fuel density is highly temperature-sensitive. An uncorrected reading at field temperature is meaningless for comparison with the BIS specification (which is specified at 15°C) or the invoice density.

7. Density Reference Table (at 15°C)

Fuel / Adulterant Density at 15°C (kg/m³) BIS Standard Adulteration Indicator
Petrol / MS (BS VI) 720 – 775 IS 2796 / IS 17586 Drop below 720 suggests lighter solvents / naphtha mixing
HSD / Diesel (BS VI) 820 – 845 IS 1460 / IS 16531 Drop toward lower bound may indicate kerosene; note density test has limitations at low adulteration levels (<20% v/v)
PDS Kerosene (SKO) 790 – 800 IS 1459 Density overlaps with diesel at upper end — blue dye detection more reliable indicator
Naphtha (light) 660 – 700 — Significantly lowers petrol density below 720; highly volatile

8. Limitations of Field Tests — What Officers Must Know

Critical Scientific Finding — Density Test Limitations

Research published in the International Journal of Environmental Science and Technology (Springer) and Discover Applied Sciences (Springer Nature) has established that density alone is not a reliable indicator of diesel-kerosene adulteration at low adulteration levels (<20% v/v). "No appreciable density variation at different levels of adulteration was observed. Density was within the prescribed value even at higher adulteration." Kinematic viscosity showed greater sensitivity, but still failed at low levels. Only molecular-level analysis (GC, HPLC, HRMS) provides definitive confirmation.

What Field Tests Can and Cannot Do

Field Tests Can

Screen for gross adulteration (>20–30% v/v); detect water contamination; identify blue dye from PDS kerosene visually; detect lighter solvents through density and evaporation tests; establish grounds for sampling and referral to laboratory.

Field Tests Cannot

Confirm adulteration below ~20% v/v by density alone; quantify the exact percentage of adulterant; be used directly as evidence in court — laboratory confirmation is mandatory for prosecution.

9. OMC Field Test Kits & Marker Detection

Oil Marketing Companies (Indian Oil, BPCL, HPCL) provide field test kits to authorised inspectors and Quality Control personnel. These are designed for:

  • Solvent marker detection: A trace chemical marker is added to petrol at the refinery — the field kit detects the marker's presence or absence, identifying unmarked (likely adulterated) fuel
  • Kerosene blue dye detection: Colour reaction to confirm presence of the mandatory blue dye added to PDS kerosene in India — a positive reaction in diesel sample confirms kerosene mixing
  • Solvent detection: Reagent-based colour reaction tests for specific adulterants
Legal Position

Field kit results are preliminary screening indicators only. For any enforcement action, seizure, or prosecution, samples must be sent to an accredited laboratory (NABL or government-recognised) for confirmation by ASTM D4052 (digital densitometer), GC, or other standard method. The field test result alone is not admissible as conclusive evidence.

Laboratory analysis (GC/HPLC/HRMS) is the gold standard — only these methods can confirm adulteration at low levels (<20% v/v)

10. Signs of Adulteration — Diesel & Petrol

Signs of Diesel (HSD) Adulteration

  • Blue colour tinge — direct evidence of PDS kerosene (mandatory blue dye)
  • Lower flash point (kerosene flash point ~37°C vs diesel ~52°C min)
  • Density near lower bound of 820–845 range
  • Reduced cetane index — poorer ignition quality
  • Increased smoke and reduced exhaust opacity
  • Engine knocking and vibration complaints from consumers
  • Reduced fuel efficiency / increased fuel consumption

Signs of Petrol (MS) Adulteration

  • Oily patch on filter paper — fails the evaporation test
  • Density outside 720–775 kg/m³ range
  • Reduced octane rating — engine knocking under load
  • Hard starting, especially in cold weather
  • Spark plug deposits and choking of fuel injector nozzles (gum formation)
  • Reduced mileage / vehicle performance complaints

11. Red Flags for Field Officers

Sudden unexplained surge in sales volume — significantly above normal daily throughput without a corresponding increase in deliveries
Persistent density below invoice density — consistent readings at the lower bound or below the invoice density across multiple measurement dates
Multiple consumer complaints — misfiring, reduced mileage, hard starting from multiple customers at the same outlet
Tank lorry irregularities — seal tampering, compartment lids opened, unauthorised stops between the depot and the retail outlet, GPS route deviations
Dispensing unit seal tampering — W&M (Weights & Measures) seals on totaliser units found broken or tampered with
Nearby presence of SKO dealers — proximity to kerosene retail points or unaccounted kerosene consumption in the area
Abnormal stock variation — physical stock significantly higher or lower than computed from sales and receipts records (beyond ±0.1% for MS/HSD)

12. Documentation & Sample Drawing Procedure

When Adulteration Is Suspected — Immediate Steps

Record the density reading and tank temperature at the time of observation
Compare observed density with invoice density and BIS specification range
Note any visible colour anomaly (e.g., blue tinge, cloudiness)
Conduct filter paper test (for MS) and record result with photograph
Draw samples in triplicate using clean, dry, sealed containers — one for laboratory, one for dealer, one as spare/counter-sample
Prepare Panchnama at the spot in the presence of the dealer and at least two independent witnesses
Record seal numbers on each sample container
Obtain signatures of dealer, witnesses, and field officer on Panchnama and sample labels
Hand over one sealed sample to the dealer — obtain signed acknowledgement
Dispatch laboratory sample to accredited NABL/government lab under proper forwarding letter — maintain chain of custody record
Note invoice density from the delivery challan and compare with observed tank density in the Panchnama
Legal Requirement

Laboratory confirmation is mandatory before any prosecution. Field observations and Panchnama establish grounds for seizure and sampling. The RFSL / NABL laboratory report is the primary admissible evidence. Ensure the chain of custody from seizure site to laboratory is fully documented — any break in this chain will be exploited by defence counsel.

📝 Summary

This guide provides field officers and enforcement teams with practical, scientifically grounded tools to detect fuel adulteration in Motor Spirit and High Speed Diesel. The key methods — in increasing order of reliability — are: visual and colour check → filter paper evaporation test → water detection → density test → OMC field kit → laboratory analysis (GC/HPLC/HRMS).

Critical limitation to remember: Density testing alone cannot reliably detect kerosene adulteration below 20% v/v in diesel. Kinematic viscosity, flash point, and aniline point provide better sensitivity. Only molecular-level laboratory analysis provides definitive confirmation. All field tests are screening tools — they guide action, not replace judicial evidence.
