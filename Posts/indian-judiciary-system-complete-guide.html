Structure, Hierarchy, Powers and Constitutional Framework

The Indian Judiciary is a single integrated system of courts that administers both central and state laws. It functions as an independent and impartial branch of the government, ensuring justice and protecting the rights of citizens as enshrined in the Constitution of India.

üèõÔ∏è Supreme Court of India

Supreme Court of India
New Delhi

The Supreme Court is the apex judicial body and the highest constitutional court. It was established on January 28, 1950, two days after India became a republic.

Key Features:

  • Chief Justice of India (CJI): Head of the judiciary
  • Sanctioned Strength: 34 judges (including CJI)
  • Retirement Age: 65 years
  • Original, Appellate and Advisory Jurisdiction
  • Guardian of the Constitution - Power of judicial review
  • Court of Record - Its decisions are binding on all courts
Article 124-147 Original Jurisdiction Appellate Jurisdiction Writ Jurisdiction

üèõÔ∏è High Courts

High Courts

India has 25 High Courts with jurisdiction over one or more states. The Calcutta High Court is the oldest (established 1862).

Key Features:

  • Judges: Chief Justice + other judges
  • Retirement Age: 62 years
  • Appellate and Original Jurisdiction
  • Superintendence over subordinate courts
  • Writ jurisdiction under Article 226
Article 214-231 Writ Jurisdiction
Major High Courts
  • Calcutta High Court: 1862 (Oldest)
  • Bombay High Court: 1862
  • Madras High Court: 1862
  • Allahabad High Court: 1866 (Largest)
  • Delhi High Court: 1966

üèõÔ∏è District & Sessions Courts

District Courts

Each district has a District Court presided over by a District & Sessions Judge. These are the principal courts of original jurisdiction in civil and criminal matters.

Structure:

  • Civil Side: Civil Judge (Junior/Senior Division)
  • Criminal Side: Judicial Magistrate (First Class/Second Class)
  • Sessions Court: Tries serious criminal offences
  • Chief Judicial Magistrate (CJM)
  • Metropolitan Magistrates in metropolitan areas
Article 233-235 CrPC, 1973 CPC, 1908

Types of Jurisdiction

Original Jurisdiction

Power to hear cases directly, first instance.

Appellate Jurisdiction

Power to hear appeals from lower courts.

Advisory Jurisdiction

President can seek Supreme Court's opinion.

Writ Jurisdiction

Article 32 (SC) & 226 (HC) - Fundamental Rights.

Revisory Jurisdiction

Power to call for records from lower courts.

Supervisory Jurisdiction

High Courts supervise subordinate courts.

üìú Landmark Supreme Court Judgments

Kesavananda Bharati v. State of Kerala (1973)

Established the Basic Structure Doctrine - Parliament cannot amend fundamental structure of Constitution.

Maneka Gandhi v. Union of India (1978)

Expanded Article 21 - Right to life includes right to live with dignity.

Minerva Mills v. Union of India (1980)

Reaffirmed basic structure doctrine and limited Parliament's amendment power.

IR Coelho v. State of Tamil Nadu (2007)

Judicial review of Ninth Schedule laws post-1973.

Navtej Singh Johar v. Union of India (2018)

Decriminalized Section 377, struck down part of IPC.

Collegium System

Judges are appointed by the Collegium System (judges appointing judges) as per Supreme Court judgments, though the National Judicial Appointments Commission (NJAC) Act was struck down in 2015.

Supreme Court Collegium
  • CJI + 4 senior-most judges
High Court Collegium
  • Chief Justice + 2 senior judges

Removal of Judges (Impeachment)

Judges can be removed only on grounds of proved misbehaviour or incapacity through a difficult impeachment process requiring special majority in Parliament.

Article 124(4) Judges Inquiry Act, 1968

üéØ Conclusion

The Indian Judiciary stands as an independent and integrated system ensuring justice for all citizens. With the Supreme Court at its apex, followed by High Courts and subordinate courts, it forms a robust framework for upholding the rule of law and protecting fundamental rights.
