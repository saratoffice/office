The Indian Judiciary System: Structure, Hierarchy & Legal Framework

Sarat Rout January 22, 2015 12 min read Legal System
Supreme Court of India — apex body of the Indian Judiciary

The Indian Judiciary is a single integrated system of courts that administers both central and state laws. It functions as an independent and impartial branch of government, ensuring justice and protecting the rights of citizens as enshrined in the Constitution of India. From the apex Supreme Court in New Delhi to village-level Nyaya Panchayats, the system forms a continuous chain of accountability and appeal.

This guide explains the structure, powers, constitutional basis, appointment process, and landmark evolution of each tier of the Indian judicial hierarchy.

1. Regulatory Framework

Constitutional Foundation

The Indian Judiciary derives its authority from Parts V and VI of the Constitution of India, 1950. The Supreme Court is governed by Articles 124–147, High Courts by Articles 214–231, and District & Subordinate Courts by Articles 233–235. The entire system operates on the twin pillars of independence from the executive and impartiality in adjudication.

Key principle: The judiciary is the guardian of the Constitution and the ultimate arbiter of the rights of citizens against the State.

Supreme Court of India — established January 28, 1950, two days after the Republic

4. Supreme Court of India

Supreme Court of India
New Delhi

The Supreme Court is the apex judicial body and the highest constitutional court. Established on January 28, 1950, two days after India became a Republic, it has 34 judges including the Chief Justice of India (CJI) and serves as the final court of appeal, the guardian of the Constitution, and the interpreter of all laws.

Key Features

  • Chief Justice of India (CJI): Head of the judiciary
  • Sanctioned Strength: 34 judges (including CJI)
  • Retirement Age: 65 years
  • Original, Appellate, and Advisory Jurisdiction
  • Power of judicial review — Guardian of the Constitution
  • Court of Record — decisions binding on all subordinate courts
Articles 124–147 Original Jurisdiction Appellate Jurisdiction Writ Jurisdiction — Art. 32 Advisory Jurisdiction — Art. 143

5. High Courts

High Courts — Overview

India has 25 High Courts with jurisdiction over one or more states or Union Territories. Each is headed by a Chief Justice and exercises both original and appellate jurisdiction.

Key Features

  • Judges: Chief Justice + other judges; Retirement Age 62 years
  • Appellate and original jurisdiction in civil and criminal matters
  • Superintendence over all subordinate courts in the state
  • Writ jurisdiction under Article 226 — wider than Supreme Court's Art. 32
Articles 214–231 Writ Jurisdiction
Notable High Courts
  • Calcutta High Court: 1862 — oldest in India
  • Bombay High Court: 1862
  • Madras High Court: 1862
  • Allahabad High Court: 1866 — largest in India
  • Delhi High Court: 1966
  • Orissa High Court, Cuttack: 1948

6. District & Sessions Courts

District Courts

Each of India's 672 districts has a District Court presided over by a District & Sessions Judge — the principal courts of original civil and criminal jurisdiction below the High Court.

Structure

  • Civil Side: Civil Judge (Junior / Senior Division)
  • Criminal Side: Judicial Magistrate First Class / Second Class
  • Sessions Court: Tries serious criminal offences including those punishable with life imprisonment
  • Chief Judicial Magistrate (CJM) — heads the Magistrate tier
  • Metropolitan Magistrates in metropolitan areas
Articles 233–235 BNSS 2023 CPC 1908

8. Types of Jurisdiction

Jurisdictional Powers of Indian Courts

Original Jurisdiction

Power to hear cases at first instance, before any other court.

Appellate Jurisdiction

Power to hear appeals from decisions of lower courts.

Advisory Jurisdiction

President may seek the Supreme Court's opinion under Article 143.

Writ Jurisdiction

Article 32 (Supreme Court) & Article 226 (High Courts) — enforcement of fundamental rights.

Revisory Jurisdiction

Power to call for records from lower courts and correct errors of law.

Supervisory Jurisdiction

High Courts supervise all subordinate courts in the state under Article 227.

9. Timeline: Evolution of the Indian Judiciary

1773

Regulating Act

Establishment of the Supreme Court at Calcutta under British rule.

1862

High Courts Established

Calcutta, Bombay, and Madras High Courts established under the Indian High Courts Act.

1935

Federal Court

Federal Court of India established under the Government of India Act, 1935.

1950

Supreme Court of India

Established on January 28, 1950, replacing the Federal Court.

1973

CrPC, 1973

Code of Criminal Procedure enacted, restructuring the criminal court hierarchy.

2008

Gram Nyayalayas Act

Village-level courts established to bring justice closer to rural communities.

2023

BNSS — Bharatiya Nagarik Suraksha Sanhita

CrPC replaced by BNSS, 2023; modernising criminal procedure for the 21st century.

10. Landmark Supreme Court Judgments

Kesavananda Bharati v. State of Kerala (1973)

Established the Basic Structure Doctrine — Parliament cannot amend the fundamental structure of the Constitution.

Maneka Gandhi v. Union of India (1978)

Expanded Article 21 — the Right to Life includes the right to live with dignity; procedure must be fair, just, and reasonable.

Minerva Mills v. Union of India (1980)

Reaffirmed the basic structure doctrine and held that judicial review is a basic feature of the Constitution.

IR Coelho v. State of Tamil Nadu (2007)

Held that laws placed in the Ninth Schedule after April 24, 1973, are subject to judicial review.

Navtej Singh Johar v. Union of India (2018)

Decriminalised consensual same-sex relations among adults, striking down part of Section 377 IPC.

11. Appointment & Removal of Judges

The Collegium System

Judges are appointed through the Collegium System — a judge-led selection process evolved through three Supreme Court judgments (the "Three Judges Cases"). The National Judicial Appointments Commission (NJAC) Act, 2014 was struck down by the Supreme Court in 2015 as unconstitutional.

Supreme Court Collegium
  • CJI + 4 senior-most judges
High Court Collegium
  • Chief Justice + 2 senior judges

Removal of Judges — Impeachment

Judges can be removed only on grounds of proved misbehaviour or incapacity through an impeachment process requiring a special majority in both Houses of Parliament.

Article 124(4) Judges Inquiry Act, 1968

🎯 Conclusion

The Indian Judiciary stands as an independent, integrated system built to ensure justice for every citizen. With the Supreme Court at its apex, backed by 25 High Courts and over 20,000 subordinate courts, the system forms a robust, constitutionally anchored framework for upholding the rule of law and protecting fundamental rights across a nation of 1.4 billion people.
