The Indian Judiciary System: Structure, Hierarchy & Legal Framework

Sarat Rout January 22, 2015 12 min read Legal System
Supreme Court of India — apex body of the Indian Judiciary, established January 28, 1950

The Indian Judiciary is a single integrated system of courts that administers both central and state laws. It functions as an independent and impartial branch of government, ensuring justice and protecting the rights of citizens as enshrined in the Constitution of India. From the apex Supreme Court in New Delhi to village-level Nyaya Panchayats, the system forms a continuous chain of accountability and appeal — but one that is under significant stress, with over 52 million cases pending as of 2025.

This guide explains the structure, powers, constitutional basis, appointment process, landmark evolution, and current challenges of each tier of the Indian judicial hierarchy.

1. Regulatory Framework

Constitutional Foundation

The Indian Judiciary derives its authority from Parts V and VI of the Constitution of India, 1950. The Supreme Court is governed by Articles 124–147, High Courts by Articles 214–231, and District & Subordinate Courts by Articles 233–235. The entire system operates on the twin pillars of independence from the executive and impartiality in adjudication.

Key principle: The judiciary is the guardian of the Constitution and the ultimate arbiter of the rights of citizens against the State. All courts — from the Supreme Court to Nyaya Panchayats — form one integrated hierarchy administered under a single national framework.

The Pendency Crisis — 2025

As of 2025, India's courts face a staggering backlog. The Supreme Court alone has approximately 90,000 pending matters (Rajya Sabha, November 2025). High Courts carry around 63 lakh cases; the bulk — over 4.84 crore cases — are in District and Subordinate Courts. The NJDG estimates total pendency at 52.5 million cases (525 lakh), a 2% increase over 2024.

  • India's judge-to-population ratio: 21 judges per million (2024) — among the lowest globally; the US has 120
  • Over 180,000 cases have been pending for more than 30 years
  • 76% of India's prison population are undertrials awaiting trial
  • 773 Fast Track Special Courts (including 400 POCSO-exclusive) operational as of September 2025

4. Supreme Court of India

Supreme Court of India
New Delhi — Tilak Marg

The Supreme Court is the apex judicial body and the highest constitutional court. Established on January 28, 1950 — two days after India became a Republic — it replaced the Federal Court established under the Government of India Act, 1935. It has a sanctioned strength of 34 judges including the Chief Justice of India (CJI) and serves as the final court of appeal, the guardian of the Constitution, and the interpreter of all laws.

Key Features

  • Chief Justice of India (CJI): Head of the judiciary; administered by the Collegium system
  • Sanctioned Strength: 34 judges (including CJI) — all seats filled as of 2025
  • Retirement Age: 65 years
  • Original, Appellate, and Advisory Jurisdiction
  • Power of judicial review — Guardian of the Constitution
  • Court of Record — decisions binding on all subordinate courts throughout India
  • Special Leave Petition (SLP) under Article 136 — most common gateway to the Supreme Court
Articles 124–147 Original — Art. 131 Appellate — Art. 132–136 Writ — Art. 32 Advisory — Art. 143

5. High Courts

High Courts — Overview

India has 25 High Courts with jurisdiction over one or more states or Union Territories. Each is headed by a Chief Justice and exercises both original and appellate jurisdiction. High Courts also have supervisory authority over all subordinate courts in their territorial jurisdiction.

Key Features

  • Judges: Chief Justice + puisne judges; Retirement Age 62 years
  • Appellate and original jurisdiction in civil and criminal matters
  • Superintendence over all subordinate courts under Article 227
  • Writ jurisdiction under Article 226 — broader than Supreme Court's Art. 32 (covers any legal right, not just Fundamental Rights)
  • Between May 2014 and November 2025: 1,156 new High Court judges appointed; 819 Additional Judges made permanent
Articles 214–231 Writ Jurisdiction Article 227 Supervision
Notable High Courts
  • Calcutta High Court: 1862 — oldest in India; jurisdiction over West Bengal & Andaman and Nicobar Islands
  • Bombay High Court: 1862 — jurisdiction over Maharashtra, Goa, Dadra & Nagar Haveli
  • Madras High Court: 1862 — jurisdiction over Tamil Nadu & Puducherry
  • Allahabad High Court: 1866 — largest in India by judge strength
  • Delhi High Court: 1966
  • Orissa High Court, Cuttack: 1948
  • Telangana High Court: 2019 — newest, bifurcated from AP High Court

6. District & Sessions Courts

District Courts

Each of India's 672 districts has a District Court presided over by a District & Sessions Judge — the principal courts of original civil and criminal jurisdiction below the High Court. The District Judge (civil side) and Sessions Judge (criminal side) is the same person holding a dual designation. They are appointed by the Governor in consultation with the High Court.

Structure

  • Civil Side: Civil Judge (Junior / Senior Division) — handles civil suits, matrimonial disputes, insolvency matters
  • Criminal Side: Judicial Magistrate First Class / Second Class — trials, remands, bail
  • Sessions Court: Tries serious criminal offences punishable with imprisonment of more than 7 years, including life imprisonment and death penalty (subject to High Court confirmation)
  • Chief Judicial Magistrate (CJM): Heads the Magistrate tier; can impose sentences up to 7 years
  • Metropolitan Magistrates in metropolitan areas (population above 1 million)

8. Types of Jurisdiction

Jurisdictional Powers of Indian Courts

Original Jurisdiction

Power to hear cases at first instance. The Supreme Court has exclusive original jurisdiction in disputes between states or between a state and the Union (Article 131).

Appellate Jurisdiction

Power to hear appeals from lower courts — constitutional, civil, and criminal — under Articles 132–136. The SLP under Art. 136 is the widest gateway.

Advisory Jurisdiction

President may seek the Supreme Court's opinion on any question of law or fact of public importance under Article 143 (non-binding on the government).

Writ Jurisdiction

Article 32 (Supreme Court) — for enforcement of Fundamental Rights only. Article 226 (High Courts) — broader; any legal right may be enforced. Five writs: Habeas Corpus, Mandamus, Prohibition, Certiorari, Quo Warranto.

Revisory Jurisdiction

Power to call for records from lower courts and correct errors of jurisdiction or material irregularities. Available under Section 397 of BNSS (formerly CrPC).

Supervisory Jurisdiction

High Courts supervise all subordinate courts in the state under Article 227 — covers courts and tribunals, unlike Art. 226 writ jurisdiction.

Indian courts exercise six distinct types of jurisdiction — original, appellate, advisory, writ, revisory, and supervisory
The five constitutional writs — Habeas Corpus, Mandamus, Prohibition, Certiorari, Quo Warranto — are the primary tools for enforcing rights

9. Timeline: Evolution of the Indian Judiciary

1773

Regulating Act — Supreme Court at Calcutta

First Supreme Court in India established at Calcutta under the Regulating Act — the beginning of a formal judicial system under British rule.

1862

High Courts Established

Calcutta, Bombay, and Madras High Courts established under the Indian High Courts Act — replacing the Supreme Courts and Sadar Courts in the three Presidencies.

1935

Federal Court of India

Established under the Government of India Act, 1935 — served as the first truly federal judicial body with appellate jurisdiction over High Courts.

1950

Supreme Court of India — January 28

Established two days after the Constitution came into force, replacing the Federal Court. Chief Justice H.J. Kania presided over the inaugural sitting.

1973

CrPC 1973 & Kesavananda Bharati

Code of Criminal Procedure enacted, restructuring the criminal court hierarchy. The landmark Kesavananda Bharati judgment established the Basic Structure Doctrine.

1993

Second Judges Case — Collegium System

Supreme Court evolved the Collegium system for judicial appointments through Supreme Court Advocates-on-Record Association v. Union of India, removing executive primacy in judge selection.

2008

Gram Nyayalayas Act

Village-level courts established to bring justice to rural communities; mobile courts to reduce geographic barriers to access.

2023

Three New Criminal Codes

Bharatiya Nagarik Suraksha Sanhita (BNSS), Bharatiya Nyaya Sanhita (BNS), and Bharatiya Sakshya Adhiniyam (BSA) replaced CrPC, IPC, and Indian Evidence Act respectively — the most comprehensive criminal law reform in independent India.

2024–25

eCourts Phase III

Phase III of the eCourts project launched with ₹7,210 crore outlay over four years — targeting digitalisation of half of all courts by 2026 and potential resolution of 2 million additional cases per year.

10. Landmark Supreme Court Judgments

Kesavananda Bharati v. State of Kerala (1973) — 13-Judge Bench

Established the Basic Structure Doctrine — Parliament cannot amend the fundamental structure of the Constitution. Considered the most important judgment in Indian constitutional law.

Maneka Gandhi v. Union of India (1978)

Expanded Article 21 — the Right to Life includes the right to live with dignity; procedure for depriving life or liberty must be fair, just, and reasonable — not merely a procedure prescribed by law.

Minerva Mills v. Union of India (1980)

Reaffirmed the basic structure doctrine; held that judicial review and the balance between Fundamental Rights and Directive Principles are both basic features of the Constitution.

Vishaka v. State of Rajasthan (1997)

Laid down binding guidelines on sexual harassment at the workplace — the Vishaka Guidelines — which remained the operative law until the POSH Act, 2013 was enacted.

IR Coelho v. State of Tamil Nadu (2007) — 9-Judge Bench

Held that laws placed in the Ninth Schedule after April 24, 1973 are subject to judicial review if they damage or destroy any Fundamental Right under Articles 14, 19, or 21.

Navtej Singh Johar v. Union of India (2018) — 5-Judge Bench

Decriminalised consensual same-sex relations among adults, striking down part of Section 377 IPC. Held that the right to sexual orientation is integral to the right to dignity under Article 21.

K.S. Puttaswamy v. Union of India (2017) — 9-Judge Bench

Unanimously declared the Right to Privacy a Fundamental Right under Article 21 — a landmark judgment with sweeping implications for data protection, surveillance, and personal autonomy.

Landmark judgments have shaped India's constitutional law — from the Basic Structure Doctrine (1973) to the Right to Privacy (2017)
India's Constitution Bench tradition — five, seven, or nine judges decide questions of constitutional importance

11. Appointment & Removal of Judges

The Collegium System

Judges of the Supreme Court and High Courts are appointed through the Collegium System — a judge-led selection process evolved through three Supreme Court judgments (the "Three Judges Cases": 1982, 1993, 1998). The National Judicial Appointments Commission (NJAC) Act, 2014 was struck down by the Supreme Court in 2015 as unconstitutional for violating judicial independence.

Supreme Court Collegium
  • CJI + 4 senior-most judges
  • Recommends appointments & transfers
High Court Collegium
  • Chief Justice + 2 senior-most judges
  • Recommends to Supreme Court Collegium

Removal of Judges — Impeachment

Judges can be removed only on grounds of proved misbehaviour or incapacity through an impeachment process requiring a special majority in both Houses of Parliament. No Supreme Court judge has ever been successfully impeached in India's history. The process under the Judges Inquiry Act, 1968 requires a motion to be admitted and referred to an inquiry committee before a vote.

Special Leave Petition (SLP) — discretionary gateway to Supreme Court from any court or tribunal

13. Current Challenges & Reforms

Key Reform Initiatives (2024–2025)

  • eCourts Phase III (2024–2028): ₹7,210 crore project to digitise 50% of all courts by 2026 using AI-assisted case management; National Judicial Data Grid (NJDG) tracks all pending cases in real time
  • Fast Track Special Courts (FTSCs): 773 courts operational as of September 2025 — 400 exclusively for POCSO offences; 10 special courts for cases involving sitting and former MPs/MLAs
  • Three New Criminal Codes (BNSS, BNS, BSA — 2023): Introduced timelines for trials, mandatory video-recording of search and seizure, and technology-enabled evidence collection
  • Tele-Law Programme: Legal aid to rural citizens through Common Service Centres; linked to e-Lok Adalat system
  • Judge Strength: Supreme Court recommends recruiting 10,000 additional judges in lower courts; between 2014–2025, over 1,156 High Court judges appointed

🎯 Conclusion

The Indian Judiciary stands as an independent, integrated system built to ensure justice for every citizen. With the Supreme Court at its apex, backed by 25 High Courts and over 20,000 subordinate courts, the system forms a robust, constitutionally anchored framework for upholding the rule of law and protecting fundamental rights across a nation of 1.4 billion people.

Yet the judiciary faces its most pressing challenge in the form of a mounting backlog — 52.5 million pending cases as of 2025 — driven by a chronic shortage of judges, underinvestment in court infrastructure, and a rising tide of new filings. Ongoing reforms through eCourts Phase III, the new criminal codes, and Fast Track Courts offer reason for cautious optimism.
