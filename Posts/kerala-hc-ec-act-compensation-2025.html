Compensation Under the Essential Commodities Act: Procurement Price vs Controlled Price

Sarat Rout April 10, 2025 10 min read Case Law Analysis
High Court of Kerala — W.P.(C) No. 31340 of 2019 — Judgment dated 5 April 2025

When the State seizes essential commodities and the accused is ultimately acquitted, what price must the Government pay in compensation? The controlled price that applies during distribution — or the procurement price the owner actually paid? This deceptively simple question has significant financial consequences for every trader who deals in foodgrains under PDS mandates. The Kerala High Court answered it clearly in this 2025 judgment, and the answer favours the lawful trader.

2. Facts of the Case

Petitioner

Managing partner of a roller flour mill engaged in production and distribution of wheat products under PDS supply arrangements.

Date of Seizure

20 April 2007 — Raid conducted by the Taluk Supply Officer and Rationing Inspector on the petitioner's godown, on instructions from the District Collector.

Quantity Seized

2,34,610 kilograms of wheat — alleged by the authorities to be PDS wheat in illegal possession for unauthorised commercial sale.

Prosecution

Crime No. 247/2007 registered; chargesheet filed as C.C. No. 656/2007 before the Judicial First Class Magistrate Court-I, Changanacherry under Section 3(1) of the EC Act and Clause 5A of the Kerala Rationing Order, 1966.

Acquittal — 13 March 2015

After eight years of trial, the Judicial First Class Magistrate Court-I, Changanacherry passed Ext. P1 judgment dated 13.03.2015 acquitting the petitioner of all charges. The wheat had long since been disposed of by the State and could not be returned.

3. Petitioner's Claims & Procedural History

Following acquittal, the petitioner invoked Section 6C(2) of the Essential Commodities Act, 1955, which provides that where prosecution ends in acquittal and the essential commodity cannot be returned, the owner must be paid the price of the commodity as if it had been sold to the Government, along with reasonable interest from the date of seizure.

The petitioner produced invoices (Exts. P2 to P4) establishing lawful purchase of the wheat, and claimed compensation at the procurement price of ₹12 per kilogram:

₹ 28,15,320 /-
2,34,610 kg × ₹12/kg + 12% interest from 20.04.2007

Procedural History

  • Ext. P5: Representation submitted to District Collector claiming ₹28,15,320/- with 12% interest from 20.04.2007.
  • W.P.(C) No. 36483/2018: Filed when no decision was forthcoming. Disposed of with direction to the Collector to pass orders on the representation.
  • Ext. P7 Order: District Collector allowed compensation only at the controlled price — awarding ₹14,49,440/- instead of the claimed ₹28,15,320/-.
  • Present W.P.(C) No. 31340/2019: Filed challenging Ext. P7 as legally unsustainable.
Controlled Price: ₹14,49,440 (₹6.18/kg approx)
Procurement Price: ₹28,15,320 (₹12/kg)

4. Arguments Advanced

Petitioner's Counsel
  • Under Section 6C(2) read with Section 3B, the petitioner is entitled to the procurement price — not merely the controlled price.
  • The seizure itself was unlawful; the acquittal confirms the petitioner committed no offence.
  • Following acquittal, the legal mandate of Section 6C(2) is to treat the wheat as though sold to the Government — at procurement price, with interest.
  • Section 6A(5), relied on by the Collector, applies only to perishable goods or urgent sales — not to this case.
Government Pleader
  • The petitioner is entitled only to the controlled price as provided under Section 6A(5) of the Act.
  • Defended Ext. P7 order granting ₹14,49,440/- at controlled price.
  • Argued that Section 6C(2) does not override the specific provisions for price determination under Section 6A.

5. Legal Provisions Analysed

Section 6A(2) Sale of Perishable Commodities

Provides for sale of essential commodities at the controlled price where the commodity is of a perishable nature or where the Collector considers that immediate sale is necessary in the public interest. The sale proceeds are held pending the outcome of confiscation or prosecution proceedings.

Section 6A(3) & 6A(5) Return of Sale Proceeds

Mandates that if no confiscation order is passed and prosecution ends in acquittal, the sale proceeds at controlled price shall be paid to the owner. This provision applies only when goods were sold under Section 6A(2) — i.e., because they were perishable or urgent disposal was warranted.

Section 6C(2) Compensation After Acquittal — The Applicable Provision

States that where prosecution ends in acquittal and the essential commodity cannot be returned, the person concerned shall be paid the price of the commodity as if it had been sold to the Government, together with reasonable interest from the date of seizure. This is the provision the Court held to be directly applicable in this case.

Section 3B Procurement Price of Foodgrains

Governs the price at which the Government may procure foodgrains, to be fixed by the State Government with prior Central Government approval, having regard to:

  • The controlled price (if any)
  • General crop prospects
  • Consumer interests, especially of vulnerable sections
  • Recommendations of the Agricultural Prices Commission

This is the price that the Court directed to be applied under Section 6C(2).

6. Court's Findings

Why the Collector's Order Was Wrong

The respondent authorities wrongly applied Section 6A(5) to award compensation at the controlled price. Section 6A(5) applies only when goods were sold under Section 6A(2) — that is, when the seized commodity was perishable or when immediate disposal was necessary in the public interest. Neither condition was established or even alleged in this case.

The wheat was not shown to be of a perishable nature, and no urgency was cited under Section 6A(2). Accordingly, Section 6A(5) was simply inapplicable, and the Collector's reliance on it was legally unsustainable.

The applicable provision is Section 6C(2). Since the petitioner was acquitted and the wheat could not be returned, the law unambiguously mandates payment of the procurement price with reasonable interest from the date of seizure — not the controlled price.

Court held Section 6C(2) — not Section 6A(5) — governs compensation after acquittal; procurement price applies
The distinction between Section 6A(5) and Section 6C(2) carries significant financial consequence for every PDS trader

7. Final Order & Directions

Writ Petition Allowed — Ext. P7 Quashed

The Court quashed Ext. P7 order passed by the District Collector awarding compensation at the controlled price.

Directions to the District Collector:

  1. Ascertain and calculate the procurement price for the seized wheat in terms of Section 3B of the Essential Commodities Act, 1955, as applicable on the date of seizure (20.04.2007).
  2. Add reasonable interest from the date of seizure, i.e., 20 April 2007, until actual payment.
  3. Pass fresh orders within two months from the date of receipt of a copy of this judgment, in accordance with the findings of the Court.

The writ petition was allowed and disposed of accordingly.

8. Key Takeaways

The Critical Distinction

This judgment firmly establishes that the two compensation regimes under the EC Act serve different situations and cannot be conflated:

Section 6A(5)

Controlled price — applies when seized goods were sold under Section 6A(2) because they were perishable or immediate disposal was in the public interest. Sale proceeds at controlled price are returned if prosecution ends in acquittal.

Section 6C(2)

Procurement price + interest — applies when prosecution ends in acquittal and the commodity cannot be returned (for any reason). Compensation equals the price as if the commodity had been sold to the Government, plus reasonable interest from the date of seizure.

🎯 Conclusion

This judgment is an important safeguard for lawful traders against arbitrary state action. It confirms that an acquittal does not merely free the accused from criminal liability — it also triggers a substantive right to full compensation under Section 6C(2) at procurement price, not the lower controlled price. The decision will be directly relevant in any case where seized essential commodities have been disposed of and cannot be returned following acquittal.
