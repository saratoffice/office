Accurate storage capacity calculations are the foundation of sound warehouse management in the foodgrains sector. Whether planning a new godown, estimating CAP (Covered and Plinth) requirements, or computing the dry paddy equivalent for custom milling purposes, every warehouse manager, procurement officer, and civil supplies official needs a reliable figure for how many kilograms of paddy — dry or soaked — fit into one cubic foot of storage space. This technical bulletin provides those calculations, grounded in peer-reviewed bulk density data and industry practice.

1. Bulk Density of Paddy — What the Data Says

Industry & Research Benchmarks

Bulk density is the mass of a commodity per unit volume including the air spaces between grains. For paddy (rough rice), multiple reliable sources converge on the following values:

IARI (Indian Agricultural Research Institute), New Delhi — Dry seeds of Pusa Basmati varieties: 481–501 kg/m³; Pre-germinated seeds: 507–562 kg/m³.

International industry standard (IRRI / ZaccariaUSA): Rough rice = 36 lbs/ft³ = 16.33 kg/ft³ (≈ 577 kg/m³).

The practical working range for Indian paddy procurement and storage operations is 500–577 kg/m³, with a commonly used midpoint of ≈ 550 kg/m³.

2. Dry Paddy Storage Capacity per Cubic Foot (Loose)

Calculation — Dry Paddy (Fair Average Quality, Loose)

To find kilograms of dry paddy per cubic foot, divide the bulk density by the volume conversion factor:

Volume conversion: 1 m³ = 35.3147 ft³

Lower bound (481 kg/m³ — IARI Pusa Basmati dry seed):

481 kg/m³ ÷ 35.3147 ft³/m³ = 13.62 kg/ft³

Upper bound (577 kg/m³ — IRRI/industry standard 36 lbs/ft³):

577 kg/m³ ÷ 35.3147 ft³/m³ = 16.34 kg/ft³

Working midpoint (550 kg/m³ — practical warehouse average for Indian paddy varieties):

550 kg/m³ ÷ 35.3147 ft³/m³ = 15.57 kg/ft³ ≈ 15.5 kg/ft³
Standard Working Figure

For procurement and warehouse planning purposes, 15 kg per cubic foot (rounded down for conservative estimation) is the standard figure used in Indian foodgrain storage operations. The industry benchmark of 36 lbs/ft³ = 16.33 kg/ft³ confirms this range.

≈ 15 – 16 kg / ft³

3. Paddy Stored in Gunny Bags per Cubic Foot

Calculation — 50 kg Jute Gunny Bags (Standard Indian Procurement)

Step 1 — Volume occupied by one 50 kg bag (using loose bulk density):

50 kg ÷ 15.5 kg/ft³ = 3.23 ft³ per bag

Step 2 — Net paddy per cubic foot in bag storage:

50 kg ÷ 3.23 ft³ = 15.47 kg/ft³ ≈ 15 kg/ft³
Why the Figure Is Similar to Loose Storage

Bagged storage maintains approximately the same bulk density as loose paddy because the bags conform to the grain's natural packing. The slight compression during bag filling partially offsets the void space between stacked bags. The net result is approximately 15 kg of paddy per cubic foot, whether stored loose or in bags.

≈ 15 kg / ft³

4. Soaked Paddy — Dry Equivalent per Cubic Foot

Calculation — Dry Paddy Equivalent After 24-Hour Soaking

When paddy is soaked in water for 24 hours (as in parboiling operations), it absorbs water and swells significantly. The dry equivalent stored in 1 cubic foot therefore decreases. Research on paddy hydration (Peleg model studies at IARI and allied institutions) establishes the following:

Volume increase after 24-hr soaking: 25% – 30%

Scenario A — 25% volume expansion:

1 ft³ of soaked paddy ← 1 ft³ ÷ 1.25 = 0.80 ft³ of dry paddy 0.80 ft³ × 15.5 kg/ft³ = 12.4 kg dry equivalent

Scenario B — 30% volume expansion (conservative estimate):

1 ft³ of soaked paddy ← 1 ft³ ÷ 1.30 = 0.77 ft³ of dry paddy 0.77 ft³ × 15.5 kg/ft³ = 11.9 kg dry equivalent

Important — Weight vs Volume

After soaking, 1 cubic foot holds soaked paddy weighing more than 15 kg (due to absorbed water) — but the dry paddy equivalent (net grain weight before soaking) is only 11.5–12 kg. This dry equivalent figure is what matters for milling outturn and procurement accounting.

6. Practical Applications in Warehouse Operations

Godown Capacity Planning

  • A 5,000 MT capacity godown (FCI standard) with floor area of ~3,330 m² and stacking height of ~6 m contains about 2 lakh ft³ of usable space
  • At 15 kg/ft³, this corresponds to ~3,000 MT in the lower half of the stack — a common calculation checkpoint
  • OSCSC rice receiving centres average 4.10 lakh MT total capacity across 215 godowns

Transportation & Custom Milling

  • A standard truck (1,000 ft³ payload volume) can carry approximately 15 MT of dry paddy in bags
  • For parboiling: 1 MT of dry paddy requires approximately 83.3 ft³ of soaking tank volume (at 12 kg/ft³ dry equivalent)
  • Bag count per truck: ~300 bags of 50 kg; stacking in 6–8 layers
Statutory Reference

These calculations conform to IS 4333:1996 — Methods for Determination of Bulk Density of Foodgrains (Bureau of Indian Standards) and are used by FCI, CWC, State Warehousing Corporations, and OSCSC for godown capacity certification and procurement accounting.

7. Factors Affecting Bulk Density

Actual bulk density can vary from the working figures above. The following factors must be considered when making site-specific calculations:

Rice Variety

Pusa Basmati long-grain varieties: 481–501 kg/m³ (IARI). Short-grain common varieties: closer to 550–577 kg/m³.

Moisture Content

Safe storage moisture: ≤14% for paddy. Higher moisture increases grain weight but reduces safe stacking height. Bulk density measured at 14% MC (dry basis).

Husk Content

Paddy husk constitutes ~20% of grain weight. High-husk varieties have lower bulk density; low-husk thin-shelled varieties pack more densely.

Packing Method

Machine-filled and sewn bags pack more uniformly than hand-filled bags. Vibration during transit increases density by 3–5%.

Stacking Pattern

Cross-stacking (English bond pattern) provides better stability; brick-bond pattern maximises stack density. FCI recommends maximum 12–13 bag layers.

Godown Floor Loading

FCI standard godowns are designed for floor loading of 2.5 tonnes/m². Maximum stack weight per unit area must remain within this limit regardless of density calculations.

📌 Quick Reference — Standard Working Figures

For all Indian foodgrain godown planning, procurement accounting, and capacity assessment purposes, the following figures are the accepted working standards:

Dry Paddy (loose or bagged): 15 kg per cubic foot — based on a bulk density of ~550 kg/m³ and confirmed by the IRRI/industry benchmark of 36 lbs/ft³ = 16.33 kg/ft³.

Soaked Paddy dry equivalent: 12 kg per cubic foot — based on 25% volume expansion after 24-hour soaking, as established by hydration studies on Indian paddy varieties.
