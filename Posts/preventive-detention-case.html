S. Badri Narayan Patro v. State Of Orissa

Preventive Detention & Constitutional Rights under Article 22(5) of the Constitution of India

Sarat Rout February 17, 2025 12 min read Constitutional Law
Orissa High Court – Judgment by Justice V. Gopala Gowda, C.J.

S. Badri Narayan Patro v. State Of Orissa

Orissa High Court
Coram: V. Gopala Gowda, C.J.
Detention Order
11.8.2010 (Annexure-1)
Confirmation Order
23.9.2010 (Annexure-4)
Under Act
Prevention of Black Marketing Act, 1980

This Writ Petition has been filed by the Petitioner-detenu praying to quash the order of detention dated 11.8.2010 (Annexure-1) passed by the Collector & District Magistrate, Ganjam u/ss 3(1) & 3(2) of the Prevention of Black Marketing & Maintenance of Supplies of Essential Commodities Act, 1980 as well as the Order Dated 23.9.2010 (Annexure-4) passed by the State Government u/s 12(1) r/w Section 13 of the aforesaid Act confirming the order of detention passed by the Collector & District Magistrate against the Petitioner urging various legal contentions.

2. Facts of the Case

The Petitioner is the Managing Director & proprietor of Simla Silk Sortex Rico Mill & Biswanath Rice Mill located at Surada in the district of Ganjam. The aforesaid two mills deal in rice & paddy as Miller Agent-cum-Custom Miller for different Government agencies including Orissa State Civil Supplies Corporation Ltd. for delivery of common raw rice.

Timeline of Events

7.6.2010
Team of officers conducted raid in the godown & premises of both Mills. One worker produced six registers but failed to produce other required documents.
11.8.2010
Collector & District Magistrate passed order of detention u/ss 3(1) & 3(2) of the Act.
26.8.2010
Petitioner submitted representation against detention order.
23.9.2010
State Government confirmed order of detention (Annexure-4).

Findings During Raid:

  • Entries of purchase of paddy in Purchase Register – farmers contacted denied selling paddy or receiving cheques mentioned in register
  • Biswanath Rice Mill (Boiler Plant) was producing both common raw rice & common boiled rice without permission
  • Boiled paddy on drying yard & boiling tank; huge stock of boiled rice at outlet point
  • No separate registers for each mill – apparently to avoid inspection
  • Four cheques issued to farmers – not presented for encashment; farmers denied receiving them
  • No signatures of farmers on vendor receipts
  • Shortage: Q. 342.87.200 of paddy
  • Excess: 3.85.00 of common raw rice

Contravention of Clauses 4,7,9,10 & 14 of the Orissa Rice & Paddy Procurement (Levy) & Restrictions on Sale & Movement Order, 1982. Action u/s 6(A) of the Essential Commodities Act initiated & prosecution report u/s 7 filed.

3. Grounds of Attack

First ground: Representation made to State Government u/s 8 of the Act was not considered & no order of rejection communicated – violation of constitutional right under Article 22(5).

Relied on:

  • Rahmattulah v. State of Bihar, (1981) 4 SCC 559 : AIR 1981 SC 2069
  • K.M Abdullah Kunhi v. Union of India, AIR 1991 SCW 362

"Clause (5) of Article 22 by necessary implication guarantees the constitutional right to a proper consideration of the representation."

Unreported decision: Pankaj Kumar Tibrewal v. Government of Orissa, O.J.C No. 3550 of 2002 disposed of on 17.6.2002 – delay in initiating proceeding bad in law; detention order liable to be quashed.

Second ground: Allegations relate to contravention of Levy Control Order – normal proceedings could be taken; drastic power under the Act is abuse of process.

Third ground: Grounds of detention vague, indefinite, irrelevant & extraneous – no proper application of mind or subjective satisfaction by Collector.

4. Government's Stand

Learned Government Advocate justified both orders contending:

  • District Magistrate applied mind to records/materials seized at raid revealing illegalities prejudicial to maintenance of supply of essential commodities
  • Grounds set out in detail & communicated to detenu
  • State Government perused grounds & passed confirmation order under Sub-Section (4) of Section 3
  • All materials placed before Advisory Board which considered in detail & opined sufficient cause for detention
  • Representation placed before Advisory Board & carefully considered

5. Court's Analysis

With reference to the rival legal contentions, we have carefully examined the order of detention passed by the Collector & District Magistrate and the State Government.

Observation on timing: The District Magistrate passed order on 11.8.2010 – the same day stock verification was completed. There was little time left for detaining authority to apply mind to various grounds. Grounds of detention signed on 12.8.2010. Under Sub-Section (3) of Section 3, detaining authority must forthwith report to State Government together with grounds. File reveals Collector did not furnish grounds along with other papers for approval of State Government.

Reliance placed on Dhananjaya Das v. District Magistrate, (1982) 2 SCC 521 : AIR 1982 SC 1315 – grounds of detention must be in existence on date order passed; authority concerned must be satisfied about grounds on date of order; satisfaction must be clear on face of grounds.

6. Non-Consideration of Representation

Constitutional Violation Found

On 4.9.2010, representation ordered to be placed before Advisory Board. On 22.9.2010, Advisory Board opined sufficient grounds for detention. On 23.9.2010, suggestion given to reject representation as Advisory Board found it unsatisfactory. Same day, order passed to reject representation.

Finding: State Government neither applied mind nor independently considered representation but rejected it being influenced by opinion of Advisory Board.

Rahmattulah v. State of Bihar:

"The law is well settled that in case of preventive detention of a citizen, the obligation of the appropriate government is twofold: (1) to afford the detenu the opportunity to make a representation & to consider the representation which may result in release of the detenu & (ii) to constitute a Board & to communicate the representation of the detenu along with other materials to the Board to enable it to form its opinion & to obtain such opinion. The former is distinct from the latter... The non-consideration or an unreasonably belated consideration of the representation tantamounts to non-compliance of Clause (5) of Article 22."

K.M Abdullah Kunhi v. Union of India (Constitution Bench):

"It is not beyond the pale of controversy that the constitutional right to make representation under Clause (5) of Article 22 by necessary implication guarantees the constitutional right to a proper consideration of the representation. Secondly, the obligation of the Government to afford to the detenu an opportunity to make representation is distinct from the Government's obligation to refer the case of detenu along with the representation to the Advisory Board to enable it to form its opinion & send a report to the Government. It is implicit in clauses (4) & (5) of Article 22 that the Government while discharging its duty to consider the representation cannot depend upon the views of the Board on such representation. It has to consider the representation on its own without being influenced by any such view of the Board."

7. Court's Findings

  • File does not reveal independent consideration of representation by State Government
  • Notes indicate since Advisory Board found representation unsatisfactory, suggestion made to reject it
  • No reason assigned to reject representation
  • Representation sent to State Government on 2.9.2010; rejected on 23.9.2010 – no reason ascribed for delay
  • State Government did not file counter affidavit explaining delay

Conclusion: No independent consideration of representation; non-consideration & non-communication of order on representation independent of Advisory Board's opinion constitutes violation of constitutional right under Article 22 and failure to discharge statutory function under Section 8 of the Act.

8. Final Judgment

The Writ Petition is ALLOWED

  • Impugned orders Annexures-1 & 4 are quashed
  • Jail authorities directed to release the detenu-Petitioner forthwith if his detention is not required in connection with any other criminal case pending against him
  • No order as to costs
— V. Gopala Gowda, C.J.

Key Legal Principles Established

Principle Holding
Article 22(5) – Right to Representation Guarantees constitutional right to proper consideration of representation; non-consideration violates fundamental right
Government's Obligation vs. Advisory Board Government must consider representation independently, without being influenced by Advisory Board's opinion
Timely Consideration Unreasonably belated consideration tantamounts to non-compliance of Article 22(5)
Grounds of Detention Must be in existence on date of order; satisfaction must be clear on face of grounds

Conclusion

This case reaffirms the vital importance of procedural safeguards in preventive detention matters. The independent consideration of a detenu's representation by the government – separate from the Advisory Board's opinion – is not merely a statutory requirement but a constitutional mandate under Article 22(5). Any failure in this regard renders the detention order void.

The judgment also emphasizes that grounds of detention must exist and be considered on the date the order is passed, and any delay in considering representations must be reasonably explained. These principles continue to guide courts in protecting individual liberty against executive action.
