Article 22(5) guarantees the right to a proper, independent consideration of a detenu's representation — a constitutional safeguard against arbitrary executive action
The court held that the State Government cannot rely on the Advisory Board's opinion while considering a detenu's representation — the two obligations are constitutionally distinct

1. Background — Parties and Context

Parties and Statutory Framework

The Petitioner, S. Badri Narayan Patro, is the Managing Director and proprietor of Simla Silk Sortex Rico Mill and Biswanath Rice Mill located at Surada in the district of Ganjam, Odisha. Both mills operated as Miller Agent-cum-Custom Millers for different Government agencies, including the Orissa State Civil Supplies Corporation Ltd., for delivery of common raw rice.

The detention order was passed under the Prevention of Black Marketing & Maintenance of Supplies of Essential Commodities Act, 1980 — a central legislation enabling preventive detention of persons acting in a manner prejudicial to the maintenance of supplies of commodities essential to the community. The writ petition challenged both the original detention order and the State Government's confirmation order.

Statutory Provisions Invoked: Sections 3(1) and 3(2) of the Act (detention order by Collector); Section 12(1) read with Section 13 (confirmation by State Government); Section 8 (right of representation by detenu); Constitutional safeguard under Article 22(5) of the Constitution of India.

2. Facts of the Case

Raid, Findings & Events

Timeline of Events

7.6.2010

Team of officers conducted a raid in the godown and premises of both Mills. One worker produced six registers but failed to produce other required documents.

11.8.2010

Collector & District Magistrate, Ganjam passed the order of detention u/ss 3(1) & 3(2) of the Act.

26.8.2010

Petitioner-detenu submitted representation to the State Government against the detention order.

4.9.2010

Representation ordered to be placed before the Advisory Board.

22.9.2010

Advisory Board opined that there was sufficient cause for detention.

23.9.2010

State Government passed order confirming detention and rejected representation — on the same day, and solely on the basis of the Advisory Board's opinion.

Findings During Raid (7.6.2010)

  • Entries of purchase of paddy in the Purchase Register — farmers contacted denied selling paddy or receiving cheques mentioned in the register
  • Biswanath Rice Mill (Boiler Plant) was producing both common raw rice and common boiled rice without permission
  • Boiled paddy found on drying yard and boiling tank; huge stock of boiled rice at outlet point
  • No separate registers maintained for each mill — apparently to avoid inspection
  • Four cheques issued to farmers — not presented for encashment; farmers denied receiving them; no signatures of farmers on vendor receipts
  • Shortage: Q. 342.87.200 of paddy
  • Excess: 3.85.00 of common raw rice

Contravention of Clauses 4, 7, 9, 10 & 14 of the Orissa Rice & Paddy Procurement (Levy) & Restrictions on Sale & Movement Order, 1982. Action u/s 6(A) of the Essential Commodities Act initiated and prosecution report u/s 7 filed.

3. Grounds of Attack by Petitioner

Legal Contentions by Petitioner-Detenu

First Ground (Primary): Representation made to the State Government u/s 8 of the Act was not independently considered, and no order of rejection was communicated — constituting a direct violation of the constitutional right under Article 22(5).

Cases Relied Upon:

  • Rahmattulah v. State of Bihar, (1981) 4 SCC 559 : AIR 1981 SC 2069
  • K.M Abdullah Kunhi v. Union of India, AIR 1991 SCW 362 (Constitution Bench)

"Clause (5) of Article 22 by necessary implication guarantees the constitutional right to a proper consideration of the representation."

Unreported: Pankaj Kumar Tibrewal v. Government of Orissa, O.J.C No. 3550 of 2002 (17.6.2002) — delay in initiating proceeding bad in law; detention order liable to be quashed.

Second Ground: The allegations relate to contravention of the Levy Control Order — normal/ordinary proceedings could have been taken. The use of the drastic power of preventive detention constitutes an abuse of the process of law.

Third Ground: The grounds of detention are vague, indefinite, irrelevant, and extraneous — demonstrating no proper application of mind or subjective satisfaction by the Collector before passing the order.

4. Government's Stand

Learned Government Advocate's Contentions

  • The District Magistrate applied mind to all records and materials seized during the raid, which revealed illegalities prejudicial to the maintenance of supply of essential commodities
  • Grounds of detention were set out in detail and duly communicated to the detenu
  • The State Government perused all grounds and passed the confirmation order under Sub-Section (4) of Section 3
  • All materials were placed before the Advisory Board which considered them in detail and opined that there was sufficient cause for detention
  • The representation was placed before the Advisory Board which carefully considered it

5. Court's Analysis

Judicial Scrutiny of the Orders

With reference to the rival legal contentions, the Court carefully examined the order of detention passed by the Collector & District Magistrate and the order of the State Government.

Observation on Timing: The District Magistrate passed the detention order on 11.8.2010 — the same day on which stock verification was completed. There was therefore very little time left for the detaining authority to meaningfully apply its mind to the various grounds of detention. The grounds of detention were signed only on 12.8.2010. Under Sub-Section (3) of Section 3, the detaining authority is required to forthwith report to the State Government together with the grounds. The file revealed that the Collector did not furnish grounds along with the other papers for approval of the State Government.

Dhananjaya Das v. District Magistrate, (1982) 2 SCC 521 : AIR 1982 SC 1315

The grounds of detention must be in existence on the date the order is passed; the authority concerned must be satisfied about the grounds on the date of the order; the satisfaction must be clear on the face of the grounds of detention.

6. Non-Consideration of Representation — Constitutional Violation

Constitutional Violation Found

On 4.9.2010, representation was ordered to be placed before the Advisory Board. On 22.9.2010, the Advisory Board opined that there was sufficient cause for detention. On 23.9.2010, a suggestion was given to reject the representation on the sole ground that the Advisory Board found it unsatisfactory. On the same day — 23.9.2010 — the order was passed rejecting the representation.

Finding: The State Government neither applied its mind independently nor considered the representation on its own merits — it simply rejected the representation being influenced entirely by the opinion of the Advisory Board. No reason was assigned for the rejection of the representation.

Rahmattulah v. State of Bihar, (1981) 4 SCC 559 : AIR 1981 SC 2069

"The law is well settled that in case of preventive detention of a citizen, the obligation of the appropriate government is twofold: (i) to afford the detenu the opportunity to make a representation and to consider the representation which may result in release of the detenu, and (ii) to constitute a Board and to communicate the representation of the detenu along with other materials to the Board to enable it to form its opinion and to obtain such opinion. The former is distinct from the latter... The non-consideration or an unreasonably belated consideration of the representation tantamounts to non-compliance of Clause (5) of Article 22."

K.M Abdullah Kunhi v. Union of India (Constitution Bench), AIR 1991 SCW 362

"It is not beyond the pale of controversy that the constitutional right to make representation under Clause (5) of Article 22 by necessary implication guarantees the constitutional right to a proper consideration of the representation. Secondly, the obligation of the Government to afford to the detenu an opportunity to make representation is distinct from the Government's obligation to refer the case of detenu along with the representation to the Advisory Board to enable it to form its opinion and send a report to the Government. It is implicit in clauses (4) and (5) of Article 22 that the Government while discharging its duty to consider the representation cannot depend upon the views of the Board on such representation. It has to consider the representation on its own without being influenced by any such view of the Board."

7. Court's Findings

Violations Established

  • The file does not reveal any independent consideration of the representation by the State Government
  • Notes indicate that since the Advisory Board found the representation unsatisfactory, the suggestion was made to reject it — demonstrating that the State Government's decision was directly influenced by the Advisory Board's opinion
  • No reason was assigned for the rejection of the representation
  • The representation was sent to the State Government on 2.9.2010 and rejected on 23.9.2010 — delay of over 20 days with no reason ascribed
  • The State Government did not file a counter affidavit explaining the delay in considering the representation

Conclusion of the Court: There was no independent consideration of the detenu's representation. The non-consideration and non-communication of the order on representation — independent of the Advisory Board's opinion — constitutes a violation of the constitutional right under Article 22(5) and a failure to discharge the statutory function under Section 8 of the Prevention of Black Marketing & Maintenance of Supplies of Essential Commodities Act, 1980.

8. Final Judgment — Writ Petition Allowed

The Writ Petition is ALLOWED

  • The impugned orders — Annexure-1 (detention order dated 11.8.2010) and Annexure-4 (confirmation order dated 23.9.2010) — are hereby quashed
  • The Jail Authorities are directed to release the detenu-Petitioner forthwith if his detention is not required in connection with any other criminal case pending against him
  • No order as to costs

9. Key Legal Principles Established

PrincipleHolding
Article 22(5) — Right to Representation Guarantees a constitutional right to proper independent consideration of the representation; non-consideration or unreasonably belated consideration violates this fundamental right
Government's Obligation vs. Advisory Board The Government's duty to consider a representation is constitutionally distinct from its duty to refer the case to the Advisory Board — the Government must consider independently and cannot be influenced by the Board's opinion
Timely Consideration Unreasonably belated consideration of a representation tantamounts to non-compliance of Clause (5) of Article 22; delay must be explained by the State
Grounds of Detention Must be in existence on the date the order is passed; the detaining authority must be satisfied about the grounds on the date of the order; satisfaction must be clear on the face of the grounds
Timing of the Order Passing a detention order on the same day as stock verification, with grounds signed the next day, raises serious doubt about proper application of mind by the detaining authority

Conclusion

This case reaffirms the vital importance of procedural safeguards in preventive detention matters. The independent consideration of a detenu's representation by the Government — separate from and prior to the Advisory Board's opinion — is not merely a statutory requirement but a constitutional mandate under Article 22(5). Any failure in this regard renders the detention order void.

The judgment also emphasises that grounds of detention must exist and be applied to the mind of the detaining authority on the date the order is passed, and any delay in considering representations must be reasonably explained by the State. These principles continue to guide courts in protecting individual liberty against arbitrary executive action under preventive detention laws.
