Sithara Indane Gas Agency v. Union of India: District Collector Cannot Impose Penalties Under Section 7 of the EC Act

Sarat Rout April 25, 2025 12 min read Essential Commodities Act
Kerala High Court: District Collector cannot usurp judicial functions — only a criminal court can impose penalties under Section 7 of the EC Act

When the District Collector of Ernakulam ordered prosecution of an LPG distributor and simultaneously directed Indian Oil Corporation to impose a fine under Marketing Discipline Guidelines, the stage was set for a significant constitutional question: can an executive officer decide criminal liability under the Essential Commodities Act? The Kerala High Court answered with an emphatic no — and this judgment remains an essential reference for anyone dealing with EC Act enforcement.

3. Facts of the Case

Petitioners

W.P.(C) No. 12246/2012: Sithara Indane Gas Agency — an LPG distributor operating as a proprietary concern under Indian Oil Corporation.

W.P.(C) No. 13104/2012: Sri. Rajan Kadathukadavil — from whose premises the cylinders were seized.

The Complaint & Inspection

The Director of Civil Supplies received a complaint from a consumer organisation that LPG supplied under Government subsidy was being diverted for commercial purposes. A special squad under the Taluk Supply Officer, Muvattupuzha conducted a surprise inspection at Sri. Rajan's premises.

Seizure

11 LPG cylinders supplied for commercial purposes were seized. Sri. Rajan failed to produce any bill, invoice, or document supporting storage of those cylinders at his premises.

Show Cause Notice

Based on inspection reports linking the cylinders to the 1st petitioner, the District Collector issued Ext.P1 notice alleging violation of the LPG (Regulation of Supply and Distribution) Order, 2000 read with Section 3 of the EC Act, 1955.

Petitioners' Response to the Notice

The petitioners and Sri. Rajan submitted replies stating that the cylinders alleged to have been seized were commercial cylinders, not regulated under the Act, and accordingly there was no violation of the LPG Order, 2000 or the EC Act.

The District Collector, in his Counter Affidavit, maintained that LPG is an essential commodity regulated under the Act, that Sri. Rajan is not an authorised dealer, that violations were established, and that Ext.P4 was valid.

4. The Impugned Order (Ext.P4)

Ext.P4 — Order of the District Collector, Ernakulam

(Regulation of Supply & Distribution) Order, 2000, and thereby he has committed offence under Section 3 of the Essential Commodities Act, 1955, warranting penal action required under section 7, of the Essential commodities Act, 1955. Therefore sanction is hereby accorded for prosecution of Sri. Rajan Kadathukadavil, Kampanipeedika, Ochanthuruth, Vypin.

2. The licensee of M/s. Sithara Indane Gas Agency has committed offence under section 3 of the Essential Commodities Act, 1955 by conniving with Sri. Therefore sanction is also accorded for prosecution of the licensee of M/s. Sithara Indane LPG Distributors, Thoppumpady. The Indian Oil Corporation Ltd. is directed to initiate appropriate action against this dealer for imposing suitable fine provided under the Marketing Discipline Guidelines 1998 for LPG Distributorships.

5. Legal Provisions Discussed

Section 3 Power to Order

Empowers the Central and State Governments to regulate the production, supply, and distribution of essential commodities through Control Orders.

Section 7 Penalties

Prescribes penalties for contravening orders under Section 3. The power to impose penalty vests in the Court — not the District Collector. Imprisonment ranges from 3 months to 7 years, with fine.

Section 10A Cognizability

Declares that every offence punishable under the Act shall be cognizable — enabling police to arrest without a warrant.

Section 11 Cognizance by Court

No Court shall take cognizance of any offence under the Act except on a written report by a public servant, an aggrieved person, or a recognised consumer association. No Collector sanction is required.

Section 7, Essential Commodities Act, 1955 — Text

"7. Penalties.— [(1) If any person contravenes any order made under section 3,—
(a) he shall be punishable,—
(i) in the case of an order made with reference to clause (h) or clause (i)
    of sub-section (2) of that section, with imprisonment for a term which
    may extend to one year and shall also be liable to fine,
(ii) in the case of any other order, with imprisonment for a term which shall
    not be less than three months but which may extend to seven years and
    shall also be liable to fine..."

6. Key Judicial Findings

1. District Collector Has No Power to Impose Penal Consequences Under Section 7

Only a competent criminal court can decide whether an offence under the Act has been committed and impose penalties accordingly. The Collector's declaration that "penal action is warranted" and his "sanction for prosecution" both encroach on an exclusively judicial function.

2. Collector's Role Is Administrative, Not Judicial

The District Collector may assist in enforcement, investigation, or seizure — but cannot determine criminal liability. The language of Ext.P4 ("is guilty", "penal action warranted") went far beyond any administrative function.

3. Ext.P4 Quashed — Passed Without Jurisdiction

The Court set aside Ext.P4 (Ext.P6 in W.P.(C) No. 13104/2012) in its entirety on the ground that it was beyond the legal authority of the District Collector. The direction to IOC to impose a fine under Marketing Discipline Guidelines was equally without basis in this order.

4. Commercial LPG Regulation — Question Left Open

The Court expressly declined to decide whether commercial LPG falls within the regulatory framework of the Essential Commodities Act. This question — and all other contentions of the parties — was left open for decision in appropriate proceedings.

7. Court's Analysis

Reasoning of the Court

Under Section 7 of the Act, the power to impose penalty is upon the Court and not on the District Collector. Section 10A of the Act provides that every offence punishable under the Act shall be cognizable. Section 11 provides that no Court shall take cognizance of any offence punishable under the Act except on a report in writing of the facts constituting such offence made by a person who is a public servant, any person aggrieved, or any recognised consumer association.

Thus, the power to impose penalty under the Act is vested with the Court and it is not for the District Collector to decide whether a person has committed an offence under the Act warranting penal action under Section 7. No sanction of the District Collector is necessary for initiating prosecution under Section 7 of the Act.

Ext.P4 order passed by the District Collector is accordingly without jurisdiction and is set aside. The question as to whether commercial LPG is regulated under the Act and other contentions of the parties are left open.

8. Final Order

Operative Order of the Court

Ext.P4 order (Ext.P6 in W.P.(C) No. 13104 of 2012) passed by the District Collector is without jurisdiction and the same is set aside.

The question as to whether commercial LPG is regulated under the Act and other contentions of the parties are left open for consideration in appropriate proceedings.

Both writ petitions are disposed of accordingly.

9. Implications of the Judgment

Separation of Powers

Reiterates that executive authorities such as District Collectors cannot usurp judicial functions by adjudicating criminal liability under the EC Act.

Due Process in EC Act Cases

Prosecution must follow the path prescribed by Sections 10A and 11 — a written complaint or report, followed by cognizance by a competent criminal court.

LPG Distributors' Rights

Distributors facing similar Collector orders can challenge them on the ground of lack of jurisdiction under Section 7 — this judgment provides direct precedential support.

Commercial LPG — Unsettled Law

Whether commercial LPG falls under EC Act regulation remains judicially unresolved, leaving scope for further litigation at appropriate forums.

10. Key Takeaways

🎯 What This Judgment Establishes

⚖️

Only a Court can impose penalties under Section 7 of the Essential Commodities Act — not the District Collector, not any other executive authority.

📋

The Collector's role is purely administrative. He may investigate and seize, but cannot declare guilt or sanction prosecution.

🚫

No prior sanction from the Collector is required to initiate prosecution under Section 7. A written complaint or report under Section 11 suffices.

❓

Whether commercial LPG is regulated under the EC Act remains an open question — left expressly undecided by this judgment.
