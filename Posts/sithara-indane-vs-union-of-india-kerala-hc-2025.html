Sithara Indane Gas Agency v. Union of India: District Collector Cannot Impose Penalties Under Section 7 of EC Act

Legal Research Desk April 25, 2025 12 min read Essential Commodities Act
Kerala High Court: District Collector cannot usurp judicial functions under EC Act

HIGH COURT OF KERALA

Hon'ble Justice Murali Purushothaman

W.P.(C) No. 12246 of 2012 & W.P(C) No. 13104 of 2012

Sithara Indane Gas Agency & Anr. v. Union of India & Ors.

Since common issues arise for consideration in these writ petitions, they were heard together and are disposed of by this common judgment. The exhibits referred to hereinbelow shall be as obtaining in W.P.(C) No. 12246 of 2012.

üîç Facts of the Case

Petitioners

W.P.(C) No. 12246/2012: Sithara Indane Gas Agency (proprietary concern) - LPG Distributor

W.P(C) No. 13104/2012: Sri. Rajan Kadathukadavil - Possessor of seized cylinders

Incident

Based on a complaint received by the Director of Civil Supplies from a consumer organization that liquefied petroleum gas supplied under Government subsidy is being diverted for commercial purposes, a special squad under the Taluk Supply Officer, Muvattupuzha conducted surprise inspection at the premises of Sri. Rajan.

Seizure

11 numbers of LPG cylinders supplied for commercial purposes were seized, as Sri. Rajan had failed to produce any bill, invoice, or other documents supporting such storage of LPG cylinders with him.

Show Cause Notice

Based on the report that cylinders were supplied to Sri. Rajan by the 1st petitioner, the District Collector issued Ext.P1 notice alleging violation of the LPG (Regulation of Supply and Distribution) Order, 2000 and Section 3 of the Essential Commodities Act, 1955.

Petitioners' Response

The petitioners and Sri. Rajan submitted reply to the notices stating that the cylinders alleged to have been seized for which the violation has been alleged were commercial cylinders not regulated under the Act and there is no violation of Order, 2000 or the Act.

üìú The Impugned Order (Ext.P4)

ORDER

Sri. Kadathukadavil Rajan, from whose premises the LPG Cylinders have been seized, is guilty of conducting unauthorized business of selling LPGas, which is prohibited under Clause 6, of the L.P.G. (Regulation of Supply & Distribution) Order, 2000, and thereby he has committed offence under Section 3 of the Essential Commodities Act, 1955, warranting penal action required under section 7, of the Essential commodities Act, 1955. Therefore sanction is hereby accorded for prosecution of Sri. Rajan Kadathukadavil, Kampanipeedika, Ochanthuruth, Vypin.

District Collector's Stand

A Counter Affidavit is filed on behalf of the District Collector stating that LPG is an essential commodity and is regulated under the Act and that Sri. Rajan is not an authorized dealer of LPG and there is violation of provisions of the Act and the Order, 2000 and the order of the District Collector is valid.

üìö Legal Provisions Discussed

Section 3

Empowers the government to regulate the production, supply, and distribution of essential commodities.

Section 7

Prescribes penalties for contravening any orders under Section 3. Power to impose penalty is upon the Court, not on the District Collector.

Section 10A

Declares that offences under the Act are cognizable.

Section 11

Prosecution can only be initiated based on a written report by a public servant, aggrieved person, or recognized consumer association.

Section 7 of the Essential Commodities Act, 1955

"7.Penalties.- [(1) If any person contravenes any order made under section 3,-
(a) he shall be punishable,-
(i) in the case of an order made with reference to clause (h) or clause (i) of sub-section (2) of that section, with imprisonment for a term which may extend to one year and shall also be liable to fine,
(ii) in the case of any other order, with imprisonment for a term which shall not be less than three months but which may extend to seven years and shall also be liable to fine..."

‚öñÔ∏è Key Judicial Findings

1. District Collector Has No Power to Impose Penal Consequences Under Section 7

The Court clarified that only a competent criminal court can decide if an offence under the Act has been committed and impose penalties accordingly. Sanction from the District Collector is not a legal requirement to prosecute under Section 7.

2. Collector's Role is Administrative, Not Judicial

The Collector may assist in enforcement or investigation but cannot determine criminal liability or authorize prosecution as done in Ext.P4.

3. Ext.P4 (Collector's Order) Quashed for Lack of Jurisdiction

The Court set aside the Collector's order on the ground that it was beyond his legal authority.

4. Issue of Whether Commercial LPG is Regulated Under the Act Remains Open

The Court did not decide whether commercial LPG falls under the regulatory framework of the Essential Commodities Act. Parties are free to pursue this matter in appropriate forums.

üìù Court's Analysis

Under Section 7 of the Act, the power to impose penalty is upon the Court and not on the District Collector. Section 10A of the Act provides that every offence punishable under the Act shall be cognizable. Section 11 provides that no Court shall take cognizance of any offence punishable under the Act except on a report in writing of the facts constituting such offence made by a person who is a public servant or any person aggrieved or any recognised consumer association.

Thus, the power to impose penalty under the Act is vested with the Court and it is not for the District Collector to decide whether a person has committed offence under the Act warranting penal action under Section 7. No sanction of the District Collector is necessary for initiating prosecution under Section 7 of the Act.

Ext.P4 order (Ext.P6 in W.P.(C) No. 13104 of 2012) passed by the District Collector is without jurisdiction and the same is set aside. The question as to whether commercial LPG is regulated under the Act and other contentions of the parties are left open.

‚úÖ Final Order

Ext.P4 order (Ext.P6 in W.P.(C) No. 13104 of 2012) passed by the District Collector is without jurisdiction and the same is set aside.

The question as to whether commercial LPG is regulated under the Act and other contentions of the parties are left open.

üìå Implications of the Judgment

Separation of Powers

Reiterates that executive authorities (like District Collectors) cannot usurp judicial functions by deciding on criminal liability.

Due Process

Prosecution under the Essential Commodities Act must follow due legal process, including filing of a report and cognizance by a criminal court.

Open Question

Leaves open the broader legal and policy question regarding commercial LPG regulation, which may affect distributors and end users in future cases.

LPG Distributors

Distributors can challenge similar Collector orders based on lack of jurisdiction under Section 7.
