02 Background — Justice Wadhwa Committee

📜 The Justice Wadhwa Committee & PDS Reforms

The Supreme Court, in WP(C) No. 196 of 2001 (People's Union for Civil Liberties v. Union of India & Ors. — the landmark Right to Food case), passed an order on 12.07.2006 constituting a Central Vigilance Committee on Public Distribution System, headed by former Supreme Court Judge Justice D.P. Wadhwa, assisted by N.C. Saxena, the Supreme Court's Commissioner on the Right to Food.

The Committee was specifically directed to investigate the maladies affecting the proper functioning of the PDS and to suggest remedial measures — particularly the mode of appointment of dealers, commission rates payable to them, how existing vigilance committees can function better, and how transparency in allotment of food stock can be achieved.

Wadhwa Committee Finding on Odisha's PDS (2010) The Committee found that private storage agents were the principal source of diversion of PDS food grains in Odisha — noting an "unholy nexus between transporters, storage agents, fair price shop owners, Department of Food and Civil Supplies, and OSCSC officials in large-scale diversion of PDS food grains meant for the poor." It found that godown staff tore bags and pilfered 2–3 kg per bag, generating 15–20 extra bags per 100 quintals of rice for diversion.

03 The Writ Petitions

📝 The Three Writ Petitions

W.P(C) No. 5689/2012

Petitioner: Storage Agent for Cuttack Sadar Block, appointed 10.11.2008.

Prayer: Quash Annexure-2 (tender call notice No. 1838 dated 28.11.2011) and the decision to appoint transport contractors; allow petitioner to continue as storage agent by extending licence under Annexure-1.

Key Fact: Petitioner continued till 31.03.2012 but did not submit a tender for appointment as Handling and Transport Contractor.

W.P(C) No. 1309/2012

Petitioners: Licensees operating as wholesaler/storage agents of rice, wheat, and sugar under PDS.

Licence Validity: Extended till 31st March 2012.

Prayer: Further extension of licences and challenge to advertisement dated 14.12.2011 (Annexure-4) inviting tenders for transport contractors.

W.P(C) No. 1310/2012

Petitioners: Licensees operating as wholesaler/storage agents under PDS.

Licence Validity: Extended till 31st March 2012.

Prayer: Further extension of licences and challenge to advertisement dated 14.12.2011 (Annexure-4) inviting tenders for transport contractors.

The petitioners collectively argued that the tender notice was against public interest and the rights of approximately 766 storage agents throughout the State, with roughly 80,000 people directly and indirectly dependent on this system. They contended that the appointment of transport contractors would raise the cost of transportation, leading to a hike in prices of controlled commodities, and that the State had not first implemented the departmental infrastructure that Wadhwa Committee had recommended before displacing private agents.

Petitioners' Core Argument The decision to appoint transport contractors was contrary to the Wadhwa Committee recommendations, which had specifically envisaged departmental operation with requisite manpower, procurement inspectors, and equipment at every RRC-cum-DSC. Without first implementing those departmental reforms, appointing private contractors merely substituted one form of private intermediary for another, defeating the Committee's purpose.

05 Legal Principles Applied

⚖️ Legal Principles on Judicial Review of Policy Decisions

The Court applied the well-settled constitutional law on the limits of judicial review of executive policy decisions. The following landmark authorities were relied upon:

Tamil Nadu Education Deptt. v. State of Tamil Nadu, AIR 1980 SC 379 "Once the principle is found to be rational, the fact that a few freak instances of hardship may arise on either side cannot be ground to invalidate the order or the policy."
Sterling Computers Ltd. v. M & N Publications Ltd., AIR 1996 SC 51 "If the decisions have been taken in bona fide manner although not strictly following the norms laid down by the Courts, such decisions are upheld… Courts while judging the constitutional validity of executive decisions must grant certain measure of freedom of play in the joints to the executive."
Ugar Sugar Works Ltd. v. Delhi Administration, (2001) 3 SCC 635 "Courts do not ordinarily interfere with the policy decisions of the executive unless the policy can be faulted on the ground of mala fide, unreasonableness, arbitrariness or unfairness."
Krishnan Kakkanth v. Govt. of Kerala, AIR 1997 SC 128 "Unless the policy decision is demonstrably capricious or arbitrary and not informed by any reason whatsoever or it suffers from the vice of discrimination or infringes any statute or provisions of the Constitution, the policy decision cannot be struck down."
Balco Employees' Union v. Union of India, (2002) 2 SCC 333 The Supreme Court held that courts do not interfere with economic policy decisions of the Government, which are best left to executive wisdom, unless clearly arbitrary, irrational, or violative of constitutional provisions.
Federation of Railway Officers Association v. Union of India, (2003) 4 SCC 289 Reaffirmed the principle that in matters of public policy, the court's jurisdiction of review is limited to examining whether the decision-making process was fair and the decision itself was not arbitrary or unreasonable.
06 Court's Findings & Final Judgment

🔍 Court's Findings & Final Judgment

After considering the submissions of all parties, the Court made the following key findings of fact and law:

#Finding
1The petitioner in W.P(C) 5689/2012 was a storage agent of Cuttack Block who continued till 31.03.2012 but did not submit a tender for appointment as Handling and Transport Contractor when invited to do so.
2OSCSC Ltd. is fully owned by the State Government and implements Government decisions under the administrative control of the Food, Supplies and Consumer Welfare Department.
3The Hon'ble Supreme Court, on the basis of the Wadhwa Commission report, ordered the State Government to ensure doorstep delivery of food grains to ration shops.
4Commissioner-cum-Secretary's letter No. 366 dated 06.01.2011 confirmed that as per Wadhwa Commission recommendation, the private storage agency system is required to be abolished.
5The Apex Committee headed by the Chief Secretary of Odisha specifically recommended abolition of private storage agency in compliance with Wadhwa recommendations.
6The Government took a policy decision not to appoint private storage agents in the State after expiry of their extended term, and to operationalise departmental storage centres instead.
7Procurement Inspectors have been posted at RRC-cum-DSCs, and each is equipped with weighing scales, moisture meters, and other requisite infrastructure for departmental operation.
8Tender call notice (Annexure-2) was issued pursuant to the policy decision of the Government; no independent illegality or infirmity was demonstrated by petitioners.
Court's Conclusion on Policy "It is settled legal proposition that policy decision taken by the State Government or its authorities/instrumentalities is beyond the purview of judicial review unless the same is found to be arbitrary, unreasonable or in contravention of the statutory provisions or violates the rights of individual guaranteed under the Statute. We do not find any fault in the said policy on the point of unreasonableness, arbitrariness, unfairness to render the policy irrational or unconstitutional."

All Writ Petitions DISMISSED — Judgment dated 06.05.2024

  • W.P(C) No. 5689/2012: Petitioner appointed as storage agent for 2008–09, extended till March 2012. A licence granted for a specific period confers no right of renewal — particularly when a policy decision has been taken by the Government to abolish the system entirely. Petition dismissed.
  • W.P(C) No. 1309/2012: No illegality demonstrated in floating the tender notice under Annexure-4. The period of validity of the licence cannot be extended beyond 31st March 2012 in light of the policy decision. Petition dismissed.
  • W.P(C) No. 1310/2012: Same grounds as W.P(C) 1309/2012. No right to continue after expiry of licence when policy changes pursuant to Supreme Court-accepted recommendations. Petition dismissed.
  • All three writ petitions are devoid of merit and accordingly dismissed. Policy upheld as rational, bona fide, and directed toward the constitutional objective of ensuring food security for the poor.

08 Key Legal Principles Established

📌 Key Legal Principles Established

Principle Court's Holding
Judicial Review of Policy Decisions Courts do not interfere with policy decisions unless they are arbitrary, unreasonable, mala fide, or unconstitutional. The threshold is high and the petitioner bears the burden of demonstrating the vice.
Rationality of Policy Once a policy is found to be rational, instances of hardship to some individuals cannot invalidate it. A handful of adversely affected parties cannot override a policy aimed at public welfare.
Bona Fide Executive Decisions Courts grant the executive a measure of freedom in bona fide decision-making even where norms are not strictly followed, provided no fundamental right or statutory provision is violated.
No Right to Licence Renewal A licence granted for a specific period carries no inherent right of renewal. After expiry, where a policy decision changes the regulatory landscape, the licencee cannot claim a continuing right to operate.
Implementation of SC-Accepted Recommendations State Governments are bound to implement recommendations of committees constituted by and reporting to the Supreme Court, once accepted by the Apex Court. Non-implementation itself could invite contempt.
Public Interest Overrides Private Business Interest The primary objective of PDS is to ensure food security for the poor. Private business interests of storage agents cannot override the State's obligation to reform the system to eliminate diversion and corruption.

Key Takeaways

  • Wadhwa Committee's mandate: The Supreme Court's WP(C) 196/2001 (Right to Food case) directly empowered the Committee to investigate PDS corruption; its accepted recommendations carry effective statutory force on State Governments
  • Structural corruption identified: The Committee found an "unholy nexus" involving storage agents, transporters, FPS owners, and officials responsible for large-scale diversion of PDS grain in Odisha — making systemic reform essential
  • Policy decision upheld: The Court found the Government's policy to replace private storage agents with departmental storage centres and transport contractors to be rational, bona fide, and squarely within the executive's policy-making prerogative
  • Judicial review limits confirmed: Courts will not interfere with executive policy decisions implemented in good faith pursuant to Supreme Court-accepted committee recommendations merely because they adversely affect private business interests
  • No right to renewal: A licence issued for a specific period carries no vested right of renewal; once the regulatory framework changes by policy, the licence expires with its term
  • Public welfare primacy: The judgment reaffirms that in matters of food security and public distribution, the interests of the beneficiary population — the poor who depend on PDS — override the commercial interests of private intermediaries in the supply chain
