Essential Commodities (Amendment) Act, 2020 — A Structured Analysis

Legal & Policy Desk April 6, 2026 12 min read Legal Analysis
From continuous government control to trigger-based regulation — the 2020 amendment reshaped India's agricultural commodity law

The Essential Commodities (Amendment) Act, 2020 received Presidential assent on 27 September 2020 — one of three landmark farm-sector laws passed in that parliamentary session. Its central move was to shift India's commodity regulation from continuous, discretionary government intervention to a conditional, trigger-based framework under which key agricultural commodities are deregulated under normal market conditions and controlled only when prices spike past defined thresholds.

1. Core Shift Introduced by the 2020 Amendment

The Stated Objective

To remove "stringent restrictions on stock, movement and price control of agricultural foodstuffs" in order to attract private investment in agricultural marketing and infrastructure, promote market-driven pricing, and retain safeguards only for extraordinary situations.

Key dates: Lok Sabha — 15 Sep 2020 · Rajya Sabha — 22 Sep 2020 · Presidential Assent — 27 Sep 2020

Deregulated commodities
Cereals Pulses Edible Oilseeds & Oils Onions Potatoes
Fertilizers — Unaffected

Fertilizers are not covered by the 2020 deregulation. They remain subject to continuous regulatory control under the EC Act, independent of the five deregulated food commodities above.

2. Pre-2020 Regulatory Framework

Before the amendment, the government exercised wide and largely unfettered powers under Section 3 of the EC Act, 1955 — including stock limits, licensing, and movement restrictions — applied proactively at administrative discretion, without any price threshold requirement.

Government Powers

  • Impose stock limits on any trader, retailer, or processor
  • Require licensing for storage and movement
  • Restrict inter-state movement of commodities
  • Apply controls uniformly across the supply chain
  • Invoke powers at administrative discretion — no price threshold required

Problems Identified

  • Controls sometimes worsened price volatility instead of reducing it
  • Could not distinguish between speculative hoarders and genuine supply-chain participants
  • Discouraged investment in cold storage and warehousing
  • Suppressed open stock reporting, reducing market data quality
  • Obstructed development of commodity futures markets

3. Post-2020 Framework: Triggers, Thresholds & Exemptions

3.1 Extraordinary Circumstances Only

Under the amended Section 3, supply of specified food items may be regulated only under extraordinary circumstances. The Act specifies four triggers:

  • War
  • Famine
  • Grave natural calamity
  • Extraordinary price rise

3.2 Price Trigger Thresholds for Stock Limits

When a price rise qualifies as an "extraordinary" trigger, stock limits can be imposed only upon breaching the following thresholds:

100%
Horticultural Produce
e.g. Onions, Potatoes, Tomatoes
50%
Non-Perishable Food Items
e.g. Cereals, Pulses, Edible Oilseeds
Benchmark for price rise computation: compared with the previous 12-month average or the 5-year average — whichever is lower

3.3 Exemptions from Stock Limits

Even when stock limits are imposed, the following categories are exempt provided holdings remain within stipulated thresholds:

Exempt Entities

Processors — up to installed processing capacity  |  Exporters — up to export demand  |  Value-chain participants — involved in production, processing, packaging, storage, transport, or distribution

Not Exempt — PDS Stocks

Stocks held under the Public Distribution System (PDS) and Targeted PDS are explicitly excluded from these exemptions. Government orders relating to PDS continue to apply regardless of the amendment.

4. Implementation Mechanism

Stock-limit orders continue to be issued under Section 3 of the EC Act, 1955 via Gazette notifications. The post-2020 enforcement architecture includes:

  • Mandatory stock declarations by traders, wholesalers, and processors — on dedicated government portals (e.g. foodstock.dfpd.gov.in)
  • Inspections by the Inspector of Supplies and state enforcement agencies
  • Penal provisions under Sections 6 and 7 of the EC Act for violations
  • Violation of stock limits → entity must liquidate excess stock within 15 days of the notification

5. Post-2020 Enforcement Cases

Wheat Stock Limits — Multiple Rounds

2024 – 2026

The Government invoked EC Act powers multiple times to impose wheat stock limits — a classic demonstration of the post-2020 trigger-based approach in practice.

24 June 2024

First imposition under the Removal of Licensing Requirements, Stock Limits and Movement Restrictions on Specified Foodstuffs (Amendment) Order, 2024. Applicable to all states and UTs.

Sep 2024 & Dec 2024

Order revised twice — adjusting limits for traders, retailers, and processors based on evolving supply assessments.

27 May 2025

Fresh order issued, applicable until 31 March 2026. Traders and wholesalers capped at 3,000 MT. Weekly stock declaration mandated.

February 2026

Government withdrew the wheat stock limit order. Private stocks stood at approximately 81 lakh MT — about 30 lakh MT higher year-on-year. Wholesale prices declined from ₹2,970/quintal to ~₹2,852/quintal, confirming comfortable supply.

Compliance Requirement

All wheat-holding entities required to declare stock position every Friday on foodstock.dfpd.gov.in. Non-registration or violation attracted action under Sections 6 & 7 of the EC Act.

Pulses & Onion Stock Controls

2021 – 2022

Temporary stock limits were imposed on pulses and onions during price spikes in 2021–2022, consistent with the post-2020 trigger-based mechanism. These were withdrawn after market prices stabilised and supplies normalised.

Pattern Observed

Short-duration, targeted imposition during a crisis → withdrawal after stabilisation. This episodic pattern demonstrates the amendment's intended design: dormant regulation, activated only when needed.

Potato — Specific Regulation Under the Post-2020 Regime

2020 – Present

Potatoes are classified as horticultural produce, so stock limits can only be triggered when retail prices rise by ≥ 100% over the benchmark average — a significantly higher bar than for non-perishables.

2020 (Post-amendment)

  • Wholesalers — 25 MT limit
  • Retailers — 2 MT limit
  • Applied alongside onion controls
  • Withdrawn after price stabilisation

2021 – 2023

  • Temporary, localised restrictions during monsoon disruptions
  • Limits varied by state — typically 100–500 quintals for traders
  • No nationwide uniform imposition
Recent Position (2024–2026)

No major nationwide stock limits on potatoes. Annual production has stabilised above 50 million MT. Prices have remained below the 100% trigger threshold. The government has instead relied on NAFED buffer operations, the Operation Greens (TOP Scheme), and the MIEWS price monitoring portal.

7. Current Status (as of April 2026)

  • EC (Amendment) Act 2020 framework remains unchanged and in force
  • Wheat stock limits imposed in May 2025 were withdrawn in February 2026 — private stocks at 81 LMT, prices softening
  • No major nationwide limits on potatoes, pulses, or edible oils currently active
  • Weekly stock disclosure obligations continue for wheat-holding entities on the DFPD portal
  • Government continues price monitoring via the Department of Consumer Affairs dashboard
Note — Farm Laws Context

The EC Amendment 2020 was one of three interconnected farm-sector laws passed in September 2020. The other two — the Farmers' Produce Trade and Commerce Act and the Farmers' Agreement on Price Assurance Act — were repealed in November 2021 following sustained farmers' protests. The EC Amendment Act 2020 was not repealed and continues to remain in force.

Key Takeaways

🌾

Farmer Incentive

Removes fear of sudden government intervention; enables better price discovery for produce

🏗️

Private Investment

Encourages cold storage, warehousing, and supply chain modernisation without regulatory risk

🛡️

Consumer Protection

Price-triggered safety net retained for genuine crises — limits activate when prices spike sharply

⚖️

Balance

Markets free under normal conditions; government steps in only when price rise crosses defined thresholds
