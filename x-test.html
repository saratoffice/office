Shiv Kumar Sharma v. State of M.P. — FIR Quashed: EC Act & Section 409 IPC

Gwalior Bench, M.P. High Court  |  Section 482 CrPC / Section 528 BNSS 2023  |  January 13, 2026 — Key lessons for food supply administration officers

Sarat Rout April 14, 2026 14 min read Legal Analysis
Gwalior Bench, Madhya Pradesh High Court — FIR against cooperative society FPS employee quashed under Section 482 CrPC on three independently fatal legal grounds

On January 13, 2026, the Gwalior Bench of the Madhya Pradesh High Court exercised its inherent powers under Section 482 of the CrPC — now mirrored as Section 528 of the BNSS, 2023 — to quash a criminal case against a cooperative society Assistant Manager who also served as a Government Fair Price Shop (FPS) seller. The court identified three independent and fatal legal deficiencies in the FIR, each sufficient on its own to warrant quashing.

For officers managing PDS distribution and enforcement, this judgment is a direct, practical guide — it identifies precisely the procedural and legal errors that collapse otherwise meritorious prosecutions, and how to avoid them at the FIR drafting stage itself.

Shiv Kumar Sharma v. State of Madhya Pradesh

MP High Court — Gwalior Bench
Section 482 CrPC — FIR Crime No. 62/2020 Quashed

An Assistant Manager of a Cooperative Society and FPS seller was prosecuted under Section 409 IPC (criminal breach of trust by public servant) and Sections 3 & 7 of the EC Act, 1955. The High Court quashed the FIR and all consequential proceedings on three independently fatal grounds: (A) no specific Control Order cited in the FIR, (B) cooperative society employee is not a "public servant" under Section 21 IPC, and (C) vague omnibus allegations without material particulars.

1. Background — Section 482 CrPC and the Bhajan Lal Framework

Section 482 CrPC — Inherent Powers / Section 528 BNSS 2023

Section 482 of the Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973 preserves the inherent powers of the High Court to make such orders as are necessary to prevent abuse of the process of any court or to otherwise secure the ends of justice. The provision does not confer any new power — it only recognises and preserves powers which already inhere in the High Court. As the Supreme Court held in State of Karnataka v. Muniswami (AIR 1977 SC 1489), inherent jurisdiction under this provision may be exercised in three circumstances: to give effect to an order under the Code, to prevent abuse of the process of the court, and to secure the ends of justice. These powers have been carried forward, without substantive change, as Section 528 of the Bharatiya Nagarik Suraksha Sanhita (BNSS), 2023.

The jurisdiction under Section 482 is discretionary — a party approaching the High Court must do so with clean hands. Courts have repeatedly cautioned that these inherent powers must be exercised sparingly and with great caution, and should not be used to stifle genuine prosecutions or as a substitute for statutory remedies such as bail.

State of Haryana v. Bhajan Lal — AIR 1992 SC 604 (Category 1 — Applied in this case)

The Supreme Court laid down seven categories of cases where the High Court should exercise its inherent powers to quash an FIR. The Gwalior Bench applied Category 1:

"Where the allegations made in the first information report or the complaint, even if they are taken at their face value and accepted in their entirety, do not prima facie constitute any offence or make out a case against the accused."

2025–26 Jurisprudence — Evolving Two-Tier Test

The Supreme Court's evolving approach now employs a two-tier inquiry: (1) the classical Bhajan Lal test — if the FIR allegations, taken as true, do not constitute a criminal offence, quash; (2) a contextual tier examining delays, the civil nature of the dispute, and documentary evidence that directly contradicts the FIR allegations. Critically, courts cannot rely on investigation reports or look beyond the FIR itself at the quashing stage — the power to consider investigation reports belongs exclusively to the Magistrate.

2. Who Was the Petitioner? Profile and Context

Shiv Kumar Sharma — Cooperative Society Employee & FPS Seller

The petitioner was an Assistant Manager of a Cooperative Society who simultaneously served as a seller at a Government Fair Price Shop (FPS) — the last-mile PDS retail outlet through which subsidised food grains, sugar, and kerosene are distributed to beneficiary households under the National Food Security Act, 2013.

In this dual capacity, he was responsible for: collecting sale proceeds from beneficiaries, depositing them into a designated bank account, maintaining POS machine transaction records and stock registers, and distributing commodities only to entitled beneficiaries. He was placed under suspension and an FIR was subsequently registered against him.

Why the Employing Entity Is the Critical Fact

The petitioner was employed by a Cooperative Society — not directly by the State Government. This distinction — cooperative society employee vs. government employee — is the legal fact that made Section 409 IPC inapplicable, as analysed in Finding B. The source of the employee's authority determines whether the "public servant" requirement under Section 21 IPC is met.

3. Allegations in the FIR (Crime No. 62/2020)

Four Categories of Alleged Misconduct

  • Collecting sale proceeds from food grains distributed to PDS beneficiaries but not depositing them into the designated bank account — alleged misappropriation of government funds
  • Failing to maintain official records — including distribution registers, stock registers, and POS machine daily transaction logs
  • Continuing to distribute food grains unauthorizedly after being suspended from service, including operating the POS machine during the suspension period
  • Failing to return the POS machine and official documents after suspension was imposed
Provisions Invoked — Both Found Legally Inapplicable on the Facts

Section 409 IPC — Criminal breach of trust by a public servant (punishment: up to life imprisonment, or up to 10 years + fine). Sections 3 & 7 of the Essential Commodities Act, 1955 — Contravention of a Control Order (punishment: minimum 3 months up to 7 years). The court found both provisions legally inapplicable for three independent reasons.

4. Finding A — No Specific Control Order Cited (EC Act)

A

Failure to Name a Specific Control Order — Section 7 EC Act Cannot Be Invoked

The court applied the strict and well-settled requirement that governs every prosecution under Section 7 of the Essential Commodities Act, 1955:

  • The Rule: Section 7 EC Act does not create a standalone offence. It is a parasitic penal provision — it attaches only when a specific Control Order issued under Section 3 of the Act has been contravened. Without identifying that specific order, Section 7 simply cannot be triggered.
  • The Flaw in this FIR: The FIR invoked "Sections 3/7 of the EC Act" in a formulaic, general manner without identifying any specific Control Order — such as the Madhya Pradesh Public Distribution System (Control) Order or any Central Government order under Section 3 — that the petitioner had allegedly violated, or the specific clause of that Order which was breached.
  • The Result: A prosecution under Section 7 built on nothing more than a bare citation of the Act itself is legally unsustainable. Continuing such proceedings amounts to an abuse of the process of court.
Dhanraj s/o Anandrao Mohod v. State of Maharashtra
Criminal Application (APL) No. 728 of 2015 · Bombay HC (Nagpur Bench) · March 16, 2016 · Citation: 2016 ALLMR(Cri) 2208

"In absence of it being shown that there was any order made under Section 3 of the said Act that had been contravened, the proceedings for an offence punishable under Section 7 of the said Act would not be tenable. The continuation of these proceedings therefore would amount to an abuse of the process of law."
Correct Drafting — How to Cite the Control Order

Every FIR and complaint under Section 7 EC Act must: (1) identify the Control Order by full name and year (e.g., "Odisha Public Distribution System (Control) Order, 2016"); (2) cite the specific clause number violated; and (3) explain in concrete terms how the accused's conduct breached that clause. Example: "Clause 9(3) of the [State] PDS Control Order, [Year], which requires the FPS dealer to deposit daily sale proceeds within 48 hours, has been violated because the accused collected ₹ ____ from [date] to [date] and failed to deposit the same, as established by inspection report No. ____ dated ____." A general invocation of Sections 3/7 without this specificity will not survive a Section 482 petition.

5. Finding B — Cooperative Society Employee Not a "Public Servant" under Section 21 IPC

B

Section 409 IPC Cannot Apply — Cooperative Society Employees Fall Outside Section 21 IPC

Section 409 IPC is the aggravated form of criminal breach of trust — carrying punishment of up to life imprisonment or up to 10 years. Its application is restricted to persons acting in their capacity as a public servant, banker, merchant, factor, broker, attorney, or agent. The term "public servant" for this purpose carries the same meaning as in Section 21 IPC.

  • The Finding: The Court held that an employee of a Cooperative Society — and by extension a Fair Price Shop dealer deriving authority from that society — does not fall within the definition of "public servant" under Section 21 IPC.
  • The Result: Section 409 IPC could not be applied. The maximum applicable provision for the alleged breach of trust was Section 406 IPC — criminal breach of trust — carrying a maximum punishment of only 3 years.
  • Charge Flexibility: The appropriate section can be corrected at the stage of framing of charges — but an FIR built on the wrong provision risks quashing before trial can even begin.

This legal position is long-settled and has been affirmed repeatedly from the Supreme Court level downwards:

State of Maharashtra v. Laljit Rajshi Shah and Others
Supreme Court of India · AIR 2000 SC 937 : (2000) 2 SCC 699

The Supreme Court affirmed the Bombay High Court's finding that the Chairman and members of the Management Committee of a Cooperative Society in Maharashtra are not public servants within the meaning of Section 21 IPC — and therefore cannot be prosecuted for an offence under Section 409 IPC.
Brijpal Singh v. State of U.P. and Another
Allahabad High Court · Neutral Citation: 2024:AHC:61469 · Justice Arun Kumar Singh Deshwal · April 8, 2024 · 2024 LiveLaw (AB) 260

"The employee and officer of the cooperative society are not public servants as per Section 21 of I.P.C. for the purpose of offences mentioned in IPC. Therefore, they cannot be prosecuted under Section 409 IPC but definitely can be prosecuted for the breach of trust under Section 406 IPC. However, appropriate Section can be added or removed at the time of framing of charges."
Shanmugham v. State of Tamil Nadu (1997) & Rabindra Nath Bera v. State of West Bengal (2012)
Both courts held that the President and Secretary of a Cooperative Society are not public servants within the meaning of Section 21 IPC.
Section 406 vs Section 409 — The Controlling Distinction

Section 406 IPC (criminal breach of trust, maximum 3 years) applies to anyone who dishonestly misappropriates property entrusted to them. Section 409 IPC (up to life imprisonment) is reserved for public servants, bankers, merchants, factors, brokers, attorneys, and agents. A cooperative society employee or FPS dealer typically falls in neither the "public servant" nor "banker/agent" category when handling PDS food grains under a cooperative society agreement.

Exception: If an FPS is directly operated by a State Government employee (e.g., a Gram Panchayat secretary appointed as FPS agent under a government order and thus qualifying as a "public servant" under Section 21 IPC), Section 409 may apply — this must be verified with legal advice before framing charges.

6. Finding C — Vague Omnibus Allegations Without Material Particulars

C

FIR Lacks Material Particulars — Bald Assertions Unsupported by Documentary Evidence

Independent of Findings A and B, the Court found that the FIR failed to meet the minimum threshold of specificity required to constitute a triable case. The deficiencies were:

  • The FIR did not state the exact amount of money allegedly collected from beneficiaries and not deposited — no figure, no accounting period, no computation
  • It did not provide specific dates on which each alleged act of misappropriation, unauthorized distribution, or failure to return the POS machine occurred
  • The allegation of "unauthorised distribution after suspension" was a bald assertion — unsupported by any documentary evidence such as POS transaction data, beneficiary muster rolls, inspection reports, or audit findings at the FIR stage

In criminal procedure, such allegations are labelled "omnibus allegations" — broad accusations that do not disclose specific facts sufficient to make out a prima facie case. The Supreme Court in Bhajan Lal explicitly identified these as grounds for quashing under its guidelines.

What "Material Particulars" Means in Practice for PDS FIRs

A PDS misappropriation complaint must contain: (a) the exact period during which the irregularity occurred (e.g., "April 2019 to June 2019"); (b) the specific amount involved, computed from POS data and bank deposit records (e.g., "₹42,350 collected between [dates] but not deposited, per bank statement and POS records attached"); (c) the dates of each specific distribution or transaction in question; and (d) references to existing documentary evidence available before the FIR is filed — audit report No. ____, inspection report dated ____. Without these particulars, the complaint is an omnibus allegation and is vulnerable to quashing under Section 482 CrPC / Section 528 BNSS 2023.

7. Final Judgment — FIR and All Proceedings Quashed

Petition Allowed — FIR Crime No. 62/2020 and All Consequential Proceedings Quashed

The Gwalior Bench held that continuing the criminal proceedings would amount to an abuse of the process of court. The FIR — even if accepted entirely at face value — did not disclose any cognisable offence attracting either Section 409 IPC or Section 7 of the EC Act. Three independently fatal deficiencies existed simultaneously:

  • No Control Order cited — Section 7 EC Act prosecution legally untenable without specifying the Control Order and clause violated
  • Wrong penal section — cooperative society employee is not a "public servant" under Section 21 IPC; Section 409 IPC inapplicable; correct provision is Section 406 IPC (criminal breach of trust)
  • Omnibus allegations — FIR lacked specific dates, exact amounts, and documentary support required to constitute prima facie material particulars

The court applied Bhajan Lal Category 1: allegations, even if accepted entirely, did not prima facie constitute any offence against the accused.

9. Practical Implications for Food Supply Officers

For Supply Inspectors, District Food Officers, and all officers who recommend or draft FIRs for PDS irregularities — these four action points must be observed to ensure prosecutions are not quashed at the threshold:

1. Always Cite the Specific Control Order with Clause Number

When recommending an FIR for any EC Act violation, the complaint must name the Control Order in full (e.g., "Odisha Rajya PDS Control Order, [Year], Clause [No.]") and explain in concrete terms how the accused's specific act contravened that specific clause. Never use the formula "under Sections 3/7 EC Act" without this foundation — the prosecution will not be legally tenable. This is the single most commonly exploited procedural gap in EC Act FIRs across all High Courts.

2. Back Allegations with Specific Figures, Dates, and Documents

Allegations of "non-deposit of sale proceeds" or "unauthorised distribution" must be supported at the complaint stage by: exact amounts computed from POS data and bank deposit receipts; specific dates of each transaction or distribution; and references to existing documentary evidence — audit reports, inspection reports, beneficiary muster rolls — available before the FIR is filed. Bald, undated, unquantified allegations categorised as omnibus may be quashed before trial even begins, wasting the entire investigative effort.

3. Use Section 406 IPC — Not Section 409 — for Cooperative / FPS Staff

For PDS irregularities involving misappropriation by a cooperative society employee or FPS dealer (whether individual, cooperative, or private entity), the legally correct provision is Section 406 IPC (criminal breach of trust, maximum 3 years), not Section 409 IPC. Invoking Section 409 against cooperative or FPS staff has been consistently rejected by courts from the Supreme Court level downwards since 2000. The appropriate section can be corrected at the framing of charges stage — but an FIR invoking Section 409 risks quashing before the case reaches trial.

4. Apply the Three-Point Vulnerability Test Before Filing

Before any PDS FIR is submitted, check it against the three defects identified in this judgment: (a) Is a specific Control Order — with clause number — cited? (b) Is the correct IPC section used (Section 406, not 409, for cooperative/FPS employees)? (c) Does the FIR specify exact dates, amounts, and reference supporting documents? A complaint that passes all three tests is substantially harder to quash under Section 482 CrPC / Section 528 BNSS 2023, and much more likely to result in a conviction.

The Broader Lesson

This judgment does not suggest the petitioner's alleged conduct was not serious. Failure to deposit PDS proceeds, unauthorised distribution after suspension, and non-return of a government POS machine are genuine integrity failures. The lesson is that a legitimately meritorious prosecution can be quashed on procedural and legal grounds alone. Correct law + correct procedure + specific documentary evidence = a sustainable prosecution.

⚖️ Conclusion

The Shiv Kumar Sharma judgment from the Gwalior Bench of the Madhya Pradesh High Court (January 13, 2026) is a clear, three-pronged reminder of the minimum legal prerequisites for prosecuting PDS irregularities under the EC Act and IPC. Each of the three grounds — no Control Order citation, wrong penal section for the accused's legal status, and absence of material particulars — was independently sufficient to quash the FIR. Together, they represent the threshold every enforcement action must cross.

The legal position on cooperative society employees not qualifying as "public servants" under Section 21 IPC has been settled since the Supreme Court's judgment in Laljit Rajshi Shah (SC 2000) and reaffirmed by the Allahabad High Court in Brijpal Singh (2024). The Control Order citation requirement has been equally clear since Dhanraj v. Maharashtra (Bombay HC 2016). This judgment confirms that both principles operate with equal force in Madhya Pradesh — and that failure on any one of these three grounds will lead to quashing under Section 482 CrPC / Section 528 BNSS 2023.
