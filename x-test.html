Petroleum Storage & Transport Rules

Sarat Rout April 4, 2026 8 min read 1,250 views

Under the Petroleum Act, 1934 and Petroleum Rules, 2002 (as amended), storage, transport, and handling of petroleum is strictly governed by the Chief Controller of Explosives — PESO. Petroleum is classified into three hazard classes by flash point, each carrying distinct container requirements, air-space rules, licence thresholds, and safety obligations. Applies across India; special exemptions exist for Defence Forces.

Class-wise Petroleum Rules

Class A — Highly Inflammable

e.g. Petrol, Naphtha, Aviation Spirit
Flash Point < 23°C
Container requirements

Material

Tinned, galvanised, or rust-proof steel / iron — approved by the Chief Controller of Explosives. Glass bottles permitted only up to 2.5 L (laboratory use, approved).

Design & sealing

Airtight screw plugs or caps; leak-proof and durable construction; must always be in good operational condition.

Air space

Minimum 5% air space must be maintained inside every container.

Labelling

Must clearly read: "Petrol / Motor Spirit — Highly Inflammable"

Licence exemptions

No licence required for quantities ≤ 30 litres (not for sale). Permitted storage: glass / stoneware ≤ 1 L; metal containers ≤ 25 L.

Motor vehicle exemption

Fuel already in a vehicle tank → no licence. Own-use reserve up to 100 L allowed. Quantities above 30 L must be kept in an isolated location.

Defence exemption

Container rules under this class do not apply to containers owned by the Defence Forces of India.

Class B — Moderately Inflammable

e.g. Kerosene, Diesel-range products
Flash Point 23°C – 65°C
Container requirements

Material

Must be made of approved steel or iron.

Air space

Minimum 5% air space required.

Delivery rules

Petroleum may only be dispatched to a licence holder or authorised agent, a port authority / railway administration, or any person legally authorised to receive it without a licence.

Without-licence delivery permitted if quantity ≤ 15,000 L in airtight approved containers. The receiver must dispose of / use it immediately. If quantity exceeds 2,500 L, a dry-chemical fire extinguisher must be present on-site.

Kerosene decantation

Allowed for licence holders. Must transfer into containers ≤ 2,500 L immediately after delivery.

Licence exemption

No licence needed if total quantity ≤ 2,500 L and no single container exceeds 1,000 L.

Exemptions

Defence Forces of India; kerosene supplied for licensed decantation purposes.

Class C — Less Volatile Oils

e.g. Furnace Oil, Lubricating Oil, High-speed Diesel (above 65°C)
Flash Point > 65°C

Material

Must be approved steel or iron containers.

Air space

Minimum 3% air space — relaxed owing to lower volatility.

Licence exemption: No licence required if total stored / transported quantity ≤ 45,000 L, provided storage is in approved containers as per the rules.

Defence exemption

Rules do not apply to containers owned by the Defence Forces of India.

General restriction on all classes: Petroleum may not be delivered arbitrarily. The licence type, permitted quantity, and all safety conditions must be satisfied. The sender bears responsibility for safe handling and legal compliance.

At a Glance

Key thresholds compared across all three classes

Class A

Strictest rules · highly inflammable · max container 300 L · licence-free ≤ 30 L

Class B

Moderate controls · bulk transport regulated · licence-free storage ≤ 2,500 L

Class C

Relaxed rules · low volatility · licence-free up to 45,000 L

Statutory References

These rules derive from the Petroleum Act, 1934 (Sections 4, 5, 6, 8, 11) and the Petroleum Rules, 2002 (Chapters II, III, V, VI). The Chief Controller of Explosives has powers to enforce conditions, inspect premises, and grant exemptions.

Non-compliance attracts penalties — including imprisonment and fines — under Section 10 of the Petroleum Act. Always verify the latest amendments through PESO (Petroleum and Explosives Safety Organisation) official notifications.
