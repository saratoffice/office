The Schedule

The Schedule attached to the Act lists the commodities that are declared as essential commodities under this law.

Introduction

The Constitution of India originally had 395 articles, 22 parts, and 8 schedules when it was adopted in 1950. Over the years, through amendments, it has grown to 448 articles, 25 parts, 12 schedules, and 104 amendments.

Article 246 of the Constitution deals with how law-making powers are divided between the Union Government and the State Governments. It refers directly to the three lists found in the 7th Schedule.

The 7th Schedule — Three Lists

1. Union List (List I)

  • Only the Central (Union) Government can make laws on subjects in this list.
  • These are matters of national importance — defence, foreign affairs, banking, atomic energy, etc.
  • Originally contained 100 items (amended over time).

2. State List (List II)

  • Only State Governments can make laws on subjects in this list.
  • These are matters of local importance — police, public health, agriculture, local government, etc.
  • Originally contained 61 items (reduced through amendments).

3. Concurrent List (List III)

  • Both the Central and State Governments can legislate on these subjects.
  • If there is a conflict, the Central law prevails.
  • Contains 52 items — education, marriage, trade unions, criminal law, etc.

Entry No. 33 of the Concurrent List is directly relevant to the Essential Commodities Act.

Entry No. 33 of the Concurrent List

Trade and commerce in, and the production, supply and distribution of —

  • (a) products of any industry where Parliament has declared Central control to be in the public interest, and imported goods of the same kind;
  • (b) foodstuffs, including edible oilseeds and oils;
  • (c) cattle fodder, including oilcakes and concentrates;
  • (d) raw cotton, whether ginned or unginned, and cotton seed; and
  • (e) raw jute.

Why Was This Act Needed?

In 1955, India was facing a serious food shortage and was dependent on imports — for example, wheat supplied by the United States — to feed its population. During festival seasons, prices would rise sharply as traders deliberately limited supply to push up prices — a practice known as creating artificial scarcity.

To stop this exploitation and ensure that essential goods like pulses and foodgrains remained available at fair prices, the government enacted the Essential Commodities Act, 1955.

Historical Background

The roots of this Act go back to 1939, when the British Government of India enacted rules under the Defence of India Act to regulate essential goods during World War II (1939–1945). After the War, the Essential Supplies (Temporary Powers) Ordinance, 1946 was introduced, which was later converted into a full Act in 1946.

After Independence in 1947, the Act was extended twice — by resolutions passed in 1948 and 1949. Finally, in 1955, Parliament passed the Essential Commodities Act as a permanent law, replacing all the temporary earlier legislation. It came into force on 1 April 1955.

Scope of the Act

This Act extends to the whole of India. It was enacted to ensure that essential goods remain available to ordinary consumers and to protect them from exploitation by traders who may try to profit by creating shortages.

Main Objectives

  • To prevent hoarding and black marketing of essential foodstuffs and maintain their adequate supply.
  • To ensure equitable distribution — meaning that essential goods must be accessible to everyone, especially the poor, at fair prices.

What Happens When a Commodity Is Declared Essential?

Once a commodity is declared essential under the Act, the government gets the power to:

  • Regulate its production, distribution, and supply.
  • Impose stock limits (how much stock a trader can keep), price controls, and distribution restrictions.
  • Issue orders to prevent hoarding and black marketing of those goods.

Structure of the Act

The Act has 16 main sections and 1 Schedule. The sections provide the legal framework for issuing control orders, while the Schedule lists the commodities subject to regulation — such as food grains, oils, medicines, and fuels.

Section 1 — Short Title and Extent

(1) This Act is called the Essential Commodities Act, 1955.

(2) It extends to the whole of India.

Section 2 — Definitions

(a) Collector: The main district officer in charge. This also includes Additional Collectors, Sub-Divisional Officers, or any other officer authorised by the Collector to exercise the same powers under this law.

(b) Food-crops: The Act does not give a specific definition, but the term covers crops meant for both humans and animals. For example, sugarcane is considered a foodstuff, but tea is not.

(c) Notified order: An order that has been officially published in the Government's Official Gazette (the official publication of government notifications).

(d) State Government: In the case of a Union Territory, this means the administrator of that territory.

(e) Sugar: This includes any sugar with more than 90% sucrose content (including sugar candy), khandasari sugar, bura sugar, crushed sugar, powdered sugar, and raw sugar produced in a vacuum pan factory.

Key Court Decisions on "Foodstuff"

  1. Satpal Gupta v. State of Haryana: Cattle and poultry foods are included within the meaning of "foodstuff." So the term covers food for both humans and animals.
  2. State of Bombay v. Virkumar Gulab Chand Shah: Turmeric falls within the scope of foodstuff. The Court held that foodstuff includes raw materials, things used in the process of preparing food, and things used in the preparation itself.
  3. S. Samuel, M.D., Harrisons v. Union of India: Tea is not a foodstuff. The Court decided that tea is merely a stimulant — it is neither used in the preparation of food nor does it have nutritional value.

Section 2A — Essential Commodities

(1) What is an essential commodity? An essential commodity is any product listed in the Schedule attached to this Act. Currently, the Schedule specifies seven categories:

  1. Drugs — medicines and pharmaceutical products as defined under the Drugs and Cosmetics Act, 1940.
  2. Fertilizers — inorganic, organic, and mixed fertilizers used in agriculture.
  3. Foodstuffs — especially edible oilseeds, edible oils, and other food essentials.
  4. Hank yarn made wholly from cotton (important for the handloom and textile industries).
  5. Petroleum and its products — petrol, diesel, kerosene, LPG, etc.
  6. Jute — in raw or textile form, used in packaging and other industries.
  7. Seeds:
    1. Seeds of food crops, fruits, and vegetables;
    2. Seeds of cattle fodder; and
    3. Jute seeds.

(2) Changes to the list: The Central Government can add or remove any commodity from this list if it considers it necessary in the public interest. Such changes must be made after consulting State Governments.

(3) Temporary declaration: The government can also declare any commodity as essential for a period of up to six months, extendable if required. The reason must be stated in an official notification.

(4) & (5) Parliament's oversight: Any changes to the Schedule must be placed before both Houses of Parliament after they are made.

Section 3 — Powers to Control Production, Supply and Distribution

This is the heart of the Act. It gives the government broad powers to regulate or control the production, supply, and distribution of essential goods — to prevent hoarding, black marketing, and price rises, and to ensure fair distribution during shortages or emergencies.

Section 3(1) — Central Government's Power to Regulate or Prohibit

If the Central Government believes it is necessary to ensure a steady supply of essential commodities, to make them available at fair prices, or to secure supplies for the defence of India (such as during war or military operations), it can issue orders to regulate or ban the production, supply, distribution, and trade of those essential goods.

Section 3(1A) — Regulation of Foodstuffs in Extraordinary Circumstances

This provision was added by the Essential Commodities (Amendment) Act, 2020 (effective 5 June 2020). Under this amendment, the government can regulate the supply of certain foodstuffs — cereals, pulses, potatoes, onions, edible oilseeds, and oils — only under special situations:

  • War
  • Famine
  • Major price rises
  • Serious natural disasters

Stock limits can only be imposed if retail prices rise by:

  • 100% or more for horticultural (perishable) produce; or
  • 50% or more for non-perishable agricultural foodstuffs.

Exceptions: Stock limits do not apply to processors or value-chain participants (those involved in processing, packaging, storage, transport or distribution) if their stock does not exceed their installed processing capacity or export demand. They also do not apply to orders made under the Public Distribution System (PDS).

Section 3(2) — Specific Powers the Government May Use

In addition to the general powers under Section 3(1), the Central Government can make specific rules about essential commodities. These include:

(a) Regulating production: Requiring licenses or permits for producing or manufacturing essential commodities.

(b) Using arable (unused) land for farming: The government can require that unused land be used to grow food crops — to increase cultivation of specific food crops.

(c) Controlling prices: Setting maximum prices at which essential goods can be bought or sold, to keep prices fair.

(d) Regulating storage and distribution: Controlling how goods are stored, transported, sold, and consumed — this may include requiring permits or licenses for these activities.

(e) Preventing hoarding: Stopping people or businesses from deliberately withholding essential goods from the market to create shortages and drive up prices.

(f) Compulsory sale to the government: Requiring anyone holding stock of an essential commodity to sell part or all of it to the government or government-controlled entities, at specified prices.

(g) Regulating harmful transactions: Restricting or stopping business activities involving foodstuffs or essential commodities if those activities could harm the public.

(h) Collecting data and statistics: Requiring businesses to provide data on production, sale, and distribution of essential goods — to help the government regulate them effectively.

(i)

  1. Record-keeping and inspections: Requiring businesses to maintain accounts and records, and allowing inspectors to examine them. Specific information must be provided on demand.
  2. Issuing licenses and permits: Issuing official licenses, permits, or other documents for businesses, with applicable fees and security deposits. Deposits may be forfeited if conditions are violated.

(j) Search and seizure:

  1. Authorised officers may enter and search premises, vehicles, ships, or animals if a violation of the rules is suspected.
  2. If illegal goods are found, the officer can seize them.
  3. Books, accounts, or relevant documents may also be seized. The business owner is entitled to take copies during inspection.

Section 3(3) — Price Payments for Essential Commodities

When someone sells an essential commodity under a government order, the price they receive is determined as follows:

  1. Agreed price: If both parties agree on a price (within controlled price rules), that agreed price applies.
  2. Controlled price: If no agreement is reached, the government-fixed controlled price (if one exists) applies.
  3. Market price: If no controlled price has been set, the prevailing market rate at the time of sale applies.

Section 3(3A) — Special Provisions for Foodstuffs

  1. If the Central Government believes it is necessary to control rising prices or prevent hoarding of food in a particular area, it can issue a notification in the Official Gazette fixing the price at which that food will be sold in that area — even if this overrides earlier rules.
  2. Such a notification is valid for up to three months.
  3. The price charged for the food after such a notification will be: the agreed price (if consistent with any controlled price); failing that, the controlled price; and failing that, the average market rate in that area for the three months before the notification was issued.
  4. The average market rate will be determined by a government-appointed officer and will be final and not subject to challenge in court.

Section 3(3B) — Compensation When No Price Notification Exists

If someone is ordered to sell foodgrains, edible oilseeds, or edible oils to the Central or State Government (or its agents or corporations) and no price notification exists (or it has expired), they will be paid a procurement price. This price is set by the State Government with prior approval from the Central Government, and is based on:

  1. Any existing controlled price for that commodity.
  2. The overall crop prospects (expected harvest).
  3. The need to ensure the food is available to consumers — especially vulnerable groups — at reasonable prices.
  4. Recommendations from the Agricultural Prices Commission.

Section 3(3C) — Price Determination for Sugar

When a sugar producer is directed by the government to sell sugar, the price is decided by the Central Government, taking into account:

  1. The cost of producing sugar — including the cost of converting sugarcane and transporting it to the factory.
  2. A reasonable profit or return on investment for the sugar business.

The Central Government may set different prices for different areas, factories, or types of sugar.

Provisional price for earlier seasons: If the price for sugar produced up to the 2008-09 season was fixed provisionally, the final price will be determined under the rules that existed before 1 October 2009.

Section 3(3D) — Control Over Sale and Movement of Sugar

The Central Government can direct that no producer, importer, or exporter of sugar can sell, give away, or move sugar from factory storage or warehouses without following specific government directions.

Exception: This rule does not prevent a producer or importer from pledging sugar as collateral to a scheduled bank. However, even in such a case, the bank may only sell the pledged sugar with the prior permission of the Central Government.

Section 3(4) — Appointment of an Authorised Controller

If the Central Government believes it is necessary to maintain or increase production of an essential commodity, it can appoint a person — called an authorised controller — to oversee and manage part or all of a business involved in producing that commodity.

The authorised controller must:

  1. Follow government instructions — but cannot give directions that go against any law governing the management of the business, unless the order expressly permits it.
  2. Manage the business — every manager and person in charge must comply with the controller's directions.

Section 3(5) — Notification of Orders

Any order made under Section 3 must be communicated as follows:

  1. General orders: Must be published in the Official Gazette.
  2. Orders directed at individuals:
    1. By delivering or handing it directly to the individual; or
    2. If direct delivery is not possible, by affixing it to the outer door or a visible part of the person's premises. A written report must be prepared and signed by two witnesses from the neighbourhood.

Section 3(6) — Parliament Oversight

  • Every order made under Section 3 must be laid before both Houses of Parliament after it is issued. This ensures that elected lawmakers can review the government's regulatory actions.

Section 4 — Imposition of Duties on State Governments

  • An order under Section 3 can delegate powers and responsibilities to the Central Government, State Governments, or their officers.
  • It also allows such orders to give directions to government officials on how to exercise their powers.
  • In simple terms, this section allows both Central and State government bodies to implement and enforce Section 3 orders at the ground level.

Section 5 — Delegation of Powers

The Central Government can, through an official order, allow certain powers given to it under Section 3 to also be exercised by:

  1. Officers or authorities under the Central Government — lower-ranking officials or departments.
  2. State Governments or their officers.

The order will clearly specify which powers are being delegated and under what conditions.

Section 6 — Effect of Orders Inconsistent with Other Laws

If an order is made under Section 3, it will override any conflicting rules or laws that are not part of this Act. So even if another law says something different, the Essential Commodities Act order will prevail.

Section 6A — Confiscation of Essential Commodities

(1) Seizure and Reporting

  • If essential commodities are seized under a Section 3 order, a report must be made promptly to the Collector.
  • After inspection, if the Collector finds that an order was violated, they can direct the confiscation of:
  1. The seized essential commodity itself;
  2. The containers (packages or boxes) holding the goods; and
  3. Any vehicles, animals, or vessels used to transport the goods.

Exception 1 — Farmers: Foodgrains or edible oilseeds seized from a producer (farmer) cannot be confiscated if they are owned by the farmer for personal use.

Exception 2 — Commercial transporters: If an animal, vehicle, or vessel was being used for hire (commercial transport), the owner may choose to pay a fine equal to the market value of the seized goods, instead of having the vehicle or animal confiscated.

(2) Sale of Seized Goods

If the seized goods are perishable or if the public interest requires it, the Collector may sell them:

  1. At the controlled price, if one has been fixed; or
  2. At a public auction, if no price is fixed.

For foodgrains, the Collector may sell them through fair price shops at prices fixed by the Central or State Government.

(3) How Sale Proceeds Are Handled

The money from the sale of seized goods is paid to the owner if:

  1. The Collector decides not to confiscate the goods; or
  2. The confiscation order is overturned on appeal; or
  3. The person is acquitted (found not guilty) in a related court case.

Section 6B — Show-Cause Notice Before Confiscation

No confiscation order can be passed against any person without first giving them a written show-cause notice — stating the grounds for confiscation and giving them a fair opportunity to respond in writing. This protects individuals from arbitrary action.

Section 6C — Appeal Against Confiscation Order

(1) Appeal Process

Anyone aggrieved by a confiscation order under Section 6A may appeal to the judicial authority (District and Sessions Judge) appointed by the State Government, within one month of being informed of the order. The court can confirm, modify, or cancel the confiscation order.

(2) Compensation After Modification or Acquittal

If the order is modified or cancelled, or if the person is acquitted, and the goods cannot be returned, the person is entitled to compensation — calculated as if the goods were sold to the government, plus reasonable interest from the date of seizure.

The price is determined based on the type of goods:

  1. Foodgrains, edible oilseeds or edible oils — as per Section 3(3B).
  2. Sugar — as per Section 3(3C).
  3. Other essential commodities — as per Section 3(3).

Section 6D — Confiscation Does Not Bar Other Punishments

  • If the Collector orders confiscation of goods, this does not protect the person from other legal punishments.
  • Confiscation is separate from — and does not prevent — penalties like imprisonment or fines under Section 7.

Section 6E — Bar of Jurisdiction in Certain Cases

  1. When essential commodities (or related vehicles, animals, or packaging) are seized and are pending confiscation, only the Collector (or the State Government) has authority over those goods.
  2. No other court, tribunal, or authority can interfere with — or make any decision about — who gets the goods, what happens to them, or when they are returned or disposed of.
  3. This rule applies even if other laws say otherwise.

Section 7 — Penalties for Violation

Punishment for Violations

Any person who violates an order issued under Section 3 is punishable under Section 7 as follows:

  • Imprisonment: Minimum 3 months, up to 7 years.
  • Fine: May be imposed in addition to imprisonment.
  • Confiscation: Essential commodities, vehicles, or packaging may be seized.
  • Licence cancellation: Business operations may be suspended or cancelled.
  • Repeat offences: Attract stricter punishment.

Courts may reduce punishment below the minimum only for special reasons. Mens rea (intention) is not required for prosecution under this section.

Hoarding & Black Marketing

Hoarding essential commodities to sell at higher prices is prohibited under Section 3 read with Section 7.

  • Punishment: Imprisonment up to 7 years and/or fine.
  • Strict action is taken especially during emergencies involving food, fuel, or medicines.

Exceeding Stock Limits

  • Keeping more stock than the prescribed limit is an offence under Section 3(2)(f) read with Section 7.
  • Punishment includes imprisonment, fine, and confiscation of excess stock.

Violating Price Control Orders

  • Selling essential commodities above government-fixed prices is a punishable offence.
  • Punishment: Imprisonment and/or fine.

Production, Supply & Distribution Violations

  • Any irregularity in production, supply, or distribution of essential commodities is punishable.
  • Punishment: Imprisonment and/or fine.

Non-Compliance with Control Orders

  • Failing to comply with government control orders (e.g., not declaring stock, violating ration rules) is an offence.
  • Punishment: Up to 7 years imprisonment and/or fine.

False Information or Misrepresentation (Section 9)

  • Providing incorrect stock data or price details is punishable.
  • Punishment: Imprisonment up to 5 years and/or fine.

Adulteration or Substandard Goods

  • Selling adulterated or poor-quality essential commodities is a serious offence.
  • Punishment: Imprisonment, fine, seizure of goods, and/or licence cancellation.

Section 7A — Recovery of Amounts as Arrears of Land Revenue

  • Unpaid amounts under this Act may be recovered with 15% simple interest per annum.
  • Recovery is treated in the same way as land revenue or public demand — a quick and effective process.
  • No court may issue an order restraining such recovery.
  • If any recovery is found to be invalid, it must be refunded with interest.

Section 8 — Attempts and Abetment

This section ensures that the law punishes not just those who commit violations, but also those who try to commit them or help others do so.

  1. Attempt to violate: If someone tries to break an order under Section 3 — even if the attempt is not completed — the law treats it as if the violation already happened.
  2. Abetment: If someone encourages, assists, or incites another person to violate an order, they too are treated as having violated the order.

Both the person who attempts the violation and the one who abets it can be held legally responsible and face the same penalties as the actual offender.

Section 9 — False Statement

A person who:

  1. Knowingly provides false information when required to give information under any Section 3 order; or
  2. Records a false entry in any book, account, declaration, return, or other document required to be maintained or submitted under this Act;

…shall be punishable with:

  • Imprisonment up to five years; or
  • A fine; or
  • Both.

Section 10 — Offences by Companies

(1) If a company violates an order under Section 3, every person who was in charge of and responsible for the company's day-to-day operations at the time of the violation will also be considered guilty. However, if a person can prove that the violation happened without their knowledge or that they took all reasonable steps to prevent it, they will not be punished.

(2) If the violation was caused by the approval, assistance, or negligence of a director, manager, or other officer of the company, that person will also be guilty and can be punished.

Section 10A — Offences to Be Cognizable

  • Every offence under this Act is a cognizable offence.
  • This means the police can arrest the accused without a warrant and begin an investigation without first obtaining court permission.
  • This allows immediate action to be taken against violations.

Section 10B — Publication of Information About Convicted Companies

(1) When a company is convicted under this Act, the court may order certain details about the company to be made public — its name, place of business, the nature of the offence, and the fact of conviction. This is published at the company's own expense in newspapers or in any manner the court directs.

(2) Publication cannot happen until the time limit for appeal has passed, or until any appeal has been decided.

(3) The cost of publication is treated as a fine imposed by the court and recovered from the company accordingly.

Section 10C — Presumption of Culpable Mental State

(1) In prosecutions under this Act where a guilty mind (culpable mental state) is required to prove an offence, the court will presume that the accused had such a mental state. The accused must prove they did not have it.

(2) "Culpable mental state" includes intention, motive, knowledge of a fact, and belief or reason to believe in something that makes an act unlawful.

(3) In court, a fact is considered proven only if the judge is fully convinced beyond a reasonable doubt — not merely if it seems likely.

Section 11 — Cognizance of Offences

No court can take cognizance of (that is, formally begin proceedings for) any offence under this Act unless a written complaint of the facts has been made by:

  1. A public servant (as defined under Section 21 of the Indian Penal Code); or
  2. Any person aggrieved by the offence; or
  3. A recognised consumer association, whether or not the person making the report is a member of that association.

Who Is a Public Servant?

Under Section 21 of the IPC, public servants include judges, government officers, police officers, municipal employees, and anyone performing official duties on behalf of the government.

Recognised Consumer Association

A voluntary association registered under the Companies Act, 1956 or any corresponding law — such as consumer guidance societies or NGOs working in the field of consumer rights.

Section 12 — Special Provision Regarding Fine

Notwithstanding the limits on fines set by Section 29 of the Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973, this section empowers certain Magistrates to impose fines exceeding ₹5,000 for violations of Section 3 orders. This power may be exercised by:

  • A Metropolitan Magistrate; or
  • A Judicial Magistrate of the First Class, specially empowered by the State Government.

Section 12A — Power to Try Summarily

(1) Summary Trial

  • The Central Government may, by notification in the Official Gazette, declare certain orders under Section 3 as "special orders" eligible for summary trial — a faster, simplified procedure.

(2) Duration of Notification

  • The notification remains in force for two years unless cancelled earlier. Cases already begun under the notification continue to be tried summarily after expiry.

(3) Competent Authorities

  • First Class Judicial Magistrates or Metropolitan Magistrates specially empowered by the State Government may conduct summary trials, following the procedure in Sections 262–265 of the CrPC, 1973.

(4) Maximum Punishment in Summary Trial

  • Imprisonment up to 1 year; or
  • Fine up to ₹5,000; or
  • Both. If more severe punishment seems warranted, the case may be converted to a regular trial.

(5) Restriction on Appeals

  • No appeal lies if the sentence is imprisonment up to one month and fine up to ₹2,000. Appeals are permitted if the sentence exceeds these limits.

(6) Pending Cases

  • Cases pending at the time the provision comes into force — where no witnesses have yet been examined — shall be tried summarily. If the Magistrate is not empowered for summary trial, the case is transferred to a competent Magistrate.

Section 12B — Grant of Injunction by Civil Courts

  • No civil court can grant an injunction (a court order stopping someone from doing something) that would restrain the Central Government, State Government, or any public officer from taking action under this Act.
  • Any application for such an injunction can only be made after giving prior notice to the relevant government or officer.

Section 13 — Presumption as to Orders

When an order made under this Act is produced before a court, the court shall presume that it was duly made by the competent authority. The burden of proof lies on the person challenging the order — they must show that it was not issued by a competent authority or was improperly made.

This presumption is based on Section 114 of the Indian Evidence Act, 1872, which permits courts to presume the existence of certain facts unless disproved.

Illustration

If a government officer issues an order under this Act and it is challenged in court, the court will presume that the officer acted lawfully and within authority unless the challenger provides evidence to the contrary.

Section 14 — Burden of Proof in Certain Cases

  • Where an accused person is alleged to have done an act without the required permit, licence, or authorisation, the burden of proving that such authorisation existed lies on the accused.
  • This is an exception to the general rule of criminal law, where the prosecution normally bears the burden of proving guilt.

Section 15 — Protection of Action Taken Under the Act

(1) Protection for Individuals

No suit, prosecution, or other legal proceeding shall lie against any person for anything done in good faith pursuant to an order under Section 3.

(2) Protection for the Government

No legal proceeding shall lie against the Government for any damage caused by actions taken in good faith under this Act.

Section 15A — Prosecution of Public Servants

No court can take cognizance of an offence alleged to have been committed by a public servant while acting in the discharge of their official duties under Section 3 — without prior sanction from:

  • The Central Government, if the public servant works under the Union Government; or
  • The State Government, if the public servant works under the State Government.

Why This Matters

  1. It encourages effective enforcement without fear of personal liability.
  2. It protects honest officials from harassment lawsuits.
  3. Protection applies only when actions are taken in good faith and within lawful authority.

Illustration

If a Civil Supplies Officer seizes wheat stock believing it violates a Section 3 order, and it later turns out the trader had a valid permit, the officer cannot be prosecuted — provided the action was taken honestly and within the scope of official powers.

Section 16 — Repeals and Savings

(1) Repeals

The following earlier laws were repealed by this Act:

  • (a) The Essential Commodities Ordinance, 1955.
  • (b) Any other law in any State that previously controlled production, supply, distribution, or trade in essential commodities.

(2) Savings

  • Any orders, licences, permits, or appointments made under the repealed laws before the commencement of this Act remain valid and effective.
  • They are treated as if they were made under this Act and continue until replaced by new orders or licences issued under this Act.

(3) Application of the General Clauses Act, 1897

The repeal is subject to Section 6 of the General Clauses Act, 1897, which ensures continuity of legal proceedings and rights despite the change in law.

Landmark Judgments

1. Nathu Lal v. State of Madhya Pradesh (AIR 1966 SC 43)

Facts

  • The appellant, Nathu Lal, was a foodgrain dealer in Dhar, Madhya Pradesh.
  • He was found storing 885 maunds and 2¼ seers of wheat without a licence as required under Section 7 of the Act.
  • He claimed he had applied for the required licence and believed it would be granted based on assurances from government officers.

Held

  • The Supreme Court held that mens rea (guilty intention) is an essential element for proving an offence under Section 7.
  • Even though this is welfare legislation, the requirement of intention cannot simply be ignored.
  • Since Nathu Lal lacked a guilty intention, he was acquitted despite a technical violation of the law.

2. State of Madhya Pradesh v. Narayan Singh & Others (AIR 1989 SC 1789)

Facts

  • The respondents were truck drivers, cleaners, and helpers who were caught transporting fertilizer bags from Indore to Maharashtra without the required permit under the Fertilizers (Movement Control) Order, 1973.
  • They had invoices but no permits, and claimed ignorance of the permit requirement and of the nature of the goods.

Held

  • Section 7 was amended in 1967 to include the words "whether knowingly, intentionally, or otherwise" — introducing the principle of strict liability.
  • Under strict liability, intent or knowledge is not always required to establish guilt.
  • However, the Supreme Court found that the prosecution had failed to establish wrongful intent or negligence on the part of the respondents. No punishment was imposed.

Conclusion

  • The Essential Commodities Act, 1955 is a vital piece of legislation designed to protect the public interest by regulating the production, supply, and pricing of essential goods.
  • The Act gives wide powers to the Central Government to ensure equitable distribution and to prevent hoarding and black marketing.
  • Early judicial decisions stressed the need for mens rea (guilty intention), but later amendments introduced strict liability for certain offences.
  • Despite this shift, courts continue to demand clear and convincing evidence before convicting anyone under the Act.
  • The 2020 Amendment introduced important safeguards — restricting government intervention in markets except in genuine extraordinary circumstances — thereby balancing consumer protection with the freedom of farmers and traders.
