What Is DCF? — The Most Important Idea in Investing
Explained with a Simple ₹100 Example Anyone Can Understand

Imagine someone offers you two choices. Choice A: I give you ₹1,000 today. Choice B: I give you ₹1,000 three years from now. Which would you prefer? Almost everyone picks Choice A — and they are right to. The ₹1,000 you receive today can be invested in a fixed deposit, a mutual fund, or even a savings account and will grow to more than ₹1,000 in three years. Future money is worth less than present money. This single insight is the entire foundation of DCF valuation.

Now apply this to a company. A business generates cash flows every year — money left over after paying all its costs, taxes, and reinvestment needs. Those future cash flows are valuable to the owner (i.e., the shareholder). But how valuable? DCF answers this by calculating what those future cash flows are worth in today's money — their present value. Add them all up, and you get the intrinsic value of the business. Compare that to the market price, and you know whether the stock is undervalued, overvalued, or fairly priced.

Intrinsic value can be defined simply: it is the discounted value of the cash that can be taken out of a business during its remaining life.

— Warren Buffett, Berkshire Hathaway Annual Letter, 1992
The DCF Formula
Intrinsic Value  =   Σ [ FCFt ÷ (1+r)t ]  +  Terminal Value ÷ (1+r)n
FCF = Free Cash Flow each year  |  r = Discount Rate (your required return)  |  t = Year number  |  n = Final projection year
2
The Six Building Blocks — Know These Before You Calculate
Revenue · EBITDA · PAT · Free Cash Flow · Present Value · Terminal Value

Think of DCF as a six-storey building. Each floor is built on the one below it. You cannot get to the top floor (intrinsic value) without understanding each level. Here they are, explained in plain language:

📈
Floor 1 — Revenue (Sales)
The total money a company earns from selling its products or services. It is the starting point of every financial model. We project how fast revenue will grow each year.
Revenue = Units Sold × Price per Unit
💰
Floor 2 — EBITDA
Earnings Before Interest, Taxes, Depreciation, and Amortisation. Think of it as operating profit before the accountants deduct non-cash items. The EBITDA margin (%) tells you how profitable the core business is.
EBITDA = Revenue × EBITDA Margin %
🏦
Floor 3 — PAT (Net Profit)
Profit After Tax — what is left for shareholders after deducting interest payments, depreciation, and income tax. This is the "bottom line." PAT margin = PAT ÷ Revenue.
PAT = EBITDA − D&A − Interest − Tax
💵
Floor 4 — Free Cash Flow (FCF)
The actual cash the business generates for its owners — after paying for capital expenditure (building factories, buying equipment) and working capital changes. FCF is what you actually discount in DCF. PAT + D&A − CapEx − ΔWC.
FCF = PAT + D&A − CapEx − ΔWC
📉
Floor 5 — Present Value (PV)
Each year's FCF is discounted back to today's value using the discount rate (also called WACC — Weighted Average Cost of Capital). This accounts for the fact that future money is worth less than present money.
PV = FCF ÷ (1 + r)t
∞
Floor 6 — Terminal Value (TV)
A business does not stop at Year 5 or Year 10. Terminal Value captures all future cash flows beyond the projection period. It is typically the largest component of DCF. Usually 60–80% of intrinsic value comes from TV.
TV = FCFn × (1+g) ÷ (r − g)
What Is the Discount Rate (WACC)?

The discount rate (r) reflects the risk of the investment and your required rate of return. For Indian equities, analysts typically use 10–14% as the discount rate. A higher discount rate means higher risk (or higher opportunity cost) — which makes future cash flows worth less today. A stable, large-cap company might use 10–11%; a high-growth, higher-risk company might use 12–14%.

The terminal growth rate (g) is the rate at which FCF grows forever after the projection period. For Indian companies, a 4–6% long-term growth rate is typical — roughly equal to India's long-term nominal GDP growth rate minus company-specific factors.

Key Insight — StableCo India: Overvalued in This Scenario

StableCo's market price (₹390) is above the DCF intrinsic value (₹350) — suggesting the stock is overvalued by about 10% under these assumptions. Critical Lesson: DCF Is Extremely Sensitive to Small Input Changes

Look at the table above. With base case assumptions (g=5%, r=11%), intrinsic value = ₹355. But if you just change the discount rate to 9% (keeping everything else the same), intrinsic value jumps to ₹445 — a 25% increase. If you change the terminal growth rate from 5% to 7% (with r=11%), it jumps to ₹491.

This is the fundamental challenge with DCF: small, seemingly reasonable changes in assumptions lead to dramatically different valuations. This is why DCF should never be used alone — always triangulate with other methods: P/E ratios, EV/EBITDA multiples, dividend discount models, and qualitative judgement about the business.

Critical Observations — What DCF Gets Right and Where It Fails
Honest Assessment of the Most Powerful — and Most Misused — Valuation Tool
1
Terminal Value Dominance — The "Far Future" Problem

In all three examples, the Terminal Value accounted for 79–82% of the total enterprise value. This means most of the "value" in a DCF model comes from what happens after your projection period — from Year 6 onwards, theoretically forever. The uncomfortable truth: we have very little ability to predict what a company's cash flows will look like 10 or 20 years from now. This makes DCF most reliable for stable, predictable businesses (utilities, FMCG, telecoms) and least reliable for high-growth or cyclical companies.

2
The WACC Assumption — Where Analysts Disagree Most

The Weighted Average Cost of Capital (WACC) requires knowing the cost of equity, cost of debt, and capital structure. The cost of equity itself is derived from the Capital Asset Pricing Model (CAPM): Cost of Equity = Risk-Free Rate + Beta × Equity Risk Premium. For India, analysts typically use the 10-year Government of India bond yield (~7%) as the risk-free rate and an equity risk premium of 4–6%. A beta of 1.0 means the stock moves with the market. But betas change over time, risk premiums are debated, and CAPM itself has well-documented limitations. The discount rate is as much art as science.

3
Free Cash Flow vs. Net Profit — Why FCF Matters More

Many retail investors focus on PAT (net profit) when evaluating stocks. DCF teaches a more sophisticated lesson: it is Free Cash Flow that matters, not reported profit. A company can show high PAT but generate little or no FCF if it requires heavy CapEx (like a steel plant or airport) or if its working capital keeps expanding as the business grows. Conversely, an asset-light business (like an IT company or consumer brand) converts a high percentage of profits into actual cash, making its FCF much closer to its PAT. This is why asset-light businesses command premium valuations in DCF.

4
Margin of Safety — Benjamin Graham's Most Important Concept

Benjamin Graham, the father of value investing, insisted on buying stocks only when the market price is significantly below the intrinsic value — creating a "margin of safety." The purpose: to protect against errors in your DCF assumptions. If your model is even 20% wrong (which is likely), a stock bought at a 30% discount to intrinsic value still makes money. The margin of safety is not a guarantee — it is insurance against the uncertainty baked into every DCF model. Most value investors require at least a 20–30% margin of safety before investing. TechCo India (18% MoS in our example) is borderline; GrowthCo and StableCo do not qualify under this strict standard.

Key Takeaways

What You Have Learned — Summary

💡
The Core Idea
DCF values a company by calculating what its future cash flows are worth in today's money. Sum those present values and add the Terminal Value — that's intrinsic value. Compare with market price to decide if the stock is worth buying.
📊
FCF Is King
Revenue → EBITDA → PAT → Free Cash Flow. The most important number in DCF is FCF — actual cash left after running the business and investing for growth. High PAT with low FCF is a red flag.
⚠️
Terminal Value Dominates
80%+ of DCF value typically comes from Terminal Value. Be conservative with your terminal growth rate assumption. Even 1% change in TV growth rate can change intrinsic value by 20–40%.
🎯
Always Sensitivity Test
Never use one set of assumptions. Always run a sensitivity table varying WACC (±2%) and terminal growth rate (±2%) to see the range of possible intrinsic values. Make your investment decision based on the range, not a single number.
🛡️
Demand a Margin of Safety
Only buy when the market price is meaningfully below your intrinsic value estimate — ideally 20–30% below. This cushion protects you from errors in your own model and from market mispricing reversion timing.
🔗
Don't Use DCF Alone
DCF is powerful but imprecise. Always use it alongside relative valuation (P/E, EV/EBITDA, P/B) and qualitative assessment of business quality — competitive moat, management quality, industry tailwinds.
Conclusion

The Story's Moral — Investing Is an Art Grounded in Math

DCF valuation is simultaneously the most theoretically correct method of valuing a business and the most practically challenging to execute well. It forces you to think deeply about a company's future — its competitive position, growth trajectory, capital requirements, and risk profile. In doing that thinking, you build the judgment that separates great investors from average ones.

Our three Indian examples illustrated the breadth of outcomes DCF can produce: TechCo India (asset-light IT, undervalued), StableCo India (FMCG defensive, slightly overvalued by our assumptions), and GrowthCo India (pharma high-growth, fairly priced). The numbers are illustrative, but the logic is real and universal.

The Beginner's Starting Point

If you are new to investing, start with these three steps. First: read a company's Annual Report and understand its business model. Second: estimate its revenue growth rate for the next 5 years based on industry trends and competitive position. Third: use a conservative DCF (high discount rate, low terminal growth) to estimate intrinsic value. If the stock trades at a significant discount — you may have found a worthwhile investment. If it doesn't — be patient and wait.

As Warren Buffett said: "It is far better to buy a wonderful company at a fair price than a fair company at a wonderful price." DCF helps you recognise both.
