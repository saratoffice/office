Background & Facts

Factual Matrix

The genesis of this appeal lies in a tragic domestic fire incident. Rashid Khan (Respondent No. 4), the son of the appellants, was the registered consumer of a domestic LPG gas connection (Consumer No. 1848894) supplied through M/s Sharda Gas Company, a dealer of Indian Oil Corporation. The registered address for the connection was River View Colony, Near ITI, Bilaspur — however, the cylinder was actually being used at Tirthani Gali, Sarkanda, Bilaspur, where Rashid Khan resided along with his younger brother Rahees Khan.

On 17 November 2015, Rahees Khan — a post-graduate in Economics, pursuing a Ph.D. from Central University Bilaspur, and a qualified ITI and B.Ed. holder who had even cleared the CTET and Patwari examinations — purchased a refilled gas cylinder (Receipt No. 104618) from the dealer's godown. Upon lighting the gas stove for cooking, the cylinder allegedly caught fire due to gas leakage, reportedly caused by the absence of a safety washer. The resulting fire spread across the room. Rahees Khan suffered severe burns over approximately 70% of his body. Despite being rushed to Jawahar Lal Nehru Hospital and Research Centre, Bhilai, he succumbed to his injuries on 22 November 2015.

The appellants, claiming to be legal successors of the deceased, lodged a consumer complaint before the State Consumer Disputes Redressal Commission, Chhattisgarh, seeking compensation of ₹50 lakhs towards losses, ₹10 lakhs for mental agony, interest at 18% per annum, and costs. The State Commission dismissed the complaint vide order dated 03 June 2017, giving rise to the present first appeal before the NCDRC.

Legal Issues

Core Questions of Law

I
Whether the deceased Rahees Khan — brother of the registered consumer — qualifies as a "consumer" under Section 2(1)(d) of the Consumer Protection Act, 1986, so as to confer locus standi on his parents to file the complaint?
II
Whether the fire incident was caused due to a defective LPG cylinder (specifically, absence of the safety washer) supplied by the respondents, thereby establishing deficiency of service?
III
Whether the repudiation of the insurance claim by United India Insurance Company (Respondent No. 3) on the ground that the accident did not occur at the registered premises was valid?
IV
Whether the parents (appellants) have any independent locus standi to file a consumer complaint under the Act when the registered consumer himself (Respondent No. 4) is alive and was impleaded as a party?
Arguments Advanced

Contentions of the Parties

Appellants' Submissions

Counsel Sh. R.K. Bhawnani advanced that the term "hire" under Section 2(1)(d)(ii) is not statutorily defined and should be interpreted expansively to mean "avail" or "use." He argued that the deceased, as a beneficiary of the gas connection availed by his brother (the registered consumer) with the latter's knowledge and tacit approval, squarely falls within the inclusive definition of "consumer." Reliance was placed upon the Supreme Court's landmark ruling in M/s Spring Meadows Hospital & Anr. v. Harjot Ahluwalia, AIR 1998 SC 1809, which held that a beneficiary of services is also a consumer under the Act.

The appellants emphatically urged that the Nuksani Panchanama prepared by the police immediately after the incident — which recorded the absence of the safety rubber from the cylinder's regulator — was a vital piece of evidence wrongly ignored by the State Commission. Counsel also drew support from the NCDRC's own earlier decision in Indian Oil Corporation Limited v. Late Om Prakash Seth & Others (17 September 2014) to bolster the argument on consumer locus standi in LPG fire cases.

Respondents' Contentions

Respondent No. 2 (IOC) countered that the spot inspection conducted by its Field Officer/Assistant Manager on 11 February 2016 revealed that only 200 grams of gas was short in the cylinder, strongly suggesting no gas leakage from the cylinder. There was no burn mark on the cylinder. The stove was being used on the floor rather than at the prescribed height above the cylinder, pointing to negligence on the part of the deceased. The investigation further indicated that the deceased was using a smaller, market-procured cylinder — which was the likely cause of the fire.

Respondent No. 3 (United India Insurance) defended its repudiation on the ground that the gas connection was registered at River View Colony but was being used unauthorisedly at Sarkanda — a different address — in violation of the terms of the insurance policy and applicable rules. All respondents jointly argued that the appellants had failed to discharge the onus of proving that the fire was caused by the LPG cylinder supplied by the dealer.

The Decision

Reasoning of the National Commission

The NCDRC, speaking through Justice Dr. Sudhir Kumar Jain, upheld the finding of the State Commission that the appellants lacked locus standi as consumers under the Consumer Protection Act, 1986. The Commission's reasoning rested on a careful, contextually-sensitive reading of Section 2(1)(d).

While the Commission acknowledged — and agreed with — the broad proposition that a gas cylinder/connection is necessarily registered in the name of one family member but is commonly used by others, and that the definition of "consumer" must not be given an unduly restrictive interpretation, it drew a critical distinction. The Commission accepted that family members using a gas connection with the registered consumer's approval are broadly consumers under the Act. However, it held that such an expansive reading could not be stretched to allow third parties other than the registered consumer to file an independent complaint when the registered consumer himself is alive and before the court.

The present complaint is not filed by the respondent no. 4 being the registered consumer…the definition of the consumer cannot be extended to such extent to include any family member when the actual consumer is available.

— NCDRC, per Dr. Sudhir Kumar Jain, J. (Para 18)

The Commission distinguished Spring Meadows on facts: in that case, the parents filed a complaint on behalf of their minor child who was the actual consumer — it was a case of a helpless beneficiary who could not sue independently. In the present case, the deceased was the brother of the registered consumer, not the consumer himself. Crucially, Respondent No. 4 — the actual registered consumer — was alive, present before the Commission, and had even been impleaded as a party. The appellants were not filing on behalf of the registered consumer, but were asserting an independent right arising from the death of the deceased (who was not the registered consumer). This, the Commission held, fell outside the legislative scheme of the Act.

On the factual question of the fire's cause, the Commission noted — without disturbing the State Commission's findings — that the respondents' evidence (particularly the spot inspection report and the statements of Shashank Jaiswal and Vikram Kumar) sufficiently rebutted the allegation of cylinder defect. Having dismissed the appeal on the preliminary ground of locus standi, the Commission rightly found it unnecessary to adjudicate the remaining factual disputes.

Final Verdict
⚖ Operative Order

The First Appeal No. 1532 of 2017 was dismissed. The NCDRC affirmed the impugned order of the State Commission dated 03 June 2017. The Commission, however, preserved the appellants' liberty to initiate appropriate legal proceedings before a civil court for claiming compensation, with the benefit of limitation as may be permissible under law.

Critical Analysis

Commentary & Observations

①
On the "Consumer" Definition — A Purposive vs. Literal Tension

The Commission's approach highlights an enduring tension in consumer jurisprudence between purposive construction (which would extend protection to all genuine users of a service) and the textual boundary of Section 2(1)(d). The finding that the appellants lacked locus standi — not because the deceased was not a "user" of the gas connection, but because the registered consumer is alive and available — is a pragmatically sound but arguably limiting approach. It leaves open the critical question: had Respondent No. 4 also died in the fire, would the outcome have been different?

②
Distinguishing Spring Meadows — A Nuanced but Consequential Line

The distinction drawn between Spring Meadows (minor child — parents as natural representatives) and the present case (adult brother of the registered consumer — parents of the deceased) is legally sound. However, it underscores a gap in the Act: where a non-registered family member dies due to defective services, and the registered consumer either refuses or fails to bring a complaint, the bereaved family may be left without a remedy under consumer law. This lacuna arguably calls for legislative attention or a purposive judicial interpretation in an appropriate future case.

③
The Panchanama Evidence — A Missed Opportunity?

The Nuksani Panchanama prepared by the police on 17 February 2016 — which specifically noted the absence of the safety washer from the cylinder regulator — was a contemporaneous official document of considerable evidentiary weight. The State Commission's dismissal of this document in favour of the corporate investigation report prepared by IOC's own Field Officer raises legitimate concerns about the relative weight accorded to independent police documentation versus an interested party's internal report. The NCDRC, having dismissed on maintainability, did not re-examine this evidence — which may have warranted a different outcome on merits.

④
Insurance Repudiation — The "Registered Premises" Clause

Respondent No. 3's repudiation of the claim on the ground that the accident did not occur at the registered premises of the consumer reflects a contractual technicality that may disproportionately prejudice lower-income consumers who frequently change residences without formally updating their connection details with dealers. The validity of such repudiation clauses in the context of a public utility — where the insurer is effectively the safety net for the entire consumer base of a gas dealer — merits separate scrutiny by insurance regulators.

Legal Significance

Takeaways & Broader Implications

📋
Locus Standi
Consumer complaints must be filed by the registered consumer or a legally recognised beneficiary; third-party family members cannot independently invoke the Act when the registered consumer is available.
⚖️
Purposive Construction
While beneficial legislation demands a liberal reading, courts will not extend it to supplant the registered consumer's right to litigate — the Act has structural limits even in sympathetic factual matrices.
🔍
Evidentiary Weight
Official police documents (panchanama) should receive appropriate weight against interested-party investigation reports in establishing defective product liability in LPG fire cases.
🛡️
Insurance Liability
The validity of "registered premises" exclusion clauses in LPG consumer insurance policies remains an open question with significant implications for millions of consumers who informally change residences.
Conclusion

Summing Up

The NCDRC's decision in Hussain Khan v. M/s Sharda Gas Company is a measured, legally coherent ruling that respects the textual boundaries of the Consumer Protection Act, 1986 while gesturing towards its remedial purpose. The Commission correctly identified that the appellants' complaint failed not on the merits of the underlying tragedy, but on the fundamental jurisdictional precondition of being "consumers" under the Act.

That said, the case is a stark reminder of the inadequacies in the current legal framework for LPG fire accidents — particularly where the actual user of a domestic gas connection is not the registered subscriber. The Act has since been overhauled by the Consumer Protection Act, 2019, which introduced broader definitions and an expanded class of complainants. Whether the outcome would differ under the 2019 Act presents a worthwhile academic and practical inquiry. For the appellants, the NCDRC has preserved their civil remedy — the road to compensation, while longer, remains open.
