Statutory Framework

Applicable Laws & Orders

Section 7, Essential Commodities Act, 1955

Any person who contravenes any order made under Section 3 of the Act shall be punishable with imprisonment for a term which may extend to seven years, or with fine, or with both. The section covers violations of control orders relating to production, supply, distribution, and trade of essential commodities — including foodgrains, edible oils, sugar, and LPG.

The prosecution alleged violations of two subordinate control orders issued under the Essential Commodities Act:

PDS (Control) Order, 2001

Governs the functioning of Fair Price Shops (FPS) under the Public Distribution System. Mandates proper maintenance of stock registers, sale registers, and cash memos. Prohibits diversion, misappropriation, or sale of PDS commodities (rice, wheat, sugar) outside the authorised beneficiary list (APL/BPL/AAY cardholders).

Delhi Specified Articles (Regulation of Distribution) Order, 1981

A Delhi-specific order issued under the EC Act that regulates the distribution of specified food articles (SFA) — wheat, rice, and sugar — through licensed FPS dealers. Violations attract prosecution under Section 7 of the EC Act.

Factual Matrix

Background & Facts

FPS No. 9097 operating under the name M/s Mohit Store at Khasra No. 4/23/1, A-13, Main 25 Foota Road, Nathu Pura, Delhi, held a Fair Price Shop licence in the name of Smt. Vijaya Kumari. Satish Kumar functioned as the salesman at the said FPS. On 11 April 2007, a team of Food & Supply Department officials, accompanied by representatives of the Central Vigilance Committee on PDS, conducted an inspection of the premises.

11.04.2007 ~1:00 pm
Inspection team comprising Asst. Commissioner H.P. Meena, Inspector Rakesh Gupta (Circle 55), Inspectors Gopal Singh Kirola and Ashish Mishra (Enforcement Branch), and three CVC-PDS representatives visited FPS No. 9097. The team identified Satish Kumar as the salesman present.
Physical Verification
On checking the stock register, the stock of APL rice was shown as nil. However, physical inspection of the premises revealed 2.49 quintals (249 kg) of rice in loose/open condition and 3 kg of wheat flour (atta) — neither of which appeared in the records as supplied stock.

Legal Questions

Issues for Determination

I

Whether the 2.49 quintals of rice found at FPS No. 9097 was PDS-supplied stock (i.e., from the Food & Supply Department) and therefore capable of constituting misappropriation under the EC Act?

II

Whether the prosecution has proved a discrepancy or irregularity in the accounts (stock register, sale register, cash memos) maintained by the accused at the FPS?

III

Whether the failure to take a sample of the seized rice for comparison with the department-supplied variety is fatal to the prosecution case?

IV

Whether the non-examination of independent public witnesses — despite their availability at the time of inspection — creates a reasonable doubt that entitles the accused to acquittal?

Defence Case

Defence of the Accused

Both accused denied all allegations under Section 313 Cr.P.C. and claimed false implication. Their primary defence was that the rice found at the FPS was privately purchased from the open market and was not PDS-supplied stock. To substantiate this, the defence examined DW-1 Vinod Kumar, the alleged seller.

Collapse of Defence Witness

DW-1's testimony fell apart entirely in cross-examination. He admitted that he never disclosed the alleged sale to the investigating officer, the Food & Supply Department officers, or the court at any prior stage of the investigation or trial. He produced no original receipt, no trade licence, and no documentary evidence of dealing in rice. He further admitted that he does not deal in rice at present. The court treated this testimony as concocted. However — crucially — the prosecution's own case also independently collapsed on evidentiary grounds, rendering the quality of the defence testimony secondary to the overall assessment of reasonable doubt.

Judicial Analysis

Appreciation of Evidence & Findings

Ground I — No Sample Taken: Identity of Rice Unproven

The most significant evidentiary lacuna in the prosecution case was the complete failure to preserve a sample of the seized rice for comparison with the variety supplied by the Food & Supply Department. PW-1, PW-3, and PW-4 all admitted in cross-examination that no separate sample was taken from the FPS at the time of inspection.

Why Sampling Was Critical

The prosecution's entire case rested on the allegation that the 2.49 quintals of rice found at the FPS was PDS-supplied stock that had been misappropriated — i.e., that it was the same rice issued under APL quotas but not distributed to card holders. Without a sample comparison, there is no scientific or documentary basis for this identification. The rice could have been privately sourced — as the accused indeed claimed. In the absence of sampling, the link between the found rice and the department-supplied rice was purely inferential and unverified.

Ground II — No Discrepancy in Accounts

Multiple prosecution witnesses — the very officers who conducted the inspection — positively admitted in cross-examination that the accounts maintained at FPS No. 9097 showed no discrepancy:

Consistent Account Books — Prosecution's Own Admission

PW-1 admitted that the sale register tallied with the cash memos and there was no discrepancy in the accounts maintained at the shop. PW-2 admitted that entries in the cash memos matched the stock register and sale register correctly. PW-4 admitted that cash memos were in accordance with the sale register and stock register up to 31 March 2007.

Additionally, the FPS had received no SFA supply for the month of April 2007 until the date of inspection — confirmed by all material witnesses. The nil stock shown in the register was therefore consistent with records and consistent with no supply having been made in April 2007.

Ground III — 3 kg Atta Not a Specified Food Article

A portion of the prosecution's allegation related to the 3 kg of wheat flour (atta) found at the FPS. PW-2, PW-3, and PW-4 all categorically admitted that atta does not come under the category of Specified Food Articles (SFA) and that it was not supplied by the Food & Supply Department. The prosecution effectively abandoned this part of its case through its own witnesses, and the court correctly noted that the atta allegation was entirely unsustainable.

Ground IV — No Complaint from Ration Cardholders

In a case alleging systematic misappropriation of PDS stock meant for the poor, the complete absence of any complaint from ration cardholders is a significant indicator. PW-1 admitted that no cardholder had lodged any complaint about supply of FPS material. PW-2 admitted that no ration cardholder had made any complaint from January 2007 till the inspection date. This strongly suggests that the FPS was, in fact, distributing entitlements to its beneficiaries.

Ground V — Non-Examination of Independent Witnesses

The inspection was conducted at approximately 1:00 pm on a busy commercial road (25 Foota Road, Nathu Pura) — a day-time inspection in a populated area. The prosecution failed to join any independent public witness despite their manifest availability. The IO (PW-9) admitted that he arrived at the spot in the evening (~7:30–8:00 pm) and no independent witness was examined at any stage. The court extensively relied upon the law on this point:

The fact that no independent witness though available, was not examined and not even an explanation was sought to be given for not examining such witness is a serious infirmity in the prosecution case.

— Supreme Court in Hem Raj v. State of Haryana, AIR 2005 SC 2110

Before conducting a search the concerned police officer is required to call upon some independent and respectable people of the locality to witness the search… if it is found that no attempt was even made by the concerned police officer to join with him some persons of the locality who were admittedly available to witness the recovery, it would affect the weight of evidence of the Police Officer.

— Supreme Court in Sahib Singh v. State of Punjab, AIR 1997 SC 2417
Precedents Applied

Legal Precedents Relied Upon

Hem Raj v. State of Haryana — AIR 2005 SC 2110

Unexplained omission to examine available independent witnesses is a serious infirmity, especially where the testimony of eye-witnesses raises doubts about their presence at the time of the occurrence.

Sahib Singh v. State of Punjab — AIR 1997 SC 2417

Where police officers made no attempt to join independent and respectable local persons as witnesses despite their availability, the weight of police evidence is adversely affected — though not its admissibility.

D. V. Shanmugham v. State of A.P. — AIR 1997 SC 2583

If independent witnesses were available but not examined, and only related witnesses testified, the prosecution case must be scrutinised with greater care and caution.

Roop Chand v. State of Haryana — CC Cases 3 (HC)

Where police failed to join independent witnesses despite availability, and failed to note names of those who refused, the explanation for non-joining is an afterthought and liable to be rejected.

Pawan Kumar v. The Delhi Administration — 1989 Cr.LJ 127 (Delhi)

At minimum, the IO should have deposed that an attempt was made to join public witnesses or that they refused. Absence of even this attempt is a circumstance throwing doubt on the recovery.

Gurbel Singh v. State of Punjab — 1991 Crl. Rev. No. 504 (P&H) & Dhanpat v. State of Punjab — 2000 CC Cases HC 52

Non-joining of independent witnesses has been held fatal to the prosecution case, and accused is entitled to benefit of doubt where no satisfactory explanation is given.

Massa Singh v. State of Punjab — 2000 (2) CC Cases HC 11

The IO was obliged to take assistance of independent witnesses from a public place to lend authenticity to the investigation conducted.

Final Verdict

Judgment & Order

  Operative Order — MM-02 (Central), Tis Hazari, Delhi

Having appreciated the totality of evidence, the court found that the prosecution had failed to prove the guilt of the accused persons beyond reasonable doubt. The benefit of doubt was accordingly extended to both accused.

The court's cumulative reasoning (Para 40):

  • No discrepancy found in records/accounts maintained at FPS No. 9097
  • No sample of seized rice taken to establish identity with department-supplied stock
  • No complaint lodged by any ration cardholder against the accused
  • No independent witnesses examined, despite availability at the inspection site
  • Atta allegation abandoned — admitted not to be a department-supplied SFA
  • FPS received no SFA in April 2007 till inspection — consistent with nil register entry

Both accused — Satish Kumar and Vijaya Kumari — are acquitted of all charges under Section 7 of the Essential Commodities Act. Bail bonds with surety under Section 437A Cr.P.C. accepted for six months.

Critical Commentary

Observations & Analysis

1
The Sampling Lacuna — A Structural Flaw in PDS Enforcement

The failure to take a sample of the seized rice is arguably the most significant lapse in this case — and it is not unique to it. In PDS enforcement cases, the identity of the commodity (whether the found rice is the same variety as the government-issued APL quota rice) is the foundational factual question. Without a preserved sample and forensic comparison, the allegation of misappropriation remains purely speculative. This is not a technicality — it goes to the heart of whether any offence was committed at all. Enforcement agencies handling EC Act cases must institutionalise mandatory sampling as a non-negotiable step in inspection protocol.

2
Accounts in Order — What the Prosecution's Witnesses Conceded

It is an unusual and damaging feature of this case that the prosecution's own witnesses — the inspecting officers — categorically admitted in cross-examination that the FPS accounts showed no discrepancy, and that no ration cardholder had ever complained. These admissions effectively supported the defence's position. When the very officials who conducted the raid acknowledge clean accounts, the prosecution faces an uphill task that it could not surmount. The Magistrate correctly drew an inference from these admissions that the accused had been distributing PDS material to card-holders without irregularity.

3
Non-Examination of Independent Witnesses — A Recurring Institutional Problem

The failure to join independent public witnesses to inspection/recovery proceedings is one of the most consistently litigated issues in criminal trials involving police or enforcement agencies. The Supreme Court has, across decades, unambiguously held that this failure — when unexplained — is fatal or at least seriously prejudicial to the prosecution. The inspection in this case was conducted in broad daylight at 1:00 pm on a busy main road. The inspecting team comprised multiple officials. There is no conceivable reason why at least one independent person from the locality could not have been associated. The court's reliance on seven separate precedents on this point underscores how entrenched — and how unaddressed — this problem remains in enforcement practice.

4
Delay — From Offence (2007) to Judgment (2020): Thirteen Years

The case was instituted in February 2008 for an offence committed in April 2007. The judgment was delivered in February 2020 — nearly thirteen years after the incident and over twelve years after institution. This extraordinary delay (though regrettably not unusual in lower criminal courts) has multiple consequences: witnesses' memories fade (PW-3 repeatedly said "I do not remember due to lapse of time"), documents lose context, and the trial's deterrent value is entirely negated. For EC Act enforcement — which is time-sensitive by nature — such delays are particularly counterproductive. The Criminal Procedure Code and subsequent procedural reforms have repeatedly emphasised speedy trial as a constitutional requirement under Article 21, but the systemic reality of pending caseloads in subordinate courts remains a persistent challenge.

5
Nil Stock in Register — An Ambiguous Indicator

One counterpoint worth noting: while the prosecution's case failed on the evidence available, the presence of 2.49 quintals of unrecorded rice at a shop whose register showed nil stock is an inherently suspicious circumstance. The defence explanation (privately purchased from Vinod General Store) collapsed completely under cross-examination. The rice could have been privately sourced; it could also have been diverted from a prior supply cycle not reflected in available records. The court correctly found that reasonable doubt existed — but the underlying facts leave some residual ambiguity. This underscores why forensic sampling and independent witnesses are indispensable, not merely procedural preferences.

Legal Significance

Takeaways & Broader Implications

🧪
Mandatory Sampling
In all EC Act / PDS enforcement cases, seized food articles must be sampled and separately preserved at the time of inspection for comparison with department-supplied stock. Failure to do so will typically prove fatal to the prosecution's case.
👥
Independent Witnesses
Inspections, searches, and recoveries conducted in public areas or commercial premises must be witnessed by independent local persons. The investigating/enforcement officer must make a genuine, documented effort to join such witnesses — not merely mention their unavailability as an afterthought.
📒
Account Verification
Prosecution must independently establish that account books are irregular or false — not simply rely on a physical excess of stock while its own witnesses confirm clean accounts. In-house account reconciliation by enforcement officers at the time of inspection, with documented findings, is essential.
⏱️
Trial Delays
A 13-year lag between offence and judgment fundamentally undermines EC Act enforcement. Speedy trial is constitutionally mandated. EC Act cases should be prioritised for special summary trial procedures where available under the Act.
📋
SFA Scope Clarity
Enforcement officers must be clear about what commodities constitute Specified Food Articles (SFA) under applicable orders. Including atta — a non-SFA — in the charge-sheet was a basic error that weakened the prosecution's credibility before the court.
⚖️
Standard of Proof
The constitutional standard — proof beyond reasonable doubt — applies fully to EC Act offences. This is not a civil or quasi-civil matter. Enforcement agencies should treat EC Act prosecutions with the same evidentiary rigour as other serious criminal cases and must ensure their investigation and documentation meets that standard.
Conclusion

Concluding Remarks

The acquittal of Satish Kumar and Vijaya Kumari in this case is a textbook illustration of how even a facially suspicious situation — a Fair Price Shop salesman found with 249 kg of unrecorded rice — can result in acquittal when the investigative apparatus fails to follow basic evidentiary protocols. The prosecution's failure on five independent grounds (no sampling, no account discrepancy, no SFA status for atta, no cardholder complaint, no independent witness) left the court with no viable path to conviction without violating the foundational criminal law principle of proof beyond reasonable doubt.

This case is not an indictment of the accused — the court itself noted the suspicious circumstances. It is an indictment of the enforcement and investigative process. The Essential Commodities Act was enacted to protect the food security of the poorest citizens. Its purpose is defeated not only by dishonest FPS operators, but equally by prosecutions that are assembled without the evidentiary discipline necessary to secure convictions in court. The lessons from this case — on sampling, independent witnessing, account scrutiny, and trial timelines — are institutional and systemic, and deserve attention from enforcement agencies and policymakers alike.
