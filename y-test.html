🛢️ Petroleum Storage & Transport Rules (India)

Simplified Guide Based on the Petroleum Act, 1934 & Petroleum Rules, 2002

Sarat Rout April 4, 2026 8 min read 1,250 views
Petroleum rules classify inflammable liquids into Class A, B, C with varying safety requirements

Regulatory Framework

Under the Petroleum Act, 1934 and the Petroleum Rules, 2002 (as amended), storage, transport, and handling of petroleum are strictly regulated by the Chief Controller of Explosives, Petroleum and Explosives Safety Organisation (PESO). The rules classify petroleum into three hazard classes based on flash point, each with distinct container requirements, air space rules, licence exemptions, and safety protocols.

Applicability: Whole of India, with special exemptions for Defence Forces.

🔴 Class A — Highly Inflammable

Examples: Petrol (Motor Spirit), Naphtha, Aviation Turbine Fuel

Flash Point < 23 °C

Container Requirements

Material

Must be tinned, galvanised, or rust-proof steel/iron, approved by the Chief Controller of Explosives. Glass bottles only up to 2.5 litres (lab use, specifically approved).

Design & Safety

Airtight screw plugs or caps. Must be leak-proof, durable, and maintained in good condition at all times.

Air Space

Minimum 5% free air space must be maintained inside every filled container to allow for thermal expansion.

Labelling

Must clearly display: "Petrol / Motor Spirit — Highly Inflammable"

Licence Exemption

≤ 30 L

No licence required for quantities up to 30 litres (not for sale). Storage conditions: glass/stoneware containers must not exceed 1 litre each; metal containers must not exceed 25 litres each.

Motor Vehicle Exemption

Fuel already in the vehicle tank → no licence needed. Up to 100 litres extra Class A petroleum may be stored for own vehicle use. If storing more than 30 L, it must be kept in an isolated location away from buildings.

Defence Forces Exception

Container rules under Class A do not apply to containers owned and operated by the Defence Forces of India.

🟡 Class B — Moderately Inflammable

Examples: Kerosene, Diesel (certain grades), Aviation Spirit

Flash Point 23 °C – 65 °C

Container Material

Must be approved steel or iron containers only.

Air Space

Minimum 5% free air space required in each container.

Dispatch & Delivery Rules

Petroleum Class B can only be lawfully delivered to:

Licence Holders

A valid licence holder or their legally authorised agent.

Port / Railway Authorities

Port authorities or railway administrations as per transit rules.

Exempt Persons

Any person legally authorised to possess petroleum without a licence.

Special Delivery Without Licence

Class B petroleum can be delivered without a licence if quantity ≤ 15,000 litres, stored in airtight approved containers. The receiver must dispose of or use the petroleum immediately. If quantity exceeds 2,500 litres, a dry-chemical fire extinguisher must be present.

Decantation Rule (Kerosene)

Permitted for licence holders only. Kerosene must be transferred into containers not exceeding 2,500 litres immediately upon decantation.

Licence Exemption

≤ 2,500 L

No licence required for storage or transport if total quantity ≤ 2,500 litres AND no single container exceeds 1,000 litres.

Exceptions (Class B)

Rules do not apply to Defence Forces of India, or to kerosene specifically supplied for licensed decantation purposes.

🟢 Class C — Less Volatile Petroleum

Examples: Furnace Oil, Lubricating Oil, High-Speed Diesel (certain grades)

Flash Point > 65 °C

Container Material

Must be approved steel or iron containers only.

Air Space

Minimum 3% free air space required — less than Class A & B due to lower volatility.

Licence Exemption

≤ 45,000 L

No licence required if total quantity stored or transported does not exceed 45,000 litres, provided containers comply with approved standards.

Exceptions (Class C)

Container rules do not apply to containers owned and operated by the Defence Forces of India.

⚠️ General Restrictions on Delivery — All Classes

Petroleum cannot be delivered to just anyone. The delivery must match: the receiver's licence type and permitted quantity. The sender is legally responsible for ensuring safe handling and legal compliance at the point of delivery.

Statutory References

These rules derive from the Petroleum Act, 1934 (Sections 4, 5, 6, 8, 11) and Petroleum Rules, 2002 (Chapters II, III, V, VI). The Chief Controller of Explosives has powers to enforce conditions, inspect premises, and grant exemptions in special circumstances.

Non-compliance attracts penalties including imprisonment and fines under Section 10 of the Petroleum Act.

Always verify the latest amendments through PESO (Petroleum and Explosives Safety Organisation) official notifications at peso.gov.in.
